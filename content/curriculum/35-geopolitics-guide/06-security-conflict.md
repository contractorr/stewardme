# Security and Conflict in Geopolitics

## Overview

Security competition remains central to geopolitics despite economic interdependence and institutional constraints. This chapter examines the spectrum of conflict from nuclear deterrence to hybrid warfare, exploring how states pursue security through military power, alliances, and unconventional means including terrorism, cyber warfare, and proxy conflicts.

### Deterrence Theory

**Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD)**:
- Both sides can inflict unacceptable damage even after absorbing first strike
- Requires survivable second-strike capability (submarines, mobile launchers)
- Stability: Neither side can win, therefore neither attacks

**Triad Components**:
1. **ICBMs** (Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles): Land-based silos, high readiness
2. **SLBMs** (Submarine-Launched): Most survivable, undetectable at sea
3. **Bombers**: Flexible, recallable, lowest readiness

**Minimum Deterrence**: France, UK, China (historically) maintain smaller arsenals sufficient to deter via guaranteed retaliation

### Erosion of Arms Control

**Historical Treaties**:
- **NPT** (1970): Non-proliferation, 5 recognized nuclear powers (P5), others prohibited
- **SALT I/II** (1970s): Capped US-Soviet strategic weapons
- **INF Treaty** (1987): Banned intermediate-range missiles in Europe
- **START I/II** (1991, 1993): Reduced strategic arsenals
- **New START** (2010): Extended to 2026, limits deployed strategic warheads to 1,550

**Recent Breakdown**:
- **2019**: US withdraws from INF (Russia violations, China not party)
- **2023**: Russia suspends New START participation
- **China's buildup**: No arms control participation, expanding arsenal
- **Iran's nuclear program**: JCPOA collapse, enrichment to 60% (90% weapons-grade)
- **North Korea**: Accelerating tests (ICBMs, MRBMs), miniaturization

**Risks**:
- **Unconstrained arms race**: Russia, China, US all modernizing
- **New technologies**: Hypersonic glide vehicles, AI-enabled decision-making reduce warning time
- **More actors**: Iran's potential breakout, Saudi Arabia's ambitions if Iran goes nuclear
- **Use taboo weakening**: Putin's nuclear threats over Ukraine, lowered threshold

### Regional Flashpoints

**South Asia**:
- **Crisis instability**: Short flight times (Delhi-Islamabad 3-5 minutes), hair-trigger alerts
- **Tactical nukes**: Pakistan's "full-spectrum deterrence" includes battlefield weapons (lowers use threshold)
- **No hotline**: Unlike US-Russia, minimal India-Pakistan crisis communication

**Middle East**:
- **Iran breakout**: Weeks from weapons-grade uranium if decides to build bomb
- **Saudi response**: Vowed to match Iran ("If they get nuclear weapons, we will too")
- **Israel's doctrine**: Opacity, presumed capability, Samson Option (use if state survival threatened)

**Korean Peninsula**:
- **DPRK miniaturization**: Uncertain if warheads fit on missiles, but advancing
- **Seoul vulnerability**: 25 million people within artillery range, conventional threat deters preemption
- **US extended deterrence**: Nuclear umbrella, but ROK public debates indigenous nukes

## Conventional Military Trends

### High-Tech Warfare

**Precision Strike**:
- Guided munitions (GPS, laser) vs. dumb bombs (WWII: thousands of bombs per target → today: 1-2)
- Long-range missiles (Russia's Kalibr, US Tomahawk, China DF-21D anti-ship)

**Networked Operations**:
- C4ISR (Command, Control, Communications, Computers, Intelligence, Surveillance, Reconnaissance)
- US advantage: Satellite constellation, data links, integrated targeting
- Vulnerability: GPS jamming, anti-satellite weapons

**Drone Revolution**:
- **ISR**: Persistent surveillance (MQ-9 Reaper, 20+ hour endurance)
- **Strike**: Armed drones (Predator, Bayraktar TB2, Iran Shahed-136)
- **Swarms**: Coordinated autonomous attacks (Ukraine's naval drones sink Russian ships)

**Hypersonics**:
- Speeds Mach 5+, maneuverable (unlike ballistic missiles), evade defenses
- Russia: Kinzhal (air-launched), Zircon (ship-launched), Avangard (glide vehicle)
- China: DF-17 (glide vehicle), DF-ZF tested
- US: Playing catch-up (Prompt Global Strike program)

### Force Projection

**US Advantages**:
- 11 supercarriers (rest of world: ~12 total, smaller)
- Global base network (Germany, Japan, Qatar, Djibouti, etc.)
- Aerial refueling fleet (500+ tankers)
- Amphibious assault ships (10 America/Wasp-class)

**China's A2/AD**:
- Deny US access to First Island Chain via missiles (DF-21D, DF-26), submarines, air defenses
- Fortress fleet: Operate within shore-based missile coverage

**Asymmetric Strategies**:
- **Iran**: Fast attack boats, mines, anti-ship missiles in Persian Gulf
- **North Korea**: Artillery, special forces, cyber attacks
- **Russia**: Electronic warfare, SAMs (S-400/S-500)

## Proxy Wars and Gray Zone Conflict

### Syria (2011-present)

**Proxies**:
- **Assad regime**: Backed by Russia (air power, Wagner), Iran (Hezbollah, IRGC, Shia militias)
- **Rebels**: Turkey-backed (FSA remnants, SNA)
- **Kurds (SDF)**: US-backed (anti-ISIS, now hold northeast)
- **ISIS**: Defeated territorially (2019), remnants persist

**Outcomes**:
- Assad survives, controls ~70% of country
- Russia gains Mediterranean bases, tests weapons
- Iran secures land corridor to Hezbollah
- Turkey prevents Kurdish autonomy
- Refugees (6.6M externally, 6.8M internally displaced) destabilize region

### Yemen (2014-present)

**Sides**:
- **Houthis** (Ansar Allah): Zaydi Shia, control Sanaa and northwest, backed by Iran (missiles, drones)
- **Yemeni government**: Backed by Saudi-led coalition (UAE, others), UAE withdrew 2019

**Humanitarian Crisis**:
- 377,000+ deaths (direct and indirect)
- 80% population needs assistance
- Cholera, famine, infrastructure destroyed

**Geopolitical Stakes**:
- Iran-Saudi rivalry
- Bab el-Mandeb chokepoint (Houthi attacks on ships, 2023-24 Red Sea crisis)
- Al-Qaeda in Arabian Peninsula (AQAP) exploits chaos

**Stalemate**: 2022 truce mostly held, low-intensity conflict, no resolution

### Libya (2011-present)

**Factions**:
- **GNA/GNU** (Tripoli): UN-recognized, backed by Turkey, Qatar
- **LNA** (Tobruk): General Haftar, backed by Egypt, UAE, Russia (Wagner)

**Proxy Dimensions**:
- Turkey vs. Egypt/UAE (regional influence)
- Migrants (route to Europe, Italy/EU engagement)
- Oil (control of export terminals)

**Status**: 2020 ceasefire, dual government persists, elections postponed

### Ukraine (2014-2022 frozen, 2022-present full war)

**2014-2022 Donbas War**:
- Russia-backed separatists (DNR, LNR)
- Russian regulars ("little green men") covertly involved
- Minsk agreements unenforced

**2022 Invasion**: Crosses threshold from proxy to direct great power conflict (covered in previous chapters)

## Terrorism and Counterterrorism

### Jihadist Threat Evolution

**Al-Qaeda**:
- Founded 1988 (Osama bin Laden), 9/11 attacks (2001)
- Affiliates: AQAP (Yemen), AQIM (North Africa), Al-Shabaab (Somalia), HTS (Syria)
- Decentralized after bin Laden killed (2011), Zawahiri killed (2022)

**ISIS**:
- Emerged from Iraq insurgency, declared caliphate (2014)
- Controlled ~40% of Iraq, ~30% of Syria at peak
- Defeated territorially (2019), leader Baghdadi killed
- **Khorasan Province** (Afghanistan, Pakistan): 2021 Kabul airport attack
- **Affiliates**: West Africa (Boko Haram faction), Sinai, Philippines

**Trends**:
- **Territoriality lost**: No safe haven like Afghanistan pre-9/11 or ISIS caliphate
- **Decentralized**: Lone wolves, inspired attacks (no direct command)
- **Africa focus**: Sahel, Horn, Mozambique (ISIS, AQIM affiliates)

### Counterterrorism Strategies

**Drone Strikes**:
- Targeted killings (bin Laden, Soleimani, Zawahiri, Baghdadi)
- Debate: Effectiveness vs. civilian casualties (backlash)

**Partner Forces**:
- Iraq/Syria: SDF, Iraqi military (US training, air support)
- Sahel: French Operation Barkhane (ended 2022), US training
- Philippines: US advisors vs. Abu Sayyaf

**Intelligence Sharing**: Five Eyes (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand), Interpol, Europol

**Countering Violent Extremism (CVE)**: De-radicalization programs, community engagement (mixed success)

### Challenges

- **Root Causes**: Governance failures, sectarianism, youth unemployment persist
- **Backlash**: US interventions (Iraq) fueled jihadism
- **Safe Havens**: Taliban-ruled Afghanistan, ungoverned Sahel, Somalia
- **Funding**: Gulf donors (historically), smuggling, kidnapping, taxation

## Cyber Warfare

### Types of Cyber Operations

**Espionage** (most common):
- State actors steal IP, military secrets, government communications
- Examples: China's APT1 (Unit 61398) stole corporate data; Russia's Cozy Bear (SolarWinds 2020)

**Sabotage**:
- Disrupt or destroy critical infrastructure
- **Stuxnet** (2010): US-Israel worm destroyed Iranian nuclear centrifuges
- **NotPetya** (2017): Russia's attack on Ukraine spread globally, $10B+ damage

**Information Operations**:
- Disinformation, election interference, propaganda
- Russia's Internet Research Agency (2016 US election, Brexit)
- China's "50 Cent Army" (pro-CCP social media posts)

**Disruptive Attacks**:
- Ransomware (criminal, but states tolerate/use): Colonial Pipeline (2021), Costa Rica government (2022)
- DDoS: Estonia (2007), Georgia (2008) by Russia
- Wiper malware: Ukraine power grid (2015, 2016), WhisperGate (2022)

### Attribution Challenge

**Technical Difficulty**: Attackers use proxies, false flags, route through multiple countries

**Political Ambiguity**: Enables deniability ("patriotic hackers" claim)

**Example**: SolarWinds hack attributed to Russia (SVR), but took months to confirm

### Cyber Deterrence Dilemmas

**Unclear Red Lines**: What constitutes act of war? (Stuxnet didn't trigger military response)

**Escalation Risk**: Cyber attack → kinetic retaliation? (Russia's Ukraine cyber → NATO involvement?)

**Retaliatory Options**:
- Cyber-for-cyber (tit-for-tat)
- Sanctions (US indicts Chinese PLA officers, limited effect)
- Kinetic (US killed Soleimani partly for cyberattacks, extreme case)

**Confidence in Attribution**: Must prove attacker to justify retaliation (intelligence sources at risk)

### Critical Infrastructure Vulnerabilities

**Targets**:
- Power grids (Ukraine 2015/16)
- Water systems (Oldsmar, FL attempt 2021)
- Financial (SWIFT, banks)
- Communications (undersea cables, satellites)
- Healthcare (ransomware)

**Defenses**:
- Air-gapping (isolate critical systems, but Stuxnet proved vulnerable)
- Redundancy and backups
- Public-private coordination (CISA in US)
- Offensive cyber capabilities (deter via threat of retaliation)

## Hybrid Warfare

### Definition

Blending conventional military, irregular tactics, cyber, economic, and information operations to achieve strategic goals below the threshold of overt war.

### Russian Hybrid Warfare

**Crimea (2014)**:
1. "Little green men" (unmarked soldiers) seize government buildings
2. Information operations (Russian-speaking population "oppressed")
3. Cyber attacks (disable Ukrainian communications)
4. Referendum (illegitimate but provides veneer)
5. Annexation (fait accompli before NATO reacts)

**Donbas (2014-2022)**:
- Covert military support to separatists
- Deny direct involvement (plausible deniability)
- Minsk agreements freeze conflict on favorable terms

**Western Democracies**:
- Election interference (2016 US, French, German elections)
- Disinformation (RT, Sputnik, social media bots)
- Energy leverage (gas cut-offs)
- Assassinations abroad (Skripal, Litvinenko poisonings)

### Chinese Hybrid Tactics

**South China Sea**:
- "Salami slicing": Incremental actions (dredging, building, militarizing) avoiding single provocation worth war
- Maritime militia (civilian fishing vessels with military support)
- Coast guard (gray hulls, not navy, lower escalation risk)

**Taiwan**:
- Military pressure (ADIZ incursions, encirclement exercises)
- Economic coercion (banned pineapples, group tours)
- Information warfare (disinformation, infiltrate media)
- Political interference (support pro-unification candidates)

**Global**:
- United Front Work Department (influence diaspora, foreign politicians)
- Economic coercion (Australia coal ban, Lithuania trade restrictions)

### Iranian Hybrid Warfare

**Proxies**: Hezbollah (Lebanon), Houthis (Yemen), PMF (Iraq), Hamas/PIJ (Palestine)

**Cyber**: Shamoon attacks on Saudi Aramco (2012, 2016), US banks (2012-13 DDoS)

**Tanker Attacks**: Gulf of Oman (2019), mines and limpet mines

**Drones/Missiles**: Saudi Aramco attack (2019), 5% of global oil supply offline

### Countering Hybrid Threats

**NATO's Response**:
- Enhanced Forward Presence (Baltic states, Poland)
- Rapid reaction forces
- Cyber defense centers
- Strategic communications (counter disinformation)

**Challenges**:
- Attribution (who to hold accountable?)
- Proportionality (Article 5 for cyber attack?)
- Speed (adversary acts faster than consensus-based NATO)

## Summary

Security competition persists despite globalization and institutional constraints. Nuclear deterrence remains stable among major powers via MAD, but arms control has eroded with INF collapse, New START suspension, and China's unconstrained buildup. Regional nuclear risks—South Asia's crisis instability, Iran's breakout potential, North Korea's advancing arsenal—pose acute dangers.

Conventional military trends favor precision, networking, and drones, with US maintaining projection advantages but China's A2/AD challenging access. European rearmament accelerates post-Ukraine, while hypersonics and AI threaten to destabilize by reducing warning time.

Proxy wars (Syria, Yemen, Libya, Donbas 2014-22) allow great powers to compete without direct confrontation, but inflict massive humanitarian costs. Terrorism evolved from Al-Qaeda's 9/11 to ISIS's territorial caliphate (now defeated) to decentralized affiliates in Africa and lone-wolf attacks, with counterterrorism shifting to partner forces and drones.

Cyber warfare introduces new vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure, enables espionage and sabotage at scale, and complicates deterrence due to attribution challenges. Hybrid warfare—Russia's Crimea seizure, China's salami-slicing in the South China Sea, Iran's proxy networks—exploits gray zones below the threshold of conventional war, challenging traditional defense doctrines.

The future of conflict is multi-domain: nuclear risks persist, conventional capabilities proliferate, cyber and hybrid tactics blur peace and war, and proxies enable deniable competition. States must navigate this complexity without triggering catastrophic escalation, while deterring aggression across multiple domains simultaneously.
