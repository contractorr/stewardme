# Economic Geopolitics

## Overview

Economic power has become central to geopolitical competition. States weaponize trade, investment, finance, and technology to coerce adversaries and reward allies. This chapter examines how economic interdependence—once seen as a source of peace—is now a domain of strategic rivalry through trade wars, sanctions, resource competition, and supply chain battles.

## Trade Wars and Economic Statecraft

### US-China Trade War (2018-present)

**Origins**:
- US trade deficit with China (~$350 billion annually)
- IP theft, forced technology transfer allegations
- China 2025 plan for tech self-sufficiency alarms US

**Phase 1 (2018-2019)**:
- Tariffs escalate to 25% on ~$370 billion Chinese goods
- China retaliates on ~$110 billion US goods (soybeans, aircraft)
- Phase One Deal (Jan 2020): China commits to purchase $200B more US goods (unmet)

**Biden Continuation**:
- Maintained Trump tariffs, added targeted restrictions
- Focus shifts to technology (semiconductors, AI, biotech)

**Impacts**:
- **US**: Higher consumer prices, supply chain disruptions, some reshoring
- **China**: Growth slowdown, accelerates domestic substitution
- **Global**: Fragmented supply chains, "friend-shoring," deglobalization

### Technology Decoupling

**US Export Controls**:
- **October 2022**: Ban on advanced semiconductor equipment to China (ASML lithography machines)
- **October 2023**: AI chip restrictions (NVIDIA A100/H100 GPUs)
- **Goal**: Delay China's military/AI development by ~10 years

**China's Response**:
- **"Dual Circulation"**: Domestic demand and innovation prioritized
- **Indigenous Chips**: SMIC 7nm production (behind TSMC's 3nm), massive subsidies
- **Rare Earths Leverage**: Controls 60%+ of global production, threatened export bans

**Europe's Dilemma**: Caught between US security concerns and China's market (VW sells 40% of cars in China)

### Critical Minerals and Supply Chains

**Strategic Dependencies**:

| Material | Primary Producer | Use | Geopolitical Risk |
|----------|------------------|-----|-------------------|
| **Rare Earths** | China (60%) | Magnets, electronics, batteries | Export restrictions |
| **Cobalt** | DRC (70%) | EV batteries | Child labor, instability |
| **Lithium** | Australia (50%), Chile (25%) | EV batteries | Resource nationalism |
| **Nickel** | Indonesia (30%) | Stainless steel, batteries | Export bans (2020) |
| **Gallium/Germanium** | China (80%+) | Semiconductors | Export controls (2023) |

**Responses**:
- **US**: Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) subsidizes domestic battery/EV production
- **EU**: Critical Raw Materials Act targets 10% domestic extraction, 40% processing
- **Reshoring**: Intel, TSMC fabs in Arizona (CHIPS Act funding)

## Sanctions as a Weapon

### Types of Sanctions

1. **Comprehensive**: Broad embargo (North Korea, Iran pre-JCPOA)
2. **Targeted**: Individuals, entities (Russian oligarchs, Chinese tech firms)
3. **Sectoral**: Specific industries (Russian energy, Iranian oil)
4. **Secondary**: Penalize third parties dealing with target (Iran sanctions extraterritorial)

### Case Studies

#### Russia (2014 and 2022 escalation)

**2014 (Crimea)**:
- Asset freezes on officials, banks, energy firms
- Tech export bans (oil exploration equipment)
- EU-US coordination, Russia retaliates with food import bans

**2022 (Ukraine Invasion)**:
- **SWIFT ban**: Major Russian banks cut from international payments
- **Central bank freeze**: $300B of reserves immobilized
- **Energy**: EU embargo on seaborne Russian oil (Dec 2022), gas imports fell 80%+
- **Tech**: Semiconductor, aviation parts, luxury goods bans

**Impact**:
- GDP contracted 2.1% (2022), rebounded slightly (war economy stimulus)
- Ruble stabilized via capital controls, energy sales to China/India
- Western brands exited (McDonald's, IKEA), local replacements
- **Lesson**: Large economy with resource exports can withstand sanctions via non-Western pivot

#### Iran

**Timeline**:
- 1979-present: US sanctions since hostage crisis
- 2012-2015: Crippling sanctions (oil exports fell 60%, banking cut off)
- 2015: JCPOA (nuclear deal) lifts sanctions
- 2018: Trump withdraws, "maximum pressure" reinstated
- 2022-present: Nuclear negotiations stalled, sanctions deepen

**Effects**:
- Currency collapse (rial lost ~80% value since 2018)
- Oil smuggling via Malaysia, China (enforcement varies)
- Domestic hardship, protests (2022 Mahsa Amini)
- Nuclear program advances (60% enrichment, near weapons-grade)

**Effectiveness Debate**: Sanctions hurt economy but regime survives, nuclear program progresses

#### North Korea

- Most sanctioned nation (UN, US, EU, others)
- Goal: Denuclearization
- Result: Nuclear arsenal grew (0 → 50-100 warheads), ICBMs tested
- Survival via China trade (90%+ of external trade), smuggling, cyber theft ($1B+ stolen via hacks)

### Sanctions Limitations

**Challenges**:
1. **Target Resilience**: Regimes prioritize survival over citizen welfare
2. **Evasion**: Shell companies, smuggling, barter, cryptocurrency
3. **Third-party Non-compliance**: China, India buy Russian oil at discount
4. **Humanitarian Harm**: Ordinary citizens suffer (medicines, food)
5. **Boomerang Effects**: Europe's energy crisis from Russian gas cutoff
6. **De-dollarization**: Overuse drives adversaries to alternatives (yuan, crypto, barter)

**Success Factors**:
- **Multilateral**: Broader participation harder to evade
- **Targeted**: Smart sanctions on elites minimize humanitarian harm
- **Accompanied by Diplomacy**: Off-ramp incentivizes compliance

## Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

### Scope and Objectives

**Launch**: 2013 by Xi Jinping

**Scale**:
- 150+ countries signed MOUs
- $1+ trillion committed (actual disbursement lower)
- Infrastructure: Ports, railways, highways, power plants

**Six Economic Corridors**:
1. China-Mongolia-Russia
2. New Eurasian Land Bridge (China-Europe rail)
3. China-Central Asia-West Asia
4. China-Pakistan (CPEC)
5. Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar
6. China-Indochina Peninsula

**Strategic Goals**:
- Secure energy/resource imports (overland routes bypass Malacca)
- Export excess industrial capacity (steel, cement)
- Gain political influence via economic dependence
- Internationalize yuan, challenge dollar dominance
- Strategic footholds (Djibouti base, Hambantota port)

### Debt Trap Diplomacy?

**Mechanism**: China lends for infrastructure, if defaulted, seizes strategic assets

**Example**: **Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port**
- China loaned $1.3B for port construction (low commercial viability)
- Sri Lanka couldn't repay, leased port to China for 99 years (2017)
- **Counterpoint**: Sri Lanka also had IMF, private debt; BRI one factor among many

**Other Cases**:
- **Djibouti**: $1.4B debt to China (~80% of GDP), risks losing port control (US base nearby)
- **Pakistan**: CPEC debt ~$60B, economic crisis, IMF bailouts
- **Zambia**: Defaulted on Chinese loans (2020), copper mines at risk

**Pushback**:
- Renegotiations (Malaysia's Mahathir canceled/reduced projects)
- G7 "Build Back Better World" (2021, rebranded as PGII - Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment)
- Transparency demands (many BRI contracts secret)

### BRI Challenges

**Implementation Issues**:
- **White Elephant Projects**: Underutilized infrastructure (Kenya's SGR railway)
- **Corruption**: Host country elites capture benefits, public bears debt
- **Environmental**: Lax standards (coal plants in Southeast Asia)
- **Local Backlash**: Chinese labor imported, limited local employment

**Geopolitical Resistance**:
- **India**: Refuses to join (CPEC crosses disputed Kashmir territory)
- **US Indo-Pacific Strategy**: Quad connectivity projects counter BRI
- **EU "Global Gateway"**: €300B investment (2021-2027) alternative

## Energy Geopolitics

### Oil and Gas Leverage

**Russia-Europe**:
- Pre-2022: 40% of EU gas from Russia (Germany 55% dependent)
- Nord Stream pipelines (NS1, NS2) increased dependence, bypassed Ukraine
- **Weaponization**: 2006, 2009 cut-offs during Ukraine disputes; 2022 retaliation for sanctions
- **EU Response**: LNG imports (US, Qatar), renewables, energy efficiency (gas demand fell 18% in 2022-23)

**Qatar**: Largest LNG exporter, plays mediator role (hosts Taliban talks, Hamas leadership), leverages energy wealth

**OPEC+**: Saudi Arabia, Russia coordinate production cuts (2016+), manage prices, geopolitical tool (2023 cuts despite US pressure to increase)

### Energy Transition Geopolitics

**Winners**:
- **China**: Dominates solar panels (80%+ production), batteries (76% manufacturing)
- **Chile, Argentina**: Lithium reserves (lithium triangle)
- **DRC**: Cobalt (EV batteries)
- **Indonesia**: Nickel processing (banned raw ore exports)

**Losers**:
- **Petrostates**: Saudi Arabia, Russia, Venezuela face stranded assets as oil demand peaks
- **Transition Costs**: Europe's deindustrialization risk if energy too expensive

**New Vulnerabilities**:
- **Supply Chain Concentration**: China's dominance creates dependence (swap oil for batteries?)
- **Mineral Scarcity**: EV scaling requires massive extraction (environmental, political challenges)

## Financial Weaponization

### Dollar Dominance

**Mechanisms**:
- 60% of global reserves held in dollars
- ~90% of forex transactions involve dollar
- SWIFT messaging system (dollar-dominated)
- US banks as correspondent banks for international transactions

**Power**: US can exclude actors from dollar system (Iran, Russia), extraterritorial sanctions enforceable

### De-dollarization Efforts

**Motivations**:
- Reduce vulnerability to US sanctions
- Diversify reserves (gold, euros, yuan)
- Assert sovereignty (not dependent on US financial infrastructure)

**Methods**:
1. **Bilateral Currency Swaps**: China-Russia trade in yuan/rubles (40%+ by 2023)
2. **Alternative Payment Systems**: China's CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System), Russia's SPFS
3. **Digital Currencies**: Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) - China's e-yuan pilot, avoid SWIFT
4. **Gold Accumulation**: Russia, China increased gold reserves (10%+ of Russia's reserves)
5. **Cryptocurrency**: Venezuela's petro (failed), sanctioned actors use Bitcoin (limited scale)

**Challenges**:
- **Network Effects**: Dollar's liquidity, depth hard to replicate
- **Trust**: Yuan not fully convertible, capital controls limit use
- **Alternatives Fragmented**: Euro, yen, yuan don't unify as replacement
- **US Advantage**: Deepest capital markets, legal protections

**BRICS Currency Talk**: Proposed common currency (2023 summit), unlikely near-term (divergent economies, political systems)

### China's Yuan Internationalization

**Progress**:
- 2015: IMF included yuan in SDR basket (3rd behind dollar, euro)
- Renminbi clearing hubs in 29 cities globally
- ~3% of global reserves (up from ~1% in 2015, far behind dollar's 60%)

**Obstacles**:
- Capital controls (prevent full convertibility)
- Lack of property rights, rule of law (investors wary)
- State influence on economy (unpredictable interventions)

## Supply Chain Realignment

### Globalization's Reversal?

**Peak Globalization** (1990-2008):
- WTO expansion, China's entry (2001)
- Just-in-time manufacturing, efficiency maximized
- Comparative advantage: Produce where cheapest

**Shocks**:
- 2008 Financial Crisis: Revealed financial interdependence risks
- COVID-19: Disrupted supply chains (PPE, semiconductors), exposed vulnerabilities
- US-China Rivalry: Decoupling pressures
- Russia-Ukraine War: Energy, food supply disruptions

### Strategies

**Reshoring**: Bring production back to home country (US CHIPS Act incentivizes domestic fabs)

**Nearshoring**: Shift to nearby countries (Mexico's maquiladoras benefit from US-China tensions)

**Friend-shoring** (Ally-shoring): Relocate to trusted partners (US-EU, Quad, IPEF members)

**Redundancy**: Multiple suppliers instead of single-source (costlier but resilient)

### Examples

**Semiconductors**:
- TSMC building fabs in Arizona, Japan, Germany (diversify from Taiwan risk)
- Intel expanding US production (Ohio mega-fab)
- Samsung increasing Texas, South Korea capacity

**Pharmaceuticals**:
- 80%+ of API (active pharmaceutical ingredients) from China/India
- COVID exposed dependence (US, EU incentivizing domestic production)

**Batteries/EVs**:
- Tesla Gigafactories in US, Germany, China (regional production)
- IRA requires North American battery content for subsidies

## Summary

Economic geopolitics has become central to great power competition. Trade wars, particularly US-China, reflect deeper strategic rivalry over technology, supply chains, and industrial capacity. Technology decoupling aims to deny adversaries critical capabilities (semiconductors, AI), risking fragmentation into rival blocs.

Sanctions are a primary tool of coercion, with Russia and Iran as major targets. While sanctions inflict economic pain, their effectiveness is limited by target resilience, evasion, and non-Western actors providing lifelines. Overuse risks accelerating de-dollarization and undermining the dollar's structural power.

China's Belt and Road Initiative uses infrastructure investment to gain influence and secure resources, but faces pushback over debt sustainability, transparency, and geopolitical encirclement. Energy geopolitics persists as Russia weaponizes gas supplies, driving Europe's painful transition, while the shift to renewables creates new dependencies on Chinese-dominated battery supply chains and critical minerals.

Financial weaponization through dollar dominance allows US extraterritorial sanctions, but provokes de-dollarization efforts via bilateral currency swaps, alternative payment systems, and CBDCs. However, network effects and trust deficits make displacing the dollar difficult.

Supply chain realignment—reshoring, nearshoring, friend-shoring—reflects declining confidence in globalization's efficiency-first logic. Resilience now matters as much as cost, with semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, and batteries prioritized for domestic/allied production. This fragmentation raises costs but reduces strategic vulnerabilities in an era of economic interdependence weaponized for geopolitical ends.
