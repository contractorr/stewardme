# Glossary

## A

**Action**: Choice made by player at decision node. Element of strategy set.

**Adverse selection**: Asymmetric information causes low-quality types to dominate market (Akerlof lemons). High-quality types withdraw → market unravels.

**Altruism**: Concern for others' welfare. Hamilton's rule: Evolves if rB > C (relatedness × benefit > cost).

**Arrow's Impossibility Theorem**: No voting system satisfies unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, non-dictatorship, and transitivity of social preferences.

**Auction**: Mechanism allocating goods to bidders. Types: First-price (pay own bid), second-price/Vickrey (pay second-highest bid), English (ascending), Dutch (descending).

**Axelrod tournament**: Computer simulations of repeated Prisoner's Dilemma. Tit-for-tat won (simple, robust, never exploited badly).

## B

**Backward induction**: Solving extensive-form game from terminal nodes to initial node. Assumes sequential rationality at every decision point.

**Bargaining**: Negotiation over surplus division. Nash solution (axiomatic): Maximize product of surplus gains. Rubinstein solution (non-cooperative): Alternating offers with discounting.

**BATNA**: Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement. Outside option if negotiation fails. Strong BATNA → higher bargaining power.

**Battle of the Sexes**: Coordination game with disagreement on which equilibrium. Multiple Nash equilibria → coordination problem.

**Bayesian game**: Game with incomplete information. Players have private types drawn from common prior distribution.

**Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE)**: Each type of each player maximizes expected payoff given beliefs about opponents' types and strategies.

**Behavioral game theory**: Incorporates bounded rationality, social preferences, learning. Explains systematic deviations from Nash predictions in experiments.

**Beliefs**: Probability distribution over states, types, or histories. Updated via Bayes' rule. Central to Bayesian games, Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium.

**Bertrand competition**: Oligopoly model where firms choose prices simultaneously. With identical products and constant marginal cost, Nash equilibrium: Price = marginal cost (zero profit).

**Best response**: Strategy maximizing payoff given opponents' strategies. BR_i(s_{-i}) = {strategies for i optimal against s_{-i}}.

**Bluffing**: Misrepresenting type/strength to influence opponent's behavior. Common in poker, military strategy, negotiations.

**Bourgeois strategy**: Hawk if owner, Dove if intruder. Respects property rights. ESS in Hawk-Dove game if V > C.

**Bounded rationality**: Limited cognitive capacity (computation, attention, memory). Models: Level-k reasoning, QRE.

**Brinkmanship**: Pushing to edge of disaster to force opponent to back down. Cuban Missile Crisis example.

## C

**Cartel**: Firms coordinating to raise prices above competitive level. Sustainable via repeated game if future valuable (δ high enough).

**Centipede game**: Sequential game with alternating moves. Backward induction → immediate defection. Experiments show multiple passes (bounded rationality, altruism).

**Chain store paradox**: Finite repetition unravels cooperation. 20 towns: Backward induction → entry in all towns despite chain's incentive to fight to build reputation.

**Cheap talk**: Costless, non-binding communication. Crawford-Sobel: Bias limits information transmission → partially revealing equilibrium.

**Chicken**: Game where mutual toughness disastrous but unilateral toughness rewarded. Commitment value (remove options). Cuban Missile Crisis structure.

**Closed-loop strategy**: Strategy conditioning on full history. Contrast with open-loop (precommitment, no conditioning).

**Coalitional game**: Players form coalitions to improve payoffs. Shapley value allocates surplus fairly.

**Commitment**: Removing options to make threat/promise credible. Cortés burning ships. Paradoxically increases payoffs.

**Common knowledge**: Everyone knows X, everyone knows everyone knows X, ad infinitum. Required for backward induction, iterated deletion of dominated strategies.

**Common prior**: Probability distribution over types/states known to all players. Harsanyi transformation introduces Nature assigning types.

**Complete information**: Payoff structure common knowledge. Contrast with incomplete information (private types).

**Condorcet paradox**: Social preferences can cycle (A > B > C > A) even if individual preferences transitive. No Condorcet winner.

**Constant-sum game**: Payoffs sum to constant in every outcome. Includes zero-sum games. Strictly competitive.

**Continuous game**: Strategy sets continuous (infinite). Example: Cournot (quantities on [0, ∞)).

**Cooperation**: Joint action benefiting all vs individual temptation to defect. Sustained in repeated games via Folk theorem.

**Coordination game**: Multiple Nash equilibria, players prefer any equilibrium to miscoordination. Focal points help select equilibrium.

**Correlated equilibrium**: Players condition strategies on correlated signal from mediator. Can Pareto-dominate Nash. Aumann (1974).

**Costly signaling**: Signal credible because differentially costly (high types find cheaper). Spence education, peacock's tail.

**Cournot competition**: Oligopoly where firms choose quantities simultaneously. Nash equilibrium: Each produces q* = (a - c)/(n + 1). Between monopoly and perfect competition.

**Credibility**: Threat/promise is credible if ex-post rational (optimal to execute once situation arises). Essential for deterrence, commitment.

**Cross-cutting relationships**: Principle in geology, analogous concept: Earlier actions constrain later ones.

## D

**Decision node**: Point in game tree where player chooses action.

**Decision theory**: Single agent optimizing against passive environment (nature). Contrast with game theory (multiple strategic agents).

**Deterrence**: Threaten punishment to prevent action. Requires capability, resolve, communication. MAD (Mutual Assured Destruction) in nuclear context.

**Discount factor (δ)**: Weight on future payoffs, δ ∈ (0, 1). Higher δ = more patient. δ = 0.9 means $1 next period worth $0.90 today.

**Dominant strategy**: Strategy best regardless of opponents' choices. Stronger than Nash equilibrium. Dominant strategy equilibrium rare but powerful.

**Dominated strategy**: Never a best response to any opponent strategy. Rational players don't play dominated strategies.

**Dutch Disease**: Resource exports appreciate currency → other sectors uncompetitive. Named for Netherlands post-North Sea gas discovery.

**Dynamic game**: Players move sequentially, observe past actions (at least partially). Represented in extensive form.

## E

**Electronic mail game**: Coordination with unreliable communication. No finite number of acknowledgments achieves common knowledge → coordination impossible.

**Equilibrium**: Solution concept predicting outcome. Nash: Mutual best responses. SPE: Nash in every subgame. BNE: Nash with types.

**ESS (Evolutionarily Stable Strategy)**: Strategy that, if adopted by population, resists invasion by rare mutant. Stronger than Nash. Maynard Smith (1973).

**Evolution of cooperation**: Cooperation emerges despite selfish incentives via repeated interaction, kin selection, reciprocity. Axelrod tournaments, Tit-for-tat.

**Expected utility**: Weighted average of utilities over outcomes, weighted by probabilities. von Neumann-Morgenstern utility function.

**Extensive form**: Game tree representation showing sequential structure, decision nodes, information sets, payoffs at terminal nodes.

## F

**Factor of Safety**: Ratio of resisting forces to driving forces (geology). Game theory analog: Robustness of equilibrium to perturbations.

**Fairness**: Preference for equitable payoff distribution. Fehr-Schmidt inequality aversion: Disutility from payoff differences.

**Fearon's rationalist explanations for war**: (1) Private information + incentive to misrepresent, (2) Commitment problems, (3) Indivisible stakes.

**Fictitious play**: Learning rule. Update beliefs about opponents' strategies from observed frequencies, best-respond to beliefs. Converges to Nash in some games.

**Finite game**: Finite players, finite strategy sets. Nash's theorem: Every finite game has Nash equilibrium (possibly mixed).

**First-mover advantage**: Benefit from moving first. Stackelberg leader produces more than Cournot quantity, earns higher profit. Commitment value.

**First-price auction**: Highest bidder wins, pays own bid. Optimal to shade bid below value (b* = [(n-1)/n]v for n bidders, uniform values).

**Fisher's principle**: 1:1 sex ratio is ESS. Equal investment in male vs female offspring maximizes fitness.

**Focal point**: Salient outcome coordinating players' expectations without communication. Schelling (1960). Examples: Meet at Grand Central, split 50-50.

**Folk theorem**: In infinitely repeated games, any individually rational payoffs sustainable as Nash equilibrium if players sufficiently patient (δ near 1).

**Forward induction**: Logic inferring opponent's plans from past actions. "Why would rational player take action A unless planning B later?"

**Framing effect**: Preference depends on how problem presented (gain vs loss frame). Violates expected utility theory.

## G

**Gale-Shapley algorithm**: Deferred acceptance matching. Produces stable matching (no blocking pairs). Strategy-proof for proposing side.

**Game**: Strategic interaction—players, strategies, payoffs. Outcome depends on all players' choices.

**Game theory**: Mathematical study of strategic interaction. von Neumann (1928), Nash (1950).

**Game tree**: See extensive form.

**Global games**: Small incomplete information selects unique equilibrium in coordination games. Morris-Shin currency attacks.

**Green-Porter model**: Cartel with imperfect monitoring. Occasional price wars triggered by bad demand shocks (can't distinguish from cheating).

**Grim trigger**: Cooperate until opponent defects once, then defect forever. Sustains cooperation in repeated Prisoner's Dilemma if δ ≥ 1/2.

## H

**Hamilton's rule**: Altruism evolves if rB > C (relatedness × benefit to recipient > cost to altruist). Explains kin selection, eusocial insects.

**Harsanyi transformation**: Convert incomplete information game to imperfect information game by introducing Nature assigning types.

**Hawk-Dove game**: Contest over resources. Hawk escalates (fights), Dove displays only. If V < C (fight costly), mixed ESS: p* = V/C.

**Hotelling model**: Spatial competition (firms choose locations on line). Minimum differentiation principle: Both locate at median.

**Hypergame**: Player i has incorrect model of game (believes wrong payoffs, strategies, or players). Can explain paradoxes, conflicts.

## I

**Imperfect information**: Some decision nodes in same information set (player cannot distinguish). Example: Simultaneous moves, poker.

**Incomplete information**: Players have private information (types, valuations) unknown to others. Harsanyi games.

**Indifference**: Player gets same expected payoff from multiple strategies. In mixed strategy equilibrium, opponents' mixing makes player indifferent.

**Individually rational payoff**: Payoff ≥ minimax payoff (worst opponent can force player to). Folk theorem applies to individually rational payoffs.

**Inequality aversion**: Disutility from payoff differences. Fehr-Schmidt: U_i = x_i - α Σ max(x_j - x_i, 0) - β Σ max(x_i - x_j, 0).

**Information asymmetry**: Players have different information. Adverse selection (pre-contractual), moral hazard (post-contractual).

**Information rent**: Surplus informed player extracts from asymmetric information. Mechanism design aims to minimize information rents.

**Information set**: Nodes in game tree player cannot distinguish. Represents imperfect information.

**Iterated deletion of dominated strategies (IEDS)**: Successively eliminate dominated strategies. Refines Nash equilibrium. Requires common knowledge of rationality.

## J

**Job market signaling**: Spence (1973). Education signals ability (doesn't increase productivity). Separating equilibrium: High ability gets education, low ability doesn't.

## K

**Kakutani fixed point theorem**: Existence result for Nash equilibrium in finite games. Best response correspondence has fixed point.

**Kin selection**: Hamilton's rule (rB > C). Genetic relatedness incentivizes altruism toward kin. Explains eusocial insects, alarm calls.

## L

**Learning**: Dynamic process converging (potentially) to equilibrium. Fictitious play, reinforcement learning, replicator dynamics.

**Lemon's problem**: Akerlof (1970). Asymmetric information in used car market. Adverse selection drives high-quality cars out → market unravels.

**Level-k reasoning**: Bounded rationality model. L0 random, L1 best-responds to L0, L2 best-responds to L1, etc. Explains beauty contest guesses (20-40, not Nash 0).

**Litigation**: Legal dispute resolved by court. Alternative to settlement. Priest-Klein: Selection bias → plaintiff wins ~50% of trials.

**Logrolling**: Vote trading in legislatures. Exchange support on bills. Can lead to inefficient outcomes (concentrated benefits, dispersed costs).

## M

**MAD (Mutual Assured Destruction)**: Nuclear deterrence. Both sides have second-strike capability → neither strikes first → stability.

**Matching pennies**: Zero-sum game. Players show penny simultaneously. Match wins for Player 1, mismatch for Player 2. No pure Nash, mixed equilibrium (50-50).

**Matching market**: Pairwise assignment without prices. Medical residency, school choice. Gale-Shapley algorithm produces stable matching.

**Maximin**: Choose strategy maximizing worst-case payoff. Optimal in zero-sum games (minimax = maximin).

**Mechanism design**: Design rules/institutions achieving desired outcomes given strategic agents. VCG, optimal auctions, matching.

**Median voter theorem**: Candidates converge to median voter's preference under single-dimensional policy, symmetric distribution, probabilistic voting.

**Minimax**: Choose strategy minimizing maximum loss opponent can inflict. In zero-sum games, minimax theorem: Value exists (von Neumann, 1928).

**Minimax payoff**: Worst payoff opponents can force player to. Individually rational payoffs ≥ minimax. Folk theorem applies above minimax.

**Mixed strategy**: Probability distribution over pure strategies. Randomization. Nash (1950): Every finite game has Nash equilibrium (possibly mixed).

**Moral hazard**: Hidden action problem. Agent takes action unobserved by principal. Incentive contracts, monitoring.

**Multi-stage game**: Game with sequential stages. Subgame perfection refines Nash.

**Multiplicity**: Multiple equilibria. Coordination problem—which equilibrium occurs? Focal points, forward induction, learning help select.

## N

**Nash equilibrium**: Strategy profile where each player best-responds to others. No unilateral profitable deviation. Nash (1950, 1951).

**Nash's theorem**: Every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium (possibly in mixed strategies).

**Network effects**: Value of product/service increases with number of users. Direct (same-side) or indirect (cross-side). Causes tipping, winner-take-all.

**No-trade theorem**: If traders rational, share common prior, posteriors common knowledge → no mutually beneficial trade (Milgrom-Stokey, 1982).

**Normal form**: Payoff matrix representation. Players, strategy sets, payoff functions. Also called strategic form.

**Nucleus**: Solution concept in cooperative games. Subset of core.

## O

**OPEC**: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Cartel coordinating oil production. Struggles with cheating, non-OPEC competition.

**Optimal auction**: Revenue-maximizing auction. Myerson (1981): Set reserve price (minimum bid). May exclude some bidders to extract rents.

**Outside option**: Payoff if negotiation fails. BATNA. Determines reservation price (minimum acceptable offer).

## P

**Paradox of voting**: Condorcet paradox (cycles), Downs paradox (cost of voting > benefit), Arrow's impossibility.

**Pareto efficiency**: Outcome where no one can be made better off without making someone worse off. Nash ≠ Pareto efficient (Prisoner's Dilemma).

**Pareto dominance**: Outcome x Pareto-dominates y if everyone weakly prefers x, someone strictly prefers x.

**Payoff**: Numerical representation of preferences. Utility. Depends on strategy profile.

**Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE)**: Sequential rationality + consistent beliefs (Bayes' rule where possible). Refinement of BNE for dynamic games.

**Perfect information**: All previous actions observed (every information set is singleton). Chess, checkers. Backward induction applies.

**Perfect recall**: Player remembers own past actions and information sets. Standard assumption (absent in "absent-minded driver" paradox).

**Player**: Decision-maker. Can be individual, firm, country, gene, algorithm.

**Plea bargaining**: Negotiation between prosecutor and defendant. Screening: guilty accept, innocent reject (if pleas < expected sentence).

**Pooling equilibrium**: All types choose same action → no information revealed. Contrast with separating equilibrium.

**PPAD-complete**: Complexity class of computing Nash equilibrium (Chen-Deng, 2006). Likely intractable but not known to be NP-hard.

**Price of Anarchy**: Ratio of social welfare at worst Nash equilibrium to optimum. Measures efficiency loss from selfish behavior.

**Prisoner's Dilemma**: Dominant strategy to defect, but mutual cooperation Pareto-dominates mutual defection. Ubiquitous structure (arms races, cartels, public goods).

**Private value**: Bidder knows own valuation (art for personal consumption). Contrast with common value (oil field—same value for all, estimates differ).

**Public good**: Non-excludable, non-rival. Free-riding problem. n-player Prisoner's Dilemma. Individual rationality → underprovision.

**Pure strategy**: Deterministic choice (no randomization). Special case of mixed strategy.

## Q

**Quantal Response Equilibrium (QRE)**: Bounded rationality model. Players best-respond noisily (higher payoff → higher probability, not deterministic). McKelvey-Palfrey (1995).

## R

**Rational choice**: Choose optimally given beliefs and preferences. Standard assumption in game theory.

**Rationalizability**: Strategy survives iterated elimination of never-best-responses. Weaker than Nash.

**Reciprocity**: Reward kind actions, punish unkind actions. Rabin (1993). Explains cooperation in experiments (Trust game, Ultimatum Game).

**Refinement**: Solution concept selecting subset of Nash equilibria. SPE, trembling-hand perfect, sequential equilibrium, forward induction.

**Regression**: In geology, sea level falls. In game theory, retreat from forward-induction reasoning.

**Reinforcement learning**: Repeat actions with higher payoffs more frequently. Bush-Mosteller, Erev-Roth. Models learning dynamics.

**Renegotiation-proof equilibrium**: No incentive to renegotiate at any history. Eliminates non-credible punishments (grim trigger not renegotiation-proof).

**Repeated game**: Stage game played multiple times. Cooperation sustainable if δ high (Folk theorem).

**Replicator dynamics**: Strategies replicate proportionally to fitness. dx_i/dt = x_i[u(s_i, x) - u(x, x)]. ESS = stable rest point.

**Reputation**: Belief about opponent's type based on observed behavior. Sustains cooperation in repeated games (Trust game, Tit-for-tat).

**Reservation price**: Minimum acceptable offer (seller) or maximum willing to pay (buyer). Determined by outside option (BATNA).

**Revelation principle**: Any outcome achievable by mechanism achievable by direct mechanism with truthful revelation.

**Revenue equivalence theorem**: Auctions allocating to highest valuation, charging zero to losers yield same expected revenue (Myerson, 1981). First-price = second-price = English = Dutch.

**Risk dominance**: Equilibrium requiring weaker beliefs about opponents. Harsanyi-Selten selection criterion.

**Rubinstein bargaining**: Infinite-horizon alternating offers with discounting. Unique SPE: Immediate agreement, first-mover advantage. As δ → 1, equal split.

## S

**Screening**: Uninformed party designs menu inducing informed party to reveal info via self-selection. Insurance contracts, job requirements.

**Second-price auction**: See Vickrey auction.

**Selection problem**: Multiple equilibria—which occurs? Focal points, Pareto dominance, risk dominance, forward induction, learning.

**Separating equilibrium**: Different types choose different actions → full revelation. Education signaling, insurance contracts.

**Sequential equilibrium**: Kreps-Wilson (1982). Sequential rationality + consistent beliefs. Refinement of PBE.

**Sequential game**: Players move in sequence, observe past actions (at least partially). Extensive form.

**Sequential rationality**: At every information set, strategy optimal given beliefs. Required for PBE, sequential equilibrium.

**Shapley value**: Fair allocation in cooperative games. Payoff = average marginal contribution across orderings. Shapley (1953), Nobel 2012.

**Signaling**: Informed player (Sender) sends costly signal to uninformed player (Receiver). Spence education, peacock's tail.

**Simultaneous game**: Players choose strategies without observing others' choices. Represented in normal form or extensive form with information sets.

**Single-crossing condition**: High type finds signal less costly than low type. Enables separating equilibrium (prevents low type from mimicking high type).

**Stackelberg competition**: Sequential quantity competition. Leader commits to quantity, follower best-responds. First-mover advantage: Leader produces more, earns more than Cournot.

**Stag Hunt**: Coordination game where cooperation is equilibrium but risky. (Stag, Stag) Pareto-dominates (Hare, Hare), but (Hare, Hare) safer.

**Stage game**: Game repeated in repeated game setting. Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma: PD is stage game.

**Static game**: All players choose simultaneously (no sequential structure). Normal form representation.

**Strategic complement**: Player's optimal action increasing in opponents' actions. Coordination games.

**Strategic form**: See normal form.

**Strategic substitute**: Player's optimal action decreasing in opponents' actions. Cournot quantities are strategic substitutes.

**Strategic voting**: Vote for candidate other than true top preference to influence outcome. Prevents worst outcome (vote for lesser evil).

**Strategy**: Complete contingent plan specifying action in every situation player might face (every information set).

**Strictly dominated strategy**: Strategy strictly worse than another regardless of opponents' play. Rational players never play.

**Subgame**: Portion of game tree starting from single node, including all successors, not breaking information sets.

**Subgame perfect equilibrium (SPE)**: Nash equilibrium in every subgame. Selten (1965), Nobel 1994. Eliminates non-credible threats. Refinement of Nash.

**Sunspot**: Extraneous random variable coordinating beliefs. Correlated equilibrium uses sunspots.

**Supermodular game**: Payoffs exhibit increasing differences (complementarities). Ensures existence of pure strategy Nash equilibria.

## T

**Takeoff**: Critical mass in network adoption. Tipping point.

**Terminal node**: Endpoint of game tree. Specifies payoffs.

**Threat**: Commitment to take action punishing opponent if certain condition met. Credible if ex-post rational.

**Tipping**: Winner-take-all outcome in markets with network effects. VHS vs Betamax, Uber vs Lyft (local markets).

**Tit-for-tat**: Cooperate initially, then copy opponent's previous move. Won Axelrod tournaments. Nice, retaliatory, forgiving.

**Trembling-hand perfect equilibrium**: Nash equilibrium surviving small probability of mistakes. Selten (1975). Eliminates equilibria relying on off-path actions never occurring.

**Trigger strategy**: Cooperate until deviation, then punish. Grim trigger (defect forever), finite punishment (defect for T periods).

**Trust game**: Sequential game. Trustor decides Trust or Not Trust. If Trust, Trustee decides Return or Keep. One-shot: Trustee keeps. Repeated: Reputation sustains Return.

**Truth-telling**: Reporting private information honestly. Vickrey auction: Dominant strategy. VCG: Dominant strategy. Optimal in some mechanisms (incentive-compatible).

**Two-sided market**: Platform connects two user groups. Network effects (cross-side). Strategy: Subsidize one side, charge other.

**Type**: Private information in Bayesian game. Valuation, cost, ability. Drawn from distribution (common prior).

## U

**Ultimatum Game**: Proposer offers split of pie, Responder accepts or rejects. SPE: Proposer takes almost everything. Experiments: 40-50% offers, low offers rejected.

**Unraveling**: Backward induction eliminates cooperation in finitely repeated games (Prisoner's Dilemma, Chain store paradox, Centipede).

**Utility**: Numerical representation of preferences. von Neumann-Morgenstern utility (expected utility over lotteries).

## V

**VCG (Vickrey-Clarke-Groves) mechanism**: Allocate to highest valuation, charge externality imposed. Truth-telling dominant strategy, efficient.

**Vickrey auction**: Second-price sealed-bid auction. Highest bidder wins, pays second-highest bid. Bidding true value is dominant strategy.

**Voting paradox**: See Condorcet paradox, Arrow's Impossibility Theorem.

## W

**Weakly dominated strategy**: Strategy weakly worse than another (at least as bad against all opponents, strictly worse against some). May be played in Nash equilibrium.

**Wilson Cycle**: In geology, supercontinent cycles. Game theory analog: Repeated cycles of cooperation-defection.

**Winner's curse**: Highest bidder in common-value auction likely overestimated value (most optimistic estimate). Rational bidders shade bids.

## Z

**Zero-sum game**: Players' payoffs sum to zero in every outcome. Strictly competitive. Minimax theorem applies. Matching pennies, poker.

## Summary

This glossary covers 200+ essential terms from game theory and strategic interaction. Core concepts: Nash equilibrium (mutual best responses), dominant strategies (best regardless of opponents), backward induction (solve from end), subgame perfection (credibility), mixed strategies (randomization), Bayesian games (incomplete information), signaling/screening (information asymmetry), repeated games (Folk theorem), evolutionary stability (ESS).

Key results: Nash's theorem (existence), Folk theorem (cooperation sustainable), Revenue equivalence (auction comparison), Arrow's Impossibility (voting paradoxes), Hamilton's rule (kin selection), Fisher's principle (sex ratios). Applications span economics (auctions, mechanism design, oligopoly, bargaining), politics (voting, deterrence, international relations), biology (evolution, cooperation, sex ratios), military (commitment, nuclear strategy), law (litigation, plea bargaining), negotiations (BATNA, anchoring).

Behavioral extensions: Bounded rationality (level-k, QRE), social preferences (inequality aversion, reciprocity), learning (fictitious play, replicator dynamics). Refinements: Trembling-hand perfect, sequential equilibrium, forward induction, correlated equilibrium. Computational: PPAD-complete (Nash computation), Price of Anarchy (efficiency loss). Game theory provides unified analytical framework revealing common strategic structures across diverse domains—once learned, transforms understanding of economics, politics, evolution, military strategy, and negotiations.
