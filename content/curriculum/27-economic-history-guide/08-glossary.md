# Economic History Glossary

## A

**Agency Problem**: Conflict of interest when one party (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal) but has different incentives. Example: Corporate managers may prioritize personal benefits over shareholder value.

**Asset Bubble**: Situation where asset prices rise far above fundamental value, driven by speculation. Eventually collapses, often triggering financial crisis.

**Autarky**: Economic self-sufficiency; policy of minimizing trade and reliance on foreign goods. Pursued by many countries during interwar period (1920s-1930s).

**Austrian School**: Economic philosophy emphasizing individual action, subjective value, and spontaneous market order. Critical of government intervention and central planning. Key figures: Mises, Hayek.

## B

**Balance of Payments**: Record of all economic transactions between a country and rest of world. Includes trade balance, capital flows, and reserve changes.

**Bank Run**: Sudden withdrawal of deposits by many customers simultaneously, typically triggered by fears of bank insolvency. Can cause otherwise solvent banks to fail.

**Barter**: Direct exchange of goods without money. Inefficient due to requiring "double coincidence of wants."

**Bimetallism**: Monetary system using both gold and silver as legal tender at fixed ratio. Subject to Gresham's Law problems.

**Bretton Woods System**: Post-WWII international monetary system (1944-1971) with dollar pegged to gold and other currencies pegged to dollar. Created IMF and World Bank.

## C

**Capital Controls**: Government restrictions on moving money across borders. Used to prevent capital flight or manage exchange rates.

**Capitalism**: Economic system characterized by private ownership of means of production, market coordination, and profit motive.

**Central Bank**: Institution managing monetary policy, often issuing currency, supervising banks, and acting as lender of last resort. Examples: Federal Reserve (U.S.), Bank of England, European Central Bank.

**China Shock**: Economic impact of Chinese import competition on U.S. manufacturing workers (1990s-2010s). Research by Autor, Dorn, and Hanson showed significant job losses and slow adjustment.

**Cliometrics**: Application of economic theory and quantitative methods to historical analysis. Named after Clio (muse of history). Pioneered by Fogel and North.

**Collective Ownership**: Means of production owned by community, workers, or state rather than private individuals. Central to socialist economic systems.

**Colonialism**: Political control of territory by foreign power, typically involving economic exploitation. European colonialism (1500s-1900s) profoundly shaped global economic development.

**Command Economy**: Economic system where government planners determine production, prices, and allocation rather than markets. Example: Soviet Union.

**Commodity Money**: Money with intrinsic value (gold, silver, cattle). Contrasts with fiat money.

**Comparative Advantage**: Principle that countries benefit from specializing in goods they produce at lower opportunity cost, even if not absolute lowest cost. Foundation of trade theory (Ricardo).

**Contagion**: Spread of financial crisis from one institution, market, or country to others through financial linkages or psychology.

**Convertibility**: Ability to exchange currency for gold (under gold standard) or other currencies at fixed rate.

**Counterfactual**: Hypothetical "what if" scenario used to assess causation. Example: "What if railroads hadn't been built?" (Fogel's research question).

**Countercyclical Policy**: Government actions to moderate economic cycles—stimulus during recessions, restraint during booms.

**Creative Destruction**: Schumpeter's term for process by which innovation destroys old industries while creating new ones. Drives capitalist dynamism.

**Currency Board**: Monetary regime with rigid peg to foreign currency and 100% reserves. Automatic, no discretion. Example: Hong Kong (pegged to USD).

## D

**Debasement**: Reducing precious metal content of coins while maintaining face value. Generates short-term revenue but causes inflation and erodes trust.

**Debt-Deflation**: Irving Fisher's theory that falling prices increase real debt burden, causing defaults and worsening depression. Relevant to Great Depression.

**Deflation**: Sustained decline in general price level. Can increase real debt burdens and discourage spending (waiting for lower prices).

**Demographic Transition**: Shift from high birth/death rates to low birth/death rates accompanying development. Creates temporary population boom.

**Dependency Theory**: View that international trade perpetuates underdevelopment by making poor countries dependent on raw material exports to rich manufacturing countries. Influential in Latin America (Prebisch).

**Developed/Developing Countries**: Distinction based on income, industrialization, and living standards. "Global North" vs. "Global South" alternative terms.

**Dodd-Frank Act**: U.S. financial regulatory reform (2010) responding to 2008 crisis. Increased oversight of banks and created systemic risk council.

## E

**Economies of Scale**: Average costs decline as production volume increases. Creates barriers to entry and can lead to monopolies.

**Enclosure Movement**: Process in Britain (1600s-1800s) where common lands privatized. Increased agricultural productivity but displaced peasants.

**Eugenics**: Pseudoscientific movement (late 1800s-early 1900s) advocating selective breeding. Influenced by misapplication of Darwin's theories. Horrific consequences including Nazi genocide.

**Exchange Rate**: Price of one currency in terms of another. Can be fixed (government-determined) or floating (market-determined).

**Export-Led Growth**: Development strategy emphasizing manufacturing for export markets. Successfully used by East Asian countries (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, China).

## F

**Factory System**: Centralized production using powered machinery and wage labor. Emerged during Industrial Revolution, replacing artisan and cottage production.

**Feudalism**: Medieval European system with land-owning lords and land-bound serfs. Decentralized political structure with hierarchical obligations.

**Fiat Money**: Currency with no intrinsic value, backed by government decree. All modern currencies are fiat money.

**Financial Crisis**: Severe disruption to financial system, often involving bank failures, credit freezes, and asset price collapses. Typically followed by recession.

**Financialization**: Increasing dominance of financial motives, markets, actors, and institutions in economy. Criticized as prioritizing short-term gains over productive investment.

**Fiscal Policy**: Government use of spending and taxation to influence economy. Keynesian theory emphasizes fiscal stimulus during recessions.

**Five-Year Plans**: Soviet central planning system setting production targets for five-year periods. Also used by China and other command economies.

**Floating Exchange Rate**: Currency value determined by supply and demand in foreign exchange markets, not fixed by government.

**Fordism**: Production system pioneered by Henry Ford combining assembly line, standardization, and high wages (enabling workers to buy products). Mass production for mass consumption.

**Free Trade**: International commerce without tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions. Contrasts with protectionism.

## G

**GATT**: General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (1947-1995). Framework for multilateral trade liberalization through negotiation rounds. Succeeded by WTO.

**GDP (Gross Domestic Product)**: Total value of goods and services produced within country in given period. Primary measure of economic output.

**Gerschenkron's Thesis**: "Advantages of Backwardness"—late industrializers can adopt proven technology, but need different institutions (larger firms, greater state role).

**Gilded Age**: Period in U.S. (1870s-1900) of rapid industrial growth, extreme inequality, and political corruption. Term from Mark Twain novel.

**Globalization**: Increasing economic integration across borders through trade, capital flows, migration, and cultural exchange. Occurs in waves, not linear process.

**Global Value Chains**: Production fragmented across countries, with different stages in different locations. Characteristic of modern globalization.

**Gold Standard**: Monetary system where currency value fixed to gold and convertible on demand. Provided price stability but deflationary bias. Classical gold standard (1870s-1914); interwar gold standard (1920s-1930s) failed.

**Great Convergence**: Period since 1990s-2000s where developing countries (especially China, India) growing faster than developed countries, narrowing income gaps.

**Great Depression**: Severe global economic crisis (1929-1939) with massive unemployment, deflation, and output collapse. Shaped modern economic policy.

**Great Divergence**: Period (roughly 1800-2000) when Western Europe and offshoots pulled far ahead economically of rest of world. Reversal beginning in recent decades.

**Great Moderation**: Period of reduced economic volatility (1980s-2007). Ended with 2008 financial crisis.

**Great Recession**: 2007-2009 economic crisis following financial crisis. Worst downturn since Great Depression.

**Gresham's Law**: "Bad money drives out good." If government overvalues one currency form, people hoard undervalued form and spend overvalued form.

**Guilds**: Organizations controlling craft production in medieval and early modern cities. Set standards and prices, limited entry, provided training.

## H

**Hyperinflation**: Extremely rapid price increases, typically defined as 50%+ per month. Destroys currency's value. Examples: Weimar Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2000s), Venezuela (2010s).

## I

**IMF (International Monetary Fund)**: International institution (created 1944) providing temporary balance of payments financing and policy surveillance.

**Import Substitution Industrialization (ISI)**: Development strategy protecting domestic industries from foreign competition to encourage local manufacturing. Used in Latin America; mixed results.

**Industrial Revolution**: Transition to mechanized factory production beginning in Britain (~1760-1840), spreading globally. Created sustained economic growth unprecedented in history.

**Inequality**: Unequal distribution of income or wealth. Measured by Gini coefficient, income shares, or wealth concentration.

**Inflation**: Sustained increase in general price level. Reduces purchasing power of money.

**Inflation Targeting**: Monetary policy framework where central bank aims for explicit numerical inflation target (often 2%). Adopted widely since 1990s.

**Institutional Economics**: Approach emphasizing how institutions (property rights, legal systems, norms) shape economic outcomes. Associated with Douglass North.

## K

**Keynesian Economics**: Macroeconomic theory (John Maynard Keynes) emphasizing aggregate demand management. Government spending can stimulate economy during recessions. Challenged Say's Law.

**Kuznets Curve**: Hypothesis that inequality rises during early development then falls. Empirical support weak; depends heavily on policy.

## L

**Labor Theory of Value**: Classical/Marxian idea that value derives from labor input. Marx used it to argue capitalists exploit workers (surplus value). Mostly abandoned in modern economics.

**Laissez-faire**: Economic philosophy of minimal government intervention. Markets self-regulate optimally. Associated with classical liberalism.

**Lender of Last Resort**: Central bank function of providing emergency liquidity to prevent financial panic. Bagehot's rule: Lend freely at penalty rate against good collateral.

**Leverage**: Use of borrowed money to amplify investment returns (and losses). High leverage creates fragility. Financial crises always involve excessive leverage.

**Luddites**: English textile workers (early 1800s) who destroyed machinery, fearing job displacement. Term now denotes anyone opposing technology.

## M

**Macroprudential Policy**: Financial regulation aimed at system-wide stability rather than just individual institution safety. Includes countercyclical capital buffers, systemic risk charges.

**Malthusian Trap**: Population growth negates productivity gains, keeping living standards near subsistence. Described by Thomas Malthus (1798). Pre-industrial societies confined to this trap.

**Marginal Revolution**: Late 1800s shift in economic theory to subjective value (utility) and marginal analysis. Founders: Jevons, Menger, Walras.

**Market Economy**: Economic system where production and allocation decisions made through decentralized markets rather than central planning.

**Mercantilism**: Economic philosophy (1500s-1700s) that national wealth equals gold/silver reserves, trade is zero-sum, and government should maximize exports and minimize imports. Criticized by Adam Smith.

**Mixed Economy**: Combines market mechanisms with government intervention. All modern economies are mixed, varying in degree.

**Monetarism**: Economic school (Milton Friedman) emphasizing money supply's importance for inflation and business cycles. Influenced by Great Depression research.

**Monetary Policy**: Central bank management of interest rates and money supply to achieve macroeconomic goals (price stability, full employment).

**Monopoly**: Single seller dominating market, able to set prices above competitive levels. Can arise from economies of scale, network effects, or government privilege.

**Moral Hazard**: Risk-taking encouraged by expectation that others bear costs. Example: Bailout expectations encourage excessive bank risk.

**Most Favored Nation (MFN)**: Trade principle requiring equal tariff treatment for all GATT/WTO members. Prevents discrimination.

## N

**NAFTA**: North American Free Trade Agreement (1994-2020) between U.S., Canada, Mexico. Eliminated tariffs among members. Controversial; renegotiated as USMCA.

**Neoliberalism**: Political-economic ideology (ascendant 1980-2008) emphasizing markets, deregulation, privatization, and free trade. Associated with Reagan, Thatcher. Questioned after 2008 crisis.

**New Deal**: U.S. President Franklin Roosevelt's programs (1933-1939) responding to Great Depression. Created Social Security, financial regulation, labor protections, public works.

## O

**Oligopoly**: Market dominated by small number of large firms. Between monopoly and perfect competition.

## P

**Panic**: Sudden financial crisis characterized by fear-driven asset liquidation, bank runs, and credit freeze. Historical examples: Panic of 1837, 1873, 1907.

**Path Dependence**: Past choices constrain future options; history matters. Small early events can have lasting effects. Example: QWERTY keyboard.

**Petrodollars**: Revenues earned by oil-exporting countries. 1970s petrodollars recycled through banks to Latin America, contributing to 1980s debt crisis.

**Phillips Curve**: Inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation. Broke down in 1970s ("stagflation"). Modern version incorporates expectations.

**Ponzi Scheme**: Fraud where returns to early investors paid from later investors' capital, not genuine profits. Named after Charles Ponzi (1920). Minsky used "Ponzi financing" for similar dynamic in credit markets.

**Populism**: Political approach appealing to "common people" against "elites." Economic populism often includes protectionism, redistribution, anti-globalization. Left and right variants.

**Privatization**: Transfer of government-owned enterprises to private ownership. Widespread in 1980s-1990s.

**Proletariat**: Marxist term for working class who own no capital and sell labor.

**Protectionism**: Trade policy using tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Contrasts with free trade.

**Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)**: Method of comparing living standards by adjusting for price level differences between countries.

## Q

**Quantitative Easing (QE)**: Unconventional monetary policy where central bank purchases long-term assets to increase money supply and lower interest rates. Used when conventional rates near zero. Major tool post-2008.

## R

**Real vs. Nominal**: Real values adjusted for inflation; nominal values not. Real wages: Purchasing power. Nominal wages: Monetary amount.

**Recession**: Period of declining economic activity, typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. Distinguished from depression by severity.

**Reserve Currency**: Currency widely held by central banks and used in international trade. U.S. dollar is primary reserve currency post-WWII.

**Reshoring**: Moving production back to home country from foreign locations. Opposite of offshoring. Increased after COVID-19 supply chain disruptions.

## S

**Say's Law**: "Supply creates its own demand." Classical belief that general gluts impossible because production generates income to buy output. Keynes rejected this.

**Secular Stagnation**: Hypothesis that advanced economies face persistent slow growth due to demographic and technological factors. Revived by Larry Summers post-2008.

**Seigniorage**: Profit from issuing currency (difference between face value and production cost). Government revenue source.

**Silk Road**: Ancient trade routes connecting East Asia to Mediterranean (roughly 130 BCE - 1453 CE). Facilitated commerce and cultural exchange.

**Socialism**: Economic system with collective ownership of means of production. Varies from democratic socialism (market with welfare state) to command socialism (Soviet model).

**Sovereign Debt Crisis**: Government unable to pay or refinance debt. Examples: Latin America (1980s), Europe (2010-2012), Argentina (multiple times).

**Stagflation**: Simultaneous high inflation and unemployment. Occurred in 1970s, contradicting Phillips Curve. Led to monetarist and supply-side policy responses.

**Stock Market Crash**: Sudden, severe decline in stock prices. Notable crashes: 1929, 1987, 2000 (dot-com), 2008.

**Structural Adjustment**: IMF/World Bank policy conditions attached to loans, typically requiring austerity, privatization, and market liberalization. Controversial.

**Subprime Mortgages**: Loans to borrowers with poor credit. Central to 2008 financial crisis when default rates soared.

**Supply-Side Economics**: View that reducing taxes and regulation increases production (supply), driving growth. Associated with Reagan administration.

**Systemic Risk**: Risk that entire financial system fails, not just individual institutions. "Too big to fail" institutions create systemic risk.

## T

**Tariff**: Tax on imports. Used for revenue and/or protecting domestic industries.

**Terms of Trade**: Ratio of export prices to import prices. Improvement: Exports become more valuable relative to imports. Deterioration: Opposite. Developing countries often face unfavorable terms of trade.

**Too Big to Fail (TBTF)**: Institution whose failure would threaten entire financial system, creating expectation of bailout. Creates moral hazard.

**Trade Balance**: Exports minus imports. Surplus: Exports > imports. Deficit: Imports > exports.

**Trade War**: Escalating protectionist measures between countries. Example: U.S.-China tariff conflict (2018-2020).

**Triangular Trade**: Atlantic trade system (16th-19th centuries) involving manufactured goods, enslaved people, and raw materials.

**Triffin Dilemma**: Contradiction facing reserve currency country: Must run deficits to supply liquidity, but deficits undermine confidence. Contributed to Bretton Woods collapse.

## U

**Unemployment**: People seeking work but unable to find it. Natural rate: Unemployment at full employment (frictional and structural, not cyclical).

**Urbanization**: Population shift from rural to urban areas. Accelerated during industrialization.

## V

**Value-Added**: Increase in value created at each production stage. GDP is sum of all value-added in economy.

**Vertical Integration**: Single firm controlling multiple stages of production. Example: Carnegie Steel owned mines, furnaces, railroads.

## W

**Wage Labor**: Employment relationship where workers sell labor for wages. Replaced artisan and serf labor with industrialization.

**Wealth Inequality**: Unequal distribution of assets (property, savings, investments). Typically more extreme than income inequality.

**Welfare State**: Government system providing comprehensive social insurance (healthcare, unemployment, pensions) and public services. Extensive in Scandinavia and Western Europe.

**World Bank**: International institution (created 1944) providing development financing. Sister organization to IMF.

**WTO (World Trade Organization)**: International institution (created 1995) governing trade rules, resolving disputes, and hosting negotiations. Successor to GATT.

## Z

**Zaibatsu**: Large Japanese industrial conglomerates (pre-WWII). Family-controlled, integrated across industries. Examples: Mitsui, Mitsubishi. Dissolved post-WWII, re-formed as keiretsu.

**Zero Lower Bound**: Constraint on monetary policy when interest rates reach zero, limiting further conventional stimulus. Led to quantitative easing and other unconventional policies.

## Key Historical Figures

**Adam Smith** (1723-1790): Scottish philosopher, wrote *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), criticized mercantilism, described market's "invisible hand."

**David Ricardo** (1772-1823): English economist, developed comparative advantage theory justifying free trade.

**Karl Marx** (1818-1883): German philosopher, wrote *Das Kapital*, developed comprehensive critique of capitalism and theory of communism.

**John Maynard Keynes** (1883-1946): British economist, developed macroeconomic theory emphasizing aggregate demand management, critiqued gold standard.

**Friedrich Hayek** (1899-1992): Austrian economist, defended free markets, argued central planning leads to authoritarianism (*Road to Serfdom*).

**Milton Friedman** (1912-2006): American economist, monetarist, researched Great Depression, advocated free markets and limited government.

**Joseph Schumpeter** (1883-1950): Austrian economist, coined "creative destruction," analyzed capitalism's dynamics.

**Hyman Minsky** (1919-1996): American economist, developed financial instability hypothesis.

**Douglass North** (1920-2015): American economist, pioneered institutional economics, emphasized property rights and institutions for development.

**Robert Fogel** (1926-2013): American economist, pioneered cliometrics, controversial research on railroads and slavery.

**Thomas Piketty** (1971-): French economist, researched inequality trends, wrote *Capital in the Twenty-First Century*.

- **1760-1840**: Industrial Revolution in Britain

## Useful Formulas and Concepts

**Rule of 70**: Doubling time ≈ 70 / growth rate. Example: 2% annual growth → doubles in 35 years.

**Compound Growth**: Future value = Present value × (1 + growth rate)^years

**Real vs. Nominal**: Real value = Nominal value / (1 + inflation rate)

**GDP Growth**: (GDP_t - GDP_{t-1}) / GDP_{t-1} × 100%

**Trade Balance**: Exports - Imports

**Current Account**: Trade balance + Net investment income + Net transfers

**Gini Coefficient**: Measure of inequality, ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). Above 0.4 considered high inequality.
