# The Americas Country Profiles

## Regional overview

The Americas stretch from the Arctic to Patagonia. That means the region includes tundra, prairies, deserts, tropical forests, mountain chains, and giant river basins. It also includes very different political traditions: settler colonial states, post-colonial republics, federal systems, presidential systems, and highly centralised states.

This chapter uses five anchor countries: the United States, Canada, Mexico, Brazil, and Argentina.

## United States

- **Flag**: 🇺🇸
- **Capital**: Washington, D.C.
- **Famous landmarks**: Statue of Liberty, Grand Canyon, Golden Gate Bridge, National Mall
- **Key facts**: The United States combines continental scale, global military reach, major financial markets, and a large domestic economy.
- **Famous regions**: Northeast, South, Midwest, Great Plains, West Coast, Mountain West
- **History snapshot**: The United States emerged from British colonies, expanded westward, fought a civil war over union and slavery, industrialised rapidly, and became a global power in the 20th century.
- **Politics snapshot**: It is a federal presidential republic with strong institutions, intense party competition, and deep debate about federal versus state power.

The United States is especially useful for understanding how scale changes geography. It has multiple economic cores, several major coastlines, and huge climatic variety.

## Canada

- **Flag**: 🇨🇦
- **Capital**: Ottawa
- **Famous landmarks**: Niagara Falls, Banff, CN Tower, Parliament Hill
- **Key facts**: Canada is geographically vast, sparsely populated relative to its size, rich in natural resources, and deeply integrated with the United States.
- **Famous regions**: Ontario, Quebec, the Prairies, British Columbia, Atlantic Canada, the North
- **History snapshot**: Canada developed through French and British colonial legacies, confederation, westward expansion, and a gradual strengthening of national institutions.
- **Politics snapshot**: Canada is a parliamentary democracy and constitutional monarchy with strong provincial politics, especially around Quebec and western energy interests.

Canada shows how climate and latitude matter. Most people live near the US border, while the far north remains central to strategy, identity, and resource politics.

## Mexico

- **Flag**: 🇲🇽
- **Capital**: Mexico City
- **Famous landmarks**: Chichen Itza, Teotihuacan, Zocalo, Copper Canyon
- **Key facts**: Mexico connects North America to Latin America and is a major manufacturing, trade, and cultural power.
- **Famous regions**: Central Plateau, Yucatan Peninsula, northern border states, southern highlands
- **History snapshot**: Mexico’s history includes Indigenous civilisations, Spanish colonial rule, independence, revolution, and long periods of centralised party dominance.
- **Politics snapshot**: Mexico is a federal republic. Politics revolve around federal power, regional inequalities, security challenges, and the US relationship.

Mexico is a key example of how geography can produce both connection and pressure. It is close to the world’s largest economy while also carrying its own large internal diversity.

## Brazil

- **Flag**: 🇧🇷
- **Capital**: Brasilia
- **Famous landmarks**: Christ the Redeemer, Sugarloaf Mountain, Iguazu Falls, the Amazon
- **Key facts**: Brazil is the largest country in South America by area and population. It matters because of agriculture, minerals, biodiversity, energy, and continental scale.
- **Famous regions**: Amazonia, Northeast, Southeast, South, Central-West
- **History snapshot**: Brazil developed under Portuguese colonial rule, slavery, empire, republic, military rule, and democratisation.
- **Politics snapshot**: Brazil is a federal presidential republic with strong state politics, large urban inequality, and repeated struggles around corruption and institutional trust.

Brasilia is an important reminder that capitals are sometimes built deliberately to rebalance a country away from an older coastal core.

## Argentina

- **Flag**: 🇦🇷
- **Capital**: Buenos Aires
- **Famous landmarks**: Iguazu Falls, Perito Moreno Glacier, Plaza de Mayo, the Pampas
- **Key facts**: Argentina is a major agricultural producer with a long history of immigration, export orientation, and political volatility.
- **Famous regions**: Buenos Aires region, Pampas, Patagonia, Cuyo, the northwest
- **History snapshot**: Argentina became one of the world’s richer countries in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, but later faced repeated crises, military rule, and debt instability.
- **Politics snapshot**: Argentina is a federal republic, but politics often swing between competing economic visions, populist traditions, and reform efforts.

Argentina is useful because it shows how strong resources and human capital do not guarantee stable economic outcomes.

## Summary

The Americas make more sense when you combine scale, settlement patterns, and political history. The United States and Canada reveal northern continental geography. Mexico shows the strategic significance of a border state between larger worlds. Brazil demonstrates how size and internal regions shape a federation. Argentina shows the importance of political economy in a resource-rich country. Together they give you a strong starting frame for the hemisphere.
