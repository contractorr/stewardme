# Africa Country Profiles

## Regional overview

Africa is not one political or cultural block. It is a continent of deserts, rainforests, savannahs, highlands, major river systems, and long coastlines. It contains Arab North Africa, densely populated West African urban corridors, the East African highlands and rift systems, and the industrial and mining centres of Southern Africa.

To build a useful starting map, this chapter focuses on five anchor countries: Egypt, Nigeria, Ethiopia, Kenya, and South Africa.

## Subregions to know

| Subregion | Representative countries | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| North Africa | Egypt, Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia | Mediterranean access, Arab identity, and the Sahara edge |
| Sahel | Mauritania, Mali, Niger, Chad, Sudan | Dryland belt linking North and sub-Saharan Africa, often shaped by fragile states and migration routes |
| West Africa | Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, Cote d'Ivoire | Dense urban corridors, Atlantic trade, oil, and commercial hubs |
| Central Africa | Democratic Republic of the Congo, Cameroon, Gabon | Congo Basin rainforest, river systems, and major mineral wealth |
| East Africa | Ethiopia, Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda | Highlands, Rift Valley geography, ports, and Indian Ocean trade links |
| Southern Africa | South Africa, Botswana, Zimbabwe, Mozambique | Mining belts, plateau systems, and the continent's most industrialised southern economies |

## Egypt

- **Flag**: 🇪🇬
- **Capital**: Cairo
- **Famous landmarks**: Pyramids of Giza, the Sphinx, the Nile, Luxor, Abu Simbel
- **Key facts**: Egypt links North Africa, the Arab world, and the eastern Mediterranean. The Suez Canal makes it strategically important for global trade.
- **Famous regions**: Nile Delta, Upper Egypt, Sinai Peninsula, Western Desert
- **History snapshot**: Egypt is one of the world’s oldest state civilisations. Modern Egypt passed through Ottoman control, British influence, monarchy, republican revolution, and a long period of military-backed rule.
- **Politics snapshot**: Egypt is formally a republic, but the state has long been shaped by the military, a powerful presidency, and a strong emphasis on internal order.

Egypt shows how one river can define a country. Most of the population lives near the Nile and its delta because so much of the surrounding land is desert. That concentration makes Cairo extraordinarily central to national life.

## Nigeria

- **Flag**: 🇳🇬
- **Capital**: Abuja
- **Famous landmarks**: Zuma Rock, Lekki Conservation Centre, Olumo Rock, Lagos skyline
- **Key facts**: Nigeria is Africa’s most populous country and one of its largest economies. It matters because of population size, oil, finance, and culture.
- **Famous regions**: Lagos and the southwest, the Niger Delta, the northern states, the southeast
- **History snapshot**: Modern Nigeria emerged from British colonial rule and became independent in 1960. Since then it has experienced coups, civil war, military periods, and democratic transitions.
- **Politics snapshot**: Nigeria is a federal republic with complex regional, ethnic, and religious balancing. Elections matter, but so do state-level power structures and informal coalitions.

Nigeria is a good reminder that the capital is not always the most internationally visible city. Abuja is the capital, but Lagos dominates in business, population, and cultural reach.

## Ethiopia

- **Flag**: 🇪🇹
- **Capital**: Addis Ababa
- **Famous landmarks**: Lalibela churches, Simien Mountains, Aksum, Blue Nile Falls
- **Key facts**: Ethiopia is one of Africa’s oldest continuous states and a major political centre in the Horn of Africa. Addis Ababa hosts the African Union.
- **Famous regions**: Addis Ababa, Oromia, Amhara, Tigray, the Ethiopian Highlands
- **History snapshot**: Ethiopia is known for a long imperial tradition and for resisting formal long-term colonisation in the same way many African states did not. The modern era includes monarchy, revolution, socialist military rule, ethnic federalism, and recent conflict.
- **Politics snapshot**: Ethiopia is a federal republic, but politics are deeply shaped by ethnicity, regional autonomy, and disputes over central authority.

Ethiopia is especially important for understanding the relationship between highland geography, imperial history, and present-day regional politics.

## Kenya

- **Flag**: 🇰🇪
- **Capital**: Nairobi
- **Famous landmarks**: Maasai Mara, Mount Kenya, Amboseli, Nairobi National Park, Lamu
- **Key facts**: Kenya is one of East Africa’s main transport, finance, and diplomatic hubs. It is important in trade and regional logistics through the port of Mombasa.
- **Famous regions**: Nairobi, the Rift Valley, the coast, central highlands, northern arid counties
- **History snapshot**: Kenya’s modern state was shaped by British colonial rule, settler politics, anti-colonial struggle, and post-independence state consolidation.
- **Politics snapshot**: Kenya is a republic with competitive elections, strong regional politics, and ongoing debates around devolution, ethnicity, and constitutional order.

Kenya is useful because it combines wildlife-tourism imagery with something more important structurally: it is a regional gateway linking inland East Africa to the Indian Ocean.

## South Africa

- **Flag**: 🇿🇦
- **Capitals**: Pretoria (executive), Cape Town (legislative), Bloemfontein (judicial)
- **Famous landmarks**: Table Mountain, Robben Island, Kruger National Park, Cape of Good Hope
- **Key facts**: South Africa has one of the continent’s most industrialised economies and one of its most layered political histories.
- **Famous regions**: Gauteng, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, Eastern Cape
- **History snapshot**: South Africa’s history includes indigenous kingdoms, Dutch and British colonial rule, mineral-driven industrialisation, apartheid, and the democratic transition led by figures such as Nelson Mandela.
- **Politics snapshot**: South Africa is a constitutional democracy, but politics are shaped by inequality, party competition, coalition pressures, and the long legacy of apartheid.

South Africa is one of the clearest examples of how geography, resources, and political institutions interact over time. Mining, ports, internal migration, and regional identity all matter.

## Summary

These profiles give you a structured way to remember Africa through anchor states rather than scattered facts. The main lesson is diversity. Africa contains different ecological zones, political systems, historical paths, and regional roles. If you can explain how the Nile shapes Egypt, how federalism and population shape Nigeria, how highlands and ethnicity shape Ethiopia, how logistics shape Kenya, and how resource history shapes South Africa, you already have a stronger map of the continent than most casual readers.
