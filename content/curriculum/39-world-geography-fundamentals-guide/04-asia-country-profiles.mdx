# Asia Country Profiles

## Regional overview

Asia contains the world’s largest population centres, several of its oldest civilisations, major manufacturing hubs, critical shipping routes, and some of its most important strategic flashpoints. It is too large and diverse to understand as a single block, so this chapter focuses on six anchor countries.

This chapter emphasizes East, South, and Southeast Asia. The next chapter covers the Middle East and Central Asia as a separate strategic zone because those countries are often better understood through dryland geopolitics, empire routes, and energy geography.

## Subregions to know

| Subregion | Representative countries | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| East Asia | China, Japan, the Koreas, Taiwan, Mongolia | Manufacturing depth, dense urban coasts, and major security flashpoints |
| South Asia | India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal | Huge population concentration, monsoon agriculture, and post-colonial state systems |
| Mainland Southeast Asia | Vietnam, Thailand, Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos | River valleys, borderland politics, and continental trade links into China and India |
| Maritime Southeast Asia | Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei | Archipelagic geography, chokepoints, shipping routes, and dispersed island politics |

## China

- **Flag**: 🇨🇳
- **Capital**: Beijing
- **Famous landmarks**: Great Wall, Forbidden City, Bund, Terracotta Army
- **Key facts**: China is a continental-scale state, a major manufacturing power, and one of the world’s central geopolitical actors.
- **Famous regions**: North China Plain, the coast, Xinjiang, Tibet, Sichuan Basin, Pearl River Delta
- **History snapshot**: China’s history includes long imperial dynasties, foreign incursions, revolution, civil war, communist rule, reform-era growth, and renewed centralisation.
- **Politics snapshot**: China is a one-party state led by the Communist Party. Political authority is highly centralised even though the country is regionally diverse.

China is easiest to remember if you separate the densely populated eastern river-and-coastal core from the thinner-populated western frontier. That split helps explain why transport, industry, migration, and internal security are all so important to the state.

## India

- **Flag**: 🇮🇳
- **Capital**: New Delhi
- **Famous landmarks**: Taj Mahal, Red Fort, Varanasi, Gateway of India
- **Key facts**: India is one of the world’s largest democracies and population centres, with major linguistic, religious, and regional diversity.
- **Famous regions**: Indo-Gangetic Plain, Deccan Plateau, Himalayas, Kerala, Rajasthan, northeast India
- **History snapshot**: India’s modern state emerged from ancient civilisations, imperial traditions, British colonial rule, independence, and partition.
- **Politics snapshot**: India is a federal parliamentary democracy. Elections are huge in scale, and politics mix national parties, state politics, identity, and development.

India is a good example of how one state can contain many political worlds at once. The Hindi belt, peninsular south, Himalayan borderlands, and northeast all matter differently, which is why regional politics remain so important.

## Japan

- **Flag**: 🇯🇵
- **Capital**: Tokyo
- **Famous landmarks**: Mount Fuji, Tokyo Tower, Fushimi Inari, Hiroshima Peace Memorial
- **Key facts**: Japan is an island nation with major industrial and technological influence, strong institutions, and a close US alliance.
- **Famous regions**: Honshu, Hokkaido, Kyushu, Shikoku, Okinawa
- **History snapshot**: Japan developed through imperial courts, samurai rule, rapid modernisation in the Meiji period, imperial expansion, defeat in World War II, and postwar reconstruction.
- **Politics snapshot**: Japan is a parliamentary constitutional monarchy. Politics are stable by regional standards, though shaped by demographic pressure and security debates.

Japan's geography makes the sea central to national life. Population, industry, and transport cluster along narrow coastal corridors, while energy dependence and maritime security keep external sea lanes strategically important.

## Indonesia

- **Flag**: 🇮🇩
- **Capital**: Jakarta
- **Famous landmarks**: Borobudur, Komodo National Park, Bali temples, Prambanan
- **Key facts**: Indonesia is the world’s largest archipelagic state and a major maritime country sitting across critical sea lanes.
- **Famous regions**: Java, Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Papua, Bali
- **History snapshot**: Indonesia emerged from maritime trade networks, Dutch colonial rule, anti-colonial independence struggle, authoritarian centralisation, and democratic reform.
- **Politics snapshot**: Indonesia is a presidential republic with significant regional diversity and an important balance between national unity and local identity.

Indonesia is useful because it forces you to think in sea lanes rather than land borders. Java may dominate politically and demographically, but the country only works as a state because shipping, ports, and island-to-island administration hold it together.

## South Korea

- **Flag**: 🇰🇷
- **Capital**: Seoul
- **Famous landmarks**: Gyeongbokgung Palace, DMZ, N Seoul Tower, Jeju Island
- **Key facts**: South Korea is a major advanced economy, technology power, and US ally facing a permanent security challenge from North Korea.
- **Famous regions**: Seoul Capital Area, Gyeongsang, Jeolla, Gangwon, Jeju
- **History snapshot**: Korea experienced dynastic rule, Japanese occupation, national division after World War II, war, authoritarian development, and democratisation.
- **Politics snapshot**: South Korea is a presidential democracy with strong institutions, polarised elections, and a constant national-security context.

South Korea shows how security pressure can coexist with advanced industrial development. The result is a state that is globally connected through technology and trade while remaining permanently shaped by the unresolved Korean conflict.

## Singapore

- **Flag**: 🇸🇬
- **Capital**: Singapore
- **Famous landmarks**: Marina Bay Sands, Gardens by the Bay, Merlion, Changi
- **Key facts**: Singapore is a city-state whose significance comes from trade, finance, shipping, and state capacity rather than territorial scale.
- **Famous regions**: Central Business District, Marina Bay, Jurong, Sentosa
- **History snapshot**: Singapore developed from a British trading outpost into an independent city-state after separation from Malaysia in 1965.
- **Politics snapshot**: Singapore is a parliamentary republic with a highly capable state, long-term ruling-party dominance, and strong emphasis on order and planning.

Singapore matters because it proves that location and state capacity can matter more than land area. Its role as a port, financial hub, and logistics node is tied directly to its position on one of the world's busiest maritime routes.

## Comparing the six

| Country | Main pattern |
|------|---------------|
| China | Scale, central control, manufacturing, great-power strategy |
| India | Democratic scale, regional diversity, post-colonial statehood |
| Japan | Island industrial power with postwar stability |
| Indonesia | Archipelago, maritime geography, regional diversity |
| South Korea | Security pressure, export economy, democratic state |
| Singapore | Small territory, outsized global role |

## Summary

Asia is central to the modern world because it combines population, production, shipping, finance, and geopolitics. These six countries show different ways power can work: through size, democracy, industrial depth, maritime location, alliance systems, or exceptional state capacity. If you understand how they differ, you have a strong foundation for understanding Asia more broadly.
