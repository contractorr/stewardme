# Middle East & Central Asia Country Profiles

## Regional overview

This region links Europe, Asia, and Africa. It contains major energy reserves, religious centres, strategic straits, and routes that empires, merchants, and militaries have contested for centuries. It also contains very different state forms, from monarchies to republics to post-Soviet systems.

## Subregions to know

| Subregion | Representative countries | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Anatolia and the Turkish straits | Turkey | Bridge zone between Europe and Asia controlling Black Sea access |
| Arabian Peninsula and Gulf | Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Oman, Kuwait | Oil and gas concentration, monarchies, and maritime chokepoints |
| Iranian plateau | Iran | Large civilisational core linking mountains, deserts, and Gulf access |
| Levant | Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, Syria | Religious centrality, strategic density, and long-running conflict systems |
| Caucasus | Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan | Mountain corridor between Black and Caspian Seas shaped by imperial competition |
| Central Asia | Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan | Steppe and dryland region balancing Russia, China, energy, and trade corridors |

## Turkey

- **Flag**: 🇹🇷
- **Capital**: Ankara
- **Famous landmarks**: Hagia Sophia, Bosporus, Cappadocia, Ephesus
- **Key facts**: Turkey sits at the junction of Europe, the Middle East, the Black Sea, and the eastern Mediterranean.
- **Famous regions**: Istanbul region, Anatolia, Aegean coast, Black Sea coast, southeast
- **History snapshot**: Turkey emerged from the core of the Ottoman Empire and was refounded as a republic under Mustafa Kemal Ataturk after imperial collapse.
- **Politics snapshot**: Turkey is a presidential republic, but politics are shaped by strong executive power, military history, nationalism, and balancing between multiple regions.

Turkey is best understood as a crossroads state. Control of the straits, proximity to Europe, and influence in the Middle East all matter at once, which is why its domestic politics often have major external consequences.

## Saudi Arabia

- **Flag**: 🇸🇦
- **Capital**: Riyadh
- **Famous landmarks**: Mecca, Medina, Al-Ula, Kingdom Centre
- **Key facts**: Saudi Arabia is central because of oil, religious significance, and its role in Gulf and Arab politics.
- **Famous regions**: Riyadh region, Hejaz, Eastern Province, Asir, Najd
- **History snapshot**: The modern kingdom formed through 20th-century territorial consolidation around the Al Saud dynasty and later transformed through oil wealth.
- **Politics snapshot**: Saudi Arabia is an absolute monarchy. Politics revolve around royal authority, state modernisation, religion, and regional security.

Saudi Arabia's geography helps explain its importance. The combination of holy cities, desert interior, Gulf energy infrastructure, and Red Sea position makes it simultaneously a religious centre and a strategic energy state.

## Iran

- **Flag**: 🇮🇷
- **Capital**: Tehran
- **Famous landmarks**: Persepolis, Azadi Tower, Isfahan mosques, Golestan Palace
- **Key facts**: Iran is a large civilisational state with mountains, deserts, energy reserves, and major strategic influence across the Middle East.
- **Famous regions**: Tehran, Isfahan, Khuzestan, Persian Gulf coast, Iranian plateau
- **History snapshot**: Iran’s history includes ancient Persian empires, monarchy, foreign intervention, the 1979 revolution, and the creation of an Islamic Republic.
- **Politics snapshot**: Iran mixes republican institutions with strong clerical oversight and security-state influence.

Iran makes sense when you combine civilisational depth with hard geography. Mountains, plateau terrain, and access to both the Gulf and broader West Asian routes have helped sustain a large state with a strong sense of historical continuity.

## Israel

- **Flag**: 🇮🇱
- **Capital**: Jerusalem (seat of government; status disputed internationally)
- **Famous landmarks**: Western Wall, Dome of the Rock, Dead Sea, Masada
- **Key facts**: Israel is a small but strategically central state shaped by religion, migration, conflict, technology, and military power.
- **Famous regions**: Jerusalem area, Tel Aviv coast, Galilee, Negev, coastal plain
- **History snapshot**: Israel was established in 1948 after British mandate rule and in the aftermath of the Holocaust, leading to repeated wars and ongoing conflict with Palestinians and neighbouring states.
- **Politics snapshot**: Israel is a parliamentary democracy with coalition politics, strong security institutions, and constant debate over borders, religion, identity, and constitutional balance.

Israel is one of the clearest examples of strategic density. Short distances, contested borders, religious symbolism, and advanced military and technological capacity all compress politics into a very small geographic space.

## Kazakhstan

- **Flag**: 🇰🇿
- **Capital**: Astana
- **Famous landmarks**: Bayterek Tower, Charyn Canyon, Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi, steppe landscapes
- **Key facts**: Kazakhstan is Central Asia’s largest state by area, rich in oil, gas, and uranium, and strategically important because it borders Russia and China.
- **Famous regions**: Astana, Almaty region, Caspian west, central steppe, Turkestan south
- **History snapshot**: Kazakhstan’s modern state emerged from nomadic steppe history, Russian imperial expansion, Soviet rule, and independence after 1991.
- **Politics snapshot**: Kazakhstan is a presidential republic with strong executive traditions, gradual institutional change, and careful balancing between major powers.

Kazakhstan is useful because it shows how a large land state can matter through location as much as population. Steppe scale, energy resources, and the need to balance Russia, China, and wider Eurasian routes define much of its strategy.

## Summary

The Middle East and Central Asia reward geographic thinking. Turkey shows the power of crossroads geography. Saudi Arabia demonstrates the combination of monarchy, energy, and religious centrality. Iran shows the endurance of civilisational identity and regional influence. Israel illustrates how strategic density and contested history shape a state. Kazakhstan shows how large land states can matter through resources and position even without huge populations.
