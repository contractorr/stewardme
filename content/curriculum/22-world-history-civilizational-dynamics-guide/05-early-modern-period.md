# Early Modern Period (~1450–1800)

## European Expansion & Colonialism

### Why Europe?

The "Great Divergence" question: why did a relatively small, resource-poor peninsula dominate the globe?

**Contributing factors:**
1. **Political fragmentation** — ~500 competing polities → couldn't suppress innovation; Columbus rejected by Portugal, funded by Spain; competition drove exploration
2. **Geography** — Atlantic-facing; easy access to Americas (shorter than from China)
3. **Military competition** — Constant warfare → arms race → advanced military technology
4. **Institutional development** — Property rights, contract law, corporations (Dutch East India Company, 1602 = first modern corporation)
5. **Coal + colonies** (Pomeranz) — English coal near navigable waterways; New World resources (sugar, silver, cotton) financed industrialization
6. **Disease advantage** — Eurasian disease ecology (centuries of plague) gave Europeans immunity Amerindians lacked

### The Columbian Exchange

Biological exchange between Old and New Worlds after 1492 — arguably most consequential event in human history since agriculture.

**Old World → New World:**
- Diseases: smallpox, measles, influenza, typhus → killed 50–90% of indigenous Americans (largest demographic catastrophe in history; ~55 million deaths by 1600)
- Animals: horses, cattle, pigs, sheep, chickens
- Crops: wheat, rice, sugarcane, coffee
- Technology: iron, wheel, gunpowder

**New World → Old World:**
- Crops: maize, potatoes, tomatoes, tobacco, cacao, chili peppers, rubber, vanilla
- Diseases: possibly syphilis (debated)
- Silver: Potosí (Bolivia) → financed Spanish Empire and global trade; caused inflation in Europe and China

**Impact of New World crops:** Potatoes and maize dramatically increased European/Asian caloric output → population growth. Ireland's population tripled 1700–1845 (then crashed in potato famine). China's population expanded into previously marginal lands using American crops.

### Spanish Empire
- Conquered Aztec (Cortés, 1519–1521) and Inca (Pizarro, 1532–1533) empires
- Extracted massive silver (Potosí: ~45,000 tonnes; Zacatecas)
- *Encomienda* system: forced indigenous labor; devastating mortality
- Controlled Philippines (named for Philip II); Manila galleon trade (silver to China for silk/porcelain)
- Declined: overextension, inflation from silver, costly European wars, British naval superiority

### Portuguese Empire
- First global maritime empire; established trading posts (not large territorial colonies initially)
- Key nodes: Goa (India), Malacca (Malaysia), Macau (China), Brazil, Angola, Mozambique
- Controlled spice trade from Indian Ocean
- Small population (~1 million) limited colonization capacity; relied on naval superiority and alliances

### Atlantic Slave Trade (~1500–1870)

| Period | Estimated Enslaved Transported | Primary Destination |
|---|---|---|
| 1500–1600 | ~400,000 | Spanish Americas, Brazil |
| 1600–1700 | ~1.8 million | Caribbean, Brazil |
| 1700–1800 | ~6.1 million | Caribbean, Brazil, North America |
| 1800–1870 | ~3.3 million | Brazil, Cuba |
| **Total** | **~12.5 million** | (~1.8 million died in transit) |

- Triangular trade: European manufactured goods → Africa → enslaved people → Americas → raw materials (sugar, cotton, tobacco) → Europe
- Devastated West and Central African societies; distorted economies toward slave-raiding
- Financed Caribbean sugar plantations = most profitable colonies
- Abolished: Britain (1807 trade, 1833 slavery), France (1848), US (1865), Brazil (1888, last Western Hemisphere country)
- **Demographic impact on Africa:** Estimated 12.5 million transported + millions more killed in slave raids/wars; total African population loss (including those killed, never born) estimated at 50–100 million over 4 centuries; some regions depopulated
- **Legacy:** Racial ideologies created to justify enslavement persisted long after abolition; shaped American and Caribbean societies permanently

### Dutch Golden Age (~1600–1700)
- Netherlands: tiny country (~1.5 million people) became world's richest per capita
- **VOC** (Dutch East India Company, 1602–1799): First publicly traded company; monopolized spice trade; at peak worth ~$7.9 trillion (inflation-adjusted)
- **WIC** (Dutch West India Company): Atlantic trade; briefly held Brazil (1630–1654); New Amsterdam (→ New York)
- Amsterdam: world's financial center; Bank of Amsterdam (1609); stock exchange; tulip mania (1637)
- Cultural peak: Rembrandt, Vermeer, Spinoza, Huygens, Leeuwenhoek (microscope)
- Declined: wars with England and France; small population couldn't sustain global empire

### Africa in the Early Modern Period
Not just victim of slave trade; had own dynamics:
- **Ethiopian Empire:** Maintained independence; Jesuit missions (1550s–1630s) ultimately expelled; remained Orthodox Christian
- **Kongo Kingdom:** Complex centralized state; adopted Christianity (1491); devastated by slave trade
- **Asante Empire** (Ghana, ~1670–1902): Gold-rich; centralized bureaucracy; golden stool; actively engaged in slave trade before abolition
- **Benin Kingdom** (Nigeria): Bronze/brass casting (Benin Bronzes — sophisticated art looted by British 1897); complex state
- **Zulu Kingdom** (1816–1897): Shaka Zulu's military innovations (*impi* regiments, short stabbing spear/*iklwa*, age-regiment system); *Mfecane* upheaval reshaped southern Africa

## Gunpowder Empires

### Ottoman Empire (see Ch. 04) — peaked ~1520–1566

### Safavid Empire, Persia (1501–1736)
- **Shah Ismail I** (1501–1524): Founded Safavid state; forcibly converted Iran to Twelver Shia Islam; created Shia-Sunni geopolitical divide that persists today
- **Battle of Chaldiran (1514):** Ottomans defeated Safavids using gunpowder artillery; established Ottoman-Safavid border roughly where Turkey-Iran border remains
- **Shah Abbas I** (1588–1629): Military reform (slave soldiers *ghulams* from Caucasus), new capital Isfahan ("half the world" — pop ~600,000; stunning architecture: Naqsh-e Jahan square, mosques, bridges), alliance with Europeans against Ottomans, expelled Portuguese from Hormuz (1622)
- Silk trade: major export to Europe; Armenian merchants in Julfa (Isfahan) as commercial intermediaries
- Declined: weak successors, Afghan invasion (1722), Nader Shah briefly restored power then assassinated (1747)

### Mughal Empire, India (1526–1857)
- Founded by **Babur** (1526, Battle of Panipat: 12,000 troops with artillery defeated 100,000 under Ibrahim Lodi; gunpowder decisive); descended from both Timur and Genghis Khan
- **Humayun** (1530–1556): Lost empire, regained it; introduced Persian cultural influence
- **Akbar** (1556–1605): Greatest Mughal; religious tolerance (*Din-i-Ilahi* — syncretic faith; *sulh-i-kul* = universal peace); *mansabdari* system (ranked hierarchy of officials); abolished *jizya* (tax on non-Muslims); revenue system under Todar Mal; 9 "jewels" (navratnas) of court including Birbal, Tansen
- **Jahangir** (1605–1627): Patron of miniature painting; Nur Jahan (wife) wielded significant power
- **Shah Jahan** (1628–1658): Taj Mahal (1632–1653, ~20,000 workers, white marble from Rajasthan, semi-precious stones from across Asia); Peacock Throne; imperial overreach in Deccan wars
- **Aurangzeb** (1658–1707): Expanded territory to maximum (~4 million km², ~150 million people) but reversed religious tolerance; reimposed *jizya*; destroyed Hindu temples; alienated Hindus, Sikhs, Marathas; 27-year Deccan campaign drained treasury
- Mughal India = ~25% of world GDP in 1700; textile exports (muslin, calico) dominated global markets
- Post-Aurangzeb fragmentation → Maratha Confederacy, regional nawabs → British East India Company gradually took control (1757 Battle of Plassey: Clive bribed Mir Jafar; 3,000 EIC troops defeated 50,000 Nawab's forces)

### Comparison: Three Gunpowder Empires

| Feature | Ottoman | Safavid | Mughal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peak area | ~5.2M km² | ~2.5M km² | ~4M km² |
| Peak population | ~35M | ~10M | ~150M |
| Religion | Sunni Islam | Shia Islam | Sunni Islam (tolerant under Akbar) |
| Military elite | Janissaries (devshirme) | Qizilbash + ghulams | Mansabdars (ranked nobles) |
| Key weakness | Military conservatism | Shia-Sunni conflict | Religious intolerance (Aurangzeb) |
| Decline factor | Failure to industrialize | Weak successors | Maratha/Sikh resistance + British |

## Renaissance, Reformation, Scientific Revolution

### Renaissance (~1350–1600)
- Cultural revival beginning in Italian city-states (Florence, Venice, Rome)
- Recovery of classical texts (via Islamic preservation + Byzantine refugees after 1453)
- **Key shift:** Humanism — focus on human capacity, individual achievement, secular knowledge
- Art: perspective (Brunelleschi), anatomy (Leonardo, Michelangelo), oil painting (Van Eyck)
- Political thought: Machiavelli's *The Prince* (1513) — politics as it is, not as it should be
- Printing press (Gutenberg, ~1440): revolutionary information technology; books cheapened 80% by 1500; enabled Reformation

### Protestant Reformation (1517–1648)
- **Luther's 95 Theses (1517):** Attacked indulgences; rapidly spread via printing press
- Core Protestant ideas: *sola scriptura* (scripture alone), *sola fide* (faith alone), priesthood of all believers
- **Calvin** (Geneva): predestination, work ethic, theocratic governance → Calvinism spread to Netherlands, Scotland, Huguenots
- **Henry VIII** (England): broke with Rome over divorce → Church of England (1534)
- **Counter-Reformation:** Council of Trent (1545–1563), Jesuits, Inquisition
- **Wars of Religion:** French Wars of Religion (1562–1598), Thirty Years' War (1618–1648, ~8 million dead, ~20% of German population)
- **Peace of Westphalia (1648):** Ended Thirty Years' War; established principle of state sovereignty; each prince chooses religion of territory; origin of modern international system

### Scientific Revolution (~1543–1687)
- Covered in history-of-science-guide; key for civilizational dynamics:
- Copernicus (1543: heliocentric model), Galileo (1610s: telescope observations; persecuted by Inquisition), Kepler (planetary motion laws), Newton (*Principia* 1687: gravity, motion, calculus)
- Shift from Aristotelian/scholastic authority to empirical/mathematical method
- Royal Society (1660), Académie des Sciences (1666) — institutionalized science; peer review
- **Why Europe?** Printing press (rapid dissemination), university system (~70 universities by 1500), competition between states (no single authority could suppress), patronage from multiple courts, Protestant emphasis on individual inquiry
- **Why not China/Islamic world?** China had institutional unity that could suppress (Zheng He's fleet disbanded; printing with characters less revolutionary); Islamic world's scientific tradition declined after ~12th century (debated: political fragmentation, Mongol destruction, madrasah curriculum narrowing, or Ottoman emphasis on religious over natural philosophy)

## Rise of Nation-States

### Westphalian System (post-1648)
- State sovereignty: each state has supreme authority within borders
- Non-interference in internal affairs
- Balance of power as organizing principle
- Replaced: feudal overlapping authorities, papal supremacy, imperial claims

### Key State-Building Developments
- **Standing armies** replaced feudal levies (France under Louis XIV: ~400,000)
- **Bureaucracies** grew: tax collection, census, regulation
- **Mercantilism:** State direction of economy for national power; colonies as captive markets
- **Absolutism:** Louis XIV ("L'état, c'est moi"), Peter the Great (Russia), Frederick the Great (Prussia)
- **Constitutional alternatives:** English Bill of Rights (1689), Dutch Republic

### The European Balance of Power

Major powers and their strategic positions:

| Power | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| France | Largest population, rich agriculture, central location | Continental enemies on multiple borders |
| Britain | Naval supremacy, island security, colonial empire | Small army; needed continental allies |
| Habsburg Austria | Central position, dynastic lands | Multi-ethnic; no natural frontiers; Ottoman threat |
| Prussia | Efficient army, bureaucracy | Small, surrounded, resource-poor |
| Russia | Vast territory, large population | Underdeveloped; harsh climate; limited warm-water ports |
| Ottoman Empire | Strategic position, large military | Declining relative to European military innovation |

## East Asia in the Early Modern Period

### Ming Dynasty (1368–1644)
- Restored Chinese rule after Mongol Yuan; ethno-nationalism
- **Zheng He's voyages** (1405–1433): Massive treasure fleets (300+ ships, 27,000 crew) reached East Africa; then abruptly halted
- **Why stopped:** Confucian officials viewed trade as beneath China's dignity; factional politics; cost
- **Consequence:** China voluntarily withdrew from maritime competition; Portugal filled the vacuum 70 years later
- Great Wall rebuilt; Ming tombs; porcelain exports
- Fell to Manchu invasion → Qing Dynasty

### Qing Dynasty (1644–1912)
- Manchu (non-Chinese) rulers; adopted Chinese institutions
- Expanded territory: Tibet, Xinjiang, Mongolia, Taiwan
- **Kangxi** (1661–1722) and **Qianlong** (1735–1796): prosperity, territorial peak
- Population tripled (150M → 450M) partly due to American crops (maize, sweet potatoes)
- Increasingly closed to foreign trade (Canton system); rejected British trade overtures (Macartney Mission, 1793: "We possess all things")

### Tokugawa Japan (1603–1868)
- **Sengoku period** (1467–1615): "Warring States"; feudal lords (*daimyo*) fought for supremacy
- Three unifiers: Oda Nobunaga (1560s–1582, introduced mass musket tactics at Battle of Nagashino 1575), Toyotomi Hideyoshi (1582–1598, unified Japan, invaded Korea twice: 1592, 1597), Tokugawa Ieyasu (1600–1616, won Battle of Sekigahara 1600 with 89,000 troops → established shogunate)
- **Sakoku** (closed country, 1633–1853): Minimal foreign contact; only Dutch and Chinese at Nagasaki (Dejima island); Christians expelled/executed; prevented colonization
- Paradoxically: high literacy (~40% male, ~15% female — among world's highest), urbanization (Edo/Tokyo ~1 million by 1700 = world's largest), sophisticated culture (kabuki, ukiyo-e, haiku)
- Rigid social hierarchy: samurai (~6% of population) → farmers → artisans → merchants (but merchants grew wealthy; samurai impoverished)
- **Sankin-kotai**: Daimyo required to spend alternate years in Edo; controlled regional lords; stimulated road network and commerce
- "Pax Tokugawa": 250 years of internal peace; rice-based economy; ~31 million population by 1720
- **Rangaku** ("Dutch studies"): Japanese scholars learned Western science through Dutch books at Nagasaki; maintained some awareness of Western developments

### Joseon Korea (1392–1897)
- Founded by Yi Seong-gye; neo-Confucian state; capital Hanyang (Seoul)
- **King Sejong** (1418–1450): Created Hangul alphabet (1443); simplest and most scientific writing system ever designed (praised by linguists); promoted science, agriculture, music
- *Yangban* elite: hereditary scholarly-official class; Confucian civil service exams
- Japanese invasions (1592, 1597): **Admiral Yi Sun-sin** — invented *geobukseon* (turtle ship); won 23/23 naval battles; one of history's greatest admirals; Battle of Myeongnyang (1597): 13 ships defeated 133 Japanese ships
- Manchu invasions (1627, 1636): forced to become Qing tributary
- "Hermit Kingdom": restricted foreign contact (like Tokugawa Japan)

### Southeast Asia in the Early Modern Period
- **Ayutthaya** (Siam, 1351–1767): Major regional power; cosmopolitan trading hub; Japanese, Chinese, Persian, European communities; destroyed by Burma (1767); rebuilt as Bangkok
- **Toungoo Dynasty** (Burma, 1510–1752): Largest empire in Southeast Asian history under Bayinnaung (1550s–1581); united Burma, conquered Siam, Laos
- **Mataram Sultanate** (Java, 1587–1755): Largest Islamic state in Southeast Asia; controlled central/eastern Java; gradual Dutch encroachment
- **Spanish Philippines** (1565–1898): Manila galleon trade (Acapulco–Manila, 1565–1815); Chinese goods → silver from Americas; Catholic conversion; only major Southeast Asian colony with strong Western cultural transformation
- **VOC (Dutch East India Company, 1602–1799)**: World's first multinational corporation; monopolized spice trade; conquered Indonesian archipelago; Batavia (Jakarta) as headquarters; at peak worth ~$7.9 trillion (inflation-adjusted)

## The Great Divergence (~1750+)

By ~1750, Western Europe began pulling ahead of the rest of the world in income per capita. Why?

| Factor | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Coal** | Britain had accessible coal near waterways; China's coal was inland, far from population |
| **Colonies** | Provided raw materials, captive markets, sugar/cotton profits financed investment |
| **Institutions** | Property rights, patent law, limited government, Glorious Revolution (1688) |
| **Culture** | "Enlightenment" values: empiricism, progress, individual rights, skepticism of authority |
| **Competition** | Fragmented Europe → states competed for military/economic advantage → innovation rewarded |
| **Slavery** | Atlantic slave trade generated capital; cotton/sugar plantations fueled industry |
| **Contingency** | Many factors; could have gone differently |

**Pomeranz's argument:** As late as 1750, Yangtze Delta and England were comparable in living standards, markets, proto-industry. Two "windfalls" tipped the balance: (1) easily accessible coal, (2) New World resources obtained through colonialism.

**Debate continues.** No consensus on relative weight of factors.

## The Enlightenment (~1685–1815)

**Intellectual revolution that shaped modern political thought:**

| Thinker | Key Ideas | Work |
|---|---|---|
| John Locke (1632–1704) | Natural rights (life, liberty, property); social contract; right to revolution | *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) |
| Montesquieu (1689–1755) | Separation of powers (executive, legislative, judicial) | *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748) |
| Voltaire (1694–1778) | Freedom of speech/religion; critique of Church; wit as weapon | *Candide* (1759) |
| Rousseau (1712–1778) | General will; popular sovereignty; social contract; noble savage | *The Social Contract* (1762) |
| Adam Smith (1723–1790) | Invisible hand; division of labor; free markets (see economic-history-guide) | *Wealth of Nations* (1776) |
| Kant (1724–1804) | Perpetual peace through republican government; categorical imperative | *Perpetual Peace* (1795) |
| Diderot (1713–1784) | *Encyclopédie* (1751–1772): 28 volumes; systematized all knowledge; subversive | *Encyclopédie* |

**Impact:** American Revolution, French Revolution, abolition movements, constitutional government, secularism, human rights discourse. But also: justified colonialism through "civilizing mission"; Eurocentric universalism.
