# Ancient Civilizations (~3500 BC – 500 BC)

## The Agricultural Revolution → Urbanization Pipeline

Agriculture emerged independently in at least 7 locations:

| Region | Date | Key Crops/Animals |
|---|---|---|
| Fertile Crescent | ~9500 BC | Wheat, barley, sheep, goats, cattle |
| China (Yellow/Yangtze) | ~8000 BC | Rice, millet, pigs |
| Mesoamerica | ~7000 BC | Maize, squash, beans |
| Andes | ~6000 BC | Potatoes, quinoa, llamas |
| Sahel (Africa) | ~5000 BC | Sorghum, millet, cowpeas |
| Eastern North America | ~4000 BC | Sunflower, squash |
| New Guinea | ~7000 BC | Taro, yams, bananas |

**Agriculture → surplus → specialization → social hierarchy → writing → cities → states**

This pipeline took ~5,000 years in Mesopotamia (fastest) vs never completing in some regions. Key bottleneck: domesticable large mammals (Eurasia had 13 of 14 candidates).

## Mesopotamia (~3500–539 BC)

### Sumer (~3500–2004 BC)
- First cities: Uruk (pop ~40,000 by 3000 BC), Ur, Lagash, Eridu
- **Writing:** Cuneiform (~3200 BC) — initially for accounting, later literature
- **Innovations:** Wheel, plow, sailboat, bronze metallurgy, 60-base math (still in our hours/minutes), ziggurats
- City-states with temple economies; priest-kings → secular kings
- *Epic of Gilgamesh* — oldest surviving literature (~2100 BC)
- Constant warfare between city-states over water rights

### Akkadian Empire (~2334–2154 BC)
- **Sargon of Akkad** — first empire builder; unified Sumer by conquest
- Centralized administration, standardized weights/measures
- Controlled trade routes from Mediterranean to Persian Gulf
- Collapsed after ~180 years — drought + internal rebellion (Turchin pattern)

### Babylon
- **Old Babylonian** (~1894–1595 BC): Hammurabi's Code (282 laws; eye-for-an-eye; class-differentiated justice)
- **Neo-Babylonian** (~626–539 BC): Nebuchadnezzar II, Hanging Gardens (maybe), destroyed Jerusalem (586 BC)

### Assyria (~2500–609 BC)
- Most militaristic Mesopotamian power; iron weapons, siege warfare, cavalry
- Terrorized conquered peoples (mass deportations, flaying, impalement) as deliberate strategy
- Neo-Assyrian Empire (~911–609 BC) controlled Mesopotamia, Levant, Egypt
- Library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh — 30,000+ clay tablets; preserved Sumerian literature
- Collapse: overextension + coalition of Babylonians and Medes

## Ancient Egypt (~3100–332 BC)

| Period | Dates | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Early Dynastic | ~3100–2686 BC | Unification (Narmer/Menes), hieroglyphics |
| Old Kingdom | 2686–2181 BC | Great Pyramids of Giza (Khufu ~2560 BC), centralized state |
| First Intermediate | 2181–2055 BC | Fragmentation, provincial power |
| Middle Kingdom | 2055–1650 BC | Reunification, expansion into Nubia, classical literature |
| Second Intermediate | 1650–1550 BC | Hyksos invasion (introduced horse/chariot) |
| New Kingdom | 1550–1077 BC | Imperial peak: Thutmose III, Hatshepsut, Akhenaten, Ramesses II |
| Third Intermediate + Late | 1077–332 BC | Decline, Libyan/Nubian/Persian rule |

**Why Egypt lasted so long:** Geographic isolation (deserts east/west, sea north, cataracts south), Nile's reliable floods, cultural conservatism, strong religious-political unity (pharaoh = living god).

**Key innovations:** Papyrus, 365-day calendar, monumental architecture, mummification, surgical techniques (Edwin Smith Papyrus).

### Key Pharaohs of the New Kingdom

| Pharaoh | Reign | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Ahmose I | 1550–1525 BC | Expelled Hyksos; founded New Kingdom |
| Hatshepsut | 1479–1458 BC | Female pharaoh; massive building program; Punt expedition; mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahri |
| Thutmose III | 1458–1425 BC | "Napoleon of Egypt"; 17 campaigns; Battle of Megiddo (~1457 BC, earliest battle with detailed record); expanded empire to Euphrates |
| Amenhotep III | 1386–1349 BC | Diplomatic peak; Amarna Letters show international system |
| Akhenaten | 1353–1336 BC | Religious revolution: monotheistic worship of Aten; moved capital to Amarna; neglected foreign affairs; reversed after death |
| Tutankhamun | 1332–1323 BC | Minor king; famous for intact tomb (discovered 1922 by Howard Carter) |
| Ramesses II | 1279–1213 BC | 66-year reign; Battle of Kadesh (~1274 BC) vs Hittites → world's first known peace treaty; Abu Simbel temples |
| Ramesses III | 1186–1155 BC | Defeated Sea Peoples (~1178 BC); last great pharaoh; assassinated by harem conspiracy |

## Nubia and the Kingdom of Kush (~2500 BC – 350 AD)

- Southern neighbor of Egypt (modern Sudan); often overlooked
- **Kerma** (~2500–1500 BC): First Nubian kingdom; rivaled Egypt; massive mud-brick temples (*deffufa*)
- Conquered by Egypt's New Kingdom; later regained independence
- **Napatan Period** (~750–590 BC): Kushite pharaohs conquered Egypt — 25th Dynasty (Piye, Taharqa); ruled Egypt for ~70 years
- **Meroitic Period** (~590 BC–350 AD): Capital at Meroe; iron smelting center; own script (Meroitic, partially undeciphered); pyramids (more than Egypt's — over 200 steep-sided pyramids)
- Traded with Rome, India; female rulers (*kandake*/queens) wielded real power
- Declined under pressure from Axum (Ethiopia)

**Significance:** Demonstrates sophisticated African civilization contemporary with/independent of Egypt. Challenges narrative of Egypt as sole ancient African civilization.

## Phoenicia (~1500–300 BC)

- Coastal city-states in modern Lebanon: Tyre, Sidon, Byblos, Berytus (Beirut)
- Master shipbuilders and navigators; dominated Mediterranean trade
- Founded colonies: Carthage (~814 BC, Tunisia), Cádiz (Spain), settlements across Mediterranean
- **Alphabet** (~1050 BC): Simplified writing system (~22 consonant letters); adapted by Greeks (who added vowels) → Latin alphabet → basis of most modern European scripts
- Purple dye (Tyrian purple) from murex snails; extremely expensive; associated with royalty
- Circumnavigated Africa (~600 BC, commissioned by Egyptian pharaoh Neco II, per Herodotus)
- Never formed unified state; city-states competed/cooperated; eventually absorbed by Assyria, Babylon, Persia, then Alexander

## Indus Valley / Harappan Civilization (~3300–1300 BC)

- Cities: Mohenjo-daro, Harappa (pop ~40,000 each)
- **Remarkable features:** Grid-planned cities, standardized brick sizes, advanced drainage/sewage, public baths, standardized weights
- No deciphered writing (Indus script remains undecoded)
- No evidence of monumental temples, palaces, or kings — possibly more egalitarian?
- **Decline:** Climate change (monsoon shift), river course changes (Ghaggar-Hakra drying), possibly earthquakes
- NOT destroyed by "Aryan invasion" (outdated theory); gradual de-urbanization over centuries

## Early China

### Shang Dynasty (~1600–1046 BC)
- First historically verified Chinese dynasty (oracle bones confirm)
- Bronze casting (most sophisticated in ancient world — ritual vessels)
- Oracle bone divination → earliest Chinese writing
- Walled cities, chariots, stratified society
- Human sacrifice in royal burials (hundreds of victims)

### Zhou Dynasty (~1046–256 BC)
- **Western Zhou** (1046–771 BC): Feudal system, "Mandate of Heaven" concept
- **Eastern Zhou** (771–256 BC): Decentralization, constant warfare
  - **Spring and Autumn Period** (771–476 BC): ~170 states → ~40; Confucius (551–479 BC)
  - **Warring States** (476–221 BC): ~40 states → 7 major; Legalism, Daoism, Mohism; iron weapons, crossbow, cavalry, massive armies (~1 million)

**Mandate of Heaven:** Heaven grants authority to virtuous ruler; natural disasters/rebellion = mandate lost. Legitimized dynastic change. Chinese equivalent of divine right but with built-in accountability.

## Ancient Persia Before the Achaemenids

### Elamite Civilization (~3200–539 BC)
- Southwest Iran (Khuzestan); contemporary with Sumer
- Capital Susa; own writing system (Proto-Elamite, then Elamite cuneiform)
- Sacked Babylon multiple times; destroyed Kassite dynasty (~1158 BC)
- Cultural bridge between Mesopotamia and Iranian plateau
- Absorbed into Achaemenid Empire (covered in Ch. 03)

### Medes (~678–549 BC)
- First Iranian empire; capital Ecbatana (modern Hamadan)
- Allied with Babylon to destroy Assyria (fall of Nineveh, 612 BC)
- Conquered by Cyrus the Great → became Achaemenid core territory

## Mesoamerica

### Olmec (~1500–400 BC)
- "Mother culture" of Mesoamerica (Gulf Coast, Mexico)
- Colossal stone heads (17 known; basalt, up to 3.4m tall, ~50 tonnes; transported 80+ km without wheels)
- Ball game (*ullamaliztli*), calendar systems (Long Count originated here?), possible writing
- Long-distance trade in obsidian, jade, cacao; trade network extended to Guatemala, central Mexico
- Influenced all subsequent Mesoamerican civilizations: calendar, cosmology, ritual practices
- San Lorenzo (~1500–900 BC) and La Venta (~900–400 BC) = major centers

### Early Maya (~2000 BC – 250 AD, Preclassic)
- City-states in Yucatan, Guatemala, Belize, Honduras
- Advanced astronomy: predicted solar eclipses, tracked Venus cycle to 1 day error per 6,000 years
- Mathematics: vigesimal (base-20) system; concept of zero independently (~300 BC, possibly earliest)
- Hieroglyphic writing: fully deciphered only in late 20th century (breakthroughs by Yuri Knorosov, Linda Schele, David Stuart)
- Slash-and-burn + raised fields + terracing; more sophisticated agriculture than traditionally assumed
- El Mirador — massive Preclassic city (La Danta pyramid: 72m tall, one of largest structures by volume ever built)
- Nakbe, Cival, San Bartolo (murals dating to ~100 BC showing sophisticated art)

### Norte Chico / Caral (Peru, ~3000–1800 BC)
- Oldest known civilization in the Americas; predates Olmec by ~1,500 years
- Caral: monumental architecture, sunken circular plazas, quipu-like recording devices
- **No pottery, no writing, no evidence of warfare** — uniquely peaceful early civilization?
- Based on maritime-agricultural economy (anchovy fishing + cotton + squash)
- 30+ sites in Supe Valley; population possibly 20,000+

## The Bronze Age Collapse (~1177 BC)

**One of history's greatest civilizational catastrophes.** Within ~50 years, virtually every major civilization in the Eastern Mediterranean collapsed or severely contracted.

### Before the Collapse (c. 1250 BC)
Interconnected system of great powers:
- **Egypt** (New Kingdom, Ramesses II–III)
- **Hittite Empire** (Anatolia)
- **Mycenaean Greece**
- **Kassite Babylon**
- **Assyria** (Middle period)
- **Cyprus** (copper supplier)
- **Ugarit** and Levantine city-states
- International trade, diplomacy (Amarna Letters), gift exchange between "Great Kings"

### The Collapse (c. 1200–1150 BC)

| Civilization | Fate |
|---|---|
| Hittite Empire | Completely destroyed; capital Hattusa burned |
| Mycenaean Greece | Palace centers destroyed; writing lost; 300-year "Dark Age" |
| Ugarit | Destroyed, never reoccupied |
| Cyprus | Major destruction |
| Egypt | Survived but severely weakened; never regained power |
| Kassite Babylon | Collapsed (Elamite invasion) |
| Assyria | Contracted but survived |

### Causes (Eric Cline's Synthesis)
Not one cause but a "perfect storm" of interconnected failures:

1. **"Sea Peoples"** — Migrating/raiding groups (possibly displaced by drought in their homelands); Ramesses III fought them off but at enormous cost
2. **Earthquakes** — Archaeological evidence of destruction at multiple sites consistent with seismic activity (Eastern Mediterranean sits on active fault zones)
3. **Drought/climate change** — Pollen cores and speleothems show prolonged drought ~1200 BC; Ugarit's last letter mentions famine
4. **Systems collapse** — Tightly interconnected trade network meant disruption cascaded; loss of tin supply → no bronze → military vulnerability
5. **Internal rebellion** — Evidence of deliberate destruction of palaces (ruling class targeted)

**Key lesson:** Highly interconnected systems are efficient but fragile. When multiple stresses hit simultaneously, cascading failures can destroy an entire civilizational network. Directly relevant to modern globalization.

### Aftermath
- **Iron Age begins** — iron working spread as bronze supply chains collapsed (iron ore is ubiquitous; tin is not)
- **Alphabetic writing** — Phoenicians developed simplified alphabet (vs complex cuneiform/hieroglyphics); democratized literacy
- **Smaller polities** — Israel, Phoenician city-states, Philistines, Aramean kingdoms
- **Power vacuum** → eventually filled by Neo-Assyrian Empire, then Persia

## Patterns in Ancient Civilizations

### Why Civilizations Emerged Where They Did
1. **River valleys** — Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Indus, Yellow River (irrigation, transport, fertile soil)
2. **Climate stability** — Holocene (post-10,000 BC) warming enabled agriculture
3. **Domesticable species** — Fertile Crescent had the most candidates
4. **Geographic proximity** — Civilizations that could interact developed faster

### Common Features of Early States
- Monumental architecture (legitimation + labor organization)
- Writing systems (accounting → law → literature)
- Social stratification (elites, priests, merchants, laborers, slaves)
- Organized religion (legitimation of hierarchy)
- Specialized military
- Long-distance trade networks
- Patriarchal gender hierarchies (near-universal in complex states)

### Early Collapse Patterns
- **Overextension** — Akkadian, Assyrian empires stretched beyond administrative capacity
- **Environmental degradation** — Salinization in Mesopotamia (wheat yields halved over centuries), deforestation in Greece/Lebanon
- **Climate shifts** — 4.2 kiloyear event (drought ~2200 BC) contributed to Old Kingdom Egypt collapse, Akkadian collapse, Indus decline
- **Elite extraction** — Over-taxation/forced labor → rebellion (pattern repeats in every ancient civilization)
- **External pressure** — Migration/invasion during periods of internal weakness
- **Systems interdependence** — Bronze Age Collapse shows interconnected trade systems amplify shocks (see Ch. 08 for modern parallels)
