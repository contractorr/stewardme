# Modern Era (~1800–Present)

## The Age of Revolutions (1775–1848)

### American Revolution (1775–1783)
- 13 colonies vs Britain; grievance: taxation without representation
- **Declaration of Independence (1776):** Enlightenment principles (natural rights, consent of the governed)
- **Constitution (1787):** Federal republic, separation of powers, Bill of Rights (1791)
- Significance: first successful colonial independence war; inspired others; but preserved slavery, excluded women/natives

### French Revolution (1789–1799)
- Causes: fiscal crisis (debt from American war), food shortages, Enlightenment ideas, noble privilege, weak king (Louis XVI)
- **Key events:** Bastille (1789), Declaration of Rights of Man, abolition of feudalism, execution of king (1793), Reign of Terror (Robespierre, ~17,000 executed), Thermidorian Reaction, Directory
- **Napoleon** (1799–1815): Coup → First Consul → Emperor; Napoleonic Code (civil law basis for much of world); conquered most of Europe; defeated at Waterloo (1815)
- Spread: nationalism, popular sovereignty, secularism, metric system, civil law

### Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)
- Only successful slave revolution in history
- Colony of Saint-Domingue (richest in Caribbean; sugar); ~500,000 enslaved, ~30,000 free people of color, ~40,000 whites
- Led by Toussaint Louverture, completed by Jean-Jacques Dessalines
- Defeated French, Spanish, and British armies; declared independence 1804
- Punished by international isolation; France demanded 150M francs "indemnity" (Haiti paid until 1947)

### Latin American Independence (~1810–1830)
- Inspired by American/French Revolutions + Napoleonic invasion of Spain (1808) created power vacuum
- **Simón Bolívar** (1783–1830): "Liberator"; freed Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia; dreamed of unified Latin America (Gran Colombia, 1819–1831, fragmented)
- **José de San Martín** (1778–1850): Liberated Argentina, Chile, Peru; crossed Andes with army (1817)
- **Mexico:** Hidalgo (1810, peasant revolt), Morelos, Iturbide (independence 1821)
- **Brazil:** Unique — Portuguese prince Pedro declared independence (1822); peaceful transition; empire until 1889
- **Haiti:** Already independent (1804); inspired other movements but isolated by racist international order
- **Result:** ~20 new republics by 1830; but independence didn't bring democracy — caudillo (strongman) politics, racial hierarchies persisted, economic dependence on exports (coffee, sugar, minerals) continued colonial patterns

### 1848 Revolutions
- Wave of revolutions across Europe (France, German states, Italian states, Habsburg Empire, Hungary)
- Driven by: nationalism, liberalism, working-class grievances, food crisis (potato blight, grain prices)
- **France:** Louis-Philippe abdicated; Second Republic; universal male suffrage; Louis-Napoleon elected → coup → Napoleon III
- **Habsburg Empire:** Hungarian revolt (Kossuth), Czech revolt, Italian revolt; all suppressed with Russian help
- **German states:** Frankfurt Parliament attempted unification; offered crown to Prussian king; rejected; dissolved
- Almost all suppressed within 1–2 years; but demonstrated popular demand for constitutional government
- Long-term: unification movements in Italy (1861) and Germany (1871); peasant emancipation accelerated

## Industrial Revolution (~1760–1900)

**Covered in economic-history-guide for economic aspects. Focus here: political and civilizational consequences.**

### Phases

| Phase | Period | Key Technologies | Center |
|---|---|---|---|
| First | ~1760–1840 | Steam engine, cotton textile, iron, canals | Britain |
| Second | ~1840–1914 | Railways, steel, chemicals, electricity, telegraph | Britain, Germany, USA |

### Political Consequences
- **Urbanization:** Britain 20% urban (1800) → 80% urban (1900); created industrial working class
- **Class conflict:** Labor movements, trade unions, Marxism (Communist Manifesto 1848, Capital 1867)
- **State expansion:** Factory Acts, public health legislation, compulsory education, policing
- **Nationalism intensified:** Industrialization required literacy, standardized language, national markets
- **Military transformation:** Mass-produced weapons, railways for logistics, telegraph for command → industrial warfare

## Nationalism and Unification

### Italian Unification (1815–1871)
- Mazzini (ideology), Cavour (diplomacy), Garibaldi (military)
- Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont as nucleus; wars against Austria; Garibaldi's southern campaign
- Unified 1861; Rome added 1870

### German Unification (1864–1871)
- Prussia under Bismarck: "blood and iron" — three wars (Denmark 1864, Austria 1866, France 1870–71)
- German Empire proclaimed at Versailles (1871); Kaiser Wilhelm I
- Shifted European balance of power: Germany = largest economy in Europe by 1900

### Nationalism as Force
- **Constructive:** Unified peoples, created shared identity, enabled modern states
- **Destructive:** Exclusionary (who belongs?), drove ethnic cleansing, justified imperialism, led to world wars
- Benedict Anderson's "imagined communities" — nations are socially constructed through print capitalism, education, shared myths

## New Imperialism (~1870–1914)

### The Scramble for Africa (1881–1914)
- Berlin Conference (1884–1885): European powers carved up Africa with no African representation
- By 1914: only Ethiopia and Liberia independent; rest colonized

| Colonial Power | Key Territories |
|---|---|
| Britain | Egypt, Sudan, Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, South Africa, Rhodesia |
| France | West Africa, Equatorial Africa, Madagascar, Algeria |
| Belgium | Congo (Leopold II: ~10 million killed in rubber extraction) |
| Germany | Tanganyika, Namibia (Herero genocide 1904), Cameroon |
| Portugal | Angola, Mozambique |
| Italy | Libya, Eritrea, Somalia |

### Justifications (all self-serving)
- "Civilizing mission" / "White Man's Burden" (Kipling)
- Economic: raw materials, markets, investment opportunities
- Strategic: naval bases, prevent rival acquisition
- Prestige: national glory

### Asian Colonialism
- **India:** British Raj (1858–1947); de-industrialized Indian textiles (India went from ~25% world manufacturing in 1750 to ~2% by 1900); extracted wealth; famines under British rule killed ~30–60 million (Late Victorian Holocausts, Mike Davis); railways built primarily for extraction
- **Southeast Asia:** French Indochina (Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia), Dutch East Indies (Indonesia), British Burma/Malaya, Spanish/then American Philippines (1898)
- **China:** Not formally colonized but "carved up" into spheres of influence; Opium Wars (1839–42: Britain forced China to accept opium trade; 1856–60: Anglo-French burned Summer Palace); unequal treaties; Hong Kong ceded (1842); Treaty Ports; Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864, ~20–30 million dead — deadliest civil war in history; millenarian Christian movement)
- **Japan:** Avoided colonization through rapid modernization (Meiji Restoration 1868); "rich country, strong army" (*fukoku kyohei*); defeated China (1894–95, First Sino-Japanese War) and Russia (1905, Russo-Japanese War — first Asian victory over European power in modern era; shattered myth of white supremacy)
- **Meiji Restoration specifics:** Abolished feudalism, conscript army, universal education, railroad/telegraph, constitution (1889), parliamentary Diet; industrialized in ~40 years; became imperial power (annexed Korea 1910, Taiwan 1895)

## World War I (1914–1918)

### Causes
- **Alliance system:** Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) vs Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy)
- **Militarism:** Arms race, especially naval (Britain vs Germany)
- **Imperialism:** Colonial competition generated tensions
- **Nationalism:** Especially in Balkans ("powder keg of Europe"); pan-Slavism vs Austro-Hungarian multiethnic empire
- **Trigger:** Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (Sarajevo, June 28, 1914)

### The War
- Expected to last weeks; lasted 4+ years
- Western Front: trench warfare from Belgium to Switzerland; industrial slaughter (machine guns, artillery, poison gas)
- **Key battles:** Marne (1914, stopped German advance), Verdun (1916, ~700K casualties), Somme (1916, ~1.1M casualties), Passchendaele (1917)
- Eastern Front: more mobile but equally devastating; Russia collapsed (Revolution 1917)
- Ottoman front: Gallipoli (1915–16), Armenian Genocide (~1.5M killed, 1915–23)
- US entered 1917; Germany exhausted → Armistice November 11, 1918

### Casualties and Consequences

| Category | Number |
|---|---|
| Military dead | ~10 million |
| Civilian dead | ~7 million |
| Wounded | ~21 million |
| Empires destroyed | 4 (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German) |

- **Treaty of Versailles (1919):** Germany blamed (Article 231), massive reparations, territorial losses, military restrictions; created resentment → WWII
- League of Nations (failed: no US, no enforcement)
- Mandate system: former Ottoman/German territories administered by victors (set up future Middle East conflicts)
- Russian Revolution (1917) → Soviet Union; ideological alternative to capitalism
- Spanish Flu (1918–1919): ~50 million dead; more than the war itself

## World War II (1939–1945)

### Causes
- Versailles resentment + Great Depression + rise of fascism
- Hitler's Nazi Germany: revanchism, racial ideology, *Lebensraum*, rearmament
- Appeasement: Munich Agreement (1938) gave Czechoslovak Sudetenland to Germany
- Nazi-Soviet Pact (August 1939): divided Poland

### European Theater
- German *Blitzkrieg*: Poland (1939), France (1940, fell in 6 weeks), Balkans
- Battle of Britain (1940): RAF defended; Germany couldn't invade
- **Operation Barbarossa** (June 1941): Germany invaded USSR; reached Moscow suburbs; Stalingrad (1942–43) = turning point (~2M casualties)
- D-Day (June 6, 1944): Allied invasion of Normandy; liberation of Western Europe
- Soviet push westward: took Berlin (May 1945)
- **Holocaust:** Systematic murder of ~6 million Jews + ~5 million others (Roma, disabled, homosexuals, POWs, political prisoners)

### Pacific Theater
- Japan conquered Southeast Asia, Pacific islands (1941–42)
- Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941): brought US into war
- Island-hopping campaign; brutal fighting (Iwo Jima, Okinawa)
- **Atomic bombs:** Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945, ~80,000 immediate dead), Nagasaki (Aug 9, ~40,000); Japan surrendered Aug 15

### Casualties and Consequences

| Category | Number |
|---|---|
| Total dead | ~70–85 million (~3% of world population) |
| Soviet Union | ~27 million (most of any country) |
| China | ~15–20 million |
| Germany | ~6–7 million |
| Japan | ~2.5–3 million |
| Holocaust | ~6 million Jews + ~5 million others |

- **United Nations** founded (1945); Security Council with veto powers (P5)
- **Bretton Woods** system: IMF, World Bank, dollar as reserve currency
- **Nuremberg Trials:** Established principle of individual criminal responsibility for war crimes
- **Nuclear age:** Arms race, MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction)
- European colonial empires fatally weakened → decolonization

## Decolonization (1945–1975)

| Region | Key Events | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| South Asia | India/Pakistan independence (1947); partition (~1–2M killed, ~15M displaced) | Negotiated + mass violence |
| Southeast Asia | Indonesia (1945), Vietnam (1954/1975), Myanmar (1948) | Mix of negotiation and war |
| Middle East | Israel (1948), Egyptian revolution (1952), Algerian War (1954–62, ~1M killed) | Often violent |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | Ghana (1957, first), wave of independence 1960; Congo crisis; Portuguese colonies (1975) | Mostly negotiated; some violent |
| Caribbean | Jamaica (1962), Trinidad (1962) | Mostly peaceful |

**Neocolonialism critique:** Formal independence but continued economic dependence; former colonial powers maintained influence through trade relationships, military bases, and financial leverage. CFA franc zone (West/Central Africa) still tied to French monetary policy as of 2020s.

## Chinese Revolution and Mao's China

### Fall of the Qing (1912)
- Humiliation: Opium Wars, unequal treaties, Taiping Rebellion, Boxer Rebellion (1899–1901, siege of foreign legations in Beijing; multinational intervention; $330M indemnity imposed)
- **Sun Yat-sen** (1866–1925): "Father of the Nation"; Three Principles (nationalism, democracy, people's livelihood); Xinhai Revolution overthrew Qing (1911); Republic proclaimed 1912
- Warlord era (1916–1928): China fragmented; competing military governors

### Chinese Civil War + Japanese Invasion
- Nationalists (Kuomintang/KMT, Chiang Kai-shek) vs Communists (CCP, Mao Zedong)
- **Long March** (1934–1935): CCP retreated ~9,000 km; 80,000 started, ~8,000 survived; established Mao's leadership
- Japanese invasion (1937–1945): Nanjing Massacre (1937, ~200,000–300,000 killed); United Front against Japan; 15–20 million Chinese killed in Sino-Japanese War
- Civil War resumed 1946–1949; CCP won with peasant support; People's Republic proclaimed October 1, 1949; Nationalists fled to Taiwan

### Mao Era (1949–1976)
- Land reform: landlords executed (~1–2 million); land redistributed to 300 million peasants
- **Great Leap Forward** (1958–1962): Forced collectivization + crash industrialization; backyard steel furnaces; worst famine in human history (~30–45 million dead; grain exported while millions starved)
- **Cultural Revolution** (1966–1976): Purge of "capitalist roaders"; Red Guards attacked teachers, intellectuals, officials; universities closed 4+ years; ~500,000–2 million killed; generation's education destroyed
- Nixon visit (1972): US-China rapprochement; anti-Soviet alignment

### Post-Mao China
- **Deng Xiaoping** (effective leader 1978–1992): "Reform and Opening Up"; Special Economic Zones (Shenzhen grew from 30,000 to 10+ million); market reforms within party control; "black cat, white cat" pragmatism
- Tiananmen Square (June 4, 1989): Pro-democracy protests crushed; hundreds–thousands killed; CCP chose economic reform without political liberalization
- GDP growth ~10%/year for 30 years; 800 million lifted from poverty; world's second-largest economy by 2010; joined WTO 2001
- **Xi Jinping** (2012–present): Centralization; anti-corruption campaign; Belt and Road Initiative ($1 trillion+ infrastructure); assertive foreign policy; technology competition with US; Xinjiang/Hong Kong crackdowns; "wolf warrior" diplomacy

## Indian Independence and Post-Colonial India

- **Indian National Congress** (founded 1885): Initially moderate petitioners; radicalized under Bal Gangadhar Tilak, then Gandhi
- **Mohandas Gandhi** (1869–1948): *Satyagraha* (non-violent resistance); Salt March (1930, 388 km); Quit India Movement (1942); assassinated by Hindu nationalist (1948)
- **Muhammad Ali Jinnah** (1876–1948): Muslim League leader; two-nation theory; demanded Pakistan
- **Partition (August 15, 1947):** ~1–2 million killed in communal violence; ~15 million displaced — largest mass migration in history; birth of India and Pakistan
- **Jawaharlal Nehru** (PM 1947–1964): Non-alignment, planned economy (five-year plans), secular democratic state, IITs founded
- Kashmir dispute: unresolved since 1947; three wars (1947, 1965, 1971); nuclear-armed standoff
- **Bangladesh** (1971): East Pakistan seceded; West Pakistani military crackdown (Operation Searchlight, ~300,000–3 million killed); Indian intervention; independence

## 20th Century Latin America

Often omitted from world history; shouldn't be — 600+ million people.

### Mexican Revolution (1910–1920)
- First major social revolution of 20th century; preceded Russian Revolution by 7 years
- Overthrow of Porfirio Díaz (dictator 35 years); figures: Zapata (land reform, "Tierra y Libertad"), Villa (northern rebel), Carranza, Obregón
- ~1–2 million dead; Constitution of 1917 (progressive: land reform, labor rights, secular education)

### Cold War Interventions
| Country | Year | Event |
|---|---|---|
| Guatemala | 1954 | CIA overthrew elected Árbenz (threatened United Fruit Company) |
| Cuba | 1959 | Castro's revolution; 1961 Bay of Pigs; 1962 Missile Crisis |
| Chile | 1973 | CIA-backed coup against elected Allende; Pinochet dictatorship (1973–1990, ~3,000 killed) |
| Argentina | 1976–1983 | Military junta; "Dirty War" (~30,000 "disappeared") |
| Nicaragua | 1979–1990 | Sandinista revolution; US-funded Contras |
| El Salvador | 1979–1992 | Civil war; US-backed government vs leftist FMLN; ~75,000 dead |

### Key Patterns
- **Import substitution industrialization** (ISI, 1930s–1980s): State-led industrial development; initial growth then stagnation
- **Dependency theory** (Prebisch, Frank): Global capitalism structured to keep periphery poor; raw material exports subsidize industrial core
- **Debt crisis** (1980s): "Lost decade"; structural adjustment programs; neoliberal reforms (Washington Consensus)
- **Pink tide** (2000s): Left-wing governments elected across region (Chávez, Lula, Morales, Kirchner)

## The Cold War (1947–1991)

### Structure
- Bipolar: USA (liberal capitalism) vs USSR (communism)
- Nuclear deterrence: MAD prevented direct conflict between superpowers
- Proxy wars: Korea (1950–53), Vietnam (1955–75), Afghanistan (1979–89), Angola, Nicaragua, etc.
- Alliance systems: NATO (1949) vs Warsaw Pact (1955)
- Non-Aligned Movement: India, Yugoslavia, Egypt attempted neutrality

### Key Events

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 1948–49 | Berlin Blockade/Airlift |
| 1950–53 | Korean War (~3M dead) |
| 1956 | Hungarian uprising crushed; Suez Crisis |
| 1961 | Berlin Wall built; Bay of Pigs |
| 1962 | Cuban Missile Crisis (closest to nuclear war) |
| 1965–75 | Vietnam War (~3.5M dead) |
| 1979–89 | Soviet-Afghan War ("USSR's Vietnam") |
| 1989 | Fall of Berlin Wall |
| 1991 | USSR dissolved (Dec 25) |

### The Russian Revolution and Soviet Union (Context)
- **February Revolution (1917):** Tsar Nicholas II abdicated; Provisional Government under Kerensky
- **October Revolution (1917):** Bolsheviks (Lenin) seized power; "Peace, Land, Bread"
- **Civil War (1917–1922):** Reds vs Whites; foreign intervention; ~7–12 million dead (combat, famine, disease)
- **Lenin** (1917–1924): War Communism, then New Economic Policy (NEP); created USSR (1922)
- **Stalin** (1924–1953): Forced collectivization (~5–7 million dead in Ukraine famine/*Holodomor* 1932–33); industrialization (USSR became #2 industrial power in 15 years); Great Purge (1936–38, ~750,000 executed, millions in Gulag); WWII victory but at catastrophic cost
- **Khrushchev** (1953–1964): De-Stalinization; Sputnik (1957); Cuban Missile Crisis
- **Brezhnev** (1964–1982): "Stagnation era"; détente; Prague Spring crushed (1968); Afghanistan invasion (1979)

### Why the USSR Collapsed
- Economic stagnation (command economy couldn't innovate; GDP growth fell from ~6%/year in 1960s to ~2% in 1980s)
- Arms race burden (military ~25% of GDP vs US ~6%)
- Afghanistan quagmire (1979–1989; ~15,000 Soviet dead; "USSR's Vietnam")
- Nationalist movements in republics (Baltics, Ukraine, Caucasus, Central Asia)
- Gorbachev's reforms (*glasnost*/openness, *perestroika*/restructuring) → unintended acceleration of collapse
- Information: citizens could see Western prosperity via radio, TV, samizdat
- Chernobyl (1986): Exposed systemic dysfunction; Gorbachev cited as transformative moment
- **December 25, 1991:** Gorbachev resigned; Soviet flag lowered at Kremlin; 15 independent states

## Post-Cold War (~1991–Present)

### 1990s: "End of History" Optimism
- Fukuyama (1992): Liberal democracy as final form of government
- NATO/EU expansion; WTO (1995); globalization accelerated
- Challenges to optimism: Yugoslav Wars (1991–2001, ethnic cleansing, ~140K dead), Rwanda genocide (1994, ~800K Tutsis killed in 100 days), Somalia

### 2000s: 9/11 and the War on Terror
- September 11, 2001: Al-Qaeda attacks; ~3,000 dead
- Afghanistan War (2001–2021); Iraq War (2003–2011); destabilized Middle East
- Arab Spring (2011): Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, Syria; mostly failed → civil wars, authoritarian restoration

### 2010s–2020s: Return of Great Power Competition
- China's rise: second-largest economy; Belt and Road Initiative; South China Sea; Xi Jinping consolidation
- Russia resurgent: Georgia (2008), Crimea (2014), Ukraine (full invasion 2022)
- Multipolarity: BRICS, declining US hegemony, institutional strain
- Populism/nationalism in Western democracies (Brexit, Trump, European far-right)
- COVID-19 (2020+): ~7M+ official dead (probably 15–20M excess); exposed supply chain fragility; accelerated deglobalization

## The 20th Century in Perspective

**Most transformative century in human history:**
- World population: 1.6B (1900) → 6.1B (2000)
- Two world wars: ~100M dead combined
- Nuclear weapons: existential risk introduced
- Decolonization: European empires dissolved
- Technology: electricity, aviation, nuclear, computing, internet, space travel
- Living standards: global GDP per capita ~5x; life expectancy ~32→67 years
- But also: totalitarianism (fascism, communism: ~100M killed by their own governments in 20th century per various estimates)

## Africa Post-Independence

### Challenges
- Artificial colonial borders (53 states, many multi-ethnic with no pre-colonial unity)
- Extractive institutions inherited from colonialism; weak bureaucracies
- Cold War: superpowers supported dictators for strategic alignment (Mobutu in Congo, Mengistu in Ethiopia)
- Structural adjustment programs (1980s–90s): IMF/World Bank imposed austerity; debated results

### Key Events
| Event | Date | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Congo Crisis | 1960–65 | Lumumba assassinated (CIA/Belgian involvement); Mobutu seized power; 32-year kleptocracy |
| Nigerian Civil War (Biafra) | 1967–70 | ~1–3 million dead; oil politics; ethnic conflict |
| Rwandan Genocide | 1994 | ~800,000 Tutsis killed in 100 days; UN/international failure |
| South African Apartheid ends | 1994 | Mandela elected; peaceful transition; Truth and Reconciliation Commission |
| Ethiopian-Eritrean War | 1998–2000 | ~100,000 dead; border dispute |

### Post-2000 Trends
- Economic growth (Africa averaged ~5% GDP growth 2000–2015; "Africa Rising" narrative)
- Demographic boom: youngest continent; population projected 2.5 billion by 2050
- Mobile technology leapfrogging (M-Pesa in Kenya; mobile banking without traditional banking infrastructure)
- Persistent challenges: governance, corruption, climate vulnerability, debt

## Middle East Since 1945

### Israel-Palestine Conflict
- **Balfour Declaration** (1917): Britain endorsed "national home for Jewish people" in Palestine
- UN Partition Plan (1947); Israel declared independence (1948); Arab-Israeli War; ~700,000 Palestinian refugees (*Nakba*)
- Wars: 1948, 1956 (Suez), 1967 (Six-Day War: Israel seized West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, Golan Heights), 1973 (Yom Kippur)
- Camp David Accords (1978): Egypt-Israel peace (first Arab state to recognize Israel)
- Oslo Accords (1993): Palestinian Authority created; two-state solution framework; stalled
- Ongoing: occupation, settlements, periodic wars in Gaza, no resolution

### Iranian Revolution (1979)
- Shah overthrown; Ayatollah Khomeini established Islamic Republic
- US Embassy hostage crisis (444 days); Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988, ~1 million dead)
- Theocratic governance model; regional influence through proxies (Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis)

### Iraq and the Gulf
- Iran-Iraq War (1980–88): Saddam Hussein vs Khomeini; chemical weapons used; ~1 million dead
- Gulf War (1991): Iraq invaded Kuwait; US-led coalition expelled Iraq; "highway of death"
- Iraq War (2003–2011): WMD claims; Saddam toppled; insurgency; ~200,000+ Iraqi dead; ISIS emerged from chaos
