# Medieval & Post-Classical World (~500–1500 AD)

## Islamic Civilization (622–~1500)

### Rise of Islam
- **Muhammad** (570–632): Merchant in Mecca → prophet; Hijra to Medina (622, Year 1 of Islamic calendar); returned to conquer Mecca (630)
- **Rashidun Caliphate** (632–661): Four "Rightly Guided" caliphs; conquered Sasanian Persia entirely and Byzantine Levant/Egypt within 20 years
- Speed of conquest extraordinary: Arab armies defeated two superpowers (Byzantine, Sasanian) within a generation

### Umayyad Caliphate (661–750)
- Capital: Damascus; hereditary succession (controversy → Sunni/Shia split)
- Expanded to Spain (711, Tariq ibn Ziyad) through North Africa; east to Sindh (Pakistan)
- At peak: largest empire yet (~11.1 million km²)
- Arabic became administrative language; coinage standardized; Dome of the Rock built (691)
- Overthrown by Abbasid revolution (750); Umayyad survivor fled to Spain → Emirate of Córdoba

### Abbasid Caliphate (750–1258)
- Capital: Baghdad (founded 762) — world's largest city by ~900 (pop ~1 million)
- **Islamic Golden Age** (~750–1258): translation movement (Greek, Persian, Indian texts → Arabic), original contributions in algebra (al-Khwarizmi), optics (Ibn al-Haytham), medicine (Ibn Sina/*Avicenna*), history (Ibn Khaldun), philosophy (Averroes)
- House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) — translation/research center
- Fragmented politically after ~900: Fatimids in Egypt, Buyids in Iran, local dynasties
- **Mongol sack of Baghdad (1258):** Destroyed libraries, killed caliph; symbolic end of Golden Age

### Al-Andalus (711–1492)
- Muslim Iberia; *Convivencia* (coexistence of Muslims, Christians, Jews — idealized but real cultural exchange)
- Córdoba under Umayyads: pop ~500,000; largest European city; libraries with 400,000+ books
- Reconquista gradually pushed south: Toledo (1085), Córdoba (1236), Seville (1248), Granada (1492)
- Transmission of classical knowledge to medieval Europe via Arabic translations (Aristotle, Ptolemy, Galen)

### Ottoman Empire (1299–1922)
- Founded by Osman I in Anatolia; conquered Constantinople (1453, Mehmed II → Istanbul)
- Peak under Suleiman the Magnificent (1520–1566): Balkans, Hungary, North Africa, Middle East
- **Devshirme system:** Christian boys conscripted, converted, trained as Janissaries (elite soldiers) or administrators → meritocratic but coercive
- Millet system: religious communities (Christian, Jewish) had internal autonomy
- Declined from ~1700: military defeats, failure to industrialize, nationalist separatism
- "Sick Man of Europe" by 19th century; dissolved post-WWI

## Byzantine Empire (330–1453)

- Eastern Roman Empire; continuous from Constantine's founding of Constantinople (330)
- Survived when West fell because: wealthier, more urbanized, better-defended capital, stronger bureaucracy, navy (Greek fire)
- **Justinian I** (527–565): Reconquered Italy, North Africa; *Corpus Juris Civilis* (codified Roman law → basis of civil law systems globally); Hagia Sophia built
- **Iconoclasm controversy** (726–843): Imperial ban on religious images → conflict with papacy → widened East-West Christian divide
- **1054 Great Schism:** Formal split between Catholic (Rome) and Orthodox (Constantinople) Christianity
- **Fourth Crusade (1204):** Crusaders sacked Constantinople (Christian city!) → Latin Empire → fatally weakened Byzantium
- Fell to Ottoman Turks (1453); scholars fled to Italy → contributed to Renaissance

## Tang and Song China

### Tang Dynasty (618–907)
- Often considered China's golden age; capital Chang'an = world's largest city (~1 million)
- Cosmopolitan: Persians, Arabs, Turks, Japanese in capital; Silk Road peak
- Buddhism peaked then faced persecution (845); Confucianism reasserted
- Civil service exam system expanded → meritocratic bureaucracy (in theory)
- Innovations: gunpowder (initially fireworks/signals), woodblock printing, porcelain
- Collapsed: An Lushan Rebellion (755–763, ~36 million dead — possibly deadliest pre-modern event), then warlordism

### Song Dynasty (960–1279)
- **Northern Song** (960–1127): Capital Kaifeng
- **Southern Song** (1127–1279): Lost north to Jurchen Jin; capital Hangzhou

**Song China was arguably the world's most advanced civilization:**

| Innovation | Significance |
|---|---|
| Movable type printing (Bi Sheng, ~1040) | 400 years before Gutenberg |
| Compass (navigation) | Enabled maritime exploration |
| Gunpowder weapons (fire arrows, bombs, rockets) | First military applications |
| Paper money (*jiaozi*) | First government-issued banknotes |
| Mechanical clock (Su Song, 1088) | Astronomical clock tower |
| Coke-fueled blast furnaces | Iron production ~125,000 tonnes/year (not matched in Europe until 1700s) |
| Canal lock gates | Advanced water engineering |
| Rice paddy cultivation | Champa rice → double cropping → population boom |

Population reached ~100 million (largest in world). GDP per capita may have been highest globally.

### The Needham Question: Why Didn't China Industrialize First?

Proposed answers:
- **High-level equilibrium trap** (Mark Elvin): Cheap labor made capital-intensive innovation unnecessary
- **Lack of competitive states** (Mokyr): Unified empire could suppress innovation that threatened stability; Europe's fragmentation forced competitive adoption
- **Cultural factors** (controversial): Confucian emphasis on classics over practical knowledge; exam system rewarded literary skill not science
- **Geographic** (Pomeranz): China lacked easy-access coal near population centers + had no colonies for resource extraction
- **Institutional** (Acemoglu/Robinson): Extractive institutions didn't reward innovation; no patent system, weak property rights for merchants
- Likely multicausal; no single satisfying answer

## Mongol Empire (1206–1368)

### Genghis Khan (1162–1227)
- United Mongol tribes; proclaimed Genghis Khan ("Universal Ruler") 1206
- Conquered: Northern China (Jin dynasty), Central Asia (Khwarezmia), Persia, parts of Russia
- **Military innovations:** Decimal organization, meritocratic promotion, composite bow (lethal at 500m), feigned retreats, psychological warfare (slaughter cities that resisted; spare those that surrendered)
- **Yasa** (legal code): Religious tolerance, diplomatic immunity, census, postal system (*yam*)
- Estimated 40 million killed during conquests (~10% of world population)

### Successor States

| Khanate | Territory | Duration | Notable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yuan Dynasty (China) | China, Mongolia, Tibet | 1271–1368 | Kublai Khan; Marco Polo visited; Grand Canal extended |
| Chagatai Khanate | Central Asia | 1227–1347 | Controlled Silk Road heartland |
| Golden Horde | Russia, Ukraine, Kazakhstan | 1227–1502 | "Tatar Yoke" over Russia ~240 years |
| Ilkhanate | Persia, Iraq, Caucasus | 1256–1335 | Destroyed Abbasid caliphate; converted to Islam |

### Pax Mongolica (~1250–1350)
- Largest contiguous empire ever: ~24 million km²
- Protected trade routes → Silk Road golden age
- Transmitted: gunpowder, printing, compass from China westward
- Also transmitted: **bubonic plague** from Central Asia to Europe (via Genoese traders at Caffa, 1347)
- Mongol postal system (*yam*): relay stations every 25-30 miles; messages crossed empire in days

## Feudal Europe (~500–1500)

### Early Medieval (500–1000)
- Fragmented post-Roman West; Germanic kingdoms (Franks, Visigoths, Ostrogoths, Anglo-Saxons)
- **Charlemagne** (768–814): Frankish king; crowned "Emperor of the Romans" by Pope (800); brief reunification of Western Europe; promoted literacy, standardized weights/measures
- Carolingian Empire fragmented after his death → France, Germany, Italy
- **Viking Age** (793–1066): Norse raided, traded, and settled from Newfoundland to Baghdad; founded Normandy, Kievan Rus, Iceland; advanced shipbuilding

### Feudal System
- **Structure:** King → lords → vassals → knights → peasants/serfs
- Land (fief) exchanged for military service and loyalty
- **Manorialism:** Economic system; self-sufficient estates; serfs bound to land
- **Three estates:** Those who pray (clergy), those who fight (nobility), those who work (peasants)
- Church as unifying institution; Pope vs Emperor power struggle (Investiture Controversy)

### The Crusades (1095–1291)
- Pope Urban II called First Crusade (1095): retake Holy Land from Seljuk Turks
- **First Crusade (1095–1099):** Captured Jerusalem; established Crusader states
- **Third Crusade (1189–1192):** Richard I vs Saladin; Jerusalem remained Muslim but Christian pilgrimage guaranteed
- **Fourth Crusade (1202–1204):** Diverted to sack Constantinople; devastating for Byzantine Empire
- **Children's Crusade (1212):** Thousands of young people set out; most died or were enslaved; illustrates popular religious fervor
- **Impact:** Increased East-West trade, transmitted knowledge (Arabic science, medicine), intensified anti-Muslim/anti-Jewish sentiment; military failure overall
- Crusader states (Jerusalem, Antioch, Tripoli, Edessa) lasted 1–2 centuries; all eventually recaptured
- **Saladin** (1137–1193): Kurdish commander; united Egypt/Syria; retook Jerusalem (1187, Battle of Hattin); respected by both sides for chivalry

### The Black Death (1347–1353)

**Deadliest pandemic in human history. Killed ~75–200 million people in Eurasia.**

- Caused by *Yersinia pestis* (bacterium); spread by fleas on rats + pneumonic (airborne) transmission
- Arrived in Europe via Genoese ships from Crimea (1347); spread along trade routes
- **Mortality:** ~30–60% of European population (25–50 million); some regions lost 80%+
- Recovery to pre-plague population took ~150 years

**Consequences:**
- **Labor shortage** → serfs gained bargaining power → end of serfdom in Western Europe
- Peasant revolts (English Peasant Revolt 1381, Jacquerie in France 1358)
- Wages rose; land values fell; social mobility increased
- Religious crisis: Church couldn't explain/stop plague → flagellants, scapegoating of Jews, proto-Reformation questioning
- Long-term: contributed to conditions enabling Renaissance and eventual industrialization

## Sub-Saharan Africa

Often excluded from world history narratives; shouldn't be.

### Major States and Empires

| Empire/State | Region | Period | Notable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aksum/Axum | Ethiopia | ~100–940 AD | Christian kingdom; controlled Red Sea trade; obelisks |
| Ghana Empire | West Africa | ~300–1200 | Gold-salt trade; "land of gold" |
| Mali Empire | West Africa | ~1235–1600 | Mansa Musa's hajj (1324) — richest person in history?; Timbuktu = center of learning; Mansa Musa crashed gold prices in Cairo |
| Songhai Empire | West Africa | ~1464–1591 | Largest pre-colonial African state; Timbuktu's peak |
| Great Zimbabwe | Southern Africa | ~1100–1450 | Stone architecture; Indian Ocean trade; gold exports |
| Kilwa Sultanate | East Africa | ~957–1513 | Swahili coast; Indian Ocean trade; gold, ivory, slaves |
| Kingdom of Kongo | Central Africa | ~1390–1914 | Centralized state; early Portuguese contact |

### Bantu Expansion (~3000 BC – ~500 AD)
- Bantu-speaking peoples migrated from West/Central Africa across sub-Saharan Africa
- Brought iron working, agriculture, Bantu languages (~500 languages today; 1/3 of Africa)
- One of the largest and longest migrations in human history
- Displaced/absorbed earlier hunter-gatherer populations (Khoisan, Pygmy)

### Ethiopian Highlands
- **Aksumite Empire** (~100–940 AD): One of four great empires of antiquity (per Mani); controlled Red Sea trade; converted to Christianity ~340 AD (King Ezana); minted own coins; obelisks at Aksum up to 33m
- **Zagwe Dynasty** (1137–1270): Rock-hewn churches of Lalibela (11 churches carved from solid rock, ~1200 AD)
- **Solomonic Dynasty** (1270–1974): Claimed descent from Solomon and Queen of Sheba; *Kebra Nagast* (national epic)
- Ethiopia never colonized (defeated Italy at **Battle of Adwa, 1896** — 100,000 Ethiopian troops defeated 17,700 Italians; only African victory over European colonial army in this era)

### Swahili Coast Civilization (~800–1500)
- Chain of city-states from Somalia to Mozambique: Kilwa, Mombasa, Zanzibar, Lamu, Mogadishu
- Swahili culture: Bantu + Arab/Persian fusion; Swahili language (Bantu structure, Arabic loanwords)
- Indian Ocean trade: exported gold, ivory, enslaved people, iron; imported porcelain, textiles, spices
- Kilwa Kisiwani: Ibn Battuta (1331) described it as "one of the most beautiful cities in the world"
- Husuni Kubwa palace: largest pre-colonial structure in sub-Saharan East Africa
- Declined after Portuguese arrival (Vasco da Gama bombarded cities, 1498–1505)

## Southeast Asia (Often Neglected in World History)

### Khmer Empire (~802–1431)
- Capital: **Angkor** (Cambodia); at peak city covered ~1,000 km²; pop ~750,000–1 million (largest pre-industrial city)
- **Angkor Wat** (built ~1113–1150 by Suryavarman II): Largest religious monument ever built; originally Hindu (Vishnu), later Buddhist
- Advanced hydraulic engineering: massive reservoir (*baray*) system; managed monsoon water
- Hindu-Buddhist syncretism; Sanskrit inscriptions; Indian cultural influence via trade
- Declined: environmental degradation (deforestation, canal silting), Thai (Ayutthaya) invasions, climate shifts (mega-droughts alternating with mega-floods per tree-ring data)

### Srivijaya (~650–1377)
- Maritime empire based in Sumatra; controlled Strait of Malacca (key chokepoint for East-West trade)
- Buddhist center: Chinese monk Yijing (671) described 1,000+ monks studying there
- Traded with China, India, Arabs; controlled spice trade routes
- Declined under Chola (Indian) raids (1025) and Majapahit expansion

### Majapahit (~1293–1527)
- Java-based Hindu-Buddhist empire; claimed suzerainty over much of modern Indonesia, Malaysia
- Prime minister Gajah Mada (1331–1364): "Palapa Oath" to unite Nusantara (archipelago)
- Nagarakertagama (1365): epic poem describing empire's extent and tributary network
- Transitioned to Islam in 15th–16th centuries

### Champa (~192–1832)
- Hindu kingdom in central/southern Vietnam; rival of Khmer and Vietnamese (Dai Viet)
- Indian cultural influence: Sanskrit, Shiva worship, temple architecture (My Son sanctuary)
- Gradually absorbed by Vietnamese expansion southward (Nam tien)

### Pagan Kingdom (~849–1297, Burma/Myanmar)
- First unified Burmese state; capital Bagan
- **Bagan temples**: 10,000+ Buddhist temples built 1000–1200 AD; 2,000+ survive
- King Anawrahta (1044–1077): unified Burma; adopted Theravada Buddhism
- Fell to Mongol invasion (1287)

## Medieval Japan and Korea

### Heian Japan (794–1185)
- Capital Kyoto; aristocratic court culture; *The Tale of Genji* (Murasaki Shikibu, ~1000 AD) = world's first novel
- Fujiwara clan dominated through intermarriage with emperors
- Buddhism + Shinto syncretism; elaborate court rituals
- Provincial warrior class (samurai) grew in power

### Kamakura Shogunate (1185–1333)
- First military government; emperor became figurehead
- Survived Mongol invasions (1274, 1281): *kamikaze* ("divine wind") typhoons destroyed Kublai Khan's fleets
- 1274 invasion: 900 ships, 40,000 troops; 1281 invasion: 4,400 ships, 140,000 troops — one of largest naval invasions before D-Day
- Zen Buddhism spread among warrior class

### Goryeo Korea (918–1392)
- Unified Korean peninsula; name → "Korea"
- **Jikji** (1377): Oldest known book printed with movable metal type (78 years before Gutenberg)
- Celadon pottery; advanced culture; tributary relationship with China
- Mongol vassalage (1270–1356): maintained Korean identity but suffered economically
- Transitioned to Joseon Dynasty (1392–1897)

## Pre-Columbian Americas

### Maya Classical Period (250–900 AD)
- City-states: Tikal, Calakmul, Palenque, Copán
- Advanced astronomy, mathematics (zero), hieroglyphic writing (fully deciphered only in late 20th century)
- 365-day and 260-day interlocking calendars; Long Count dating
- **Classic Maya Collapse** (~800–900): Cities abandoned; causes debated (drought, warfare, deforestation, overpopulation — likely combination)

### Aztec Empire (1428–1521)
- Triple Alliance: Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, Tlacopan (dominated by Tenochtitlan)
- Tenochtitlan: built on island in Lake Texcoco; pop ~200,000–300,000 (larger than most European cities)
- Chinampas (floating gardens) — intensive agriculture
- Human sacrifice on large scale (religious/political function; numbers debated: 20,000/year?)
- Conquered by Cortés (1519–1521) with indigenous allies, European diseases, and technological advantage

### Inca Empire (1438–1533)
- Tawantinsuyu ("Four Quarters"): Andes from Ecuador to Chile; ~10 million people
- **No writing system** — used *quipu* (knotted strings) for accounting/record-keeping
- Road system: ~40,000 km of roads, rope bridges, relay runners (*chasqui*)
- Sophisticated terrace agriculture at high altitude; freeze-dried potatoes (*chuño*)
- Labor tax system (*mit'a*) instead of money economy
- Conquered by Pizarro (1532–1533); civil war between Atahualpa and Huáscar weakened empire

## Trade Networks

### Silk Road (~200 BC – 1450 AD)
- Not one road but network of routes from China through Central Asia to Mediterranean
- Traded: silk, spices, precious metals, horses, paper, gunpowder, religions (Buddhism, Islam, Christianity)
- Oasis cities: Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar
- Declined with: Mongol Empire's fragmentation, Black Death, maritime alternatives

### Indian Ocean Trade
- Monsoon-driven: seasonal winds enabled predictable sailing schedules
- Connected East Africa, Arabia, India, Southeast Asia, China
- Traded: spices, textiles, porcelain, gold, slaves
- Swahili coast, Gujarat, Malacca Strait = key nodes
- Mostly peaceful commercial exchange (contrast with Atlantic slave trade)

### Trans-Saharan Trade
- Gold and salt primary commodities; also slaves, ivory, textiles
- Camel caravans across Sahara (introduced ~300 AD)
- Enabled West African empires (Ghana, Mali, Songhai)
- Timbuktu as trade/intellectual hub

## Key Patterns

1. **Islam as connector:** Created cultural/commercial zone from Spain to Indonesia; Arabic as lingua franca of scholarship; preserved and transmitted Greek philosophy
2. **Mongols as catalyst:** Destroyed but also connected; accelerated technology transfer (gunpowder, printing, compass went west; plague also went west)
3. **Black Death as leveler:** Destroyed population but empowered labor; creative destruction; contributed to conditions for Renaissance
4. **China's sophistication:** Song China centuries ahead in technology but didn't industrialize (see Needham Question above; cross-ref with Ch. 05 Great Divergence)
5. **Americas in isolation:** Developed independently; no domesticated large animals, no iron, no wheel (for transport), no immunity to Eurasian diseases — catastrophic consequences upon contact (Ch. 05)
6. **Southeast Asia as crossroads:** Hindu-Buddhist-Islamic cultural layers; maritime trade empires; often omitted from Western histories but connected China, India, and Middle East
7. **Africa's integration:** Sub-Saharan Africa connected to global trade (gold, ivory, enslaved people) via trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean networks; not isolated as colonial narratives claimed
8. **Japan's pattern:** Borrowed heavily from China (writing, Buddhism, governance) but adapted to local conditions; island geography enabled selective adoption/rejection of foreign influences
