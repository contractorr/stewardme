# Classical Empires (~500 BC – 500 AD)

## The Axial Age (~800–200 BC)

Karl Jaspers' concept: independent emergence of foundational philosophical/religious traditions across Eurasia.

| Region | Thinkers/Traditions | Core Ideas |
|---|---|---|
| Greece | Socrates, Plato, Aristotle | Reason, logic, democracy, ethics, metaphysics |
| Israel | Hebrew prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah) | Monotheism, ethical covenant, social justice |
| India | Buddha, Mahavira, Upanishadic sages | Liberation from suffering, karma, dharma, non-violence |
| China | Confucius, Laozi, Mozi, Zhuangzi | Social harmony, virtue ethics, naturalism, universal love |
| Persia | Zoroaster (earlier, ~1000 BC?) | Cosmic dualism, free will, judgment, heaven/hell |

**Why simultaneously?** Debated. Possible factors: urbanization enabled intellectual specialization; iron age trade connected regions; literacy spread beyond priesthoods; political fragmentation created competition for ideas.

**Significance:** These traditions still structure most human moral/political thought 2,500 years later.

## Achaemenid Persian Empire (550–330 BC)

**Largest empire the world had yet seen.** At peak: ~5.5 million km², ~44% of world population (~35 million of ~80 million).

### Key Features
- **Founded by Cyrus the Great** (559–530 BC): Conquered Medes, Lydia, Babylon
- **Cyrus Cylinder** — sometimes called "first human rights charter" (freed Babylonian captives, restored temples)
- **Administrative innovation:** Satraps (provincial governors) with imperial inspectors ("Eyes and Ears of the King")
- **Royal Road:** Susa to Sardis (~2,700 km), courier system (messages in ~7 days vs 90 days walking)
- **Religious tolerance:** Zoroastrianism dominant but didn't force conversion
- **Darius I** (522–486 BC): Standardized coinage (daric), weights, tax system; built Persepolis; canal from Nile to Red Sea

### Decline
- Xerxes' failed invasion of Greece (480–479 BC) — Thermopylae, Salamis, Plataea
- Increasing satrapal autonomy; succession crises
- Conquered by Alexander (334–330 BC); Darius III defeated at Issus and Gaugamela

## Classical Greece (~800–146 BC)

### The Polis System
- ~1,000 independent city-states; fundamental unit of Greek life
- Typical polis: 5,000–10,000 citizens (adult free males); much larger total population including women, slaves, metics (resident foreigners)
- **Athens:** Democracy (direct, not representative); ~30,000 citizens of ~300,000 total; excluded women, slaves (~80,000), metics
- **Sparta:** Military oligarchy; dual kings, council of elders, assembly; helot (serf) labor system; women had more rights than elsewhere in Greece

### Persian Wars (490–479 BC)
- **Marathon (490 BC):** Athenian hoplites defeated larger Persian force; established Athenian confidence
- **Thermopylae (480 BC):** 300 Spartans + allies delayed Xerxes' army; strategic sacrifice
- **Salamis (480 BC):** Athenian navy destroyed Persian fleet; decisive naval battle
- **Plataea (479 BC):** Greek coalition defeated Persian army; ended invasion threat

**Significance:** Greek independence preserved; Athenian naval power established → Delian League → Athenian Empire. Persian Wars created foundational Greek identity: "free" Greeks vs "despotic" Persians (oversimplified but enormously influential).

### Athenian Democracy and Culture
- **Pericles** (dominant 461–429 BC): Expanded democracy; built Parthenon (447–432 BC); Delian League treasury moved to Athens
- Drama: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides (tragedy); Aristophanes (comedy)
- Philosophy: Socrates (469–399 BC, executed for "corrupting youth"), Plato (428–348 BC, *Republic*, Academy), Aristotle (384–322 BC, Lyceum, tutor to Alexander)
- History: Herodotus ("Father of History"), Thucydides (political realism)
- Medicine: Hippocrates (~460–370 BC); empirical approach; Hippocratic oath
- Mathematics: Euclid, Pythagoras, Archimedes (later, Syracuse)
- Athens' population at peak: ~300,000 (citizens + metics + slaves); only ~30,000 adult male citizens could vote

### Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC)
- Athens vs Sparta + allies; Thucydides' account = foundational text of political realism
- **Thucydides Trap:** Rising power (Athens) threatens established power (Sparta) → war
- Athens' plague (430 BC, killed Pericles), Sicily expedition disaster (415–413 BC)
- Sparta won with Persian financial support; Athenian democracy temporarily overthrown
- Result: All Greek city-states weakened → vulnerable to Macedon

### Alexander the Great (336–323 BC)
- Conquered Persia, Egypt, Central Asia, reached India in 11 years
- Empire at death: ~5.2 million km²; died at 32 (Babylon, 323 BC; cause debated)
- **Hellenization:** Greek language, culture, urban planning spread across Middle East/Central Asia
- **Successor kingdoms (Diadochi):** Ptolemaic Egypt, Seleucid Empire, Antigonid Macedon
- Alexandria → intellectual capital of Mediterranean (Library, Museion)

## Roman Republic and Empire (509 BC – 476 AD)

### Republic (509–27 BC)

**Constitutional structure:**
- Senate (300 → 600 → 900 members; aristocratic advisory body)
- Consuls (2, annual, mutual veto)
- Tribunes (protect plebeians; veto power: *sacrosanctitas*)
- Assemblies (varied by type; voted on laws, elections)

**Expansion:**
- Italian peninsula (by 264 BC)
- Punic Wars vs Carthage: First (264–241 BC, Sicily); Second (218–201 BC, Hannibal crossed Alps, Cannae, Scipio at Zama); Third (149–146 BC, Carthage destroyed)
- Greece, Anatolia, Gaul, Spain, North Africa (by 50 BC)

**Republican crisis (133–27 BC):**
- Wealth inequality from conquest → landless citizens, slave labor displaces free farmers
- Gracchi brothers (133, 123 BC) — land reform, assassinated
- Marius' military reforms: professional army loyal to commanders, not state
- Sulla's dictatorship (82–79 BC) — proscriptions
- Caesar: conquered Gaul, crossed Rubicon (49 BC), dictator, assassinated (44 BC)
- Octavian defeats Antony → becomes Augustus (27 BC)

### Empire (27 BC – 476 AD)

**Pax Romana (27 BC – 180 AD):**
- Peak: ~5 million km², 55–70 million people (~25% of world population)
- ~85,000 km of roads; aqueducts; concrete (Roman innovation)
- Common currency, law (ius gentium), language (Latin west, Greek east)
- "Five Good Emperors" (96–180 AD): Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius

**Crisis of the Third Century (235–284 AD):**
- 26 emperors in 50 years (most assassinated)
- Plague (Plague of Cyprian ~250 AD), inflation, currency debasement
- Germanic/Persian frontier pressure
- Temporary breakaway states (Gallic Empire, Palmyrene Empire)

**Late Empire and Fall:**
- Diocletian (284–305): Tetrarchy, administrative reforms, price controls, persecution of Christians
- Constantine (306–337): Reunified empire, founded Constantinople, legalized Christianity (Edict of Milan, 313)
- Theodosius I (379–395): Christianity = state religion; last to rule united empire
- Western Empire progressively lost territory to Germanic peoples
- **476 AD:** Romulus Augustulus deposed by Odoacer — conventional "fall" date

### Why Rome Fell (Multiple Theories)

| Theory | Proponent | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Moral decay | Gibbon (1776) | Christianity sapped martial vigor; luxury corrupted |
| Military overextension | Various | Frontier too long; barbarian pressure increased while army quality declined |
| Economic collapse | Various | Currency debasement, trade disruption, fiscal crisis, overtaxation |
| Plague | McNeill | Antonine Plague (~165 AD), Plague of Cyprian (~250 AD) reduced population/tax base |
| Lead poisoning | Gilfillan | Lead pipes/cookware caused cognitive decline (mostly debunked as primary cause) |
| Systems collapse | Tainter | Diminishing returns on complexity; maintaining empire cost more than it yielded |
| Climate | Harper (2017) | Roman Climate Optimum ended ~200 AD; Late Antique Little Ice Age (~536 AD) |
| Cliodynamic | Turchin | Secular cycle: elite overproduction, popular immiseration, fiscal crisis |

**Reality:** Multicausal. No single factor sufficient. Western Empire fell; Eastern (Byzantine) survived another 1,000 years — same "civilization," different outcome.

## Han Dynasty China (206 BC – 220 AD)

Roughly contemporary with Rome; comparable in size, population, sophistication.

### Western Han (206 BC – 9 AD)
- Founded by Liu Bang after Qin dynasty collapse (peasant origin → emperor)
- **Emperor Wu (141–87 BC):** Expansionist; Silk Road opened (Zhang Qian's missions); Confucianism as state ideology; civil service exams
- Peak: ~6 million km², ~60 million people
- Innovations: paper (Cai Lun, ~105 AD), seismograph, blast furnace, crossbow mass production

### Wang Mang Interregnum (9–23 AD)
- Usurped throne; attempted radical reforms (land redistribution, abolished slavery)
- Reforms failed; Yellow River changed course → famine; rebellion

### Eastern Han (25–220 AD)
- Restored Han dynasty; continued cultural/technological flourishing
- **Decline:** Eunuch/consort clan power struggles, peasant rebellions (Yellow Turban Rebellion, 184 AD), warlordism
- Collapsed into Three Kingdoms (220–280 AD)

### Han-Rome Comparison

| Feature | Roman Empire | Han Dynasty |
|---|---|---|
| Population | ~55–70 million | ~60 million |
| Area | ~5 million km² | ~6 million km² |
| Administration | Provincial governors (legates) | Commanderies with centrally appointed officials |
| Military | Professional legions | Conscript + professional mix |
| Religion | Polytheism → Christianity | Confucianism (state), Daoism, Buddhism (arriving) |
| Writing | Latin alphabet | Chinese characters |
| Collapse | 476 AD (West) | 220 AD → reunified 589 (Sui) |
| Post-collapse | Fragmentation; no reunification | Reunification pattern continued |

**Key difference:** China reunified after every fragmentation; Roman West never did. Why? Geographic unity (North China Plain), shared writing system, Confucian bureaucratic tradition, Mandate of Heaven ideology encouraging reunification.

## Maurya and Gupta India

### Maurya Empire (322–185 BC)
- **Chandragupta Maurya:** Conquered Nanda dynasty; possibly met Alexander's generals
- **Kautilya's *Arthashastra*:** Political treatise on statecraft, espionage, economics — "Indian Machiavelli" 1,800 years before Machiavelli
- **Ashoka (268–232 BC):** After bloody Kalinga conquest, converted to Buddhism; Rock Edicts promoting dharma (non-violence, tolerance, welfare); first large-scale Indian empire (~5 million km²)
- Collapsed after Ashoka; fragmentation

### Gupta Empire (320–550 AD)
- Founded by Chandragupta I (not to be confused with Maurya founder); expanded by Samudragupta (~335–375, "Indian Napoleon")
- **Chandragupta II** (Vikramaditya, ~380–415): Peak; defeated Shakas; patron of arts/sciences
- "Golden Age of India": mathematics (zero, decimal system, Aryabhata calculated Earth's circumference within 1%), astronomy, literature (Kalidasa's *Shakuntala*), medicine (Sushruta — rhinoplasty, cataract surgery, 300+ surgical procedures described)
- **Nalanda University** (founded ~5th century): Buddhist center of learning; students from China, Korea, Tibet, Central Asia; 10,000+ students at peak
- Hindu temple architecture flourished; Ajanta/Ellora caves (painting, sculpture)
- Declined under Huna (Hunnic) invasions from ~455; fragmented by ~550

## Parthian Empire (247 BC – 224 AD)

Often overlooked; filled gap between Seleucids and Sasanians:
- **Arsacid dynasty**; Iranian; controlled Mesopotamia, Persia, parts of Central Asia
- Major Roman rival: defeated Crassus at **Battle of Carrhae (53 BC)** — 20,000 Romans killed, 10,000 captured; Crassus killed; worst Roman defeat since Cannae
- Parthian shot: mounted archers firing backward while retreating; iconic cavalry tactic
- Decentralized "feudal" structure; vassal kings retained autonomy
- Controlled Silk Road trade; middlemen between Rome and China
- Overthrown by Sasanians (224 AD)

## Sasanian Empire (224–651 AD)

Last pre-Islamic Persian empire; Rome's/Byzantium's great rival:
- Founded by Ardashir I; Zoroastrianism as state religion
- **Shapur I** (240–270): Captured Roman Emperor Valerian at Battle of Edessa (260 AD) — only time a Roman emperor captured alive by foreign enemy
- **Khosrow I** (531–579): "Anushirvan" (Immortal Soul); administrative reforms, Academy of Gondishapur (medical/scientific center, translated Greek/Indian texts)
- **Khosrow II** (590–628): Overextended fighting Byzantium; nearly conquered Constantinople; exhausted both empires → easy Arab conquest
- Contributions: influenced Islamic art, administration, court culture; preserved Greek philosophy; advanced irrigation (*qanat* system)
- Fell to Arab Muslim armies 637–651 (Battles of Qadisiyyah 636, Nahavand 642)

---

## Kushan Empire (~30–375 AD)

Central Asian empire connecting Silk Road trade:
- Yuezhi origin (displaced from China by Xiongnu)
- **Kanishka I** (~127–150 AD): Patron of Buddhism; Fourth Buddhist Council; Gandhara art (Greco-Buddhist sculpture)
- Territory: Afghanistan, Pakistan, northern India, parts of Central Asia
- Key Silk Road intermediary: connected Rome, China, India
- Spread Buddhism from India into Central Asia → China
- Multi-cultural: used Greek script, Iranian titles, Indian religions, Chinese goods

## Classical Empire Patterns

### Why Empires Rise
1. **Military innovation** — Persian cavalry, Roman legions, Macedonian phalanx
2. **Administrative capacity** — Tax collection, communication, law
3. **Ideological legitimation** — Divine mandate, civilizing mission
4. **Geographic advantage** — Control of trade routes, defensible borders
5. **Demographic advantage** — Larger population = larger army/tax base

### Why Empires Fall
1. **Overextension** — Frontier costs exceed revenues
2. **Elite fragmentation** — Succession crises, civil wars
3. **Fiscal crisis** — Currency debasement, overtaxation → economic decline
4. **External pressure** — Nomadic invasions coinciding with internal weakness
5. **Epidemics** — Population loss → military/fiscal weakening
6. **Environmental stress** — Climate change, soil exhaustion

### The ~200-Year Pattern
Many classical empires lasted roughly 200–300 years in their strong phase:
- Achaemenid Persia: 220 years
- Macedon/Alexander: ~13 years (but successor states ~250 years each)
- Roman Republic (strong phase): ~200 years
- Roman Empire (Pax Romana): ~200 years
- Han China: ~200 years (each half)
- Maurya India: ~140 years
- Parthia: ~470 years (but weak central control)
- Sasanian Persia: ~425 years
- Kushan: ~200 years (strong phase)

Consistent with Turchin's secular cycle theory; exceptions (Parthia, Sasanians) may reflect decentralized structures that reduced secular cycle pressures.

## Religions of the Classical Period

| Religion | Origin | Spread Mechanism | Political Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zoroastrianism | Iran, ~1000 BC? | Imperial adoption (Achaemenid, Sasanian) | Influenced Judaism, Christianity, Islam (heaven/hell, judgment, dualism) |
| Buddhism | India, ~500 BC | Ashoka's missions; trade routes (Silk Road) | State religion in Maurya India, Kushan, Southeast Asia, China/Japan |
| Confucianism | China, ~500 BC | State adoption (Han dynasty onward) | Shaped Chinese bureaucracy, education, social order for 2,000+ years |
| Hinduism | India, ~1500 BC+ (evolved) | Cultural diffusion; never missionary | Caste system; temple architecture; spread to Southeast Asia |
| Judaism | Levant, ~1000 BC+ | Diaspora after Roman destruction of Temple (70 AD) | Monotheism; influenced Christianity and Islam |
| Christianity | Levant, ~30 AD | Missionary activity; Roman roads; Constantine's adoption (313) | Became dominant religion of Roman/Byzantine Empire; shaped Western civilization |
