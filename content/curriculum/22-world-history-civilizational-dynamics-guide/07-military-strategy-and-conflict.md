# Military Strategy & Conflict

## Foundational Theorists

### Sun Tzu (~5th century BC)
**Work:** *The Art of War*

Core principles:
- "All warfare is based on deception"
- Win without fighting if possible (supreme excellence)
- Know yourself and know your enemy → never defeated
- Exploit enemy weaknesses; avoid strengths
- Speed and surprise over brute force
- Terrain shapes strategy; water metaphor (adapt, flow around obstacles)
- Intelligence (espionage) is foundational

**Modern relevance:** Influenced Mao's guerrilla warfare, business strategy, cybersecurity thinking.

### Thucydides (~460–400 BC)
**Work:** *History of the Peloponnesian War*

Not a military manual but foundational for strategic thought:
- **Thucydides Trap:** Rising power threatens established power → war (Athens vs Sparta; US vs China?)
- **Melian Dialogue:** "The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" — power politics stripped of pretense
- War caused by fear, honor, and interest
- Democracy's strategic weaknesses: emotional decision-making, demagogues, short-termism
- Plague as strategic factor (weakened Athens)

### Carl von Clausewitz (1780–1831)
**Work:** *On War* (posthumous, 1832)

Most influential Western military theorist:
- **"War is the continuation of politics by other means"** — war serves political objectives
- **Fog of war:** Uncertainty, incomplete information, friction
- **Friction:** Everything in war is simple, but simple things are difficult
- **Center of gravity:** The source of power that if destroyed causes collapse (enemy army, capital, alliances, public will)
- **Culminating point of victory:** Advance too far and you overextend → attacker becomes weaker than defender
- **Trinity:** War = interaction of (1) violence/passion (people), (2) chance/probability (military), (3) rational calculation (government)
- **Absolute vs real war:** Theoretical war escalates infinitely; real war is constrained by politics, friction, resources

### Antoine-Henri Jomini (1779–1869)
- Clausewitz's rival; more prescriptive, systematic
- Emphasized: interior lines, concentration of force at decisive point, geometric approach to strategy
- Influenced 19th-century military education (West Point, Sandhurst); US Civil War generals trained on Jomini
- Less philosophical than Clausewitz; more practical; preferred clear principles over ambiguity

### Alfred Thayer Mahan (1840–1914)
**Work:** *The Influence of Sea Power upon History* (1890)

- Sea power determines national greatness; control of sea lanes = commercial/military dominance
- Decisive fleet battles (not commerce raiding) are key to naval supremacy
- Influenced naval arms race: Germany, Japan, US all built blue-water navies after reading Mahan
- Britain's empire validated thesis; Japan's victory at Tsushima (1905) confirmed it
- Still relevant: 90% of world trade by sea; South China Sea disputes are Mahanian

### Giulio Douhet (1869–1930)
**Work:** *The Command of the Air* (1921)

- Air power would make ground warfare obsolete; strategic bombing of cities breaks civilian morale
- "Command of the air" = prerequisite for victory
- Influenced WWII strategic bombing (Hamburg, Dresden, Tokyo firebombing, Hiroshima/Nagasaki)
- **Critique:** Strategic bombing rarely broke morale (London Blitz strengthened resolve; German production increased under bombing until 1944); but air superiority proved essential

### B.H. Liddell Hart (1895–1970)
- **Indirect approach:** Avoid enemy's strength; attack where unexpected; maneuver > attrition
- Advocated mechanized warfare; influenced German *Blitzkrieg* concept (Guderian credited Liddell Hart)
- Criticized WWI frontal assault doctrine; argued for strategic paralysis of enemy command
- "The profoundest truth of war is that the issue of battle is usually decided in the minds of the opposing commanders"

### Mao Zedong (1893–1976) — Military Theorist
- Fused guerrilla warfare with revolutionary politics (details below in Guerrilla section)
- **On Protracted War** (1938): Three phases; time favors the insurgent; space traded for time
- Influenced Ho Chi Minh, Che Guevara, FARC, African liberation movements

## Evolution of Warfare

### Ancient Warfare (~3000 BC – 500 AD)

| Innovation | Period | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Bronze weapons/armor | ~3000 BC | Professionalized warfare; elite warriors |
| Chariot | ~2000 BC | Mobile shock weapon; dominated for ~1000 years |
| Iron weapons | ~1200 BC | Cheaper, more available → larger armies |
| Phalanx (Greece) | ~700 BC | Heavy infantry formation; citizen-soldiers |
| Roman legion | ~300 BC | Flexible manipular system; professional army; engineering |
| Cavalry (steppe nomads) | Various | Composite bow from horseback; hit-and-run; Mongols perfected |
| Siege warfare | Various | Rams, towers, catapults; starving out cities |

**Key battles:**
- **Megiddo (~1457 BC):** Thutmose III defeated Canaanite coalition; earliest battle with detailed tactical record; Egyptian chariot/infantry coordination; surprise approach through narrow Aruna pass
- **Kadesh (~1274 BC):** Ramesses II vs Hittites; largest chariot battle ever (~5,000 chariots total); tactical draw; led to first known peace treaty
- **Marathon (490 BC):** 10,000 Athenian hoplites defeated ~25,000 Persians; Miltiades strengthened wings, weakened center → pincer; proved heavy infantry could beat Persian combined arms on favorable ground; Pheidippides' run → modern marathon (26.2 miles)
- **Gaugamela (331 BC):** Alexander defeated Darius III's 250,000+ with ~47,000; oblique attack with companion cavalry → shattered Persian center; ended Achaemenid Empire; demonstrated decisive battle concept
- **Cannae (216 BC):** Hannibal's double envelopment; ~50,000–70,000 Romans killed in single day (worst single-day loss in Western military history); weak center drew Romans in, then cavalry closed escape; template for encirclement studied by every military academy since; Schlieffen Plan (1914) inspired by Cannae
- **Carrhae (53 BC):** Parthian mounted archers annihilated 7 Roman legions under Crassus; ~20,000 killed, 10,000 captured; demonstrated lethality of mobile missile cavalry against heavy infantry in open terrain (cross-ref Ch. 03)
- **Teutoburg Forest (9 AD):** Germanic tribes under Arminius ambushed 3 Roman legions (~20,000); worst Roman defeat since Cannae; ended Roman expansion east of Rhine; Augustus: "Varus, give me back my legions!"
- **Adrianople (378 AD):** Gothic heavy cavalry destroyed Roman legions; Emperor Valens killed; signaled cavalry ascendance over infantry in European warfare for ~1,000 years

### Medieval Warfare (~500–1500)

| Innovation | Impact |
|---|---|
| Stirrup (~6th century in Europe) | Enabled heavy cavalry charges; armored knights |
| Castle fortification | Defensive dominance; sieges central to warfare |
| Longbow (English) | Defeated armored cavalry at range (Crécy 1346, Agincourt 1415) |
| Gunpowder (from China, ~1300s in Europe) | Cannons destroyed castle walls → end of feudal warfare |
| Pike formation (Swiss) | Infantry could defeat cavalry again |

**Key battles:**
- **Tours/Poitiers (732):** Charles Martel's Frankish infantry defeated Abd al-Rahman's Arab-Berber cavalry; stopped Muslim advance into Western Europe (significance debated: Gibbon called it world-changing; modern historians note Muslims were overextended raiders, not conquerors)
- **Manzikert (1071):** Seljuk Turks under Alp Arslan defeated Byzantine Emperor Romanos IV; opened Anatolia to Turkish settlement; eventually led to Ottoman Empire; one of most consequential battles in history
- **Hastings (1066):** Norman cavalry + archers + infantry defeated Anglo-Saxon shield wall; William the Conqueror; Harold killed (arrow to eye?); Norman feudalism imposed on England
- **Hattin (1187):** Saladin destroyed Crusader army in waterless terrain; captured True Cross relic; retook Jerusalem; demonstrated importance of logistics and terrain denial
- **Ain Jalut (1260):** Egyptian Mamluks under Qutuz/Baybars defeated Mongol army in Galilee; first major Mongol defeat; stopped westward expansion into Africa/Mediterranean; Mamluks used feigned retreat (Mongol tactic) against Mongols
- **Crécy (1346):** English longbowmen devastated French cavalry; ~1,500 French knights killed; arrow storms at 200m range; end of mounted knight dominance beginning
- **Agincourt (1415):** Henry V's ~6,000 English (mostly longbowmen) defeated ~12,000–36,000 French; muddy terrain channeled cavalry into kill zone; ~6,000 French killed; English losses ~400; Shakespeare immortalized it
- **Fall of Constantinople (1453):** Mehmed II's 80,000+ troops + massive bombard cannon (Orban's cannon: 8m long, fired 600kg stone balls) breached Theodosian Walls after 53-day siege; ended Byzantine Empire; shockwave across Europe; demonstrated gunpowder could defeat medieval fortifications

### Early Modern: Gunpowder Revolution (~1500–1800)

| Innovation | Impact |
|---|---|
| Musket + pike (tercio) | Spanish dominance 1500s; slow, heavy formations |
| *Trace italienne* (bastion fort) | Star-shaped forts resistant to cannon; transformed siege warfare |
| Line infantry (volley fire) | Maximized firepower; required drill and discipline (Maurice of Nassau, Gustavus Adolphus) |
| Naval artillery | Ships as floating gun platforms; broadside warfare |
| Bayonet | Combined musket + pike → single weapon; eliminated pikemen |

**Key battles:**
- **Panipat (1526):** Babur's 12,000 with matchlocks + field artillery defeated 100,000 under Ibrahim Lodi; gunpowder + tactical genius founded Mughal Empire; First Panipat one of most consequential battles in Asian history
- **Lepanto (1571):** Holy League (Spain, Venice, Papal States) ~200 galleys defeated Ottoman fleet ~250 galleys; ~30,000 Ottoman dead; last great galley battle; proved Ottoman naval power could be checked; Cervantes fought here (lost use of left hand)
- **Spanish Armada (1588):** 130 Spanish ships vs English fleet; English used superior gunnery, fire ships, weather ("Protestant Wind"); Spanish lost ~60 ships; ended Spanish bid for naval supremacy; marked rise of English sea power
- **Breitenfeld (1631):** Gustavus Adolphus's Swedish army demonstrated mobile combined-arms: lighter cannon, cavalry charges, flexible infantry formations; defeated Imperial *tercio*; revolution in military tactics; Sweden became great power
- **Blenheim (1704):** Marlborough + Prince Eugene marched 250 miles in 5 weeks (strategic surprise), then defeated Franco-Bavarian army; prevented French hegemony in Europe; demonstrated operational art (campaign-level maneuver)
- **Plassey (1757):** Clive's 3,000 EIC troops defeated Nawab's 50,000 (mostly through bribery of Mir Jafar); opened British conquest of India; tiny battle, enormous consequences
- **Trafalgar (1805):** Nelson's 27 ships defeated Franco-Spanish 33 ships; Nelson killed; divided enemy line into segments (breaking from parallel line doctrine); established British naval supremacy for 100 years; Napoleon couldn't invade Britain
- **Tsushima (1905):** Japanese fleet annihilated Russian Baltic Fleet (sailed 18,000 miles to reach battle); 21 Russian ships sunk vs 3 Japanese torpedo boats; first modern naval battle decided by gunnery; confirmed Mahan's theories; shattered European myth of racial superiority

### Napoleonic Warfare (1803–1815)
- **Levée en masse:** Conscription; nation under arms; 600,000+ French soldiers
- **Corps system:** Self-sufficient army units that could march separately, fight together
- Combined arms: infantry, cavalry, artillery coordinated at scale
- Speed and decisiveness: Napoleon sought to destroy enemy army in one battle

**Key battles:**
- **Austerlitz (1805):** "Battle of the Three Emperors" (Napoleon vs Austrian/Russian); Napoleon feigned weakness on right, drew allies into attacking, then split their line with hidden corps + devastating cavalry charge on weakened center; ~27,000 Allied casualties vs ~9,000 French; destroyed Third Coalition; often called greatest battle victory in history
- **Borodino (1812):** Napoleon vs Kutuzov; bloodiest single day of Napoleonic Wars (~70,000 casualties); pyrrhic French "victory" — took Moscow but found it burned; retreat from Moscow destroyed Grande Armée (600,000 entered Russia; ~100,000 returned); Clausewitz's "culminating point" incarnate
- **Leipzig (1813):** "Battle of the Nations"; 600,000+ combatants (largest battle before WWI); Napoleon defeated by combined Austrian, Prussian, Russian, Swedish armies; ended French dominance of Central Europe
- **Waterloo (1815):** Wellington's combined force held ground until Blücher's Prussians arrived; Napoleon's Imperial Guard repulsed (first time); ~50,000 casualties in one day; ended era; Napoleon exiled to St. Helena

### Industrial Warfare (1850–1945)

| Innovation | Impact |
|---|---|
| Rifled musket/breech-loader | Increased range and accuracy 5-10x → defense dominated |
| Railroad | Strategic mobility; supply revolution |
| Telegraph/radio | Command and control at distance |
| Machine gun | Devastating defensive weapon → trench warfare |
| Barbed wire | Cheap, effective obstacle → reinforced trench warfare |
| Poison gas | Area denial; psychological weapon; ~90,000 killed in WWI |
| Tank | Break trench stalemate (WWI); combined arms mobile warfare (WWII) |
| Aircraft | Reconnaissance → bombing → air superiority → strategic bombing |
| Submarine | Commerce raiding; Battle of the Atlantic |
| Nuclear weapons | Made total war between great powers potentially suicidal |

**Key battles:**
- **Gettysburg (1863):** Turned US Civil War; Lee's invasion of North repulsed; Pickett's Charge (12,500 men across 1 mile of open ground against fortified position → ~50% casualties in 50 minutes); demonstrated futility of frontal assault against rifled weapons + prepared defenses; ~50,000 total casualties in 3 days
- **Sedan (1870):** Prussian army encircled French army + Napoleon III; 100,000 French captured including emperor; Prussian victory in 6 weeks shocked Europe; demonstrated power of Krupp steel artillery, railway mobilization, general staff system; unified Germany
- **Tannenberg (1914):** Hindenburg/Ludendorff's double envelopment of Russian 2nd Army; 90,000 POWs, 30,000 killed; used intercepted unencrypted Russian radio messages; tactical triumph but didn't knock Russia out of war
- **Verdun (1916):** Falkenhayn's attrition strategy ("bleed France white"); 10-month battle; ~700,000 casualties (roughly equal); epitome of industrial attrition warfare; "they shall not pass" (*Ils ne passeront pas*)
- **Somme (1916):** British offensive; 57,470 British casualties on first day (worst in British military history); 141 days; ~1.1 million total casualties; gained 6 miles; tanks first used (September 15, 1916); demonstrated industrial warfare's futility without tactical innovation
- **Brusilov Offensive (1916):** Russian general Brusilov attacked along 300-mile front (not concentrated); surprised Austro-Hungarians; ~1.6 million casualties combined; most successful Allied offensive of WWI; demonstrated innovative infiltration tactics
- **Stalingrad (1942–43):** Hitler's 6th Army encircled by Soviet Operation Uranus; 5 months of urban warfare (fighting room to room); Field Marshal Paulus surrendered 91,000 survivors (of 300,000); ~2M total casualties; turning point of WWII; demonstrated urban warfare negates technological advantage
- **El Alamein (1942):** Montgomery defeated Rommel in North Africa; first major British victory; "Before Alamein we never had a victory. After Alamein we never had a defeat" (Churchill)
- **Midway (1942):** 4 Japanese carriers sunk vs 1 American; codebreaking (decrypted JN-25) enabled ambush; 5-minute window when dive bombers caught Japanese carriers rearming; turned Pacific War; carrier-based air power replaced battleship as decisive weapon
- **Kursk (1943):** Largest tank battle in history (~6,000 tanks); German Operation Citadel defeated by Soviet defense in depth; Germany lost strategic initiative permanently on Eastern Front
- **Normandy/D-Day (June 6, 1944):** 156,000 troops landed on 5 beaches across 50-mile front; 5,000 ships, 13,000 aircraft; deception operation (FORTITUDE) convinced Germans main attack was at Calais; largest amphibious operation in history; opened Western Front
- **Operation Bagration (1944):** Soviet offensive destroyed German Army Group Center; 400,000+ German casualties in 2 months; larger and more decisive than D-Day but less known in Western histories

### Cold War and Post-Cold War (~1945–Present)

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| Nuclear deterrence / MAD | Prevented great power direct war; but enabled proxy wars |
| Guerrilla/insurgency warfare | Mao's people's war; Vietnam; Afghanistan; asymmetric advantage |
| Counterinsurgency (COIN) | Hearts and minds; population-centric; mixed results |
| Precision-guided munitions | "Smart bombs"; reduced collateral damage (in theory); Gulf War (1991) |
| Network-centric warfare | Information superiority; real-time intelligence; drone warfare |
| Cyber warfare | Stuxnet (2010); infrastructure disruption; below threshold of armed conflict |
| Hybrid warfare | Blend of conventional, irregular, cyber, information operations (Russia in Ukraine 2014) |
| Drone warfare | Armed UAVs (Predator/Reaper from 2001); 2020 Nagorno-Karabakh (Turkish Bayraktar TB2 devastated Armenian armor); Ukraine (FPV drones as cheap anti-armor) |

**Key post-WWII conflicts:**
- **Korean War (1950–53):** North Korean invasion; UN/US intervention; Chinese intervention (300,000 troops crossed Yalu River); stalemate near original border; ~3 million dead; demonstrated limits of conventional war in nuclear age
- **Dien Bien Phu (1954):** Vietnamese under Giap besieged French fortress; artillery dragged to surrounding heights by hand; French defeat ended colonial rule in Indochina; demonstrated logistics ingenuity can overcome technological inferiority
- **Six-Day War (1967):** Israel preemptive air strikes destroyed Egyptian/Syrian/Jordanian air forces on ground; conquered Sinai, West Bank, Golan Heights in 6 days; demonstrated air superiority + armor + speed; reshaped Middle East
- **Yom Kippur War (1973):** Egypt/Syria surprised Israel; Egyptian crossing of Suez Canal (innovative water-cannon breaching of sand walls); initial Arab success reversed by Israeli counterattack; demonstrated anti-tank guided missiles (ATGMs) and SAMs could challenge armor/air superiority
- **Falklands War (1982):** Britain retook islands 8,000 miles from home base; Exocet missiles sank HMS Sheffield; demonstrated naval vulnerability to anti-ship missiles; logistical achievement
- **Gulf War (1991):** 100-hour ground campaign; "left hook" flanking maneuver; precision-guided munitions debut; 292 coalition KIA vs ~20,000+ Iraqi; "Revolution in Military Affairs" proclaimed (premature)
- **Ukraine War (2022+):** Combined arms defense; drone warfare (FPV, Bayraktar); artillery dominance; trench warfare return; information warfare; Western sanctions + arms supply; demonstrated that conventional great power war still possible in nuclear age

## Revolution in Military Affairs (RMA)

Theory that periodically, technological/organizational changes fundamentally transform warfare:

| RMA | Period | Key Change |
|---|---|---|
| Infantry revolution | ~1300s | Gunpowder infantry replaced cavalry |
| Artillery fortress | ~1500s | Bastion forts negated medieval castles |
| Napoleonic | ~1800s | Mass conscription, corps system, decisive battle |
| Industrial | ~1860s | Railroads, telegraph, rifled weapons |
| Mechanized/air | ~1920s–40s | Tanks, aircraft, radio → blitzkrieg |
| Nuclear | ~1945 | Deterrence fundamentally changed great power relations |
| Information | ~1990s | Precision munitions, networking, surveillance |
| AI/autonomous | ~2020s+ | Drones, autonomous weapons, AI-enabled C2 (emerging) |

## Principles of War

Distilled across traditions:

| Principle | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Objective** | Clear, decisive, attainable goals |
| **Offensive** | Seize, retain, exploit initiative |
| **Mass** | Concentrate combat power at decisive point |
| **Economy of force** | Minimum necessary effort on secondary objectives |
| **Maneuver** | Place enemy at disadvantage through flexible movement |
| **Unity of command** | Single commander for every objective |
| **Security** | Never permit enemy to gain unexpected advantage |
| **Surprise** | Strike enemy at time/place/manner unprepared |
| **Simplicity** | Clear, uncomplicated plans and orders |

## How Military Innovation Drives Civilizational Change

1. **Gunpowder** → End of feudalism (castles obsolete; peasant with musket > knight); centralized states funded by taxation to maintain expensive armies
2. **Naval technology** → European colonial empires; global trade; oceanic empires replaced land empires
3. **Industrial warfare** → Total war; mass mobilization; state expansion; welfare states (built to maintain fighting populations)
4. **Nuclear weapons** → Cold War structure; deterrence; proxy wars; arms control regimes
5. **Information warfare** → Asymmetric advantage for technically advanced states; but also empowers non-state actors (cyber, drones)

## Guerrilla Warfare and Insurgency

### Mao Zedong's People's War
Three phases:
1. **Strategic defensive:** Build base areas, win peasant support, avoid battle
2. **Strategic stalemate:** Expand bases, harass enemy, build regular forces
3. **Strategic offensive:** Conventional operations to defeat weakened enemy

### Key Principle
"The guerrilla must move among the people as a fish swims in the sea" — population support is center of gravity.

### Key Insurgencies and Lessons

| Conflict | Period | Insurgent | Outcome | Key Lesson |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| American Revolution | 1775–83 | American colonists | Won | External support (France) decisive |
| Spanish guerrillas vs Napoleon | 1808–14 | Spanish irregulars | Won | Coined "guerrilla"; tied down 250,000 French troops |
| Boer War | 1899–1902 | Boer commandos | Lost (politically won) | British used concentration camps; Boers got favorable peace |
| Chinese Civil War | 1927–49 | CCP | Won | Mao's three phases executed perfectly |
| Vietnam | 1955–75 | Viet Cong/NVA | Won | US won every major battle but lost war; political will decisive |
| Algeria | 1954–62 | FLN | Won | France won militarily (Battle of Algiers) but lost politically |
| Afghanistan (Soviet) | 1979–89 | Mujahideen | Won | External support (US Stingers) + terrain + will |
| Afghanistan (US) | 2001–21 | Taliban | Won | 20-year war; $2.3 trillion spent; Taliban returned to power |
| Iraq insurgency | 2003–11 | Various | Mixed | COIN "surge" reduced violence but didn't achieve political goals |

### Why Insurgencies Succeed
- Occupy space between political grievance and military capability
- Exploit terrain (mountains, jungles, cities)
- Time is on insurgent's side (democratic powers face domestic pressure to end wars)
- External support (Vietnam had USSR/China; Afghan mujahideen had US/Pakistan)
- Information asymmetry → propaganda advantage
- Counterinsurgent faces dilemma: aggressive tactics alienate population; restraint allows insurgent freedom

### Why Insurgencies Fail
- Lose popular support (Colombia's FARC lost legitimacy through drug trafficking, kidnapping)
- State offers genuine reforms (Malayan Emergency: British addressed Chinese grievances while fighting militarily)
- Overwhelming state repression in non-democratic context (Sri Lanka crushed Tamil Tigers 2009; Chechnya; Xinjiang)
- Loss of external support (Kurdish PKK weakened when Syria expelled Öcalan 1998)

## Naval Warfare: A Separate Thread

Often treated as appendix; deserves own treatment — sea power shaped civilizations.

### Key Naval Innovations
| Innovation | Period | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Trireme (oared warship) | ~700 BC | Greek/Persian naval warfare; Salamis (480 BC) |
| Greek fire | ~672 AD | Byzantine secret weapon; flammable liquid; defended Constantinople |
| Compass + lateen sail | ~1200s | Enabled ocean navigation; European Age of Exploration |
| Broadside cannon ship | ~1500s | Ships of the line; floating artillery platforms |
| Ironclad (USS Monitor vs CSS Virginia) | 1862 | End of wooden warships |
| Dreadnought | 1906 | All-big-gun battleship; made all other warships obsolete; triggered naval arms race |
| Aircraft carrier | 1920s–40s | Replaced battleship; Midway proved carrier = dominant naval weapon |
| Nuclear submarine | 1954+ | Invisible nuclear deterrent (SSBN); guaranteed second strike |

### Decisive Naval Battles
- **Salamis (480 BC):** Themistocles lured Persian fleet into narrow strait; Greek triremes more maneuverable; ~200 Persian ships destroyed; saved Greek independence
- **Actium (31 BC):** Octavian's fleet defeated Antony/Cleopatra; decided fate of Roman Republic → Empire
- **Trafalgar (1805):** See above; 100 years of British naval supremacy
- **Tsushima (1905):** See above; validated Mahan; shook colonial world
- **Jutland (1916):** Largest battleship engagement; indecisive tactically but strategic British victory (German fleet never challenged again)
- **Midway (1942):** See above; carrier replaced battleship as capital ship
- **Battle of the Atlantic (1939–45):** U-boats vs convoys; ~3,500 merchant ships sunk; ~783 U-boats destroyed; logistics warfare; Ultra codebreaking crucial
