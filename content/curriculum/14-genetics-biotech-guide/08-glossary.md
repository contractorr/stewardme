# Genetics & Biotechnology Glossary

## Quick Reference Guide

This glossary provides concise definitions of key terms in genetics and biotechnology. Terms are organized alphabetically for easy lookup.

---

## A

**Adenine (A)**: Purine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with thymine in DNA, uracil in RNA.

**Allele**: Alternative form of a gene at a specific locus; can be dominant or recessive.

**Alternative Splicing**: Process producing multiple proteins from one gene by combining different exons.

**Amino Acid**: Building block of proteins; 20 standard types in living organisms.

**Aneuploidy**: Abnormal chromosome number (not a multiple of haploid number); e.g., trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).

**Anticodon**: Three-nucleotide sequence on tRNA complementary to mRNA codon.

**Antiparallel**: Opposite directional orientation of DNA strands (one 5'→3', other 3'→5').

**Autosome**: Any chromosome that is not a sex chromosome; humans have 22 pairs.

## B

**Base Pair (bp)**: Two complementary nucleotides joined by hydrogen bonds (A-T or G-C in DNA).

**Base Editing**: CRISPR variant directly converting one DNA base to another without double-strand breaks.

**Biotechnology**: Use of living systems or organisms to develop or modify products and processes.

**BRCA1/BRCA2**: Genes involved in DNA repair; mutations increase breast and ovarian cancer risk.

**Bt Crops**: Genetically modified plants producing Bacillus thuringiensis toxin for insect resistance.

## C

**Carrier**: Individual heterozygous for a recessive allele; phenotypically normal but can pass allele to offspring.

**Cas9**: Endonuclease protein in CRISPR system that cuts DNA at targeted location.

**Central Dogma**: Information flow from DNA → RNA → Protein (with exceptions like reverse transcription).

**Chaperone**: Protein assisting proper folding of other proteins.

**Chromatin**: Complex of DNA and histone proteins; can be condensed (heterochromatin) or loose (euchromatin).

**Chromosome**: Organized structure of DNA and proteins containing genes; humans have 46.

**Clone**: Genetically identical copy of an organism, cell, or DNA molecule.

**Codon**: Three-nucleotide sequence in mRNA specifying an amino acid or stop signal.

**Codominance**: Both alleles fully expressed in heterozygote (e.g., AB blood type).

**Complementary Base Pairing**: Specific hydrogen bonding between nucleotides (A-T, G-C in DNA; A-U in RNA).

**CRISPR**: Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats; gene-editing technology.

**Crossing Over**: Exchange of DNA segments between homologous chromosomes during meiosis.

**Cytosine (C)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with guanine.

## D

**Deletion**: Mutation removing DNA segment; can range from single nucleotide to entire chromosome.

**DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)**: Double-stranded nucleic acid carrying genetic information.

**DNA Methylation**: Addition of methyl groups to cytosine bases; epigenetic modification typically repressing transcription.

**DNA Polymerase**: Enzyme synthesizing new DNA strands during replication.

**DNA Repair**: Cellular mechanisms fixing damaged or incorrectly paired DNA.

**DNA Replication**: Process copying DNA before cell division; semiconservative mechanism.

**DNA Sequencing**: Determining precise order of nucleotides in DNA molecule.

**Dominant**: Allele expressed in heterozygotes, masking recessive allele.

**Double Helix**: Three-dimensional structure of DNA with two antiparallel strands twisted around each other.

**Duplication**: Mutation where DNA segment copied; source of new genetic material for evolution.

## E

**Electrophoresis**: Technique separating DNA, RNA, or proteins by size using electric field.

**Embryonic Stem Cell (ESC)**: Pluripotent cell from early embryo capable of becoming any cell type.

**Enhancer**: Regulatory DNA sequence increasing transcription; can be distant from gene.

**Enzyme**: Protein catalyst accelerating biochemical reactions.

**Epigenetics**: Heritable changes in gene expression without altering DNA sequence.

**Epistasis**: One gene masking or modifying another gene's effects.

**Euchromatin**: Loosely packed chromatin; transcriptionally active.

**Exon**: Coding sequence in gene retained in mature mRNA after splicing.

**Expression Vector**: Plasmid designed for gene expression in host organism.

## F

**F1 Generation**: First filial generation; offspring of P (parental) generation cross.

**F2 Generation**: Second filial generation; offspring of F1 self-cross.

**Frameshift Mutation**: Insertion or deletion not in multiples of three, shifting reading frame.

**Founder Effect**: Reduced genetic diversity when population established by small number of individuals.

## G

**Gamete**: Reproductive cell (sperm or egg) with haploid chromosome number.

**Gene**: DNA segment encoding functional product (protein or RNA).

**Gene Drive**: Genetic element biasing inheritance to spread rapidly through population.

**Gene Expression**: Process by which genetic information produces functional product.

**Gene Flow**: Transfer of alleles between populations through migration.

**Gene Therapy**: Treating disease by modifying patient's genes.

**Genetic Code**: Set of rules translating nucleotide sequences into amino acid sequences.

**Genetic Drift**: Random changes in allele frequencies, stronger in small populations.

**Genetic Engineering**: Direct manipulation of organism's genome using biotechnology.

**Genome**: Complete set of genetic material in organism.

**Genomic Imprinting**: Parent-of-origin-specific gene expression.

**Genotype**: Genetic makeup of organism.

**Germline**: Cells giving rise to gametes; mutations are heritable.

**GMO (Genetically Modified Organism)**: Organism with altered DNA through genetic engineering.

**Guanine (G)**: Purine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with cytosine.

**Guide RNA (gRNA)**: RNA molecule directing Cas9 to target DNA sequence in CRISPR.

## H

**Haploid**: Cell with single set of chromosomes (n); gametes in humans.

**Helicase**: Enzyme unwinding DNA double helix during replication.

**Hemizygous**: Having only one copy of gene (e.g., X-linked genes in males).

**Heritability**: Proportion of phenotypic variation due to genetic factors.

**Heterochromatin**: Highly condensed chromatin; transcriptionally inactive.

**Heteroplasmy**: Multiple mitochondrial genome variants within cell.

**Heterozygote**: Individual with two different alleles at a locus.

**Heterozygote Advantage**: Higher fitness of heterozygotes compared to homozygotes.

**Histone**: Protein around which DNA wraps in nucleosomes.

**Homologous Chromosomes**: Chromosome pairs with same genes at same loci (one from each parent).

**Homologous Recombination (HR)**: DNA repair using homologous template; enables precise gene editing.

**Homozygote**: Individual with two identical alleles at a locus.

**Hox Genes**: Transcription factors controlling body plan development; highly conserved.

**Human Genome Project**: International effort (1990-2003) sequencing entire human genome.

## I

**Incomplete Dominance**: Heterozygote phenotype intermediate between homozygotes.

**Indel**: Insertion or deletion mutation.

**Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell (iPSC)**: Adult cell reprogrammed to pluripotent state.

**Intron**: Non-coding sequence removed from pre-mRNA during splicing.

**Inversion**: Chromosomal mutation where segment reversed 180 degrees.

**In Vitro**: Occurring outside living organism (e.g., in test tube).

**In Vivo**: Occurring inside living organism.

## K

**Karyotype**: Complete set of chromosomes in cell, arranged by size and structure.

**Kilobase (kb)**: 1,000 base pairs of DNA or RNA.

**Knockout**: Organism or cell with specific gene inactivated.

## L

**Ligase**: Enzyme sealing breaks in sugar-phosphate backbone of DNA.

**Linkage**: Genes on same chromosome tending to be inherited together.

**Locus**: Specific position of gene on chromosome (plural: loci).

**Long Non-Coding RNA (lncRNA)**: RNA >200 nucleotides not translated to protein; regulatory functions.

## M

**Meiosis**: Cell division producing four haploid gametes from diploid cell; includes crossing over and independent assortment.

**Messenger RNA (mRNA)**: RNA carrying genetic information from DNA to ribosomes.

**Microbiome**: Community of microorganisms in specific environment (gut, soil, etc.).

**MicroRNA (miRNA)**: Small (~22 nt) regulatory RNA suppressing gene expression.

**Missense Mutation**: Point mutation changing amino acid in protein.

**Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)**: Circular DNA in mitochondria; maternally inherited.

**Mitosis**: Cell division producing two identical diploid daughter cells.

**Molecular Clock**: Using mutation rates to estimate evolutionary divergence times.

**Monoclonal Antibody**: Identical antibodies from cloned cells targeting single antigen.

**Monohybrid Cross**: Genetic cross examining one trait.

**Morphogen**: Signaling molecule forming concentration gradient, specifying cell fates.

**Mosaicism**: Presence of genetically different cell populations in single individual.

**mRNA Vaccine**: Vaccine delivering mRNA encoding antigen; cells translate to trigger immune response.

**Mutagen**: Physical or chemical agent causing mutations.

**Mutation**: Permanent change in DNA sequence.

## N

**Natural Selection**: Differential survival and reproduction of individuals with advantageous traits.

**NHEJ (Non-Homologous End Joining)**: Error-prone DNA repair pathway directly joining broken ends.

**Nonsense Mutation**: Point mutation creating premature stop codon.

**Nucleotide**: Building block of nucleic acids; consists of sugar, phosphate, and nitrogenous base.

**Nucleus**: Membrane-bound organelle containing chromosomes in eukaryotic cells.

## O

**Oncogene**: Gene that can cause cancer when mutated or overexpressed.

**Operon**: Cluster of bacterial genes transcribed together under single promoter.

**Organoid**: Miniature organ-like structure grown from stem cells in vitro.

## P

**PAM (Protospacer Adjacent Motif)**: Short DNA sequence required for Cas9 binding and cleavage.

**PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)**: Technique amplifying specific DNA sequences.

**Pedigree**: Chart showing inheritance pattern of trait through family.

**Penetrance**: Percentage of individuals with specific genotype expressing associated phenotype.

**Phenotype**: Observable characteristics of organism.

**Plasmid**: Small circular DNA molecule in bacteria; used as vector in genetic engineering.

**Pleiotropy**: One gene affecting multiple phenotypic traits.

**Pluripotent**: Capable of differentiating into any cell type (e.g., embryonic stem cells).

**Point Mutation**: Single nucleotide substitution.

**Polygenic Inheritance**: Multiple genes contributing to single trait; produces continuous variation.

**Polymerase**: Enzyme synthesizing nucleic acids (DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase).

**Polymorphism**: Common genetic variant (>1% frequency in population).

**Polyploidy**: More than two complete chromosome sets (3n, 4n, etc.).

**Primer**: Short nucleotide sequence initiating DNA synthesis.

**Prime Editing**: CRISPR variant using reverse transcriptase for versatile editing without double-strand breaks.

**Promoter**: DNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.

**Protein**: Macromolecule composed of amino acids; performs most cellular functions.

**Punnett Square**: Diagram predicting offspring genotypes and phenotypes from parental genotypes.

## R

**Recessive**: Allele expressed only in homozygotes; masked by dominant allele in heterozygotes.

**Recombinant DNA**: DNA molecules from multiple sources joined together.

**Recombination**: Formation of new allele combinations through crossing over.

**Recombination Frequency**: Percentage of recombinant offspring; indicates distance between genes.

**Regulatory Gene**: Gene encoding product controlling other genes' expression.

**Replication Fork**: Y-shaped structure where DNA unwinds during replication.

**Repressor**: Protein blocking transcription by binding operator.

**Restriction Enzyme**: Enzyme cutting DNA at specific recognition sequences.

**Retrovirus**: RNA virus using reverse transcriptase to integrate into host genome (e.g., HIV).

**Reverse Transcriptase**: Enzyme synthesizing DNA from RNA template.

**Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)**: RNA component of ribosomes.

**Ribosome**: Molecular machine synthesizing proteins by translating mRNA.

**RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)**: Single-stranded nucleic acid; types include mRNA, tRNA, rRNA.

**RNA Interference (RNAi)**: Gene silencing using small RNAs.

**RNA Polymerase**: Enzyme synthesizing RNA from DNA template.

## S

**Semiconservative Replication**: Each new DNA molecule contains one original and one new strand.

**Sex Chromosome**: Chromosome determining biological sex (X and Y in humans).

**Sex-Linked**: Gene located on sex chromosome (usually X).

**Silent Mutation**: Point mutation not changing amino acid due to genetic code degeneracy.

**Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP)**: Single base-pair variation; most common genetic variant.

**Small Interfering RNA (siRNA)**: Double-stranded RNA causing gene silencing through RNAi.

**Somatic Cell**: Any body cell except gametes; mutations not heritable.

**Splicing**: Removal of introns and joining of exons during mRNA processing.

**Start Codon**: AUG; initiates translation, codes for methionine.

**Stem Cell**: Undifferentiated cell capable of self-renewal and differentiation.

**Stop Codon**: UAA, UAG, or UGA; terminates translation.

**Synthetic Biology**: Engineering biological systems from standardized parts.

## T

**TALEN**: Transcription Activator-Like Effector Nuclease; gene-editing tool preceding CRISPR.

**Telomere**: Protective DNA sequence at chromosome ends; shortens with each cell division.

**Template Strand**: DNA strand serving as template for RNA synthesis during transcription.

**Test Cross**: Cross between organism of unknown genotype and homozygous recessive individual.

**Thymine (T)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base in DNA; pairs with adenine.

**Transcription**: Synthesis of RNA from DNA template.

**Transcription Factor**: Protein regulating gene transcription by binding specific DNA sequences.

**Transduction**: Gene transfer via virus (bacteria) or conversion of signal (eukaryotes).

**Transfer RNA (tRNA)**: RNA delivering amino acids to ribosome during translation; has anticodon.

**Transformation**: Uptake of naked DNA by cell (bacteria); malignant conversion (eukaryotes).

**Transgenic**: Organism containing genes from another species.

**Translation**: Synthesis of protein from mRNA template at ribosome.

**Translocation**: Chromosomal mutation moving segment to different chromosome; ribosome movement during translation.

**Transposable Element**: DNA sequence capable of moving within genome ("jumping genes").

**Trinucleotide Repeat**: Repetitive three-nucleotide sequence; expansion causes diseases (Huntington's, Fragile X).

**Triplet Code**: Three nucleotides (codon) specify one amino acid.

**Tumor Suppressor Gene**: Gene whose loss or inactivation contributes to cancer.

## U

**Uracil (U)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base in RNA; pairs with adenine; replaces thymine.

**UTR (Untranslated Region)**: mRNA sequence not translated; 5' and 3' UTRs regulate expression.

## V

**Vector**: DNA molecule (plasmid, virus) used to transfer genetic material into cell.

**Virus**: Infectious agent containing nucleic acid genome; requires host cell for replication.

## W

**Wild Type**: Normal, non-mutant phenotype or genotype in natural population.

## X

**X-Inactivation**: Random silencing of one X chromosome in female mammals for dosage compensation.

**Xenotransplantation**: Transplanting organs or tissues from one species to another.

**X-Linked**: Gene located on X chromosome; shows characteristic inheritance pattern.

## Z

**Zinc Finger Nuclease (ZFN)**: Gene-editing tool using zinc finger DNA-binding domains fused to nuclease.

**Zygote**: Fertilized egg; diploid cell resulting from fusion of sperm and egg.

## Common Organisms in Genetics Research

| Organism | Scientific Name | Why Used |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| Fruit fly | *Drosophila melanogaster* | Short generation time, genetic tools, development studies |
| Nematode worm | *Caenorhabditis elegans* | Simple, transparent, cell lineage mapped, RNAi discovery |
| Zebrafish | *Danio rerio* | Transparent embryos, vertebrate development, disease models |
| Mouse | *Mus musculus* | Mammalian genetics, disease models, knockout studies |
| Thale cress | *Arabidopsis thaliana* | Plant model, small genome, short generation time |
| Yeast | *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* | Eukaryotic cell biology, genetic screens, protein expression |
| E. coli | *Escherichia coli* | Bacterial genetics, recombinant protein production |
| Pea plant | *Pisum sativum* | Mendel's original experiments |

## Summary

This glossary provides definitions for essential genetics and biotechnology terms used throughout the study guide. Terms span molecular genetics (DNA, RNA, proteins), inheritance patterns (dominance, linkage), mutations and variation, gene editing technologies (CRISPR, base editing), and biotechnology applications. Understanding this vocabulary is fundamental to comprehending genetics literature, research papers, and practical applications. The abbreviations section clarifies common acronyms encountered in scientific writing, while the model organisms table explains why specific species dominate genetics research. Use this glossary as a quick reference when encountering unfamiliar terms in the other chapters or in outside reading.
