# Molecular Genetics

## Overview

Molecular genetics examines the structure and function of genes at the molecular level. This field explains how DNA stores information, replicates itself, and directs protein synthesis through transcription and translation. Understanding these fundamental processes is essential for comprehending how organisms function, develop, and pass traits to offspring.

## DNA Structure

### The Double Helix

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) consists of two polynucleotide strands twisted into a double helix. James Watson and Francis Crick, using Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography data, proposed this structure in 1953.

**Structural Components**:
- **Sugar-phosphate backbone**: Alternating deoxyribose sugars and phosphate groups form the outer rails
- **Nitrogenous bases**: Inner rungs made of paired bases
- **Antiparallel strands**: One strand runs 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5'

### The Four Bases

| Base Type | Name | Abbreviation | Pairs With | Chemical Type |
|-----------|------|--------------|------------|---------------|
| Purine | Adenine | A | T | Double-ring |
| Purine | Guanine | G | C | Double-ring |
| Pyrimidine | Thymine | T | A | Single-ring |
| Pyrimidine | Cytosine | C | G | Single-ring |

**Chargaff's Rules**: Amount of adenine equals thymine; amount of guanine equals cytosine (A=T, G=C).

**Base Pairing**:
- A-T pairs form 2 hydrogen bonds
- G-C pairs form 3 hydrogen bonds (stronger)
- Complementary base pairing enables accurate replication

### DNA Packaging

**Naked DNA**: In bacteria, circular DNA exists as a loose nucleoid.

**Chromatin**: In eukaryotes, DNA wraps around histone proteins forming nucleosomes (147 base pairs per nucleosome). These further condense into chromatin fibers.

**Chromosomes**: During cell division, chromatin condenses into visible X-shaped chromosomes, each containing one DNA molecule.

**Packaging Levels**:
1. DNA double helix (2 nm wide)
2. Nucleosomes ("beads on a string" - 11 nm)
3. 30 nm chromatin fiber
4. Looped domains (300 nm)
5. Condensed chromosome (700 nm)

## DNA Replication

Replication creates two identical DNA molecules from one original, following a **semiconservative** mechanism where each new molecule contains one original strand and one new strand.

### Key Enzymes & Proteins

| Enzyme/Protein | Function |
|----------------|----------|
| Helicase | Unwinds double helix, separating strands |
| Single-strand binding proteins | Prevent strands from reannealing |
| Topoisomerase | Relieves tension from unwinding |
| Primase | Synthesizes short RNA primers |
| DNA Polymerase III | Main enzyme adding nucleotides to new strand |
| DNA Polymerase I | Removes RNA primers, fills gaps |
| Ligase | Seals nicks in sugar-phosphate backbone |

### Replication Process

**Step 1: Initiation**
- Begins at specific sequences called origins of replication
- Helicase unwinds DNA, creating replication fork
- Single-strand binding proteins stabilize separated strands

**Step 2: Elongation**
- Primase synthesizes RNA primers (starting point for DNA polymerase)
- DNA polymerase III adds nucleotides in 5' to 3' direction only

**Leading Strand**: Synthesized continuously toward replication fork

**Lagging Strand**: Synthesized discontinuously away from fork as Okazaki fragments (1000-2000 nucleotides in prokaryotes, 100-200 in eukaryotes)

**Step 3: Termination**
- DNA polymerase I removes RNA primers, replaces with DNA
- Ligase seals nicks between Okazaki fragments
- In eukaryotes, telomerase extends chromosome ends (telomeres)

### Replication Fidelity

**Proofreading**: DNA polymerase checks each nucleotide added, error rate ~1 in 10^7

**Mismatch Repair**: Additional system catches errors after replication, final error rate ~1 in 10^10

## Transcription: DNA to RNA

Transcription synthesizes RNA from a DNA template, transferring genetic information from nucleus to cytoplasm (in eukaryotes).

### RNA vs DNA

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Sugar | Deoxyribose | Ribose |
| Bases | A, T, G, C | A, U, G, C |
| Strands | Double | Usually single |
| Stability | Very stable | Less stable |
| Location | Nucleus (eukaryotes) | Nucleus & cytoplasm |

### Types of RNA

**mRNA (messenger RNA)**: Carries genetic code from DNA to ribosomes

**tRNA (transfer RNA)**: Delivers amino acids to ribosomes during translation

**rRNA (ribosomal RNA)**: Structural and catalytic component of ribosomes

**Other RNAs**: microRNA, siRNA, lncRNA (regulatory functions covered in gene expression chapter)

### Transcription Process (Eukaryotes)

**Step 1: Initiation**
- Transcription factors bind promoter region (often containing TATA box)
- RNA polymerase II binds, forms pre-initiation complex
- DNA unwinds, creating transcription bubble

**Step 2: Elongation**
- RNA polymerase moves along template strand (3' to 5')
- Synthesizes RNA in 5' to 3' direction
- No primer needed (unlike DNA replication)
- Ribonucleotides added complementary to template (U pairs with A)

**Step 3: Termination**
- RNA polymerase reaches termination signal
- Transcript released, polymerase dissociates
- DNA strands reanneal

### RNA Processing (Eukaryotes Only)

**5' Capping**: Modified guanine nucleotide added to 5' end (protects from degradation, aids ribosome binding)

**3' Polyadenylation**: ~200 adenine nucleotides added to 3' end (poly-A tail, increases stability)

**Splicing**:
- Introns (non-coding sequences) removed
- Exons (coding sequences) joined together
- Performed by spliceosome (complex of proteins and snRNAs)
- Alternative splicing creates multiple proteins from one gene

**Primary transcript** (pre-mRNA) → **Mature mRNA** (exits nucleus)

## Translation: RNA to Protein

Translation converts mRNA's nucleotide sequence into a polypeptide's amino acid sequence at ribosomes.

### The Genetic Code

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Triplet code | Three nucleotides (codon) specify one amino acid |
| Universal | Nearly identical across all organisms |
| Degenerate | Multiple codons can code for same amino acid (64 codons, 20 amino acids) |
| Unambiguous | Each codon specifies only one amino acid |
| Non-overlapping | Codons read sequentially without overlap |

**Start Codon**: AUG (codes for methionine, initiates translation)

**Stop Codons**: UAA, UAG, UGA (signal termination, no amino acid)

### tRNA Structure

- Cloverleaf secondary structure
- L-shaped tertiary structure
- **Anticodon**: Three nucleotides complementary to mRNA codon
- **Amino acid attachment site**: 3' end binds specific amino acid
- **Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases**: Enzymes charging tRNAs with correct amino acids (20 enzymes, one per amino acid)

### Ribosome Structure

**Small subunit**: Binds mRNA, decodes codons

**Large subunit**: Catalyzes peptide bond formation

**Sites**:
- **A site** (aminoacyl): Holds incoming tRNA
- **P site** (peptidyl): Holds tRNA attached to growing chain
- **E site** (exit): Empty tRNA exits here

### Translation Process

**Step 1: Initiation**
- Small ribosomal subunit binds mRNA at 5' cap
- Scans for start codon (AUG)
- Initiator tRNA (carrying methionine) binds at P site
- Large subunit joins, forming complete ribosome

**Step 2: Elongation**
- **Codon recognition**: tRNA with complementary anticodon enters A site
- **Peptide bond formation**: Ribosome catalyzes bond between amino acids
- **Translocation**: Ribosome moves three nucleotides (one codon) downstream
  - tRNA at P site moves to E site, exits
  - tRNA at A site moves to P site
  - A site ready for next tRNA

**Step 3: Termination**
- Stop codon enters A site
- Release factor binds instead of tRNA
- Polypeptide released, ribosomal subunits dissociate
- mRNA can be translated again by multiple ribosomes (polyribosomes)

### Post-Translational Modification

**Folding**: Chaperone proteins assist proper 3D structure formation

**Cleavage**: Signal sequences removed, proteins activated

**Chemical modifications**:
- Phosphorylation (adds phosphate groups)
- Glycosylation (adds carbohydrates)
- Ubiquitination (targets for degradation)

**Protein targeting**: Signal sequences direct proteins to specific locations (ER, mitochondria, nucleus, secretion)

## Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

```
DNA → RNA → Protein
(Replication) (Transcription) (Translation)
```

**Exceptions**:
- **Reverse transcription**: RNA → DNA (retroviruses, telomerase)
- **RNA replication**: RNA → RNA (some viruses)
- **Ribozymes**: Some RNAs have catalytic activity without becoming proteins

## Real-World Examples

**Example 1: Sickle Cell Disease**
Single nucleotide change in β-globin gene (GAG → GTG) causes valine instead of glutamic acid at position 6. This alters hemoglobin structure, making red blood cells sickle-shaped under low oxygen.

**Example 2: Antibiotic Resistance**
Many antibiotics target bacterial ribosomes. Mutations in ribosomal RNA genes or acquisition of genes modifying ribosomes confer resistance, demonstrating translation's importance.

**Example 3: mRNA Vaccines**
COVID-19 vaccines deliver mRNA encoding viral spike protein. Host cells translate this mRNA, producing protein that triggers immune response without needing actual virus.

**Example 4: Nonsense-Mediated Decay**
Cells detect premature stop codons in mRNA (often from mutations) and degrade the transcript, preventing truncated, non-functional proteins that could harm cells.

**Example 5: Alternative Splicing in Disease**
SMN2 gene splicing defect causes spinal muscular atrophy. Drug nusinersen modifies splicing to include critical exon, treating this previously fatal disease.

## Key Terms

**DNA Replication**: Process of copying DNA before cell division

**Semiconservative Replication**: Each new DNA molecule has one original and one new strand

**Transcription**: Synthesis of RNA from DNA template

**Translation**: Synthesis of protein from mRNA template

**Codon**: Three-nucleotide sequence specifying an amino acid

**Anticodon**: tRNA sequence complementary to mRNA codon

**Ribosome**: Molecular machine catalyzing protein synthesis

**Intron**: Non-coding sequence removed from pre-mRNA

**Exon**: Coding sequence retained in mature mRNA

**Central Dogma**: Information flow from DNA to RNA to protein

## Summary

Molecular genetics reveals how genetic information flows from DNA's stable archive through RNA's temporary messages to proteins' functional actions. DNA replication ensures accurate copying through semiconservative mechanisms and multiple proofreading systems. Transcription transfers information to RNA, with eukaryotic processing adding complexity through splicing. Translation decodes mRNA into polypeptides using the universal genetic code, with tRNAs serving as adapters. This elegant system, remarkably conserved across life, enables both heredity and phenotype while providing targets for medicine and biotechnology. Understanding these molecular mechanisms explains how mutations cause disease and how modern therapies can intervene at each step.
