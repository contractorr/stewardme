# Module 5: Leadership & Organizational Behavior

**Time Investment:** ~10 hours
**Deep-Dive Book:** *High Output Management* by Andy Grove

---

## Why This Matters for You

PMs lead without authority. You can't fire anyone, but you need to inspire, align, and drive execution across functions. This requires understanding human motivation, organizational dynamics, and the subtle art of influence.

**What AI can do:** Analyze org structures, suggest process improvements, draft communications.
**What you must do:** Build trust, navigate politics, motivate humans, make hard calls on people.

## Part 1: Motivation

### Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

**Extrinsic motivation:** External rewards/punishments
- Money, promotions, recognition, avoiding discipline
- Works for routine, clearly-defined tasks
- Diminishes over time (hedonic adaptation)
- Can crowd out intrinsic motivation

**Intrinsic motivation:** Internal drive
- Enjoyment, meaning, growth, mastery
- Works for creative, complex work
- Self-sustaining
- What knowledge workers actually respond to

**Research finding:** For complex cognitive work, extrinsic rewards can actually *decrease* performance (Deci, Pink). Intrinsic motivation wins.

### Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose (Daniel Pink)

**Autonomy:** Control over work
- When, where, how, and with whom
- Not absence of direction, but absence of micromanagement
- PM lever: Give teams the problem, not the solution

**Mastery:** Getting better at something that matters
- Progress is deeply motivating
- PM lever: Ensure work is challenging but achievable
- Create visibility into improvement

**Purpose:** Connection to something larger
- Why does this work matter?
- PM lever: Connect daily work to customer impact
- Make the mission real, not just words

### Motivation for PMs

**You can't pay people more. You can:**

1. **Frame work meaningfully**
   - "Build this feature" → "Help 50K customers solve X problem"
   - Connect to user stories and impact

2. **Provide autonomy**
   - Involve team in "how," not just "what"
   - Protect team from unnecessary interference

3. **Create mastery opportunities**
   - Challenging problems
   - Learning stretched
   - Feedback on progress

4. **Recognize publicly**
   - Specific praise, not generic
   - "Your work on X specifically enabled Y outcome"

5. **Remove demotivators**
   - Unnecessary process, unclear goals, political BS
   - Often more important than adding motivators

### Herzberg's Two Factors

**Hygiene factors:** Prevent dissatisfaction (but don't motivate)
- Salary, job security, work conditions, company policies
- When bad → dissatisfied
- When good → not dissatisfied (but not motivated)

**Motivators:** Create satisfaction
- Achievement, recognition, meaningful work, growth
- When present → motivated
- When absent → not motivated (but not necessarily dissatisfied)

**Implication:** Fixing hygiene factors stops complaints but doesn't inspire. You need motivators.

## Part 2: Power & Organizational Politics

### Sources of Power (French & Raven)

| Power Type | Source | Example |
|------------|--------|---------|
| **Legitimate** | Formal authority | Your manager can assign work |
| **Reward** | Control over rewards | Can promote, give bonus |
| **Coercive** | Control over punishment | Can fire, give bad review |
| **Expert** | Knowledge/skill | The person who knows the system |
| **Referent** | Personal appeal | People want to be like them |
| **Informational** | Access to information | Knows what execs are thinking |

**PMs typically have:** Expert, Referent, Informational
**PMs typically lack:** Legitimate, Reward, Coercive

This is why influence matters more than authority.

### Building Power Without Authority

**Expert power:**
- Become the knowledge center for your product/domain
- Know customers better than anyone
- Understand the data deeply
- Be the person people ask

**Referent power:**
- Be someone others want to work with
- Keep commitments, build trust
- Show genuine interest in others' success
- Don't take credit for team work

**Informational power:**
- Attend the right meetings
- Build relationships across functions
- Understand what leadership cares about
- Share relevant information generously

### Organizational Politics

Politics isn't dirty—it's how things get done in groups.

**Political reality:**
- People have competing interests
- Resources are scarce
- Information is power
- Relationships matter
- Formal org charts don't tell the whole story

**Navigating politics (ethically):**

1. **Understand the landscape**
   - Who influences whom?
   - What are the informal power centers?
   - What are the unwritten rules?

2. **Build broad relationships**
   - Don't just know your team
   - Cross-functional relationships = influence

3. **Align with powerful interests**
   - What do decision-makers care about?
   - Frame your work in those terms

4. **Don't create enemies unnecessarily**
   - Today's opponent may be tomorrow's ally
   - Be tough on issues, soft on people

5. **Stay above the fray**
   - Don't gossip, don't backstab
   - If you must fight, fight for important things

## Part 3: Building High-Performing Teams

### Tuckman's Stages

Teams evolve through predictable stages:

**Forming** → **Storming** → **Norming** → **Performing** → (Adjourning)

**Forming:**
- Polite, tentative
- Unclear roles
- PM role: Provide clarity, build safety

**Storming:**
- Conflict emerges
- Challenging leadership, each other
- PM role: Navigate conflict productively, don't suppress

**Norming:**
- Establish working patterns
- Trust builds
- PM role: Reinforce good norms, address bad ones

**Performing:**
- High productivity, autonomy
- Self-managing
- PM role: Remove obstacles, protect the team

**Key insight:** Storming is necessary. Teams that skip it stay superficially polite but don't achieve high performance. Manage through conflict, don't around it.

### Psychological Safety (Amy Edmondson)

**Definition:** Belief that you won't be punished for mistakes, questions, or ideas.

**Why it matters:** Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety is #1 predictor of team performance.

**Signs of low safety:**
- People don't speak up in meetings
- Problems hidden until crisis
- "Yes people" dominate
- Blame culture after failures

**Building safety:**
- Admit your own mistakes
- Ask questions, show curiosity
- Thank people for surfacing problems
- Respond constructively to bad news
- Never punish the messenger

### Team Composition

**Ideal team characteristics:**
- Diverse perspectives (not just demographics—cognitive diversity)
- Right skill coverage
- Mix of experience levels
- Complementary working styles

**Belbin's team roles:** (useful mental model)
- Plant (creative), Resource Investigator (network)
- Coordinator (organize), Shaper (drive)
- Monitor Evaluator (analyze), Teamworker (cooperate)
- Implementer (execute), Completer Finisher (detail)
- Specialist (expertise)

**PM application:** Don't build teams of clones. Ensure coverage of different working styles.

## Part 4: Culture

### What Culture Actually Is

Culture isn't values posters. It's "how things work around here."

**Culture = shared assumptions about:**
- What matters (and doesn't)
- How to behave (and what's taboo)
- How decisions get made
- What success looks like

### Schein's Levels of Culture

```
ARTIFACTS (visible)
├─ Office layout, dress code, rituals
├─ Easy to see, hard to interpret

ESPOUSED VALUES (stated)
├─ What we say we believe
├─ May or may not match reality

BASIC ASSUMPTIONS (implicit)
├─ Unconscious beliefs
├─ Actually drive behavior
├─ Hard to change
```

**Example:**
- Artifact: "Meetings always start late"
- Espoused value: "We value respect for time"
- Basic assumption: "Senior people's time is worth more"

### Culture for PMs

**Reading culture:**
- Watch what gets rewarded, not what's said
- Who gets promoted? What behaviors?
- How are failures handled?
- What stories do people tell?

**Working with culture:**
- Align your work with cultural values
- Frame recommendations in cultural language
- Know which cultural boundaries are flexible

**Changing culture (hard):**
- Culture changes through repeated experience, not proclamation
- Change artifacts and rewards first
- Model the behavior you want
- Takes years, not months

### Netflix Culture Example

**Espoused:** "Freedom and Responsibility"

**Actual mechanisms:**
- No vacation policy (test: did productivity drop? No.)
- High performance focus (adequate performance gets severance)
- Context, not control (explain why, not how)
- Pay top of market (don't lose people over money)

These reinforce each other—freedom only works with responsibility and consequences.

## Part 5: Change Management

### Why Change Fails

Most change efforts fail. Common reasons:

1. **No burning platform** — Why change when current state is okay?
2. **Change fatigue** — Too many simultaneous changes
3. **No coalition** — Change champion alone
4. **Poor communication** — People don't understand why
5. **Victory declared too early** — Stops before embedded
6. **Not anchored in culture** — Behavior change without system change

### Kotter's 8 Steps

1. **Create urgency** — Why must we change now?
2. **Build coalition** — Get influential people on board
3. **Create vision** — Where are we going?
4. **Communicate vision** — Repeatedly, through multiple channels
5. **Remove obstacles** — Clear barriers to change
6. **Create short-term wins** — Show early success
7. **Build on wins** — Use momentum to drive more change
8. **Anchor in culture** — Make change stick

### Change for PMs

**Process changes:**
- Start with "why"
- Involve affected people in designing solution
- Pilot before broad rollout
- Measure and share results

**Product changes:**
- Help teams understand user need
- Share the "job to be done"
- Celebrate when change improves metrics

**Resistance is data:**
- Resistance isn't irrational—it signals unaddressed concerns
- Listen to understand, not to dismiss
- Often resisters see problems champions miss

### ADKAR Model (Individual Change)

| Phase | Question | PM Focus |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **A**wareness | Why change? | Communicate the need |
| **D**esire | What's in it for me? | Personal benefits |
| **K**nowledge | How do I change? | Training, documentation |
| **A**bility | Can I do it? | Practice, support |
| **R**einforcement | Will it stick? | Recognition, accountability |

## Part 6: Decision-Making Biases

### Biases in Groups

**Groupthink:**
- Desire for conformity overrides critical thinking
- Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, self-censorship
- Counter: Assign devil's advocate, anonymous input

**Confirmation bias:**
- Seek information confirming existing beliefs
- Counter: Assign team member to argue opposite, steel man opposing view

**Anchoring:**
- Over-weight first information received
- Counter: Generate options before discussing numbers

**Sunk cost fallacy:**
- Continue investing because of past investment
- Counter: Ask "If starting fresh, would we make this decision?"

**Authority bias:**
- Defer to highest-ranking person
- Counter: Have junior people speak first, anonymous votes

### Better Group Decisions

**Before the meeting:**
- Share context async (reading ahead)
- Have people form opinions before discussion
- Define decision criteria upfront

**During the meeting:**
- Start with lowest-ranking person's opinion
- Explicitly invite dissent
- Separate idea generation from evaluation
- Use structured frameworks (pros/cons, criteria matrix)

**After the meeting:**
- Document decision and rationale
- Assign owner and timeline
- Define how you'll know if it was right

### Decision Frameworks

**RAPID (Bain):**
- **R**ecommend: Who proposes?
- **A**gree: Who must agree? (veto power)
- **P**erform: Who executes?
- **I**nput: Who provides info?
- **D**ecide: Who makes final call?

Clarity prevents confusion. Assign roles explicitly.

**Two-way vs One-way doors (Bezos):**
- One-way door: Irreversible, high stakes → careful analysis
- Two-way door: Reversible, low stakes → move fast, learn

Most decisions are two-way doors. Treat them accordingly.

## Part 7: Leading Without Authority

### Influence Levers

| Lever | How to Use |
|-------|------------|
| **Expertise** | Know your domain deeply |
| **Relationships** | Invest before you need something |
| **Vision** | Paint compelling future |
| **Logic** | Clear, structured reasoning |
| **Data** | Evidence-based arguments |
| **Coalition** | Build support before big meetings |
| **Reciprocity** | Help others, build goodwill |

### Practical Tactics

**1. Own the narrative**
- Write the strategy doc
- Set the meeting agenda
- Frame the problem definition
- Whoever frames, shapes

**2. Make it easy to agree**
- Do the work for busy stakeholders
- Anticipate objections
- Provide clear recommendations
- Reduce decision burden

**3. Build trust incrementally**
- Deliver small things reliably
- Follow up when you say you will
- Admit when you're wrong
- Share credit generously

**4. Choose your battles**
- Don't fight over everything
- Save political capital for important issues
- Let small things go

**5. Align with what matters**
- Know exec priorities
- Frame work in terms of their goals
- Make stakeholders look good

### When You Have No Authority

You're a PM. An engineer doesn't want to build your feature.

**Don't:**
- Escalate immediately
- Pull rank you don't have
- Become adversarial

**Do:**
- Understand their perspective (genuinely)
- Find shared interest
- Address their concerns
- If conflict remains, involve manager collaboratively (not as punishment)

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Motivation Audit

For your team:

1. List each team member
2. For each, identify: What motivates them most? (Autonomy? Mastery? Purpose?)
3. Rate: How well is their current work serving that motivation? (1-5)
4. Identify one action to better align work with motivation for each

**Time:** 45 minutes

### Exercise 2: Power Map

For your current project:

1. Draw a map of key stakeholders
2. Label their power type(s)
3. Draw influence relationships (who influences whom?)
4. Identify: Where is your power weak? How can you build it?

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 3: Team Health Check

Assess your team against these questions:

1. Is there psychological safety? (Would people speak up with a concern?)
2. What stage are they in? (Forming/Storming/Norming/Performing)
3. What's missing in team composition? (skills, perspectives, styles)
4. What's one action to improve team effectiveness?

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 4: Culture Decode

For your organization:

1. List 3 artifacts (visible elements of culture)
2. List 3 espoused values (what leadership says)
3. List 3 basic assumptions (what actually drives behavior)
4. Where do espoused values and basic assumptions conflict?

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 5: Change Initiative Planning

Pick a change you need to drive:

1. What's the burning platform? (Why must we change now?)
2. Who needs to be in the coalition?
3. What's the vision? (One sentence)
4. What's one short-term win you can create?
5. What resistance do you expect? How will you address it?

**Time:** 45 minutes

### Exercise 6: Decision Audit

Review a recent group decision:

1. What biases might have affected it?
2. Was there psychological safety to disagree?
3. Who had what role? (Was it clear?)
4. How would you structure it differently?

**Time:** 30 minutes
