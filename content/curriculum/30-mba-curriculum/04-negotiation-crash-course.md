# Module 4: Negotiation

**Time Investment:** ~10 hours
**Deep-Dive Book:** *Never Split the Difference* by Chris Voss

---

## Why This Matters for You

PMs negotiate constantly: roadmap priorities with stakeholders, resources with engineering leads, timelines with execs, terms with partners, salaries with HR. Yet most treat negotiation as intuitive rather than skilled.

**What AI can do:** Research counterparty, model scenarios, draft proposals.
**What you must do:** Read emotions, build rapport, navigate tension, close deals.

---

## Part 1: The Core Framework

### BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)

Your BATNA is what you'll do if this negotiation fails. It's your walkaway power.

**Strong BATNA:** Other job offers, alternative vendors, ability to build in-house
**Weak BATNA:** Only option, deadline pressure, desperate need

**The rule:** Never negotiate without knowing your BATNA and trying to improve it.

**Before any negotiation:**
1. What's your BATNA?
2. How can you strengthen it?
3. What's their BATNA?
4. How can you weaken theirs? (ethically)

### Reservation Price

Your reservation price is your walkaway point—the worst deal you'd accept.

**Example:**
- Salary negotiation: "I won't accept below $180K"
- Vendor deal: "We won't pay more than $50K/year"
- Timeline: "We must ship by June 1 or we cancel"

**Calculate reservation price from BATNA:**
- If your BATNA is $175K offer elsewhere, reservation price is at least $175K
- If BATNA is terrible (no other offers), reservation price is low

### ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

ZOPA is the range where both parties can agree.

```
YOUR RESERVATION ─────────ZOPA─────────── THEIR RESERVATION
   (floor)                                     (ceiling)

Example salary negotiation:
You won't accept: <$180K          They won't pay: >$210K

ZOPA: $180K─────────────────────────────$210K

Any deal in this range works for both.
```

**No ZOPA = no deal possible.** Walk away and use your BATNA.

**Discovering ZOPA:**
- They won't tell you their reservation price
- Probe with questions, calibrated offers
- Watch for reaction intensity at different numbers

### Anchoring

The first number shapes the entire negotiation.

**Research shows:**
- First offer strongly influences outcome
- Even obviously extreme anchors affect final price
- Counter-offers are pulled toward the anchor

**Implications:**
- If you have information advantage, anchor first
- If uncertain about value, let them anchor first
- Counter extreme anchors immediately and firmly
- Anchor with a range (your ideal to their reservation)

## Part 2: Integrative vs Distributive

### Distributive Negotiation

**Zero-sum:** Your gain is their loss. Fixed pie.

**When it applies:**
- Single issue (usually price)
- One-time transaction
- No future relationship

**Tactics:**
- Strong anchoring
- Reveal little information
- Emphasize your BATNA
- Make extreme first demands
- Concede slowly

### Integrative Negotiation

**Positive-sum:** Expand the pie, then divide it.

**When it applies:**
- Multiple issues
- Different priorities
- Ongoing relationship
- Complex deals

**The key insight:** What you value highly, they may value little (and vice versa).

**Example:**
```
You care about: Fast timeline (high), price (medium), support (low)
They care about: Price (high), support (medium), timeline (low)

Integrative deal:
- You get fast timeline (high value to you, low cost to them)
- They get higher price (high value to them, medium cost to you)
- Both happy
```

### Finding Integrative Potential

**Ask questions:**
- "What's most important to you in this deal?"
- "What would make this a great outcome for your team?"
- "Are there constraints I should know about?"
- "What would you need to move on timeline?"

**Share your interests (not positions):**
- Position: "We need it by March 1"
- Interest: "We have a customer commitment that requires early delivery"

Sharing interests enables creative solutions. Sharing positions creates conflict.

### Expanding the Pie

**Techniques:**
1. **Add issues:** Can we include support, training, future discounts?
2. **Unbundle issues:** Break price into components (setup, monthly, usage)
3. **Contingent deals:** "If X happens, then Y"
4. **Different currencies:** Trade timeline for price, exclusivity for discount
5. **Shared interests:** Find things both sides want

## Part 3: Tactical Empathy (Voss Techniques)

### What Is Tactical Empathy?

Empathy isn't sympathy. It's understanding the other side's perspective to influence the negotiation.

**Chris Voss's approach:** Use empathy as a negotiation tool, not a feel-good exercise.

### Labeling

Name the emotion you observe to defuse it and build rapport.

**Formula:** "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..."

**Examples:**
- "It seems like timeline is really stressing you out."
- "It sounds like you've had bad experiences with vendors before."
- "It looks like this decision is complicated for you."

**Why it works:**
- Shows you're listening
- Defuses negative emotions (naming reduces intensity)
- Reveals information (they'll correct or expand)

**Don't say:** "I understand." (This triggers resistance—they think you don't.)

### Mirroring

Repeat the last 1-3 words as a question.

**Example:**
```
Them: "We can't possibly move on price because of budget constraints."
You: "Budget constraints?"
Them: "Yeah, we allocated everything to the platform migration, so
there's literally nothing left in the discretionary budget, but
maybe I could talk to finance about Q2 allocation..."
```

**Why it works:**
- Keeps them talking
- Reveals information
- Feels like deep listening
- Buys you time to think

### Accusation Audit

Preemptively name all the negative things they might be thinking.

**Example opening:**
```
"You probably think I'm going to ask for an unreasonable timeline.
You might feel like product always makes impossible demands.
And you're probably bracing for me to ignore your technical concerns.

That's not my goal. Let me share what we're actually trying to solve..."
```

**Why it works:**
- Defuses objections before they harden
- Shows self-awareness
- Reduces their defensive posture

### Calibrated Questions

Open-ended questions starting with "What" or "How" that put the problem on them.

**Instead of:** "Can you do it faster?"
**Try:** "How can we make this timeline work?"

**Instead of:** "Will you lower the price?"
**Try:** "What would it take to get to a number that works for our budget?"

**Power phrases:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "What's the biggest challenge you face?"
- "How would you like me to proceed?"
- "What makes this hard for you?"

**Why it works:**
- Engages them in problem-solving
- Reveals constraints and interests
- Makes them feel in control
- Avoids triggering "no"

### Getting to "That's Right"

The goal isn't "yes" (can be fake). The goal is "that's right."

**"That's right" = genuine agreement**

How to get there:
1. Summarize their position accurately
2. Label their emotions
3. Acknowledge their constraints
4. Validate their perspective

When they say "that's right," you've earned their trust.

## Part 4: Multi-Party Negotiations

### Mapping the Players

In organizational negotiations, multiple people influence decisions:

**The map:**
- **Decision maker:** Who can actually say yes?
- **Influencers:** Who do they listen to?
- **Blockers:** Who can say no but not yes?
- **Champions:** Who wants this to succeed?
- **End users:** Who will actually use the outcome?

### Coalition Building

**Strategy:**
1. Identify natural allies
2. Build their support before the main negotiation
3. Understand blocker concerns
4. Find ways to address blockers (or neutralize)
5. Present unified front

**Common mistake:** Negotiating with the wrong person. Always find the actual decision-maker.

### Managing Internal Stakeholders

Often your hardest negotiation is internal.

**Before external negotiations:**
- Align with your team on priorities
- Get explicit authority (what can you commit to?)
- Understand internal constraints
- Agree on walkaway point

**During negotiations:**
- "I need to check with my team" is a legitimate tactic
- Don't promise what you can't deliver
- Use internal process as leverage ("My finance team won't approve that")

## Part 5: Specific Negotiation Scenarios

### Salary/Comp Negotiations

**Preparation:**
1. Research market rates (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, network)
2. Know your BATNA (other offers, willingness to walk)
3. List your accomplishments with metrics
4. Determine your reservation price

**Tactics:**
- Let them go first if possible (anchoring uncertainty)
- If you must anchor, anchor high with justification
- Negotiate total comp, not just base (equity, bonus, signing, benefits)
- Use "I" language ("I was expecting..." not "My friend got...")

**Powerful phrases:**
- "What flexibility do you have on [specific element]?"
- "How did you arrive at that number?"
- "I'm really excited about this role. To make this work, I'd need [X]."
- "What would I need to demonstrate to be at the top of the band?"

**If they say no:**
- "I understand. What would it take to revisit this in 6 months?"
- Ask for non-monetary alternatives (title, equity, flexibility, review timing)

### Vendor/Partnership Negotiations

**Leverage points:**
- Multi-year vs single-year (they value predictability)
- Case study/logo rights (marketing value to them)
- Reference calls (you're their sales tool)
- Volume commitments
- Timing (end of quarter = desperate to close)

**Tactics:**
- Get multiple bids (creates real BATNA)
- Unbundle (separate setup, training, support, licenses)
- Ask for "most favored customer" clauses
- Negotiate renewal terms upfront

**Phrases:**
- "We're also talking to [competitor]. What makes you different?"
- "What's the best deal you've done for a company like ours?"
- "What would you need from us to get to [number]?"

### Internal Negotiations (Roadmap, Resources, Priorities)

**These are repeated games:** You'll negotiate with the same people next quarter.

**Key differences from external:**
- Relationship matters more than any single outcome
- Shared organizational goals (find them)
- Information is less hidden
- Reputation follows you

**Tactics:**
- Find the shared interest (company success, metrics you both own)
- Understand their constraints genuinely
- Look for trades ("We'll deprioritize X if you can support Y")
- Document agreements (alignment meetings have short memories)

**Phrases:**
- "Help me understand the constraints you're working with."
- "What would you need from me to make this work?"
- "If we could only do one thing, what would be most valuable?"

## Part 6: Common Mistakes & Advanced Tactics

### Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Negotiating with yourself**
- Don't lower your ask before they respond
- Don't concede without asking for something in return
- Let silence work for you

**2. Accepting the first offer**
- Even great first offers can improve
- At minimum, test with one counter
- They expect negotiation; first offer has margin

**3. Negotiating position instead of interests**
- "We need $100K" vs "We need to show ROI to our board"
- Interests enable creative solutions

**4. Burning bridges**
- Aggressive tactics harm long-term relationships
- Even if you "win," resentment breeds future problems
- Leave them feeling good about the outcome

**5. Poor preparation**
- Not knowing your BATNA
- Not researching their constraints
- Not having specific asks ready

### Advanced Tactics

**The Flinch:**
- Visible negative reaction to their offer
- "Oh wow, that's much more than I expected"
- Creates doubt in their position

**The Nibble:**
- Small asks at the end after deal seems done
- "Now that we've agreed on price, can you throw in training?"
- Works because they're invested in closing

**Good Cop/Bad Cop:**
- One person is tough, one is reasonable
- Creates psychological contrast
- If used against you, call it out

**Silence:**
- After making an offer or ask, stop talking
- Discomfort makes people fill the void
- Often they negotiate against themselves

**"I Need to Check":**
- Creates time and space
- "My CEO needs to approve anything over $X"
- Lets you reset if needed

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: BATNA Strengthening

For an upcoming negotiation (job, vendor, internal):

1. What's your current BATNA?
2. Rate it: Strong / Medium / Weak
3. List 3 ways to strengthen it before the negotiation
4. What's their BATNA? How could you (ethically) weaken it?

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 2: Integrative Analysis

Take a negotiation you're in or approaching:

1. List all the issues (price, timeline, scope, support, etc.)
2. Rank your priority for each (1-5)
3. Guess their priority for each (1-5)
4. Where can you trade high-value-to-you for low-value-to-them?

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 3: Tactical Empathy Practice

In your next 3 conversations (work or personal):

1. Use one mirror (repeat last 1-3 words as question)
2. Use one label ("It seems like...")
3. Use one calibrated question ("How can we..." or "What would...")

Notice the response. Did they open up? Provide more information?

**Time:** Ongoing, then 15-minute reflection

### Exercise 4: Accusation Audit

Write an accusation audit for an upcoming difficult conversation:

1. List every negative thing they might think about you/your ask
2. Draft opening sentences that name these proactively
3. Practice saying it out loud (it will feel uncomfortable)

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Exercise 5: Salary Negotiation Prep

Even if you're not job hunting, prepare as if you were:

1. Research your market rate (3 sources)
2. List your top 5 accomplishments with metrics
3. What's your BATNA? (Could you get another offer? Freelance?)
4. What's your reservation price?
5. What's your target? (Anchor point)
6. Write your opening ask

**Time:** 1 hour

### Exercise 6: Role Play

Find a colleague. Each choose a negotiation scenario. Take turns:

1. Brief each other on the scenario
2. Negotiate for 10 minutes
3. Debrief: What worked? What didn't? What did you notice?

**Time:** 1 hour total
