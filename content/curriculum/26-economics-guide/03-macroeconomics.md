# Macroeconomics

## Overview

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole, focusing on aggregate variables like national output, unemployment, inflation, and economic growth. Rather than individual markets, it examines economy-wide phenomena and the policies governments use to influence them.

Key questions include: What causes recessions and booms? Why do prices rise over time? How can governments stabilize the economy? What drives long-term growth?

## Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

### Definition and Measurement

**GDP**: The total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific period (usually quarterly or annually).

**Three Approaches to Measuring GDP**:

1. **Expenditure Approach** (most common):
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
```
- C = Consumption (household spending)
- I = Investment (business capital, residential construction, inventory changes)
- G = Government purchases
- X = Exports
- M = Imports
- (X - M) = Net exports

2. **Income Approach**:
Sum of all incomes earned in production (wages, rent, interest, profits)

3. **Production Approach**:
Sum of value added at each production stage

**Real-World Example - US GDP 2024** (approximate):
- Total GDP: $27 trillion
- Consumption: 68% ($18.4T)
- Investment: 18% ($4.9T)
- Government: 17% ($4.6T)
- Net Exports: -3% (-$800B)

### GDP vs. GNP

| Measure | Definition | Focus |
|---------|------------|-------|
| **GDP** | Production within geographic borders | Location of production |
| **GNP** | Production by nation's citizens/companies | Ownership of production |

GNP = GDP + Net Factor Income from Abroad

**Example**: Profits from a Toyota factory in Kentucky count toward US GDP but Japanese GNP.

### Nominal vs. Real GDP

**Nominal GDP**: Measured at current prices (not adjusted for inflation)

**Real GDP**: Adjusted for inflation using base-year prices (measures actual quantity changes)

```
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100
```

**Example**:
- 2020: 100 cars at $30,000 = $3 million
- 2024: 105 cars at $35,000 = $3.675 million
- Nominal growth: 22.5%
- Real growth: 5% (the actual increase in output)

### GDP Limitations

**What GDP doesn't measure**:
1. **Non-market activities**: Household production, volunteer work
2. **Underground economy**: Illegal activities, unreported income
3. **Quality improvements**: Computers today vs. 20 years ago
4. **Income distribution**: $50k average could mean everyone earns $50k or extreme inequality
5. **Sustainability**: Environmental degradation not counted
6. **Well-being**: Happiness, leisure time, health

**Alternative Measures**:
- **GNI per capita**: GDP plus foreign income, divided by population
- **HDI** (Human Development Index): Combines income, education, life expectancy
- **Genuine Progress Indicator**: Adjusts for inequality, pollution, leisure

**Real-World Example**: After the 2010 earthquake, Haiti's GDP fell dramatically, but rebuilding efforts temporarily increased GDP despite worse living conditions - illustrating that GDP measures economic activity, not welfare.

## Unemployment

### Types of Unemployment

| Type | Cause | Duration | Policy Response |
|------|-------|----------|------------------|
| **Frictional** | Job searching, transitioning between jobs | Short-term | Job search assistance, information platforms |
| **Structural** | Skills mismatch, technological change | Medium to long-term | Retraining programs, education investment |
| **Cyclical** | Economic recession, insufficient demand | Varies with business cycle | Fiscal/monetary stimulus |
| **Seasonal** | Predictable seasonal patterns | Short-term, recurring | Less concerning (expected) |

**Natural Rate of Unemployment** (NAIRU): Frictional + Structural unemployment; the unemployment rate when economy is at full employment (typically 4-6% in developed countries).

### Measuring Unemployment

**Unemployment Rate**:
```
U = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100
```

Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking work)

**Labor Force Participation Rate**:
```
LFPR = (Labor Force / Working-Age Population) × 100
```

**Limitations**:
- **Discouraged workers**: Stopped looking for work, not counted as unemployed
- **Underemployment**: Part-time workers wanting full-time positions
- **Hidden unemployment**: Workers in jobs below their skill level

**Alternative Measures (US Bureau of Labor Statistics)**:
- **U3** (official rate): Standard unemployment
- **U4**: U3 + discouraged workers
- **U5**: U4 + marginally attached workers
- **U6**: U5 + part-time for economic reasons (most comprehensive)

**Real-World Example - 2008 Financial Crisis**:
- Peak U3: 10% (October 2009)
- Peak U6: 17.1% (October 2009)
- U6 revealed much greater labor market distress

### Costs of Unemployment

**Individual Costs**:
- Lost income and reduced living standards
- Deteriorating skills
- Psychological stress, health problems
- Reduced lifetime earnings

**Social Costs**:
- Lost output (GDP below potential)
- Increased government spending (unemployment benefits, social services)
- Reduced tax revenue
- Social problems (crime, family stress)

**Okun's Law**: For every 1% increase in unemployment above natural rate, real GDP falls about 2% below potential GDP.

## Inflation

### Definition and Measurement

**Inflation**: Sustained increase in the general price level over time.
**Deflation**: Sustained decrease in the general price level (negative inflation).
**Disinflation**: Decreasing rate of inflation (inflation slowing but still positive).

### Price Indices

**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**:
Measures cost of fixed basket of goods and services purchased by typical urban consumer.

```
CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100

Inflation Rate = [(CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}) / CPI_{t-1}] × 100
```

**CPI Components (US)**:
- Housing: 42%
- Transportation: 16%
- Food: 14%
- Medical care: 9%
- Education: 3%
- Other: 16%

**Producer Price Index (PPI)**: Measures prices received by producers (wholesale prices).

**GDP Deflator**: Comprehensive measure including all goods/services in GDP, not fixed basket.

```
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
```

### CPI Biases

1. **Substitution bias**: Fixed basket doesn't account for consumers switching to cheaper alternatives
2. **New product bias**: New products enter market but not immediately in basket
3. **Quality adjustment bias**: Improvements in quality not fully captured
4. **Outlet bias**: Shift to discount retailers not fully reflected

Result: CPI likely overstates inflation by 0.5-1% annually.

### Types of Inflation

| Type | Cause | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Demand-pull** | Aggregate demand grows faster than supply | Post-COVID reopening (2021-2022) |
| **Cost-push** | Production costs increase (wages, oil) | 1970s oil shocks |
| **Built-in** | Wage-price spiral expectations | Hyperinflation scenarios |
| **Hyperinflation** | Extreme inflation (>50% monthly) | Zimbabwe 2008, Venezuela 2016-2019 |

### Costs of Inflation

**Anticipated Inflation** (expected):
- **Menu costs**: Cost of changing prices frequently
- **Shoe-leather costs**: Time/effort to minimize cash holdings (more bank trips)
- **Tax distortions**: Nominal income pushes into higher tax brackets

**Unanticipated Inflation** (unexpected):
- **Redistribution**: Debtors benefit, creditors lose (debt repaid in devalued currency)
- **Uncertainty**: Complicates long-term planning and contracts
- **Arbitrary wealth transfers**: Fixed-income earners (retirees) harmed

**Real-World Example - Zimbabwe Hyperinflation (2008)**:
- Peak monthly inflation: 79.6 billion percent
- Prices doubled every 24.7 hours
- Currency abandoned; US dollar adopted
- Caused by excessive money printing to fund government spending

### Winners and Losers from Inflation

**Winners**:
- Debtors (debt value erodes)
- Fixed-rate mortgage holders
- Governments with debt
- Real asset owners (real estate, commodities)

**Losers**:
- Creditors (repaid in devalued currency)
- Fixed-income recipients (retirees on pensions)
- Cash holders
- Workers with fixed wages

## Aggregate Supply and Demand

### Aggregate Demand (AD)

Represents total spending in the economy at different price levels.

**Components**: AD = C + I + G + (X - M)

**AD Curve slopes downward** because:
1. **Wealth effect**: Lower prices increase real wealth, boosting consumption
2. **Interest rate effect**: Lower prices reduce money demand, lowering interest rates, stimulating investment
3. **Exchange rate effect**: Lower prices make exports cheaper, increasing net exports

**Shifts in AD**:

| Factor | Effect on AD |
|--------|-------------|
| Increased consumer confidence | Shifts right (increase) |
| Higher government spending | Shifts right |
| Tax cuts | Shifts right |
| Lower interest rates | Shifts right |
| Strong foreign economies | Shifts right (exports increase) |
| Pessimistic expectations | Shifts left (decrease) |

### Aggregate Supply (AS)

Represents total production in the economy at different price levels.

**Short-Run AS (SRAS)**:
- Upward sloping (higher prices → higher production)
- Wages and some prices are "sticky" (don't adjust immediately)
- Firms can increase output by hiring more workers, running extra shifts

**Long-Run AS (LRAS)**:
- Vertical at potential GDP (full employment output)
- Economy produces at capacity regardless of price level
- Determined by factors of production (labor, capital, technology)

**Shifts in LRAS** (long-run growth):
- Increased labor force (population growth, immigration)
- Capital accumulation (investment in machinery, infrastructure)
- Technological progress
- Improved human capital (education, skills)
- Better institutions (property rights, rule of law)

### Macroeconomic Equilibrium

**Short-Run Equilibrium**: Where AD intersects SRAS
- May be above, below, or at potential GDP

**Long-Run Equilibrium**: Where AD intersects LRAS
- Economy at full employment
- Actual GDP = Potential GDP
- Unemployment at natural rate

### Economic Fluctuations

**Recessionary Gap** (AD below potential):
- High unemployment
- Downward pressure on wages and prices
- SRAS eventually shifts right (lower wages reduce costs)
- Self-correcting but slow

**Inflationary Gap** (AD above potential):
- Low unemployment
- Upward pressure on wages and prices
- SRAS eventually shifts left (higher wages increase costs)
- Leads to inflation

**Real-World Example - 2008 Financial Crisis**:
- Aggregate demand collapsed (financial crisis, reduced wealth)
- AD shifted far left
- Deep recessionary gap: unemployment reached 10%
- Output fell 4% below potential
- Slow self-correction prompted aggressive fiscal/monetary intervention

## Business Cycles

### Phases of the Business Cycle

| Phase | Characteristics | Duration (US) |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Expansion** | Rising GDP, falling unemployment, increasing investment | Average: 58 months (post-1945) |
| **Peak** | Maximum output before downturn | Turning point |
| **Contraction/Recession** | Falling GDP, rising unemployment, declining investment | Average: 11 months (post-1945) |
| **Trough** | Minimum output before recovery | Turning point |

**Official Recession Definition** (US): Two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth, or broader NBER determination based on income, employment, and production.

### Causes of Business Cycles

**Demand-Side Factors**:
- Changes in consumer confidence
- Investment volatility (firms' expectations)
- Government fiscal policy changes
- Foreign demand shocks

**Supply-Side Factors**:
- Oil price shocks (1970s)
- Natural disasters
- Technological breakthroughs
- Productivity changes

**Financial Factors**:
- Credit booms and busts
- Asset bubbles (housing, stocks)
- Banking crises

**Psychological Factors**:
- "Animal spirits" (Keynes) - waves of optimism and pessimism
- Self-fulfilling prophecies

### Historical Business Cycles

**Great Depression (1929-1939)**:
- GDP fell 27%
- Unemployment reached 25%
- Stock market crashed 89%
- Causes: Stock bubble, bank failures, poor monetary policy, trade collapse

**Great Recession (2007-2009)**:
- GDP fell 4.3%
- Unemployment reached 10%
- Housing market collapse, financial crisis
- Causes: Subprime mortgages, excessive leverage, regulatory failures

**COVID-19 Recession (2020)**:
- Shortest but sharpest post-WWII recession
- GDP fell 10% (Q2 2020)
- Unemployment briefly hit 14.7%
- Cause: Pandemic-induced shutdowns (supply and demand shock)

## Economic Growth

### Sources of Economic Growth

**Production Function**:
```
Y = A × f(K, L)
```
- Y = Output (GDP)
- A = Total Factor Productivity (technology, efficiency)
- K = Capital stock
- L = Labor force

**Growth Accounting**:
- Labor quantity (population growth, labor force participation)
- Labor quality (education, skills, health)
- Capital accumulation (investment in physical capital)
- Technological progress (innovation, R&D)

### Growth Theories

**Classical Growth Theory** (Malthus, Ricardo):
- Long-run growth limited by diminishing returns to labor
- Population growth keeps wages at subsistence level

**Neoclassical Growth Theory** (Solow Model):
- Steady-state growth determined by population and technology
- Capital accumulation alone cannot sustain growth (diminishing returns)
- Technology is key driver of long-run growth
- Predicts convergence (poor countries grow faster than rich)

**Endogenous Growth Theory** (Romer, Lucas):
- Growth can be sustained indefinitely
- Investment in human capital and R&D generates increasing returns
- Knowledge is non-rivalrous (can be used by many simultaneously)
- Policy can affect long-run growth rate

### Productivity

**Labor Productivity**:
```
Labor Productivity = Real GDP / Total Hours Worked
```

**Determinants**:
1. Physical capital per worker (machinery, equipment)
2. Human capital per worker (education, training, experience)
3. Technological knowledge (innovation, processes)

**Real-World Example - US Productivity Growth**:
- 1947-1973: 2.8% annual growth (Golden Age)
- 1973-1995: 1.4% growth (productivity slowdown)
- 1995-2004: 2.5% growth (IT revolution)
- 2004-2019: 1.3% growth (productivity puzzle)

### Policies to Promote Growth

| Policy | Mechanism | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Investment in education** | Increases human capital | Universal primary education, college subsidies |
| **R&D incentives** | Accelerates innovation | Tax credits for research, patent protection |
| **Infrastructure investment** | Improves productivity | Highways, broadband, power grids |
| **Property rights protection** | Encourages investment | Strong legal system, contract enforcement |
| **Free trade** | Access to larger markets, technology transfer | Trade agreements, reduced tariffs |
| **Stable macroeconomic environment** | Reduces uncertainty | Low inflation, predictable policy |
| **Competition policy** | Encourages efficiency | Antitrust enforcement |

## **Key Terms**

- **GDP**: Total value of final goods/services produced in economy
- **Real vs. Nominal**: Inflation-adjusted vs. current prices
- **Unemployment Rate**: Percentage of labor force without jobs
- **Natural Rate of Unemployment**: Frictional plus structural unemployment
- **Inflation**: Sustained increase in general price level
- **CPI**: Consumer Price Index measuring typical household costs
- **Aggregate Demand**: Total spending at different price levels
- **Aggregate Supply**: Total production at different price levels
- **Potential GDP**: Maximum sustainable output at full employment
- **Recession**: Period of declining economic activity
- **Business Cycle**: Fluctuations in economic activity over time
- **Productivity**: Output per unit of input (usually per worker-hour)
- **Economic Growth**: Increase in potential GDP over time

## Summary

Macroeconomics examines economy-wide variables and policies. GDP measures total output through expenditure, income, or production approaches, though it has limitations in capturing well-being. Unemployment types include frictional, structural, and cyclical, with the natural rate representing full employment. Inflation erodes purchasing power and creates costs through redistribution, uncertainty, and menu/shoe-leather effects.

Aggregate demand and supply determine short-run output and prices, with long-run supply reflecting potential GDP. Business cycles fluctuate between expansion and recession, driven by demand shocks, supply shocks, financial factors, and psychological waves. Economic growth depends on labor, capital, and technology, with productivity improvements being the key to sustained living standard increases.

Understanding these macroeconomic concepts is essential for interpreting economic news, evaluating policy proposals, and anticipating how the overall economy will affect personal and business decisions.
