# Microeconomics

## Overview

Microeconomics studies the behavior of individual economic agents - consumers, workers, and firms - and how they interact in specific markets. It examines decision-making at the granular level, analyzing how prices are determined, resources allocated, and trade occurs in individual markets.

Key questions include: Why do prices rise and fall? How do consumers decide what to buy? How do firms determine production levels? What causes market failures?

## Supply and Demand

### The Demand Curve

The law of demand states that, all else equal, as price decreases, quantity demanded increases. This inverse relationship reflects:
- **Substitution effect**: When prices fall, the good becomes relatively cheaper than alternatives
- **Income effect**: Lower prices increase purchasing power, allowing more consumption

**Demand Function**: Qd = f(P, I, Ps, Pc, T, E, N)

Where:
- P = Price of the good
- I = Income
- Ps = Price of substitutes
- Pc = Price of complements
- T = Tastes and preferences
- E = Expectations
- N = Number of buyers

**Example**: When gas prices rise, demand for SUVs decreases (substitute effect toward fuel-efficient cars) and demand for public transit increases (complement effect).

### The Supply Curve

The law of supply states that, all else equal, as price increases, quantity supplied increases. Higher prices make production more profitable, incentivizing greater output.

**Supply Function**: Qs = f(P, Pi, Pt, E, N)

Where:
- P = Price of the good
- Pi = Input prices
- Pt = Technology
- E = Expectations
- N = Number of sellers

**Example**: When coffee bean prices fall, the supply of coffee increases as production costs decrease.

### Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. The equilibrium price "clears the market" - no shortage or surplus exists.

```
At equilibrium: Qd = Qs
Price = P*
Quantity = Q*
```

**Shortage**: When price < P*, Qd > Qs. Upward pressure on price.
**Surplus**: When price > P*, Qs > Qd. Downward pressure on price.

### Shifts vs. Movements

| Change | Cause | Effect |
|--------|-------|--------|
| **Movement along demand curve** | Price change | Quantity demanded changes |
| **Shift in demand curve** | Non-price factor (income, tastes, etc.) | Entire relationship changes |
| **Movement along supply curve** | Price change | Quantity supplied changes |
| **Shift in supply curve** | Non-price factor (costs, technology, etc.) | Entire relationship changes |

**Real-World Example - COVID-19 Mask Market**:
- Initial shortage: Price too low, demand exceeded supply
- Demand shift: Pandemic increased demand (curve shifts right)
- Supply shift: Manufacturers increased production capacity (curve shifts right)
- New equilibrium: Higher quantity, variable price depending on timing

## Elasticity

Elasticity measures responsiveness of one variable to changes in another - crucial for pricing decisions, tax policy, and market analysis.

### Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

```
PED = (% change in Qd) / (% change in P)
```

| Elasticity | Value | Description | Example |
|------------|-------|-------------|---------|
| Perfectly inelastic | 0 | Quantity doesn't respond to price | Life-saving medication |
| Inelastic | 0 to -1 | Quantity responds weakly | Gasoline, salt |
| Unit elastic | -1 | Proportional response | Some consumer goods |
| Elastic | < -1 | Quantity responds strongly | Restaurant meals, luxury goods |
| Perfectly elastic | -∞ | Quantity responds infinitely | Commodity markets |

**Determinants of Elasticity**:
1. **Availability of substitutes**: More substitutes → more elastic (Coke vs. soft drinks generally)
2. **Necessity vs. luxury**: Necessities are inelastic (insulin), luxuries elastic (jewelry)
3. **Time horizon**: Demand becomes more elastic over time (short-run gas demand is inelastic; long-run includes car switching)
4. **Budget share**: Larger share of income → more elastic (furniture vs. pencils)
5. **Definition of market**: Narrowly defined markets are more elastic (Pepsi vs. soft drinks vs. beverages)

**Business Application**: If PED = -2, a 10% price increase causes 20% quantity decrease, reducing revenue. If PED = -0.5, the same price increase causes only 5% quantity decrease, increasing revenue.

### Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

```
YED = (% change in Qd) / (% change in Income)
```

- **Normal goods** (YED > 0): Demand increases with income (most goods)
  - **Necessity** (0 < YED < 1): Food, clothing
  - **Luxury** (YED > 1): Fine dining, jewelry
- **Inferior goods** (YED < 0): Demand decreases with income (instant noodles, bus travel)

**Example**: During economic growth, demand for luxury cars (YED ≈ 3) grows faster than income, while demand for fast food (YED ≈ 0.5) grows slower.

### Cross-Price Elasticity (XED)

```
XED = (% change in Qd of Good A) / (% change in Price of Good B)
```

- **Substitutes** (XED > 0): Butter and margarine (butter price rises → margarine demand increases)
- **Complements** (XED < 0): Coffee and sugar (coffee price rises → sugar demand decreases)
- **Unrelated** (XED ≈ 0): Books and gasoline

### Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

```
PES = (% change in Qs) / (% change in P)
```

**Determinants**:
- **Time**: Short-run supply is inelastic (fixed capacity); long-run is elastic (can build factories)
- **Spare capacity**: Firms with excess capacity can respond quickly
- **Storage**: Storable goods have more elastic supply
- **Factor mobility**: Can resources easily shift to this industry?

**Example**: Agricultural supply is inelastic in the short run (crops already planted) but elastic long-run (farmers can switch crops).

## Consumer Theory

### Utility Maximization

Consumers allocate budgets to maximize utility (satisfaction) subject to budget constraints.

**Budget Constraint**: I = PxX + PyY (income spent on goods X and Y)

**Utility Maximization Rule**:
```
MUx/Px = MUy/Py = ... = λ
```

Marginal utility per dollar should be equal across all goods. If MUx/Px > MUy/Py, shift spending from Y to X.

### Marginal Utility and the Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

**Total Utility (TU)**: Overall satisfaction from consumption
**Marginal Utility (MU)**: Additional satisfaction from one more unit

**Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility**: As consumption increases, each additional unit provides less satisfaction.

| Pizza Slices | Total Utility | Marginal Utility |
|--------------|---------------|------------------|
| 0 | 0 | - |
| 1 | 20 | 20 |
| 2 | 35 | 15 |
| 3 | 45 | 10 |
| 4 | 50 | 5 |
| 5 | 50 | 0 |
| 6 | 45 | -5 |

**Real-World Application**: The first glass of water in the desert is extremely valuable; the 100th glass has little value. This explains the "diamond-water paradox" - water has high total utility but low marginal utility (and thus low price), while diamonds have low total utility but high marginal utility (and thus high price).

### Indifference Curves and Budget Lines

**Indifference Curve**: Combinations of goods providing equal satisfaction
- Downward sloping (more of X compensates for less of Y)
- Cannot intersect
- Convex to origin (diminishing marginal rate of substitution)

**Budget Line**: Affordable combinations given income and prices

**Optimal Choice**: Where indifference curve is tangent to budget line (highest achievable utility).

## Market Structures

### Perfect Competition

**Characteristics**:
- Many buyers and sellers
- Homogeneous product
- Free entry and exit
- Perfect information
- Price takers (firms cannot influence market price)

**Outcome**:
- P = MC (price equals marginal cost)
- Zero economic profit in long run
- Allocatively efficient (P = MC)
- Productively efficient (production at minimum average cost)

**Examples**: Agricultural commodities (wheat, corn), stock markets

**Firm Decision Rule**: Produce where P = MC, shut down if P < AVC (average variable cost)

### Monopolistic Competition

**Characteristics**:
- Many firms
- Differentiated products (brands, quality, location)
- Free entry and exit
- Some price-setting power

**Outcome**:
- P > MC (markup over marginal cost)
- Zero economic profit in long run (entry erodes profits)
- Excess capacity (produce below minimum average cost)
- Product variety benefits consumers

**Examples**: Restaurants, clothing retailers, hair salons

**Real-World Example**: Coffee shops compete on location, atmosphere, product quality, and brand. Starbucks charges premium prices despite many competitors because product differentiation creates customer loyalty.

### Oligopoly

**Characteristics**:
- Few large firms
- High barriers to entry
- Interdependent decision-making
- Products may be homogeneous or differentiated

**Outcome**:
- P > MC
- Economic profits possible in long run
- Potential for collusion or competition
- Strategic behavior (game theory applies)

**Examples**: Airlines, automobiles, telecommunications, soft drinks

**Strategic Behavior - Prisoner's Dilemma**:

|  | Firm B: Low Price | Firm B: High Price |
|--|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Firm A: Low Price** | Both earn $5M | A earns $15M, B earns $0 |
| **Firm A: High Price** | A earns $0, B earns $15M | Both earn $10M |

Nash Equilibrium: Both choose low price (dominant strategy), earning $5M each, even though cooperation (both high price) yields $10M each. This explains why cartels are unstable without enforcement.

**Real-World Example**: OPEC attempts to coordinate oil production to maintain higher prices, but members have incentives to cheat by producing more than quotas allow.

### Monopoly

**Characteristics**:
- Single seller
- No close substitutes
- High barriers to entry (legal, resource control, economies of scale)
- Price maker

**Outcome**:
- P > MC (deadweight loss)
- Economic profit in long run
- Productive inefficiency
- Potential for price discrimination

**Examples**: Local utilities (natural monopolies), patented drugs, Microsoft Windows (historically)

**Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices to different customers for the same product
- **First-degree**: Perfect price discrimination (charge each customer their maximum willingness to pay)
- **Second-degree**: Volume discounts
- **Third-degree**: Different prices for different groups (student discounts, senior discounts)

**Real-World Example**: Airlines practice third-degree price discrimination - business travelers (inelastic demand) pay higher prices than leisure travelers (elastic demand) for the same flight.

### Market Structure Comparison

| Characteristic | Perfect Competition | Monopolistic Competition | Oligopoly | Monopoly |
|----------------|---------------------|--------------------------|-----------|----------|
| Number of firms | Many | Many | Few | One |
| Product differentiation | None | Some | None or some | N/A |
| Entry barriers | None | Low | High | Very high |
| Pricing power | None | Some | Significant | Complete |
| Long-run profit | Zero | Zero | Positive possible | Positive |
| Efficiency | High | Moderate | Low | Lowest |

## Market Failures

Markets fail when they don't allocate resources efficiently, justifying potential government intervention.

### Externalities

**Negative externality**: Costs imposed on third parties (pollution, noise)
- Market produces too much (social cost > private cost)
- Solution: Pigouvian taxes, cap-and-trade, regulation

**Positive externality**: Benefits to third parties (education, vaccination)
- Market produces too little (social benefit > private benefit)
- Solution: Subsidies, public provision

**Real-World Example - Carbon Tax**: British Columbia's carbon tax (2008) reduced fuel consumption by 5-15% while maintaining economic growth, internalizing the external cost of carbon emissions.

### Public Goods

**Characteristics**:
- **Non-rivalrous**: One person's consumption doesn't reduce availability to others
- **Non-excludable**: Cannot prevent non-payers from consuming

Markets underprovide public goods due to free-rider problem (people benefit without paying).

**Examples**: National defense, lighthouses, basic research, fireworks displays

**Solution**: Government provision funded by taxes, or mechanisms to reduce free-riding (membership organizations, social pressure).

### Information Asymmetry

When one party has more information than another, markets may fail:

**Adverse selection** (pre-contract): Low-quality products drive out high-quality (Akerlof's "Market for Lemons")
- Used car market: Sellers know quality, buyers don't → buyers assume average quality → high-quality sellers exit → market unravels
- Insurance: High-risk individuals more likely to buy insurance → prices rise → low-risk individuals drop out

**Moral hazard** (post-contract): People take more risks when insured against losses
- Health insurance: People may take fewer preventative measures
- Bank bailouts: "Too big to fail" encourages excessive risk-taking

**Solutions**: Signaling (education credentials), screening (insurance questionnaires), warranties, reputation, regulation.

### Common Resources

**Characteristics**: Rivalrous but non-excludable

**Tragedy of the commons**: Overuse of shared resources (overfishing, deforestation)
- Each individual's rational decision to use more leads to collective depletion

**Solutions**: Property rights, quotas, taxes, community management.

**Real-World Example**: Iceland's fishing quota system (ITQs - Individual Transferable Quotas) prevented overfishing by assigning property rights to fish stocks, making sustainability profitable.

## **Key Terms**

- **Supply and Demand**: Model of price determination in markets
- **Equilibrium**: Where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied
- **Elasticity**: Responsiveness of one variable to changes in another
- **Consumer Surplus**: Difference between willingness to pay and actual price
- **Producer Surplus**: Difference between price received and minimum acceptable price
- **Marginal Utility**: Additional satisfaction from one more unit
- **Perfect Competition**: Market with many firms, homogeneous products, free entry
- **Monopoly**: Single seller with no close substitutes
- **Oligopoly**: Market with few large firms
- **Externality**: Cost or benefit imposed on third parties
- **Public Good**: Non-rivalrous and non-excludable good
- **Deadweight Loss**: Efficiency loss from market distortions

## Summary

Microeconomics examines individual decision-making and market interactions. Supply and demand determine prices through equilibrium, with elasticity measuring responsiveness to changes. Consumer theory explains how individuals maximize utility subject to budget constraints, following the law of diminishing marginal utility.

Market structures range from perfect competition (efficient but rare) to monopoly (inefficient but sometimes unavoidable). Each structure has different implications for pricing, efficiency, and welfare. Firms maximize profit where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, while competitive markets achieve allocative efficiency where price equals marginal cost.

Markets fail when externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, or common resource problems exist. These failures provide economic justification for government intervention through taxes, subsidies, regulation, or direct provision. Understanding these micro-level mechanisms is essential for analyzing real-world markets, business strategy, and policy design.
