# Economics Glossary

## A

**Absolute Advantage**: Ability to produce a good using fewer resources than another producer.

**Adverse Selection**: Market failure where low-quality products drive out high-quality due to information asymmetry (before transaction).

**Aggregate Demand (AD)**: Total spending on domestic goods and services at different price levels (C + I + G + NX).

**Aggregate Supply (AS)**: Total production in economy at different price levels; short-run is upward sloping, long-run is vertical at potential GDP.

**Anchoring**: Cognitive bias where initial information disproportionately influences subsequent judgments.

**Automatic Stabilizers**: Government programs that automatically expand during recessions and contract during booms without policy changes (unemployment insurance, progressive taxes).

**Availability Heuristic**: Judging probability by ease of recalling examples.

## B

**Balance of Payments**: Record of all economic transactions between a country and rest of world.

**Balanced Budget**: Government spending equals tax revenue.

**Behavioral Economics**: Field combining psychology and economics to understand actual (not idealized) decision-making.

**Bounded Rationality**: Limited cognitive capacity leads people to satisfice rather than optimize.

**Budget Deficit**: Government spending exceeds tax revenue.

**Budget Surplus**: Government revenue exceeds spending.

**Business Cycle**: Fluctuations in economic activity alternating between expansion and contraction.

## C

**Capital Account**: Component of balance of payments recording capital transfers and non-produced asset transactions.

**Cartel**: Group of firms colluding to restrict output and raise prices (e.g., OPEC).

**Central Bank**: Institution conducting monetary policy and regulating banking system (Federal Reserve, ECB).

**Ceteris Paribus**: "All else equal" - assumption holding other variables constant when analyzing one relationship.

**Circular Flow Model**: Diagram showing money and goods flows between households, firms, and government.

**Comparative Advantage**: Ability to produce a good at lower opportunity cost than another producer; basis for trade.

**Complementary Goods**: Goods consumed together; increase in price of one decreases demand for other (coffee and sugar).

**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**: Measure of average change in prices paid by urban consumers for fixed basket of goods and services.

**Consumer Surplus**: Difference between consumers' willingness to pay and actual price paid.

**Contractionary Policy**: Policy reducing aggregate demand to cool economy (fiscal: spending cuts or tax increases; monetary: raising interest rates).

**Cost-Push Inflation**: Inflation caused by rising production costs (wages, oil, materials).

**Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)**: Percentage change in quantity demanded of one good divided by percentage change in price of another good.

**Crowding Out**: Government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment.

**Current Account**: Component of balance of payments recording trade in goods/services, investment income, and transfers.

**Cyclical Unemployment**: Unemployment caused by recession and insufficient aggregate demand.

## D

**Deadweight Loss**: Efficiency loss from market distortions (taxes, monopolies, externalities).

**Deflation**: Sustained decrease in general price level (negative inflation).

**Demand**: Relationship between price and quantity consumers willing and able to purchase.

**Demand-Pull Inflation**: Inflation caused by aggregate demand growing faster than aggregate supply.

**Depreciation (Currency)**: Decrease in currency value relative to other currencies.

**Diminishing Marginal Utility**: Each additional unit of consumption provides less additional satisfaction.

**Discount Rate**: Interest rate central bank charges commercial banks for short-term loans.

**Disposable Income**: Income after taxes available for consumption and saving.

**Dumping**: Selling exports below cost or below domestic price to gain market share.

## E

**Economic Growth**: Increase in economy's potential output over time; rightward shift in LRAS.

**Economies of Scale**: Cost per unit decreases as production volume increases.

**Elasticity**: Responsiveness of one variable to changes in another.

**Endowment Effect**: People value items they own more than identical items they don't own.

**Equilibrium**: State where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied; no tendency to change.

**Exchange Rate**: Price of one currency in terms of another.

**Expansionary Policy**: Policy increasing aggregate demand to stimulate economy (fiscal: spending increases or tax cuts; monetary: lowering interest rates).

**Externality**: Cost or benefit imposed on third parties not involved in transaction.

## F

**Federal Funds Rate**: Interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans of reserves; Fed's primary policy target.

**Financial Account**: Component of balance of payments recording investment flows (FDI, portfolio investment, reserves).

**Fiscal Policy**: Government decisions on spending and taxation to influence economy.

**Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)**: Investment in business operations in another country (building factories, acquiring companies).

**Forward Guidance**: Central bank communication about future policy intentions.

**Framing Effect**: Presentation of identical information in different ways changes decisions.

**Free Rider Problem**: People benefit from public goods without paying, leading to underprovision.

**Frictional Unemployment**: Short-term unemployment from normal job searching and transitioning between positions.

**Fungibility**: Property of money being interchangeable; $1 should be valued the same regardless of source.

## G

**GDP Deflator**: Comprehensive price index calculated as (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100.

**Giffen Good**: Inferior good where higher price increases quantity demanded (violates law of demand; extremely rare).

**Globalization**: Increasing economic integration across countries through trade, investment, technology, and migration.

**Government Debt**: Accumulated deficits over time; total amount government owes to bondholders.

**Gross Domestic Product (GDP)**: Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a period.

**Gross National Product (GNP)**: Total market value of all final goods and services produced by a country's citizens regardless of location.

## H

**Heuristics**: Mental shortcuts simplifying decision-making, sometimes leading to systematic biases.

**Human Capital**: Knowledge, skills, and capabilities embodied in people through education and experience.

**Hyperbolic Discounting**: Time-inconsistent preferences with present bias; disproportionately weight present over future.

**Hyperinflation**: Extremely high inflation (typically defined as exceeding 50% per month).

## I

**Implicit Cost**: Opportunity cost of using owned resources (not requiring cash payment).

**Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)**: Percentage change in quantity demanded divided by percentage change in income.

**Indexing**: Adjusting payments automatically for inflation (Social Security, TIPS bonds).

**Infant Industry Argument**: Protection for new industries until they achieve economies of scale and can compete internationally.

**Inferior Good**: Good whose demand decreases as income increases (ramen noodles, bus travel).

**Inflation**: Sustained increase in general price level over time.

**Inflation Targeting**: Monetary policy framework with explicit inflation goal (typically 2%).

**Interest on Reserves (IOR)**: Interest central bank pays on commercial bank reserves; primary monetary policy tool since 2008.

**Invisible Hand**: Adam Smith's metaphor for market self-regulation through individual self-interest.

## K

**Keynesian Economics**: School emphasizing aggregate demand's role in determining output; supports active fiscal policy to stabilize economy.

## L

**Labor Force**: Employed plus unemployed (actively seeking work).

**Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR)**: (Labor Force / Working-Age Population) × 100.

**Laffer Curve**: Relationship between tax rates and tax revenue (inverted U-shape); higher rates don't always increase revenue.

**Law of Demand**: All else equal, as price decreases, quantity demanded increases (inverse relationship).

**Law of Supply**: All else equal, as price increases, quantity supplied increases (direct relationship).

**Liquidity Trap**: Situation where monetary policy ineffective because people hoard cash despite low interest rates.

**Long Run**: Time period sufficient for all inputs to adjust (no fixed costs).

**Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)**: Vertical at potential GDP; economy's productive capacity when all resources fully employed.

**Loss Aversion**: Pain of losing $X exceeds pleasure of gaining $X (losses weigh ~2x gains).

## M

**Macroeconomics**: Study of economy-wide phenomena (GDP, unemployment, inflation, economic growth).

**Marginal Analysis**: Comparing additional benefits to additional costs for decision-making.

**Marginal Cost (MC)**: Additional cost of producing one more unit.

**Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)**: Fraction of additional income spent on consumption (ΔC / ΔY).

**Marginal Revenue (MR)**: Additional revenue from selling one more unit.

**Marginal Utility (MU)**: Additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit.

**Market Failure**: Situation where markets don't allocate resources efficiently (externalities, public goods, information asymmetry, monopolies).

**Market Structure**: Characteristics of market determining competition level (perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, monopoly).

**Medium of Exchange**: Function of money facilitating transactions without barter.

**Mental Accounting**: Categorizing money into mental "accounts" based on source or use, treating dollars differently.

**Microeconomics**: Study of individual economic agents' behavior (consumers, firms) and specific markets.

**Monetary Base**: Currency in circulation plus bank reserves held at central bank (M0).

**Monetary Policy**: Central bank's management of money supply and interest rates to achieve macroeconomic objectives.

**Money Multiplier**: Factor by which money supply expands from increase in reserves (1 / reserve ratio).

**Monopolistic Competition**: Market structure with many firms, differentiated products, free entry (restaurants, retail).

**Monopoly**: Market structure with single seller, no close substitutes, high barriers to entry.

**Moral Hazard**: Party takes more risks when insured against losses (information asymmetry after transaction).

**Multiplier Effect**: Total GDP increase from $1 of spending exceeds $1 due to rounds of re-spending.

## N

**Nash Equilibrium**: Outcome where no player can improve by unilaterally changing strategy (given others' strategies).

**Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU)**: Unemployment rate at full employment (frictional + structural).

**Negative Externality**: Cost imposed on third parties (pollution, noise).

**Negative Interest Rate Policy (NIRP)**: Central bank sets interest rates below zero.

**Net Exports**: Exports minus imports (X - M).

**Nominal GDP**: GDP measured at current prices (not adjusted for inflation).

**Nominal Interest Rate**: Stated interest rate (not adjusted for inflation).

**Normal Good**: Good whose demand increases as income increases.

**Normative Economics**: Value judgments about what "ought to be" (subjective).

**Nudge**: Choice architecture steering toward better decisions while preserving freedom of choice.

## O

**Okun's Law**: Relationship between unemployment and output; 1% increase in unemployment above natural rate reduces GDP ~2% below potential.

**Oligopoly**: Market structure with few large firms, high barriers to entry, interdependent decision-making.

**Open Market Operations (OMO)**: Central bank buying or selling government securities to adjust money supply; most frequently used monetary policy tool.

**Opportunity Cost**: Value of next best alternative forgone when making a choice.

## P

**Perfect Competition**: Market structure with many firms, homogeneous products, free entry/exit, perfect information; firms are price takers.

**Phillips Curve**: Inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation (short-run); vertical (no tradeoff) in long-run.

**Positive Economics**: Objective, testable statements about "what is" (factual).

**Positive Externality**: Benefit conferred on third parties (education, vaccination, R&D).

**Potential GDP**: Maximum sustainable output when economy at full employment.

**Present Bias**: Disproportionately weighting present over future in time preferences.

**Price Ceiling**: Maximum legal price (rent control); creates shortages if below equilibrium.

**Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices to different customers for same product based on willingness to pay.

**Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**: Percentage change in quantity demanded divided by percentage change in price.

**Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)**: Percentage change in quantity supplied divided by percentage change in price.

**Price Floor**: Minimum legal price (minimum wage); creates surpluses if above equilibrium.

**Producer Surplus**: Difference between price received and minimum price seller willing to accept.

**Production Function**: Relationship between inputs and output (Y = f(K, L, A)).

**Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)**: Graph showing maximum combinations of two goods producible with available resources.

**Productivity**: Output per unit of input (typically per worker or per hour).

**Progressive Tax**: Tax rate increases as income increases (higher earners pay higher percentage).

**Prospect Theory**: Theory of decision-making under risk featuring reference dependence, loss aversion, and probability weighting.

**Public Good**: Good that is non-rivalrous (one person's consumption doesn't reduce availability) and non-excludable (can't prevent non-payers from consuming).

## Q

**Quantitative Easing (QE)**: Large-scale asset purchases by central bank to inject liquidity when interest rates near zero.

**Quantitative Tightening (QT)**: Central bank reducing balance sheet by allowing assets to mature without reinvestment.

**Quota**: Quantitative limit on imports.

## R

**Rational Expectations**: Theory that people form expectations using all available information and economic models optimally.

**Real GDP**: GDP adjusted for inflation using base-year prices (measures actual quantity changes).

**Real Interest Rate**: Nominal interest rate minus inflation rate.

**Recession**: Period of declining economic activity; technically two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth or NBER determination based on multiple indicators.

**Regressive Tax**: Tax rate decreases as income increases (sales taxes disproportionately burden poor).

**Rent Control**: Price ceiling on rental housing.

**Representativeness Heuristic**: Judging probability based on similarity to stereotype, ignoring base rates.

**Reserve Ratio**: Percentage of deposits banks must hold as reserves.

**Ricardian Equivalence**: Theory that tax cuts don't stimulate economy because people save to pay future tax increases.

## S

**Satisficing**: Choosing first option meeting minimum criteria rather than searching exhaustively for optimal choice (Herbert Simon).

**Scarcity**: Limited resources relative to unlimited wants; fundamental economic problem.

**Seasonal Unemployment**: Unemployment from predictable seasonal patterns (agriculture, tourism, retail).

**Short Run**: Time period with at least one fixed input (fixed costs exist).

**Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)**: Upward sloping; higher prices lead to higher output due to sticky wages and prices.

**Shortage**: Quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied (price below equilibrium).

**Stagflation**: Simultaneous high unemployment and high inflation (1970s oil shocks).

**Status Quo Bias**: Preference for current state; tendency to stick with default options.

**Structural Unemployment**: Unemployment from skills mismatch or technological change (medium to long-term).

**Substitute Goods**: Goods that can replace each other; increase in price of one increases demand for other (Coke and Pepsi).

**Sunk Cost**: Cost already incurred that cannot be recovered; should be ignored in decision-making but often isn't.

**Supply**: Relationship between price and quantity producers willing and able to sell.

**Supply-Side Economics**: Policies focused on increasing aggregate supply through tax cuts, deregulation, and incentives for production.

**Surplus**: Quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded (price above equilibrium).

## T

**Tariff**: Tax on imported goods.

**Taylor Rule**: Formula for setting interest rates based on inflation gap and output gap.

**Time Inconsistency**: Preferences changing over time such that future self disagrees with current self's plans (present bias).

**Total Utility**: Overall satisfaction from consumption.

**Trade Balance**: Exports minus imports of goods (part of current account).

**Trade Deficit**: Imports exceed exports.

**Trade Surplus**: Exports exceed imports.

**Tragedy of the Commons**: Overuse of common resources because individuals don't bear full costs of their actions.

**Transaction Cost**: Cost of making economic exchange (search, bargaining, enforcement).

**Transfer Payment**: Government payment not in exchange for goods/services (Social Security, unemployment benefits).

## U

**Unemployment Rate**: (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100; percentage of labor force without jobs.

**Utility**: Satisfaction or happiness from consumption (ordinal, not cardinal).

## V

**Value-Added Tax (VAT)**: Tax on value added at each production stage (common in Europe).

**Variable Cost**: Cost that changes with production level (labor, materials).

**Velocity of Money**: Number of times money changes hands in a period (GDP / Money Supply).

## W

**Wealth Effect**: Changes in asset prices affect consumption (higher wealth → more consumption).

**Winner's Curse**: Winner of auction likely overpaid (bid too high) due to information asymmetry.

## Z

**Zero Lower Bound (ZLB)**: Constraint on conventional monetary policy when nominal interest rates at or near zero; people would hold cash rather than accept significantly negative rates.

## Key Formulas

**GDP (Expenditure Approach)**:
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
```

**Unemployment Rate**:
```
U = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100
```

**Inflation Rate (using CPI)**:
```
Inflation = [(CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}) / CPI_{t-1}] × 100
```

**Price Elasticity of Demand**:
```
PED = (% Δ Quantity Demanded) / (% Δ Price)
```

**Money Multiplier**:
```
Multiplier = 1 / Reserve Ratio
```

**Fiscal Multiplier**:
```
Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)
```

**Real GDP**:
```
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100
```

**Real Interest Rate**:
```
Real Rate = Nominal Rate - Inflation Rate
```

**Marginal Propensity to Consume**:
```
MPC = ΔC / ΔY
```

**Budget Balance**:
```
Balance = Tax Revenue - Government Spending
```

**Taylor Rule**:
```
Federal Funds Rate = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)
```

**Debt-to-GDP Ratio**:
```
Ratio = (Total National Debt / Annual GDP) × 100
```

## Economic Schools of Thought - Quick Reference

| School | Core Belief | Policy Prescription |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Classical** | Markets self-correct; supply creates demand | Minimal government intervention |
| **Keynesian** | Markets can fail; demand drives output | Active fiscal policy during recessions |
| **Monetarist** | Money supply drives inflation | Control money supply; limited intervention |
| **Austrian** | Markets discover information; intervention distorts | Minimal to no government role |
| **Behavioral** | People predictably irrational | Nudges and choice architecture |
| **Supply-Side** | Production incentives drive growth | Tax cuts, deregulation |
