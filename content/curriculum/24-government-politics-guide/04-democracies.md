# Democracies

## Overview

Democracy means "rule by the people." In practice, democracies vary enormously in how citizens participate, how elections work, and how power is distributed. This chapter examines types of democracy, electoral systems, and what makes democracies function effectively.

## Key Concepts

### What Makes a Democracy?

**Minimum requirements (Robert Dahl's "polyarchy"):**
1. Free, fair, frequent elections
2. Universal adult suffrage
3. Right to run for office
4. Freedom of expression
5. Alternative information sources
6. Freedom of association

**Liberal democracy adds:**
- Rule of law
- Protection of minority rights
- Independent judiciary
- Constitutional limits on power
- Civil liberties beyond voting

### Types of Democracy

#### Direct Democracy
- Citizens vote directly on policies
- No representatives as intermediaries
- Practical only at small scale or for specific issues

**Examples:**
- Ancient Athens (citizens assembly)
- Swiss referendums
- California ballot initiatives
- Town meetings (New England)

#### Representative Democracy
- Citizens elect representatives who make decisions
- Dominant form in modern world
- Representatives accountable through elections

**Variations:**
- **Trustee model:** Representatives use own judgment
- **Delegate model:** Representatives follow constituents' wishes
- **Politico model:** Mix of both depending on issue

#### Participatory Democracy
- Emphasizes broad citizen involvement beyond voting
- Community meetings, citizen juries, participatory budgeting

**Examples:** Porto Alegre (Brazil) participatory budgeting

#### Deliberative Democracy
- Emphasizes informed discussion before decisions
- Citizens' assemblies, deliberative polling

**Examples:** Ireland's Citizens' Assembly on abortion

### Electoral Systems

#### Plurality/Majoritarian Systems

**First-Past-the-Post (FPTP)**
- Whoever gets most votes wins (plurality)
- Single-member districts
- Simple, clear winner
- Tends toward two-party system (Duverger's Law)
- Can produce "wasted votes" and disproportionate results

**Used in:** USA, UK, Canada, India

**Two-Round System**
- If no majority, top two candidates face runoff
- Ensures winner has majority support

**Used in:** France (presidential), many Latin American countries

#### Proportional Representation (PR)

**Party-List PR**
- Multi-member districts
- Seats allocated proportionally to party vote share
- Closed list: party determines who fills seats
- Open list: voters can influence candidate selection
- Encourages multi-party systems

**Used in:** Israel, Netherlands, South Africa, much of Europe

**Mixed-Member Proportional (MMP)**
- Voters cast two votes: district candidate + party list
- District seats filled by FPTP
- List seats allocated to achieve overall proportionality

**Used in:** Germany, New Zealand

#### Other Systems

**Single Transferable Vote (STV)**
- Ranked-choice voting in multi-member districts
- Voters rank candidates; votes transfer based on preferences
- Achieves proportionality while allowing candidate choice

**Used in:** Ireland, Malta, Australian Senate

**Ranked-Choice/Instant Runoff (IRV)**
- Ranked preferences, single-member districts
- Eliminates candidates with fewest votes, redistributes preferences
- Ensures majority winner without separate runoff

**Used in:** Australia (House), some US cities (NYC, San Francisco)

### Comparing Electoral Systems

| System | District Size | Proportionality | Party System | Voter Choice |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|--------------|--------------|
| FPTP | Single | Low | Two-party | Candidate |
| Party-List PR | Multi | High | Multi-party | Party |
| MMP | Both | High | Multi-party | Both |
| STV | Multi | High | Multi-party | Candidate ranking |

### Measures of Democratic Quality

**Freedom House** rates countries on:
- Political rights (electoral process, participation, functioning government)
- Civil liberties (expression, association, rule of law, autonomy)
- Scale: Free, Partly Free, Not Free

**Democracy Index (Economist Intelligence Unit):**
- Electoral process and pluralism
- Functioning of government
- Political participation
- Political culture
- Civil liberties
- Categories: Full democracy, Flawed democracy, Hybrid regime, Authoritarian

### Challenges to Democracy

- **Democratic backsliding:** Erosion of democratic norms
- **Populist authoritarianism:** Elected leaders undermining institutions
- **Polarization:** Extreme partisan division
- **Disinformation:** False information undermining informed choice
- **Low participation:** Voter apathy, barriers to voting
- **Money in politics:** Wealthy interests dominating

## Real-World Examples

- **USA:** Presidential + FPTP → strong two-party system, frequent divided government
- **UK:** Parliamentary + FPTP → historically two-party, but regional parties complicate
- **Germany:** Parliamentary + MMP → multi-party coalition governments
- **Israel:** Parliamentary + PR → highly fragmented, frequent elections
- **Switzerland:** Frequent referendums, consensus government, high direct democracy

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Suffrage** | Right to vote |
| **Plurality** | Most votes (not necessarily majority) |
| **Proportional representation** | Seats match vote shares |
| **Gerrymandering** | Manipulating district boundaries for advantage |
| **Turnout** | Percentage of eligible voters who vote |
| **Duverger's Law** | FPTP tends to produce two-party systems |
| **Coalition government** | Multiple parties share power |

---

## Summary

Democracies share core features—free elections, civil liberties, rule of law—but differ greatly in structure. Electoral systems shape party systems: plurality voting tends toward two parties, while proportional representation enables multi-party competition. The quality of democracy depends not just on holding elections but on protecting rights, maintaining independent institutions, and enabling genuine citizen participation.
