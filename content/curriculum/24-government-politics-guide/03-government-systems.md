# Government Systems

## Overview

Government systems can be classified by how executive power is structured (presidential vs. parliamentary) and how power is distributed geographically (unitary vs. federal). These structural choices profoundly affect how decisions are made, accountability is maintained, and conflicts are resolved.

## Key Concepts

### Executive Systems

#### Presidential System

**Characteristics:**
- Separate election of executive (president) and legislature
- Fixed terms for both branches
- President is both head of state AND head of government
- Executive cannot be removed by legislature (except impeachment)
- Strict separation of powers

**Advantages:**
- Clear accountability (one leader)
- Stability (fixed terms)
- Direct popular mandate
- Checks and balances

**Disadvantages:**
- Potential gridlock between branches
- Winner-take-all can exclude minorities
- Difficult to remove failed leader
- Can trend toward presidentialism/authoritarianism

**Examples:** United States, Brazil, Mexico, Nigeria, Philippines

#### Parliamentary System

**Characteristics:**
- Executive (Prime Minister) emerges FROM legislature
- Head of government ≠ Head of state (often ceremonial monarch/president)
- Government can be removed by vote of no confidence
- Can call early elections
- Fusion of powers

**Advantages:**
- Unified government (executive has legislative majority)
- Flexibility (can change leadership during crises)
- Greater representation (coalition governments)
- Less gridlock

**Disadvantages:**
- Less stable (governments can fall)
- Prime Minister not directly elected
- Weaker separation of powers
- Coalition governments can be fragmented

**Examples:** United Kingdom, Canada, Germany, India, Japan, Australia

#### Semi-Presidential System

**Characteristics:**
- Directly elected president WITH significant powers
- Prime Minister responsible to legislature
- Power shared between president and PM
- President handles foreign policy/defense; PM handles domestic

**Variations:**
- **Premier-presidential:** PM and cabinet accountable only to legislature (France)
- **President-parliamentary:** President can dismiss PM (Russia, pre-2014 Ukraine)

**Examples:** France, Russia, South Korea, Taiwan, Poland

### Comparing Executive Systems

| Feature | Presidential | Parliamentary | Semi-Presidential |
|---------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Executive selection | Direct election | Legislature | Both |
| Fixed terms | Yes | No | President yes, PM no |
| Removal | Impeachment only | No confidence vote | Mixed |
| Head of state | President | Separate (monarch/president) | President |
| Typical result | Separation | Fusion | Shared power |

### Territorial Organization

#### Unitary System

**Characteristics:**
- Central government holds supreme authority
- Subnational units exist at central government's pleasure
- Uniform laws and policies nationwide
- Can delegate powers but can also reclaim them

**Advantages:**
- Efficient decision-making
- Uniform policy application
- Clear chain of command
- Less duplication

**Disadvantages:**
- Less responsive to local needs
- Can ignore regional diversity
- Power concentration risks

**Examples:** France, Japan, United Kingdom, China, most countries globally

#### Federal System

**Characteristics:**
- Constitutional division of power between central and regional governments
- Both levels have independent authority in their spheres
- Neither can abolish the other
- Usually requires supermajority to change division

**Advantages:**
- Accommodates diversity
- Laboratories of democracy (policy experimentation)
- Prevents concentration of power
- Local responsiveness

**Disadvantages:**
- Policy inconsistency across regions
- Duplication of services
- Coordination challenges
- Can entrench regional inequalities

**Examples:** United States, Germany, Australia, Canada, India, Brazil, Nigeria

#### Confederation

**Characteristics:**
- Loose alliance of sovereign states
- Central body has limited powers delegated by members
- Members can usually withdraw
- Rare in modern world

**Examples:** European Union (partial), historical US under Articles of Confederation, Switzerland (historically)

### Federal vs. Unitary Comparison

| Aspect | Unitary | Federal |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Sovereignty | Central only | Shared |
| Constitutional protection | Local powers not protected | Regional powers constitutionally guaranteed |
| Policy uniformity | High | Varies by region |
| Examples | France, Japan | USA, Germany |

## Real-World Examples

- **USA:** Presidential + Federal (strong separation, 50 states with significant powers)
- **UK:** Parliamentary + Unitary (Westminster model, devolution to Scotland/Wales/NI)
- **Germany:** Parliamentary + Federal (Chancellor, 16 Länder with real authority)
- **France:** Semi-presidential + Unitary (powerful president + PM, centralized)
- **China:** One-party + Unitary (highly centralized despite nominal regions)

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Separation of powers** | Executive, legislative, judicial branches independent |
| **Fusion of powers** | Executive emerges from and depends on legislature |
| **Vote of no confidence** | Legislature removes government |
| **Devolution** | Central government delegates powers to regions |
| **Federalism** | Constitutional power-sharing between levels |
| **Cohabitation** | President and PM from different parties (semi-presidential) |

---

## Summary

Presidential systems feature separately elected executives with fixed terms, while parliamentary systems fuse executive and legislative power through the prime minister. Semi-presidential systems combine elements of both. Geographically, unitary states concentrate power centrally, while federal states constitutionally divide it between national and regional governments. These structural choices shape political dynamics, accountability, and policy outcomes.
