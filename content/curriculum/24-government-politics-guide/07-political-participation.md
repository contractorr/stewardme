# Political Participation

## Overview

Political participation encompasses all the ways citizens engage with the political system—from voting to protesting to posting on social media. Healthy democracies depend on citizen participation, but patterns of participation vary widely based on institutions, culture, and individual resources. This chapter examines how people participate and what shapes their engagement.

## Key Concepts

### Forms of Participation

#### Conventional Participation
Activities within established institutional channels:

- **Voting** - Most common form; varies by country (35-95% turnout)
- **Campaign activities** - Donating, volunteering, attending rallies
- **Contacting officials** - Writing letters, emails, phone calls
- **Party membership** - Joining and being active in political parties
- **Community organizing** - Local civic engagement

#### Unconventional Participation
Activities outside normal channels:

- **Protests and demonstrations** - Marches, rallies
- **Civil disobedience** - Deliberate law-breaking for moral cause
- **Boycotts** - Refusing to buy products/services
- **Strikes** - Work stoppages
- **Online activism** - Hashtag campaigns, viral content
- **Violence** - Riots, terrorism (illegal and generally condemned)

### Voting Behavior

#### Why People Vote (or Don't)

**Rational choice model:**
- Benefits of voting vs. costs (time, effort)
- Paradox: rational to not vote (one vote rarely decisive)
- Yet millions vote → expressive benefits, civic duty

**Socioeconomic factors:**
- Higher education → higher turnout
- Higher income → higher turnout
- Age → turnout increases with age (peaks 60-70s)
- Race/ethnicity → gaps persist due to barriers

**Institutional factors:**
- **Registration requirements** - Automatic vs. opt-in registration
- **Voting laws** - ID requirements, early voting, mail voting
- **Election timing** - Weekday vs. weekend, holiday
- **Compulsory voting** - Mandatory participation (Australia, Belgium)

#### What Determines Vote Choice?

**Party identification:**
- Strongest predictor in most democracies
- Learned early, relatively stable
- Acts as "filter" for information

**Issues:**
- Position issues (voter agrees with candidate stance)
- Valence issues (everyone agrees on goal, debate is competence)
- "Pocketbook" vs. "sociotropic" economic voting

**Candidate characteristics:**
- Personality, appearance, competence
- More important in presidential systems

**Social cleavages:**
- Class, religion, ethnicity, urban/rural
- Different cleavages salient in different countries

### Political Parties

**Functions:**
1. **Aggregate interests** - Combine diverse demands into platforms
2. **Recruit candidates** - Identify and support office-seekers
3. **Structure choice** - Simplify options for voters
4. **Organize government** - Coordinate legislative action
5. **Mobilize voters** - Get supporters to polls

#### Party Systems

| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **One-party** | Single party dominates | China, Cuba |
| **Dominant party** | One party usually wins fair elections | Japan (LDP historically), Singapore |
| **Two-party** | Two major parties alternate | USA, UK (traditionally) |
| **Multi-party** | Several parties compete, coalitions form | Germany, Netherlands, Israel |

**Duverger's Law:** Electoral system shapes party system
- FPTP → two parties
- PR → multiple parties

#### Party Organization

- **Cadre parties** - Elite-driven, loose organization
- **Mass parties** - Large membership, strong organization
- **Catch-all parties** - Broad appeal, weaker ideology
- **Cartel parties** - State-subsidized, professional

### Interest Groups

Organizations seeking to influence policy without running candidates.

**Types:**
- **Economic groups** - Business associations, labor unions, professional groups
- **Public interest groups** - Environment, consumer protection, civil rights
- **Single-issue groups** - Focus on one cause (gun rights, abortion)
- **Government groups** - Intergovernmental lobbying

**Tactics:**
- **Lobbying** - Direct contact with officials
- **Litigation** - Using courts to advance goals
- **Grassroots mobilization** - Activating members
- **Public campaigns** - Media, advertising
- **Electoral activity** - Endorsements, donations (PACs)

**Pluralism vs. Elite Theory:**
- **Pluralists:** Many groups compete, power dispersed
- **Elite theorists:** Wealthy, organized interests dominate

### Media and Politics

#### Traditional Media Functions

1. **Information** - Report news
2. **Agenda setting** - Decide what's important
3. **Watchdog** - Investigate government
4. **Forum** - Platform for debate

#### Media Effects

- **Agenda setting** - Media doesn't tell you what to think, but what to think about
- **Priming** - Influences criteria used to evaluate politicians
- **Framing** - How issues are presented shapes interpretation

#### Social Media Transformation

**Opportunities:**
- Direct communication (bypass traditional media)
- Mobilization and organizing
- Citizen journalism
- Lower barriers to participation

**Challenges:**
- Filter bubbles and echo chambers
- Misinformation and disinformation
- Harassment and polarization
- Foreign interference

### Social Movements

Sustained collective action for social/political change.

**Stages:**
1. **Emergence** - Grievance identified
2. **Coalescence** - Organization forms
3. **Bureaucratization** - Formal structure develops
4. **Decline** - Success, failure, co-optation, or repression

**Factors for Success:**
- Political opportunity structure
- Resource mobilization
- Framing (resonant message)
- Leadership

**Examples:** Civil Rights Movement, Arab Spring, #MeToo, climate activism

## Real-World Examples

- **US voter turnout:** ~60% presidential, ~40% midterm; registration barriers significant
- **Australia:** Compulsory voting → 90%+ turnout
- **Nordic countries:** High participation, strong unions, multiple parties
- **India:** Massive electorate (900M+), strong party identification, caste as cleavage
- **Hong Kong protests (2019-20):** Social movement challenging authoritarian control

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Turnout** | Percentage of eligible voters who vote |
| **Party identification** | Psychological attachment to a party |
| **Interest group** | Organization influencing policy without seeking office |
| **Lobbying** | Attempting to influence officials directly |
| **Social movement** | Sustained collective action for change |
| **Agenda setting** | Media influence on what issues seem important |
| **Echo chamber** | Information environment reinforcing existing views |

## Summary

Political participation takes many forms beyond voting—campaigns, interest groups, protests, online activism. Voting behavior depends on party identification, issues, candidates, and institutional rules. Parties organize political competition; interest groups advocate for specific causes; media shapes what citizens know and think about. Healthy democracy requires not just elections but robust civil society and opportunities for citizen engagement.
