# Key Political Institutions

## Overview

Political institutions are the formal structures through which governments exercise power. The four main branches—legislature, executive, judiciary, and bureaucracy—perform distinct functions but interact constantly. How these institutions are designed and how they relate to each other shapes political outcomes.

## Key Concepts

### The Legislature

**Primary Functions:**
1. **Lawmaking** - Draft, debate, pass legislation
2. **Representation** - Speak for constituents
3. **Oversight** - Monitor executive branch
4. **Budget** - Approve government spending ("power of the purse")
5. **Legitimation** - Provide democratic approval for policies

#### Unicameral vs. Bicameral

**Unicameral (one chamber):**
- Simpler, faster decision-making
- Common in smaller/unitary states
- **Examples:** Sweden, New Zealand, Israel, Nebraska (US state)

**Bicameral (two chambers):**
- Upper house often represents regions or provides review
- Can slow legislation, require compromise
- **Examples:** US (Senate/House), UK (Lords/Commons), Germany (Bundesrat/Bundestag)

| Upper House Type | Selection | Role | Example |
|-----------------|-----------|------|---------|
| Federal | Represents states/regions | Protect regional interests | US Senate, German Bundesrat |
| Hereditary/Appointed | Appointed/inherited | Review, expertise | UK House of Lords |
| Directly elected | Popular vote | Co-equal legislating | Italian Senate |

#### Committee Systems

Committees do detailed legislative work:
- **Standing committees:** Permanent, by policy area
- **Select/special committees:** Temporary, specific investigations
- **Conference committees:** Reconcile differences between chambers

Strong committee systems (US Congress) give legislators more independent power; weak systems (UK) concentrate power in executive.

### The Executive

**Functions:**
1. **Policy implementation** - Execute laws
2. **Foreign policy** - Diplomacy, treaties, war
3. **Agenda setting** - Propose legislation
4. **Crisis management** - Emergency response
5. **Symbolic leadership** - Head of state role

#### Head of State vs. Head of Government

| Role | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Head of State** | Ceremonial, represents nation | Queen Elizabeth, German President |
| **Head of Government** | Runs government, makes policy | UK Prime Minister, German Chancellor |
| **Combined** | Both roles in one person | US President, French President (partial) |

#### Executive Power Variations

**Strong executives:**
- US president (separation of powers protects independence)
- French president (significant constitutional powers)

**Constrained executives:**
- UK prime minister (depends on parliamentary majority)
- German chancellor (coalition constraints)

**Collective executives:**
- Swiss Federal Council (7-member collective presidency)

### The Judiciary

**Functions:**
1. **Dispute resolution** - Settle legal conflicts
2. **Constitutional review** - Determine if laws are constitutional
3. **Rights protection** - Enforce individual rights
4. **Legal interpretation** - Clarify meaning of laws

#### Judicial Review

Power to strike down laws as unconstitutional.

**Strong judicial review:**
- US Supreme Court (established in Marbury v. Madison, 1803)
- German Constitutional Court

**Weak/No judicial review:**
- UK (parliamentary sovereignty; courts interpret but can't strike down)
- Netherlands (explicitly prohibited)

#### Judicial Independence

Essential for rule of law:
- **Tenure protection** (life terms, difficult removal)
- **Salary protection**
- **Appointment processes** (insulate from politics)

**Threats to independence:**
- Court packing
- Jurisdiction stripping
- Intimidation
- Political appointments

#### Legal Systems

| System | Basis | Role of Judges | Examples |
|--------|-------|----------------|----------|
| **Common law** | Precedent + statutes | Create law through decisions | UK, USA, Australia |
| **Civil law** | Comprehensive codes | Apply codes, less discretion | France, Germany, Japan |
| **Religious law** | Sacred texts | Interpret religious sources | Saudi Arabia, Iran |
| **Mixed** | Combination | Varies | India, South Africa |

### The Bureaucracy

**Functions:**
1. **Implementation** - Carry out policies
2. **Regulation** - Make and enforce rules
3. **Service delivery** - Provide public services
4. **Information** - Gather data, advise policymakers
5. **Adjudication** - Resolve disputes within agencies

#### Bureaucratic Models

**Weberian Ideal Type:**
- Hierarchical structure
- Merit-based recruitment
- Clear rules and procedures
- Specialization
- Political neutrality

**Principal-Agent Problems:**
- Politicians (principals) struggle to control bureaucrats (agents)
- Information asymmetry
- Goal divergence
- Bureaucratic drift

#### Controlling Bureaucracy

- **Political appointments** (US spoils system elements)
- **Legislative oversight** (hearings, budgets)
- **Administrative law** (procedures, judicial review)
- **Ombudsmen** (citizen complaints)
- **Sunset provisions** (agencies expire without renewal)

### Separation of Powers & Checks and Balances

**Montesquieu's Principle:**
Divide power among branches to prevent tyranny.

**US Example:**
| Branch | Checks on Others |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Executive** | Veto legislation, appoint judges, pardon power |
| **Legislative** | Override veto, confirm appointments, impeach, control budget |
| **Judicial** | Judicial review, lifetime tenure |

**Fusion vs. Separation:**
- **Separation** (US): Independent branches, potential gridlock
- **Fusion** (UK): Executive depends on legislative confidence, unified action

## Real-World Examples

- **US Congress:** Powerful committees, strong bicameralism, frequent gridlock with president
- **UK Parliament:** Executive dominates through party discipline; Lords mostly advisory
- **German Bundestag:** Proportional representation creates coalitions; Bundesrat represents states
- **Chinese NPC:** Rubber stamp for CCP decisions; real power in party structures

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Bicameralism** | Two legislative chambers |
| **Judicial review** | Courts can invalidate unconstitutional laws |
| **Bureaucracy** | Administrative agencies implementing policy |
| **Separation of powers** | Dividing government into independent branches |
| **Checks and balances** | Each branch can limit the others |
| **Parliamentary sovereignty** | Legislature supreme, courts cannot override |
| **Precedent** | Prior court decisions binding future cases (common law) |

---

## Summary

Political institutions structure how power is exercised. Legislatures make laws and oversee government; executives implement policy and lead; judiciaries interpret law and protect rights; bureaucracies deliver services and regulate. How these institutions are designed—their relative power, independence, and relationships—fundamentally shapes political outcomes and the quality of governance.
