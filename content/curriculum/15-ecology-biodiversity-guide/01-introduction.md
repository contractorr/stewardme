# Introduction to Ecology and Biodiversity

## Overview

Ecology studies the interactions between organisms and their environment, from individual organisms to entire biomes. Biodiversity - the variety of life on Earth - emerges from and maintains these ecological relationships. Understanding ecology is essential for managing ecosystems, conserving species, and predicting environmental change.

---

## What is Ecology?

**Ecology** = scientific study of interactions that determine distribution and abundance of organisms.

Key relationships:
1. Organism-environment interactions
2. Organism-organism interactions
3. Energy and nutrient flows through systems

### Levels of Ecological Organization

| Level | Focus | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Organism** | Individual physiology and behavior | How cacti tolerate desert conditions |
| **Population** | Single species in area | Gray wolf population dynamics in Yellowstone |
| **Community** | Multiple species interactions | Coral reef species interactions |
| **Ecosystem** | Communities + abiotic environment | Amazon rainforest energy and nutrient flows |
| **Biome** | Large regional ecosystems | Tropical rainforest biome |
| **Biosphere** | All Earth's ecosystems | Global carbon cycle |

---

## What is Biodiversity?

**Biodiversity** = variety of life at all levels of organization

### Three Levels of Biodiversity

**Genetic Diversity**
- Variation within species
- Example: Different drought-resistance genes in wheat varieties
- Critical for adaptation and resilience

**Species Diversity**
- Number and relative abundance of species
- Example: Tropical rainforests have ~50% of Earth's species on ~6% of land
- Measured by species richness and evenness

**Ecosystem Diversity**
- Variety of habitats and ecological processes
- Example: Coral reefs, mangroves, seagrass beds in coastal systems
- Provides different services and supports different species

## Why Ecology Matters

### Ecosystem Services

**Provisioning Services**
- Food, water, timber, fiber, fuel, medicines
- Value: Estimated $33+ trillion annually (global GDP ~$85 trillion)

**Regulating Services**
- Climate regulation, water purification, pollination, pest control
- Example: Bees provide $200+ billion in pollination services annually

**Supporting Services**
- Nutrient cycling, soil formation, primary production
- Foundation for all other services

**Cultural Services**
- Recreation, aesthetic, spiritual, educational values

### Conservation Challenges

Current biodiversity crisis driven by:
- **Habitat loss** - primary threat (agriculture, urbanization)
- **Overexploitation** - overfishing, hunting, harvesting
- **Invasive species** - disrupt native ecosystems
- **Pollution** - degrades habitats and harms organisms
- **Climate change** - shifting ranges and phenology

**6th Mass Extinction**: Current extinction rate 100-1000× background rate

## Historical Development

### Early Ecology

- **Alexander von Humboldt (1769-1859)** - Founded biogeography, recognized climate-vegetation relationships
- **Charles Darwin (1809-1882)** - Evolution by natural selection, ecological relationships
- **Ernst Haeckel (1866)** - Coined term "ecology" (Greek oikos = house)

### Foundation of Modern Ecology

**Arthur Tansley (1935)** - Introduced "ecosystem" concept

**Eugene Odum (1950s-1970s)** - Systematized ecosystem ecology, energy flow models

**Rachel Carson (1962)** - "Silent Spring" launched modern environmental movement

**Robert MacArthur & E.O. Wilson (1967)** - Theory of Island Biogeography

**Robert Paine (1969)** - Keystone species concept, trophic cascades

## Core Ecological Principles

### 1. Energy Flow

- Energy enters ecosystems via sunlight → photosynthesis
- Flows through food webs (producers → consumers → decomposers)
- Energy lost as heat at each transfer (~90% per trophic level)
- Energy flow is unidirectional (doesn't cycle)

### 2. Nutrient Cycling

- Nutrients (C, N, P, etc.) cycle through biotic and abiotic components
- Decomposition returns nutrients to soil/water
- Limiting nutrients control productivity
- Human activities alter global cycles (nitrogen, carbon)

### 3. Interdependence

- Species depend on each other (predation, competition, mutualism)
- Changes to one species affect others
- Ecosystem stability depends on biodiversity

### 4. Adaptation and Evolution

- Natural selection shapes organisms to local conditions
- Organisms modify their environments (niche construction)
- Coevolution drives reciprocal adaptations

---

## Key Concepts

### Niche

**Fundamental niche** - conditions where species *can* live
**Realized niche** - where species *actually* lives (limited by competition, predation)

### Carrying Capacity (K)

Maximum population size environment can sustain given resources

### Limiting Factors

Resources or conditions that restrict population growth or distribution
- Liebig's Law: Growth limited by scarcest resource
- Temperature, water, nutrients, space common limiting factors

### Succession

Directional change in community composition over time
- **Primary**: colonization of new substrate (lava flow, glacier retreat)
- **Secondary**: recovery after disturbance (fire, agriculture abandonment)

## Real-World Examples

### Yellowstone Wolf Reintroduction (Trophic Cascade)

1995: Wolves reintroduced after 70-year absence
- Wolves prey on elk → elk avoid river valleys
- Reduced browsing → willow and aspen regeneration
- Beaver populations recover (need willows for dams)
- River morphology changes → less erosion
- Songbird diversity increases in regenerated forests

Demonstrates top-down control and cascading effects through ecosystem

### Coral Reef Biodiversity

Coral reefs cover <0.1% of ocean but contain 25% of marine species
- High structural complexity creates niches
- Symbiosis (coral-zooxanthellae) enables high productivity in nutrient-poor waters
- Mutualisms (cleaner fish, anemone-clownfish)
- Threatened by warming (bleaching), acidification, overfishing

### Amazon Rainforest Hydrological Cycle

Trees recycle rainfall through transpiration
- 25-50% of rainfall from forest evapotranspiration
- Deforestation reduces rainfall → drier conditions → more fire risk
- Potential tipping point to savanna (positive feedback)

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Ecology** | Study of organism-environment and organism-organism interactions |
| **Biodiversity** | Variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels |
| **Ecosystem** | Community of organisms and their physical environment |
| **Ecosystem services** | Benefits humans obtain from ecosystems |
| **Niche** | An organism's role and resource use in its environment |
| **Trophic level** | Position in food chain (producer, primary consumer, etc.) |
| **Keystone species** | Species with disproportionate impact on ecosystem structure |

---

## Summary

- Ecology studies interactions determining organism distribution and abundance across organizational levels from organisms to biosphere
- Biodiversity encompasses genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity
- Ecosystems provide critical services worth trillions annually
- Energy flows unidirectionally through ecosystems; nutrients cycle
- Current biodiversity loss rates indicate 6th mass extinction
- Understanding ecological relationships essential for conservation and sustainable resource management
- Classic examples (Yellowstone wolves, coral reefs) demonstrate interconnectedness and cascading effects in ecosystems
