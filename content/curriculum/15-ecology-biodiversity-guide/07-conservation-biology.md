# Conservation Biology

## Overview

Conservation biology emerged in the 1980s as a crisis discipline - responding to unprecedented biodiversity loss. It integrates ecology, genetics, social sciences, and policy to prevent extinctions, protect ecosystems, and maintain evolutionary processes. Unlike pure science, conservation explicitly aims to preserve nature's diversity and ecological processes for future generations.

---

## The Biodiversity Crisis

### Current Extinction Rates

**Background Rate**: ~1 extinction per million species per year (E/MSY)
**Current Rate**: 100-1,000× background rate

**Evidence:**
- ~1% of bird species extinct since 1500
- ~2% of mammal species extinct since 1500
- ~40% of amphibians threatened
- ~25% of mammals threatened
- ~14% of birds threatened

**6th Mass Extinction**: Previous five caused by asteroids, volcanism, ice ages - this one caused by humans

### Extinction Vortex

**Small populations spiral toward extinction through reinforcing factors:**

1. **Demographic stochasticity** → Population decline
2. **Genetic deterioration** → Reduced fitness
3. **Reduced fitness** → Further population decline
4. **Environmental variation** hits harder when small
5. **Repeat** until extinction

**Example: Dusky Seaside Sparrow**
- Habitat loss → Small population (6 individuals by 1980)
- All males → No reproduction possible
- Last individual died 1987
- Could not escape vortex

## Threats to Biodiversity (HIPPO)

### H - Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

**Primary threat** to most species

**Mechanisms:**
- **Direct loss**: Conversion to agriculture, urban, infrastructure
- **Fragmentation**: Breaking continuous habitat into patches
- **Degradation**: Quality decline (pollution, invasives)

**Examples:**
- **Atlantic Forest (Brazil)**: 93% loss → ~7% remains in fragments
- **Tropical deforestation**: ~10 million hectares/year
- **Wetland loss**: 87% lost globally since 1700

**Fragmentation Effects:**
- Edge effects (altered microclimate, predation)
- Isolation (reduced gene flow, colonization)
- Reduced area (smaller populations, higher extinction)

### I - Invasive Species

**Second leading threat**

**Impacts:**
- **Competition**: Outcompete natives for resources
- **Predation**: Naïve prey vulnerable
- **Disease**: Introduced pathogens
- **Habitat modification**: Alter ecosystem structure
- **Hybridization**: Genetic swamping

**Examples:**
- **Brown tree snake (Guam)**: Eliminated 10+ native bird species
- **Burmese python (Everglades)**: 90%+ decline in mammals
- **Zebra mussels (Great Lakes)**: Outcompete native mussels, alter food webs
- **Chytrid fungus**: Amphibian extinctions worldwide (~200 species)

**Island Vulnerability:**
- Evolution without predators → Flightless birds, fearless behavior
- Introduced rats, cats, pigs devastating
- Example: 90%+ of Hawaiian bird extinctions from invasives

### P - Pollution

**Chemical Pollution:**
- **Pesticides**: DDT → Eggshell thinning in raptors
- **Heavy metals**: Mercury bioaccumulation, lead poisoning in condors
- **Endocrine disruptors**: Reproductive failures

**Nutrient Pollution:**
- **Eutrophication**: Algal blooms, hypoxia, fish kills
- **Gulf of Mexico dead zone**: 20,000 km²
- **Baltic Sea**: Widespread hypoxia

**Plastic Pollution:**
- **Entanglement**: Seals, turtles, seabirds
- **Ingestion**: Blocks digestive tract, toxins
- **Microplastics**: Ubiquitous, entering food webs

**Light/Noise Pollution:**
- **Light**: Disrupts migration, reproduction (sea turtles, birds)
- **Noise**: Interferes with communication, echolocation

### P - Population Growth (Human)

**Pressure Multiplier:**
- 8+ billion humans → Resource demand
- Consumption per capita rising
- Ecological footprint exceeds Earth's capacity

**Impacts:**
- Land conversion for agriculture, housing
- Water extraction
- Overharvesting
- Climate change (fossil fuel use)

### O - Overexploitation

**Unsustainable Harvesting:**

**Commercial Fishing:**
- **Grand Banks cod**: Collapsed 1992, hasn't recovered
- **Bluefin tuna**: 97% decline from overfishing
- **Bycatch**: 40% of catch discarded (sea turtles, dolphins, sharks)

**Bushmeat:**
- Tropical forests: Unsustainable hunting (gorillas, chimpanzees, elephants)
- Defaunation: Ecosystems without large animals

**Wildlife Trade:**
- **Legal**: ~$20 billion/year
- **Illegal**: ~$10 billion/year
- Parrots, reptiles, primates, orchids, cacti
- Often unsustainable, drives rarity

**Examples:**
- **Passenger pigeon**: Billions → Extinct (1914) from market hunting
- **Rhinoceros**: Poaching for horn (Traditional medicine, status symbols)

### Climate Change

**Rapid Changes Outpace Adaptation:**

**Direct Effects:**
- **Heat stress**: Coral bleaching, species die-offs
- **Phenology mismatches**: Bloom/migration timing disrupted
- **Range shifts**: Poleward, upslope movement

**Indirect Effects:**
- **Habitat loss**: Ice, alpine zones disappearing
- **Sea level rise**: Coastal habitat inundation
- **Ocean acidification**: Coral, shellfish impacts

**Committed Extinctions:**
- 15-37% of species by 2050 under mid-range scenarios
- Highland species "nowhere to go"

## Conservation Genetics

### Genetic Diversity Importance

**Why It Matters:**
- **Adaptation**: Genetic variation = raw material for evolution
- **Fitness**: Inbreeding reduces survival, reproduction
- **Disease resistance**: Diversity limits epidemic spread

### Inbreeding Depression

**Mechanism**: Mating among relatives → Homozygosity for deleterious alleles

**Effects:**
- Reduced survival, fertility
- Developmental abnormalities
- Increased disease susceptibility

**Examples:**
- **Florida panther**: ~20-30 individuals (1980s), genetic problems (kinked tails, heart defects, low sperm quality)
  - Solution: Introduced Texas cougars → Hybrid vigor
- **Cheetahs**: Very low diversity (past bottleneck) → Low reproductive success, disease vulnerability

### Minimum Viable Population (MVP)

**50/500 Rule (Frankel & Soulé):**
- **Ne = 50**: Prevent inbreeding short-term
- **Ne = 500**: Maintain evolutionary potential long-term
- (Ne = effective population size, typically <50% census size)

**Modern Estimates:**
- 50 too low; 500 may be insufficient
- 5,000+ for long-term viability in changing environments

### Genetic Rescue

**Strategy**: Introduce individuals from other populations to increase diversity

**Successful Examples:**
- **Florida panther**: Texas cougar introduction
- **Greater prairie chicken (Illinois)**: Minnesota birds introduced

**Risks:**
- **Outbreeding depression**: Disrupting local adaptations
- Must balance diversity gain vs. adaptation loss

## Protected Areas

### Coverage and Effectiveness

**Current Status:**
- ~17% of land protected (Aichi Target 17: at least 17% by 2020 - achieved)
- ~8% of ocean protected (Target: 10% - not achieved)

**But**: Quality varies enormously
- **Paper parks**: Exist legally but lack enforcement
- **Wrong locations**: Don't cover biodiversity priorities
- **Too small**: Insufficient for large species

### Reserve Design Principles

**Size:**
- Larger better (lower extinction, edge effects)
- Minimum sizes vary by taxon (birds: 100+ km², large mammals: 1,000+ km²)

**Shape:**
- Circular better (minimizes edge)
- Corridors connect fragments

**Location:**
- Cover biodiversity hotspots
- Represent ecosystem types
- Climate refugia, elevational gradients

**Buffer Zones:**
- Transition areas reduce edge impacts
- Sustainable use in buffers

### Types of Protected Areas (IUCN Categories)

**Category Ia/Ib**: Strict nature reserves, wilderness
**Category II**: National parks (ecosystem protection + recreation)
**Category III**: Natural monuments (specific features)
**Category IV**: Habitat/species management areas
**Category V**: Protected landscapes (sustainable use)
**Category VI**: Sustainable use areas

**Effectiveness:**
- Categories I-II most effective at preventing habitat loss
- Categories V-VI allow human use, less protection

## Conservation Strategies

### Species-Level Conservation

**Captive Breeding:**

**Successes:**
- **California condor**: 27 individuals (1987) → 500+ (captive + wild)
- **Arabian oryx**: Extinct in wild (1972) → Reintroduced → 1,000+ wild
- **Black-footed ferret**: 18 individuals (1987) → Breeding program → Reintroduced

**Challenges:**
- **Genetic adaptation to captivity**: Reduced fitness in wild
- **Cost**: Expensive (~$1 million/species/year)
- **Habitat must exist**: Pointless without wild habitat

**Best as Last Resort**: In-situ (wild) conservation preferred

### Habitat Restoration

**Goals:**
- Restore ecosystem structure, function, biodiversity
- Return degraded areas to functional state

**Examples:**
- **Wetland restoration**: Replant vegetation, restore hydrology → Bird/amphibian recovery
- **Stream restoration**: Remove dams, stabilize banks → Salmon return
- **Tallgrass prairie**: Reintroduce fire regime, native grasses → Bison, birds return

**Challenges:**
- May never fully recover to original state
- Novel ecosystems emerge (new species combinations)
- "Shifting baseline": Each generation accepts degraded conditions as normal

### Ecosystem-Based Conservation

**Ecosystem Services Approach:**
- Value nature for services (water purification, pollination, carbon storage)
- Economic arguments for conservation
- Example: New York City watershed protection cheaper than water treatment plant

**Landscape Conservation:**
- Protect entire landscapes, not just fragments
- Maintain connectivity, ecological processes
- Example: Yellowstone to Yukon corridor (Y2Y) - 3,200 km wildlife corridor

### Community-Based Conservation

**Rationale**: Local communities depend on resources, have knowledge, must benefit from conservation

**Approaches:**
- **Community reserves**: Local management
- **Payments for ecosystem services**: Compensate for conservation
- **Ecotourism**: Revenue from wildlife viewing

**Examples:**
- **Namibia community conservancies**: Wildlife populations recovered, communities benefit from tourism
- **Costa Rica PES**: Pays landowners for forest conservation

**Challenges:**
- Power imbalances
- Short-term needs vs. long-term conservation
- Ensuring equitable benefit distribution

## Prioritization and Triage

### Conservation Triage

**Concept**: Finite resources require prioritization

**Categories:**
1. **Will survive without intervention**: Low priority
2. **Can be saved with intervention**: High priority (focus here)
3. **Cannot be saved**: Low priority (controversial)

**Criticisms:**
- Ethical objections to "giving up"
- Uncertainty in predictions
- Value beyond survival probability

### Prioritization Frameworks

**EDGE (Evolutionarily Distinct, Globally Endangered):**
- Combines evolutionary uniqueness + threat
- Prioritizes: Pangolins, Aye-aye, Kakapo
- Rationale: Losing evolutionarily unique species = losing deep branches of tree of life

**Functional Importance:**
- Keystone species, ecosystem engineers
- Example: Beavers (create wetlands), elephants (maintain savanna)

**Flagship Species:**
- Charismatic megafauna attract funding
- Examples: Pandas, tigers, whales
- Umbrella effect: Protecting them protects many others

## Emerging Conservation Tools

### Genomics

**Applications:**
- **Monitor diversity**: Non-invasive (fecal, hair DNA)
- **Identify populations**: Guide translocation decisions
- **Detect hybridization**: Invasive × native crosses
- **Forensics**: Prosecute illegal wildlife trade

### Remote Sensing and Technology

**Satellite Monitoring:**
- Deforestation detection (near real-time)
- Fire tracking
- Coral bleaching

**Camera Traps:**
- Monitor elusive species (tigers, snow leopards)
- Population estimates

**Drones:**
- Survey inaccessible areas
- Anti-poaching patrols

**eDNA (Environmental DNA):**
- Detect species from water/soil samples
- Early detection of invasives

### De-Extinction?

**Techniques:**
- Cloning (frozen cells)
- Genome editing (CRISPR)
- Selective breeding (back-breeding)

**Candidates:**
- Passenger pigeon, woolly mammoth, Tasmanian tiger

**Concerns:**
- Enormous cost (opportunity cost: save existing species instead)
- Habitat often gone
- Ethical questions
- "Technological optimism" - false sense we can undo extinctions

## Success Stories

### Species Recoveries

**Bald Eagle (USA):**
- DDT → Population crash (400 pairs, 1960s)
- DDT banned, protection → 70,000+ individuals

**Humpback Whale:**
- Hunted to ~5,000 (1960s)
- Commercial whaling ban (1986) → 135,000+ (2023)

**Giant Panda:**
- Habitat loss → 1,100 individuals (1980s)
- Reserve expansion, breeding programs → 1,900+ (2016)
- Downlisted: Endangered → Vulnerable

### Ecosystem Recoveries

**Guanacaste (Costa Rica):**
- Degraded cattle pastures → Tropical dry forest restoration
- Fire management, seed dispersal → Forest regeneration

**Loess Plateau (China):**
- Severe erosion, poverty
- Terracing, reforestation, grazing bans → Erosion reduced 90%, incomes increased

## Future Challenges

### Climate Change Adaptation

**Strategies:**
- **Assisted migration**: Move species to suitable climates
- **Climate corridors**: Enable natural movement
- **Ex-situ conservation**: Seed banks, gene banks

**Uncertainties:**
- Where will species thrive in future climates?
- Unintended consequences of assisted migration?

### Novel Ecosystems

**Definition**: Species combinations with no historical analog

**Causes:**
- Invasive species established
- Climate change
- Land use change

**Question**: Do we manage for historical conditions or accept novel states?

### Reconciliation Ecology

**Concept**: Design human-dominated landscapes for biodiversity

**Approaches:**
- Wildlife-friendly farming (shade coffee, hedgerows)
- Green infrastructure in cities (green roofs, corridors)
- Working lands (forests, ranches) managed for conservation

**Necessity**: Protected areas alone insufficient (too small, too few)

## Summary

- Biodiversity crisis driven by extinction rates 100-1,000× background (6th mass extinction)
- HIPPO threats: Habitat loss (primary), invasives, pollution, human population, overexploitation, climate change
- Small populations face extinction vortex from genetic, demographic, environmental challenges
- Conservation genetics: Inbreeding depression reduces fitness; genetic rescue can help; MVP >500-5,000
- Protected areas cover ~17% land but quality variable; design principles emphasize size, connectivity, location
- Strategies include captive breeding (last resort), habitat restoration, ecosystem services, community-based conservation
- Prioritization necessary (finite resources); frameworks include triage, EDGE, functional importance
- Success stories (bald eagle, humpback whale, giant panda) show recovery possible with effort
- Emerging tools: genomics, remote sensing, eDNA monitoring
- Future challenges: climate adaptation, novel ecosystems, reconciliation ecology in human-dominated landscapes
