# Glossary of Ecological and Biodiversity Terms

## A

**Abiotic**: Non-living components of an ecosystem (water, temperature, nutrients, sunlight).

**Adaptation**: Trait that increases fitness in a particular environment, evolved through natural selection.

**Albedo**: Reflectivity of a surface; high albedo surfaces reflect more solar radiation.

**Allee Effect**: Positive relationship between population density and individual fitness at low densities; populations below threshold may spiral to extinction.

**Alpha Diversity (α)**: Species richness within a local habitat or community.

**Amensalism**: Interaction where one species is harmed while the other is unaffected (0/-).

**Aposematism**: Warning coloration signaling toxicity or danger to predators (e.g., poison dart frogs).

**Assimilation**: Absorption and incorporation of nutrients or energy by organisms.

**Autotroph**: Organism that produces organic compounds from inorganic materials (producers); includes photoautotrophs and chemoautotrophs.

## B

**Background Extinction Rate**: Natural rate of extinction (~1 extinction per million species per year).

**Batesian Mimicry**: Harmless species mimics harmful species to gain protection from predators.

**Beta Diversity (β)**: Species turnover or dissimilarity between different habitats or sites.

**Bioaccumulation**: Accumulation of toxins in organism tissues over lifetime.

**Biodiversity**: Variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels.

**Biodiversity Hotspot**: Region with ≥1,500 endemic plant species and ≥70% habitat loss; conservation priority.

**Biogeochemical Cycle**: Movement of chemical elements through biotic and abiotic components of ecosystems (e.g., carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus cycles).

**Biogeography**: Study of species distributions across space and time.

**Biomagnification**: Increasing concentration of toxins at higher trophic levels through food chain.

**Biomass**: Total mass of living organisms in a given area or trophic level.

**Biome**: Large regional ecosystem characterized by distinctive climate and organisms (e.g., tropical rainforest, tundra).

**Biosphere**: All of Earth's ecosystems; sum of all life and environments.

**Biotic**: Living components of an ecosystem (organisms and their products).

**Bottleneck Effect**: Genetic drift following severe population reduction; surviving individuals carry only subset of original genetic diversity.

**Bottom-Up Control**: Resource availability (nutrients, energy) regulates higher trophic levels.

## C

**Camouflage**: Coloration or form allowing organism to blend with environment (crypsis).

**Captive Breeding**: Breeding endangered species in controlled environments (zoos, facilities) for conservation.

**Carnivore**: Organism that eats other animals.

**Carrying Capacity (K)**: Maximum population size that environment can sustainably support given available resources.

**Character Displacement**: Evolution of differences between similar species in regions where they coexist, reducing competition.

**Chemoautotroph**: Organism using chemical energy (not sunlight) to produce organic compounds.

**Climax Community**: Stable, self-replacing community at end of succession (traditional concept; now questioned).

**Coevolution**: Reciprocal evolutionary changes in interacting species (e.g., predator-prey, mutualist partners).

**Commensalism**: Interaction where one species benefits while other is unaffected (+/0).

**Community**: All populations of different species living in same area and interacting.

**Community Assembly**: Processes determining which species occur together (environmental filtering, competition, dispersal).

**Competitive Exclusion Principle**: Two species with identical niches cannot coexist indefinitely; superior competitor excludes other (Gause's Principle).

**Conservation Biology**: Crisis discipline applying science to prevent extinctions and protect biodiversity.

**Convergent Evolution**: Independent evolution of similar traits in unrelated lineages due to similar selective pressures.

**Crypsis**: Camouflage; resembling background to avoid detection.

## D

**Decomposer**: Organism breaking down dead organic matter chemically (bacteria, fungi).

**Decomposition**: Breakdown of dead organic matter by decomposers and detritivores.

**Denitrification**: Bacterial conversion of nitrate (NO₃⁻) to nitrogen gas (N₂), returning nitrogen to atmosphere.

**Density Dependence**: Population regulation where effects change with population density (e.g., competition intensifies at high density).

**Density Independence**: Factors affecting populations regardless of density (e.g., weather events).

**Detritivore**: Organism consuming dead organic matter (detritus); earthworms, millipedes, vultures.

**Detritus**: Dead organic matter (leaves, feces, carcasses).

**Directional Selection**: Natural selection favoring one extreme phenotype, shifting population mean.

**Disruptive Selection**: Natural selection favoring both extremes over intermediate phenotypes.

**Disturbance**: Event disrupting ecosystem structure (fire, flood, windstorm, grazing).

## E

**Ecological Footprint**: Measure of human demand on Earth's ecosystems; area needed to support consumption and waste.

**Ecological Niche**: Organism's role in ecosystem, including resource use, habitat requirements, and interactions.

**Ecology**: Study of interactions between organisms and their environment, determining distribution and abundance.

**Ecosystem**: Community of organisms interacting with physical environment; biotic + abiotic components.

**Ecosystem Engineer**: Species that physically modifies environment, creating or maintaining habitats (beavers, corals, elephants).

**Ecosystem Services**: Benefits humans obtain from ecosystems (provisioning, regulating, supporting, cultural).

**Ecotone**: Transition zone between two ecosystems; often has edge effects.

**Ecotourism**: Tourism focused on experiencing natural areas, ideally supporting conservation and local communities.

**Edge Effect**: Altered conditions and species composition at boundary between habitat types.

**EDGE**: Evolutionarily Distinct, Globally Endangered - prioritization framework combining uniqueness and threat.

**Effective Population Size (Ne)**: Genetically effective breeding population size, typically smaller than census population.

**Endemic Species**: Species found only in specific geographic region and nowhere else.

**Energy Flow**: Movement of energy through ecosystem from producers through consumers; unidirectional (doesn't cycle).

**Eutrophication**: Excessive nutrient enrichment (N, P) causing algal blooms, oxygen depletion, ecosystem degradation.

**Exponential Growth**: J-shaped population growth without resource limits; dN/dt = rN.

**Extinction Debt**: Time-lagged extinctions resulting from past habitat loss; species persist temporarily before decline.

**Extinction Vortex**: Positive feedbacks (demographic, genetic, environmental) driving small populations toward extinction.

## F

**Facilitation**: Succession mechanism where early species improve conditions for later species.

**Fitness**: Reproductive success of an individual or genotype relative to others in population.

**Flagship Species**: Charismatic species used to attract conservation support (pandas, tigers); protecting them protects broader ecosystem.

**Food Chain**: Linear sequence of who-eats-whom in ecosystem.

**Food Web**: Complex network of feeding relationships in ecosystem; more realistic than simple chains.

**Founder Effect**: Genetic drift when small group colonizes new area, carrying only subset of genetic diversity.

**Fragmentation**: Breaking continuous habitat into smaller, isolated patches.

**Fundamental Niche**: Full range of conditions and resources where species could survive in absence of competition.

## G

**Gamma Diversity (γ)**: Total species richness across entire landscape or region.

**Gause's Principle**: See Competitive Exclusion Principle.

**Gene Flow**: Movement of alleles between populations through migration and interbreeding.

**Genetic Drift**: Random changes in allele frequencies in finite populations; stronger in small populations.

**Genetic Rescue**: Intentional introduction of individuals to increase genetic diversity in small, inbred populations.

**Gross Primary Production (GPP)**: Total energy captured by photosynthesis.

## H

**Habitat**: Physical environment where organism lives; includes abiotic factors and food sources.

**Habitat Heterogeneity**: Spatial variation in habitat types within area; often increases biodiversity.

**Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium**: Null model predicting stable allele frequencies without evolution; requires no selection, drift, gene flow, mutation, or non-random mating.

**Herbivore**: Organism that eats plants or algae.

**Heterotroph**: Organism obtaining energy from organic compounds produced by others (consumers).

**HIPPO**: Acronym for major biodiversity threats - Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Population (human), Overexploitation.

**Homology**: Similarity due to shared ancestry (homologous structures).

**Hypoxia**: Low oxygen conditions in water, typically harmful to aquatic organisms.

## I

**Inbreeding Depression**: Reduced fitness in offspring from mating between close relatives due to increased homozygosity for deleterious alleles.

**Inhibition**: Succession mechanism where early species prevent establishment of later species.

**Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis**: Diversity highest at intermediate disturbance levels; low disturbance → competitive exclusion, high disturbance → only tolerant species.

**Intraguild Predation**: Predation on species that are also competitors (predator and competitor combined).

**Intrinsic Rate of Increase (r)**: Maximum per capita population growth rate when resources unlimited.

**Invasive Species**: Non-native species causing ecological or economic harm in new environment.

**Island Biogeography**: Theory relating species richness to island area and isolation; immigration-extinction balance.

## K

**Keystone Species**: Species with disproportionately large effect on community structure relative to abundance; removal causes major changes.

**K-Selection**: Life history strategy favoring competitive ability in stable, crowded environments; few offspring, long life, high parental investment.

## L

**Landscape Ecology**: Study of spatial patterns and ecological processes across heterogeneous landscapes.

**Latitudinal Diversity Gradient**: Increase in species richness toward equator; one of ecology's most robust patterns.

**Liebig's Law of the Minimum**: Population growth limited by scarcest resource.

**Limiting Factor**: Resource or condition restricting population growth or distribution.

**Limiting Nutrient**: Nutrient in shortest supply relative to demand; often nitrogen (terrestrial) or phosphorus (freshwater).

**Logistic Growth**: S-shaped population growth approaching carrying capacity; dN/dt = rN(1-N/K).

**Lotka-Volterra Model**: Mathematical model of predator-prey population dynamics predicting cyclic oscillations.

## M

**Macroevolution**: Large-scale evolutionary changes over long timescales (millions of years); origin of major groups.

**Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY)**: Largest harvest rate sustainable indefinitely; theoretically at K/2.

**Mesopredator**: Mid-level predator; may increase when apex predators removed (mesopredator release).

**Metapopulation**: Set of discrete populations connected by dispersal; regional persistence despite local extinctions.

**Microevolution**: Small-scale evolutionary changes within populations over shorter timescales (generations).

**Minimum Viable Population (MVP)**: Smallest population size with specified probability of persistence for given timeframe.

**Müllerian Mimicry**: Two or more harmful species resemble each other, reinforcing learned avoidance by predators.

**Mutualism**: Interaction benefiting both species (+/+); pollination, mycorrhizae, gut symbionts.

**Mycorrhizae**: Symbiotic fungi-plant root associations; fungi provide nutrients, plant provides carbohydrates.

## N

**Natural Selection**: Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits; only mechanism consistently producing adaptation.

**Net Primary Production (NPP)**: GPP minus plant respiration; energy available to consumers.

**Niche**: See Ecological Niche.

**Niche Partitioning**: Division of resources among species to reduce competition; allows coexistence.

**Nitrogen Fixation**: Conversion of atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃) by bacteria; makes nitrogen available to organisms.

**Novel Ecosystem**: Community with species combinations lacking historical analog, often from invasives and climate change.

**Nutrient Cycling**: Circular movement of nutrients through biotic and abiotic ecosystem components.

## O

**Omnivore**: Organism feeding at multiple trophic levels (plants and animals).

**Overexploitation**: Harvesting species faster than they can reproduce; major biodiversity threat.

## P

**Parasitism**: Interaction where parasite benefits and host is harmed (+/-); often doesn't kill host immediately.

**Photoautotroph**: Organism using sunlight to produce organic compounds (plants, algae, cyanobacteria).

**Photosynthesis**: Conversion of light energy into chemical energy (glucose) by photoautotrophs; foundation of most food webs.

**Population**: Group of individuals of same species living in same area and interbreeding.

**Population Ecology**: Study of factors controlling population size, distribution, and temporal dynamics.

**Population Viability Analysis (PVA)**: Assessment of extinction probability for population given various scenarios.

**Predation**: Interaction where predator kills and eats prey (+/-).

**Primary Consumer**: Herbivore; organism feeding on primary producers (trophic level 2).

**Primary Production**: Creation of organic compounds from inorganic carbon; performed by autotrophs.

**Primary Succession**: Ecosystem development on substrate never before colonized (lava flow, glacier retreat).

**Producer**: Autotroph; organism creating organic compounds from inorganic materials (base of food web).

**Productivity**: Rate of biomass production; can refer to primary (plant) or secondary (consumer) productivity.

## R

**Realized Niche**: Actual conditions and resources used by species given competition and predation; subset of fundamental niche.

**Reconciliation Ecology**: Designing human-dominated landscapes to support biodiversity; complements protected areas.

**Redfield Ratio**: Typical C:N:P ratio in marine phytoplankton (106:16:1); used to predict nutrient limitation.

**Resilience**: Ability of ecosystem to return to original state after disturbance.

**Resistance**: Ability of ecosystem to withstand disturbance without changing.

**Restoration Ecology**: Science and practice of restoring degraded ecosystems to functional state.

**r-Selection**: Life history strategy favoring rapid population growth in unstable environments; many offspring, short life, early maturity, minimal parental care.

## S

**Secondary Consumer**: Carnivore feeding on herbivores (trophic level 3).

**Secondary Succession**: Ecosystem recovery after disturbance leaving soil intact (fire, agriculture abandonment).

**Sexual Selection**: Natural selection based on mating success rather than survival; drives evolution of ornaments, weapons.

**Shannon Diversity Index**: Diversity measure combining species richness and evenness; H' = -Σ(pi × ln pi).

**Species-Area Relationship**: Species richness increases with area; S = cA^z.

**Species Diversity**: Combination of species richness (number) and evenness (relative abundance).

**Species Evenness**: How evenly distributed abundance is among species.

**Species Richness**: Number of species in community or area.

**Stabilizing Selection**: Natural selection favoring intermediate phenotypes; reduces variation.

**Succession**: Directional change in community composition over time following disturbance or colonization.

**Sustainability**: Meeting current needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet their needs.

**Survivorship Curve**: Graph showing proportion of cohort surviving to each age; Types I, II, III reflect different mortality patterns.

## T

**Tertiary Consumer**: Carnivore feeding on other carnivores (trophic level 4).

**Tolerance**: Succession mechanism where later species tolerate conditions created by earlier species and outcompete them.

**Top-Down Control**: Predators regulate lower trophic levels; removal causes cascades.

**Top Predator**: Apex predator at top of food chain; often keystone species.

**Trophic Cascade**: Indirect effects propagating through food web when top predator abundance changes; classic example: sea otters-urchins-kelp.

**Trophic Downgrading**: Global loss of apex predators and resulting ecosystem changes.

**Trophic Efficiency**: Proportion of energy transferred between trophic levels; typically ~10%.

**Trophic Level**: Position in food chain; producers (1), herbivores (2), carnivores (3+).

**Trophic Pyramid**: Graphical representation of biomass or energy at each trophic level; typically pyramid-shaped (broad base, narrow top).

## U

**Upwelling**: Rising of nutrient-rich deep water to surface; supports high productivity in ocean.

## V

**Vestigial Structure**: Reduced, functionless remnant of structure that was functional in ancestors; evidence for evolution.

## W

**Wallace's Line**: Biogeographic boundary separating Asian and Australian fauna; defined by deep ocean channel preventing dispersal.

## Z

**Zooplankton**: Drifting or weakly swimming animals in aquatic systems; feed on phytoplankton.

## Units and Scales

**MYA**: Million years ago

**Hectare (ha)**: 10,000 m² (2.47 acres)

**km²**: Square kilometer (100 hectares)

**g/m²/yr**: Grams per square meter per year - productivity measure

**E/MSY**: Extinctions per million species per year

## Summary

This glossary covers fundamental terms in ecology and biodiversity from ecosystem processes (energy flow, nutrient cycling) through population and community ecology (competition, predation, succession) to conservation biology (extinction threats, genetic rescue, protected areas). Mastery of these terms enables understanding of ecological relationships, biodiversity patterns, and conservation challenges facing Earth's ecosystems.
