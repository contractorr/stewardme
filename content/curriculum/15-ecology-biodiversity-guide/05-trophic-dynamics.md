# Trophic Dynamics and Food Webs

## Overview

Trophic dynamics examines energy flow and feeding relationships in ecosystems. Food webs - networks of who-eats-whom - reveal how energy moves through communities and how effects cascade across trophic levels. Understanding these dynamics is critical for predicting ecosystem responses to species loss, invasions, and environmental change.

---

## Food Chains and Food Webs

### Food Chain Basics

**Simple Linear Sequence:**

Plants → Herbivore → Primary Carnivore → Secondary Carnivore

Example (Serengeti):
Grass → Wildebeest → Lion

**Trophic Levels:**
- **Level 1**: Primary producers (plants, algae)
- **Level 2**: Primary consumers (herbivores)
- **Level 3**: Secondary consumers (carnivores eating herbivores)
- **Level 4**: Tertiary consumers (top predators)
- **Decomposers**: All levels (feed on dead organic matter)

### Food Webs

**Reality**: Interconnected feeding relationships, not simple chains

**Characteristics:**
- Most consumers eat multiple prey species
- Most prey eaten by multiple predators
- Omnivory common (feeding at multiple trophic levels)
- Detrital pathways parallel living pathways

**Example - Kelp Forest Food Web:**
- Kelp → Sea urchins → Sea otters
- Kelp → Amphipods → Fish → Harbor seals
- Kelp detritus → Bacteria → Filter feeders → Fish
- Multiple pathways, interconnected

## Trophic Cascades

### Definition

**Trophic cascade** = indirect effects propagating down (or up) food chain when top predator abundance changes

**Top-Down Control**: Predators control herbivores → herbivores control plants
**Bottom-Up Control**: Nutrients control plants → plants control herbivores → herbivores control predators

### Classic Trophic Cascade: Sea Otters and Kelp Forests

**System:**
Sea otters → Sea urchins → Kelp

**Cascade:**
1. **Otters present**: Low urchin density → Kelp forests thrive → Diverse community
2. **Otters absent** (hunted for fur, 1800s-1900s): Urchin populations explode → Kelp grazed to rock → "Urchin barrens" (low diversity)
3. **Otters recover** (protected 1911): Kelp forests return

**Magnitude**: 100× difference in kelp biomass with vs. without otters

**Broader Impacts:**
- Kelp forests support fish, invertebrates, seabirds
- Provide storm protection, carbon sequestration
- Foundation for entire ecosystem

### Yellowstone Wolf Reintroduction

**System:**
Wolves → Elk → Willows/Aspen

**Cascade:**
1. **Wolves absent** (1926-1995): Elk populations high (~20,000) → Intensive browsing → Willow/aspen decline
2. **Wolves reintroduced** (1995): Elk populations decline and behavior changes → Reduced browsing
3. **Results**:
   - Willow regeneration in riparian zones
   - Beaver populations recover (need willows for food/dams)
   - Songbird diversity increases (nesting habitat)
   - River morphology changes (vegetation stabilizes banks)
   - Scavengers benefit (wolf-killed carcasses)

**Key Insight**: Effect via behavior change ("landscape of fear") as much as population reduction

### Pisaster Starfish Removal (Paine, 1969)

**Intertidal Keystone Predator Experiment**

**Setup:**
- Remove starfish (Pisaster ochraceus) from experimental plots
- Compare to control plots with starfish

**Results:**
- Without starfish: Mussels dominate → Exclude barnacles, algae, others
- With starfish: Mussels controlled → 15+ species coexist

**Cascade Mechanism:**
Starfish preferentially eat competitively dominant mussels → Prevent competitive exclusion → Maintain diversity

**Coined Term**: "Keystone species"

### Caribbean Coral Reefs

**System:**
Predatory fish → Sea urchins (Diadema) → Macroalgae → Corals

**Cascade:**
1. **Initial state**: Fish control urchins → Moderate grazing → Coral-dominated
2. **Overfishing** (1960s-1980s): Fewer fish → Urchins increase → Heavy grazing keeps algae low → Still coral-dominated
3. **Urchin die-off** (1983-1984, disease): No urchins, few fish → Algae explodes → Smothers coral → Regime shift
4. **Current**: Algae-dominated, degraded reefs

**Lesson**: Redundancy (fish + urchins both grazed algae) provided resilience; losing both caused collapse

## Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Control

### Top-Down (Predator) Control

**Characteristics:**
- Predators limit herbivore abundance
- Changes at top propagate down
- Even-numbered chains: Predators → Low herbivores → High plants
- Odd-numbered chains: Predators → Low carnivores → High herbivores → Low plants

**When Dominant:**
- Productive systems (abundant energy)
- Aquatic systems (often strong predation)

### Bottom-Up (Resource) Control

**Characteristics:**
- Nutrient/energy availability controls producers
- Plant abundance limits herbivores
- Herbivore abundance limits predators

**When Dominant:**
- Unproductive systems (nutrient-limited)
- Systems with weak predation pressure

### Reality: Both Operate

**Examples of Interaction:**

**Serengeti Ecosystem**
- Bottom-up: Rainfall → Grass growth → Wildebeest population
- Top-down: Lions → Regulate wildebeest (but weakly)
- Primary control: Bottom-up (rainfall drives system)

**Lake Ecosystems**
- Bottom-up: Nutrients → Phytoplankton
- Top-down: Fish → Zooplankton → Phytoplankton
- Biomanipulation: Remove planktivorous fish → Zooplankton increase → Phytoplankton decrease → Clearer water

## Food Web Structure and Stability

### Complexity-Stability Debate

**Early Theory (MacArthur, 1955):**
- More complex webs (more connections) = more stable
- Multiple pathways buffer against disturbance

**Mathematical Models (May, 1972):**
- Complex webs less stable in models
- More connections → more ways to destabilize

**Resolution:**
- Depends on connection type (weak vs. strong)
- Real webs have specific structures (not random)
- Weak interactions and modularity enhance stability

### Weak vs. Strong Interactions

**Strong Interactions:**
- Large effects on partner species
- Example: Keystone predation

**Weak Interactions:**
- Small effects but numerically common
- May stabilize systems by dampening oscillations
- Provide flexibility when strong interactions disrupted

### Food Web Modules

**Common Patterns:**

**Apparent Competition**
- Two prey share predator
- One prey's increase → More predators → Suppresses other prey

**Intraguild Predation**
- Predator and competitor combined
- Example: Wolves eat coyotes (competitors) and deer (shared prey)

**Trophic Omnivory**
- Feed at multiple trophic levels
- Example: Bears eat berries (producer) and fish (consumer)
- Can stabilize or destabilize depending on strength

## Energy Flow Efficiency

### Trophic Transfer Efficiency

**10% Rule (Lindeman, 1942):**
~10% of energy transferred to next trophic level

**Energy Losses:**
- **Not consumed**: Some biomass not eaten (bones, woody tissue)
- **Not assimilated**: Energy in feces
- **Respiration**: Heat loss from metabolism

**Calculation:**
Production at level n / Production at level n-1

**Typical Range:** 5-20% (aquatic systems often higher)

### Implications for Food Chain Length

**Why chains rarely exceed 4-5 levels:**

1. **Energetic Constraints**
   - Insufficient energy at higher levels
   - Can't support viable populations

2. **Dynamic Constraints**
   - Longer chains more prone to oscillations
   - Extinction risk at top

3. **Spatial/Size Constraints**
   - Top predators need large territories
   - Limited by ecosystem area

**Exceptions:**
- Aquatic systems can have longer chains (higher efficiency)
- Systems with subsidies (nutrients from outside) support more levels

## Detrital Food Webs

### Importance of Detritus

**Detritus** = Dead organic matter (leaves, feces, carcasses)

**Scale:**
- Terrestrial: 80-90% of NPP enters detrital pathway
- Aquatic: Varies widely (20-90%)

**Detrital Food Chain:**
Dead organic matter → Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) → Detritivores (earthworms, springtails) → Predators

### Decomposer Dynamics

**Microbial Loop (Aquatic)**
- Phytoplankton release dissolved organic carbon (DOC)
- Bacteria consume DOC
- Flagellates eat bacteria
- Ciliates eat flagellates
- Zooplankton eat ciliates
- Recycles nutrients back to phytoplankton

**Terrestrial Decomposition**
- Leaf litter → Fungal/bacterial breakdown
- Earthworms, millipedes, isopods fragment material
- Increases surface area for microbial action
- Nutrients released for plant uptake

### Linking Living and Dead Pathways

**Salmon Runs**
- Marine nutrients → Salmon → Bears catch salmon → Carcasses in forest
- Marine-derived nitrogen in trees 50+ meters from stream
- Links ocean productivity to terrestrial food webs

**Whale Falls**
- Dead whales sink to deep ocean
- Support specialized communities for decades
- 30-50 tons of carbon → Deep-sea food web

## Human Impacts on Trophic Dynamics

### Trophic Downgrading

**Definition**: Loss of apex predators from ecosystems globally

**Consequences:**
- Mesopredator release (mid-level predators increase)
- Herbivore population increases
- Vegetation changes
- Biodiversity loss

**Examples:**
- Dingoes removed in Australia → Fox/cat increase → Native mammal decline
- Sharks removed from reefs → Mesopredator fish increase → Herbivore decline → Algae increase

### Fishing Down the Food Web

**Pattern:**
1. Initially harvest large, high trophic level fish (tuna, swordfish, cod)
2. As depleted, shift to smaller species at lower trophic levels
3. Eventually harvest invertebrates (shrimp, jellyfish)

**Consequences:**
- Ecosystem restructuring
- Lower trophic level biomass increases (forage fish)
- But: Total productivity may decline, quality degrades
- Trophic cascades in marine systems

**Example: Northwest Atlantic**
- Cod collapse (1990s) → Shrimp, crab fisheries expand
- Ecosystem shifted to invertebrate-dominated

### Eutrophication Effects

**Bottom-Up Cascade:**
1. Excess nutrients added → Phytoplankton bloom
2. Herbivore zooplankton can't consume all → Algae sinks
3. Decomposition consumes oxygen → Hypoxia
4. Fish kills → Restructured food web

**Top-Down + Bottom-Up:**
- Remove predatory fish (fishing) + Add nutrients
- Planktivorous fish dominate → Overgraze large zooplankton
- Small zooplankton ineffective grazers → Algal blooms worsen
- "Biomanipulation": Restore predatory fish to control planktivores

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Trophic cascade** | Indirect effects propagating through food chain |
| **Top-down control** | Predators regulate lower trophic levels |
| **Bottom-up control** | Resources regulate higher trophic levels |
| **Keystone species** | Species with disproportionate ecosystem impact |
| **Trophic efficiency** | Energy transfer between trophic levels (~10%) |
| **Detrital pathway** | Food chain based on dead organic matter |
| **Trophic downgrading** | Global loss of apex predators |

---

## Summary

- Food webs are complex networks of feeding relationships with energy flowing from producers through multiple pathways
- Trophic cascades occur when predator changes propagate down food chains (sea otters-urchins-kelp, wolves-elk-willows)
- Keystone predators (Pisaster starfish) maintain diversity by preventing competitive exclusion
- Ecosystems regulated by top-down (predator) and bottom-up (resource) control; relative importance varies
- Energy transfer between trophic levels ~10% efficient, limiting food chain length to 4-5 levels
- Detrital pathways process most terrestrial NPP and link ecosystems (salmon runs, whale falls)
- Human impacts include trophic downgrading (predator loss), fishing down food webs, and eutrophication disrupting cascades
- Understanding trophic dynamics essential for conservation (maintain predators), fisheries management (protect top predators), and ecosystem restoration
