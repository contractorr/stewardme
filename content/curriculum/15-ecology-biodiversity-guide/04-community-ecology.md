# Community Ecology

## Overview

Community ecology examines interactions among species living in the same area. These interactions - competition, predation, mutualism, and others - determine community structure (species composition and abundance) and drive succession, biodiversity patterns, and ecosystem stability.

---

## Species Interactions

### Classification of Interactions

| Interaction | Species A | Species B | Example |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Competition** | - | - | Lions and hyenas compete for prey |
| **Predation** | + | - | Wolf eats deer |
| **Herbivory** | + | - | Caterpillar eats leaves |
| **Parasitism** | + | - | Tick feeds on dog blood |
| **Mutualism** | + | + | Bees pollinate flowers, get nectar |
| **Commensalism** | + | 0 | Remora fish attach to sharks |
| **Amensalism** | 0 | - | Trees shade understory plants |

*+= benefits, -= harmed, 0 = no effect*

## Competition

### Competitive Exclusion Principle

**Gause's Principle**: Two species with identical niches cannot coexist indefinitely; the superior competitor excludes the other.

**Classic Experiment (Gause, 1934):**
- Two Paramecium species in lab culture
- Separate: Both thrive
- Together: P. aurelia excludes P. caudatum
- Reason: P. aurelia uses resources more efficiently

### Niche Partitioning

**Resource Partitioning** = species divide resources to reduce competition

**Examples:**

**Darwin's Finches**
- Different beak sizes/shapes → different food sources
- Medium ground finch: Medium seeds
- Large ground finch: Large, hard seeds
- Warbler finch: Insects

**Warblers in Spruce Trees (MacArthur, 1958)**
- Five warbler species coexist in same trees
- Each forages in different zone (top, middle, bottom, inner, outer)
- Reduces competition despite using same tree species

**Anole Lizards in Caribbean**
- Multiple species on same island
- Partitioned by perch height and diameter
- Different leg lengths adapted to different perches

### Character Displacement

**Definition**: Evolution of differences between similar species in areas where they overlap

**Evidence:**
- Beak sizes in finches differ more where species overlap (sympatric) than where separate (allopatric)
- Reduces competition in overlap zone

## Predation

### Predator-Prey Dynamics

**Effects on Prey:**
- Direct: Mortality from consumption
- Indirect: Behavior changes (foraging less in dangerous areas)

**Effects on Predators:**
- Prey abundance determines predator population size
- Specialization vs. generalization trade-off

### Predator-Prey Coevolution

**Prey Defenses:**

**Chemical Defenses**
- Toxins: Poison dart frogs, milkweed plants
- Noxious compounds: Skunks, bombardier beetles
- Often coupled with warning coloration (aposematism)

**Physical Defenses**
- Armor: Turtle shells, porcupine quills
- Spines: Cacti, sea urchins
- Size: Elephants too large for most predators

**Behavioral Defenses**
- Group living: Dilution effect, many eyes for vigilance
- Alarm calls: Prairie dog sentinels
- Mobbing: Small birds harass raptors

**Camouflage**
- Crypsis: Match background (stick insects, flounder)
- Mimicry:
  - Batesian: Harmless mimics harmful (viceroy butterfly mimics toxic monarch)
  - Müllerian: Two harmful species mimic each other (wasps and bees)

**Predator Adaptations:**
- Enhanced senses (bat echolocation, shark electroreception)
- Speed and agility (cheetah, peregrine falcon)
- Ambush tactics (trapdoor spiders, anglerfish)
- Cooperation (wolf packs, lion prides)

### Herbivory Specifics

**Plant Defenses:**
- **Constitutive**: Always present (thorns, tannins)
- **Induced**: Produced after damage (nicotine in tobacco, protease inhibitors)

**Tolerance vs. Resistance:**
- Tolerance: Regrow quickly after herbivory
- Resistance: Prevent herbivory

**Evolutionary Arms Race:**
- Plants evolve toxins → herbivores evolve detoxification
- Example: Monarch caterpillars sequester milkweed toxins, become toxic themselves

## Mutualism

### Types of Mutualisms

**Nutritional Mutualisms**

**Mycorrhizae**: Fungi-plant root associations
- Fungi provide phosphorus, nitrogen → plant
- Plant provides carbohydrates → fungi
- ~90% of plant species have mycorrhizae
- Critical in low-nutrient soils

**Nitrogen-Fixing Bacteria**: Rhizobium in legume root nodules
- Bacteria convert N₂ → NH₃
- Plant provides habitat, energy
- Allows legumes to thrive in N-poor soils

**Gut Microbiomes**
- Termites: Protists digest cellulose
- Ruminants: Bacteria ferment plant material
- Humans: Gut bacteria provide vitamins, digest fiber

**Defensive Mutualisms**

**Ants and Acacia Trees**
- Acacias provide hollow thorns (shelter) and nectar
- Ants defend against herbivores and competing plants

**Cleaner Fish**
- Remove parasites from large fish
- Predators don't eat cleaners (recognition)

**Pollination Mutualisms**

**Examples:**
- Bees-flowers: Pollen transfer for nectar
- Yucca-yucca moth: Moth pollinates yucca, lays eggs in flower; larvae eat some seeds
- Fig-fig wasp: Highly specialized (one wasp species per fig species)

**Seed Dispersal Mutualisms**
- Animals eat fruit, disperse seeds in feces
- Plants provide nutrition, animals spread offspring
- Examples: Birds-berries, bats-tropical fruits

### Exploitation vs. Cooperation

Mutualisms can become parasitic if one partner cheats:
- Nectar robbers: Cut flower base, take nectar without pollinating
- Cheater yucca moths: Lay eggs without pollinating

Stability requires:
- Partner choice (plants allocate resources to helpful symbionts)
- Sanctions (legumes reduce nodules for ineffective rhizobia)
- Co-dispersal (vertical transmission of symbionts ensures aligned interests)

## Succession

### Primary Succession

**Definition**: Development of ecosystem on substrate never before colonized (e.g., new volcanic island, glacial retreat, lava flow)

**Stages:**

1. **Pioneer Species**: Lichens, mosses
   - Tolerate harsh conditions
   - Weather rock → create thin soil

2. **Early Colonizers**: Grasses, small herbs
   - Improve soil (organic matter, nitrogen fixation)
   - Modify microclimate

3. **Intermediate Species**: Shrubs, fast-growing trees
   - Shade out pioneers
   - Further soil development

4. **Climax Community**: Shade-tolerant trees
   - Self-replacing community
   - Stable until major disturbance

**Example: Glacier Bay, Alaska**
- Glacier retreat exposes bare rock
- Pioneer stage (0-10 years): Mosses, Dryas (N-fixer)
- Alder thicket (10-60 years): N-fixing shrubs
- Spruce forest (60-200 years): Shades out alder
- Hemlock replaces spruce (200+ years)

### Secondary Succession

**Definition**: Recovery after disturbance that leaves soil intact (fire, agriculture abandonment, logging)

**Faster than primary** because:
- Soil already present
- Seed bank remains
- Root systems may survive

**Example: New England Field Abandonment**
- Year 1-3: Annual weeds (crabgrass)
- Year 3-20: Perennial grasses, herbaceous plants
- Year 20-100: Pine forest
- Year 100+: Oak-hickory forest (climax)

### Mechanisms of Succession

**Facilitation**
- Early species improve conditions for later species
- Example: N-fixers enrich soil for non-fixers

**Tolerance**
- Later species tolerate conditions early species created
- Later species outcompete, not facilitated

**Inhibition**
- Early species prevent establishment of later species
- Later species establish only after disturbance/death of early species
- Example: Inhibitory chemicals (allelopathy)

### Disturbance and Succession

**Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis**
- Diversity highest at intermediate disturbance frequency/intensity
- Low disturbance: Competitive exclusion → low diversity
- High disturbance: Only disturbance-tolerant species → low diversity
- Intermediate: Prevents exclusion, allows diverse species → high diversity

## Biodiversity Patterns

### Species Diversity Measures

**Species Richness**: Number of species present

**Species Evenness**: How evenly distributed abundance is

**Shannon Diversity Index**: Combines richness and evenness
- H' = -Σ(pi × ln pi)
- Higher values = more diverse

**Example:**
- Community A: 10 species, equal abundance → High diversity
- Community B: 10 species, one dominates 95% → Lower diversity

### Factors Influencing Diversity

**Productivity**
- Often positive relationship (more energy → more species)
- But can be unimodal (very high productivity → dominance by few species)

**Spatial Heterogeneity**
- More habitat types → more niches → more species
- Example: Coral reefs (high structural complexity → high diversity)

**Predation/Disturbance**
- Can increase diversity by preventing competitive exclusion
- Example: Pisaster starfish removal → mussel dominance → lower diversity

**Time**
- Older communities accumulate more species (if undisturbed)

**Area**
- Larger areas support more species (species-area relationship)

## Community Assembly

### Deterministic vs. Stochastic Processes

**Deterministic (Niche-Based)**
- Environmental filtering: Only species adapted to conditions can persist
- Competitive interactions: Superior competitors exclude others

**Stochastic (Neutral)**
- Random colonization events
- Ecological drift: Random fluctuations in population sizes
- Priority effects: First arrival affects later colonization

**Reality**: Both processes operate; relative importance varies

### Keystone Species

**Definition**: Species with disproportionately large effect on community structure relative to abundance

**Examples:**

**Sea Otters** (California kelp forests)
- Eat sea urchins
- Without otters: Urchin populations explode → overgraze kelp → urchin barrens
- With otters: Urchins controlled → kelp forest thrives → diverse community

**Pisaster Starfish** (Intertidal)
- Paine's (1969) classic experiment
- Starfish removed → mussels dominate → exclude other species
- Starfish present → mussels controlled → high diversity

**African Elephants**
- Knock down trees → maintain savanna-woodland mosaic
- Removal → woodland encroachment → grassland species decline

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Competitive exclusion** | Two species with identical niches cannot coexist |
| **Niche partitioning** | Species divide resources to reduce competition |
| **Mutualism** | Interaction benefiting both species |
| **Succession** | Directional change in community composition over time |
| **Climax community** | Stable, self-replacing community (traditional concept) |
| **Keystone species** | Species with disproportionate impact on community |
| **Species diversity** | Combination of species richness and evenness |

---

## Summary

- Species interactions (competition, predation, mutualism) structure communities
- Competitive exclusion drives niche partitioning; similar species coexist by dividing resources
- Predator-prey coevolution produces diverse defenses (chemical, physical, behavioral) and counter-adaptations
- Mutualisms (mycorrhizae, pollination, gut microbes) are widespread and critical to ecosystem function
- Succession proceeds from pioneers to climax communities via facilitation, tolerance, or inhibition
- Intermediate disturbance maintains highest diversity by preventing competitive exclusion
- Keystone species (sea otters, starfish) have disproportionate effects on community structure
- Biodiversity patterns reflect productivity, heterogeneity, predation, time, and area
