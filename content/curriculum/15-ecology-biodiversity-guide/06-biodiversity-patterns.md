# Biodiversity Patterns and Biogeography

## Overview

Biodiversity is not evenly distributed across Earth. Understanding why some regions harbor thousands of species while others have few reveals fundamental principles governing life's distribution. Biogeography - the study of species distributions across space and time - integrates ecology, evolution, geology, and climate to explain these patterns.

---

## Global Biodiversity Patterns

### Latitudinal Diversity Gradient

**Pattern**: Species richness generally increases toward equator

**Magnitude:**
- Tropical rainforests: ~200+ tree species per hectare
- Temperate forests: 10-30 tree species per hectare
- Boreal forests: 1-5 tree species per hectare

**Holds across many taxa:**
- Plants, insects, birds, mammals, marine organisms
- One of ecology's most consistent patterns

### Hypotheses for Latitudinal Gradient

**1. Climate/Energy Hypothesis**
- More solar energy at tropics → Higher productivity → More individuals → More species
- Longer growing season → Year-round resources

**2. Evolutionary Time Hypothesis**
- Tropics climatically stable longer → More time for speciation
- Temperate regions glaciated recently → Post-glacial recolonization

**3. Area Hypothesis**
- More tropical land area → Larger populations → Lower extinction
- (Weak support: doesn't explain marine patterns)

**4. Niche Specialization Hypothesis**
- Stable climate → Narrower niches → More species can pack in
- Example: Tropical herbivorous insects often specialist on one plant species

**5. Biotic Interactions Hypothesis**
- Stronger predation, competition, parasitism in tropics
- Drives finer niche partitioning

**Likely**: Multiple mechanisms operate simultaneously

## Biodiversity Hotspots

### Definition

**Conservation International criteria:**
1. ≥1,500 endemic plant species (found nowhere else)
2. ≥70% of original habitat lost

### Major Hotspots

| Hotspot | Location | Endemics | Key Taxa |
|---------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Tropical Andes** | South America | ~45,000 plants | Orchids, hummingbirds |
| **Sundaland** | SE Asia | ~25,000 plants | Orangutans, tigers |
| **Madagascar** | Island | ~12,000 plants | Lemurs (100% endemic) |
| **Atlantic Forest** | Brazil | ~20,000 plants | Golden lion tamarin |
| **Cape Floristic** | South Africa | ~9,000 plants | Proteas |
| **Caribbean** | Islands | ~12,000 plants | Anoles, solenodon |

### Why Hotspots Matter

**Conservation Priority:**
- 36 hotspots cover ~2.5% of Earth's land
- Contain >50% of plant species
- Contain >42% of terrestrial vertebrates
- Under severe threat

**Example: Madagascar**
- Separated from Africa ~160 MYA, India ~90 MYA
- Isolation → Unique evolution
- Lemurs: 100+ species, all endemic
- 90% of original forest lost
- Urgent conservation target

## Island Biogeography

### Theory of Island Biogeography (MacArthur & Wilson, 1967)

**Core Idea**: Island species richness determined by balance between immigration and extinction

**Predictions:**

1. **Area Effect**: Larger islands have more species
   - Larger populations → Lower extinction rates
   - More habitats → More niches

2. **Distance Effect**: Islands closer to mainland have more species
   - Higher immigration rates
   - Genetic rescue from mainland

**Equilibrium Model:**
- Immigration rate decreases as species accumulate (fewer new arrivals)
- Extinction rate increases as species accumulate (smaller populations per species)
- Equilibrium where rates balance

### Evidence

**Krakatau Volcanic Eruption (1883):**
- Island sterilized by eruption
- Recolonization monitored for century
- Species accumulated, approached equilibrium
- Supported immigration-extinction balance

**Species-Area Relationship:**
S = cA^z

Where:
- S = species number
- A = area
- c, z = constants (z typically 0.15-0.35)

**Holds remarkably well:**
- Islands worldwide
- Habitat patches (mountaintops, forest fragments)

### Applications

**Habitat Fragmentation:**
- Forest fragments = habitat islands
- Small fragments lose species over time ("extinction debt")
- Isolation reduces recolonization

**Reserve Design:**
- Larger reserves better than small
- Connected reserves better than isolated
- SLOSS debate: Single Large Or Several Small?
  - Often: One large better (fewer extinctions)
  - But: Multiple reserves reduce catastrophic loss risk

## Continental Biogeographic Patterns

### Wallace's Line

**Alfred Russel Wallace (1859):**
- Deep ocean channel separates Asian and Australian fauna
- Despite close proximity, distinct assemblages
- Asian side: Tigers, rhinos, monkeys
- Australian side: Marsupials, cockatoos, unique reptiles

**Explanation:**
- Never connected by land bridge (deep water even during ice ages)
- Independent evolution after Gondwana breakup

### Continental Drift and Biogeography

**Gondwana Breakup:**
- Southern supercontinent split ~180-30 MYA
- Explains distributions:
  - **Ratites** (flightless birds): Ostrich (Africa), rhea (S. America), emu/cassowary (Australia)
  - **Southern beech** (Nothofagus): S. America, Australia, New Zealand
  - **Marsupials**: Australia, S. America (opossums)

**Continental Connections:**
- Great American Interchange (~3 MYA): Panama land bridge formed
  - N. American mammals → S. America (cats, dogs, bears, deer)
  - S. American mammals → N. America (opossums, armadillos, sloths)
  - Net: More N. American taxa succeeded in S. America

## Altitude and Depth Gradients

### Elevational Gradients

**Pattern**: Species richness typically declines with elevation

**Mechanisms:**
- Decreasing temperature, oxygen
- Smaller area at higher elevations (mountaintop as island)
- Example: Tropical mountains have high turnover between elevations

**Mid-Elevation Peak:**
- Some taxa (birds, small mammals) peak at mid-elevations
- Balance between productivity and temperature stress

### Marine Depth Gradients

**Pattern**: Richness highest in shallow waters, declines with depth

**Reasons:**
- Light limitation → No primary production below ~200m
- Pressure, temperature extremes
- Energy subsidies from above (detritus, whale falls)

**Deep Sea:**
- Low diversity per area but high beta diversity (turnover between sites)
- Many endemic species

## Alpha, Beta, and Gamma Diversity

### Definitions

**Alpha (α) diversity**: Species richness within a local site
- Example: 50 tree species in one hectare

**Beta (β) diversity**: Species turnover between sites
- Measures dissimilarity
- High β = sites share few species

**Gamma (γ) diversity**: Total species richness across landscape
- γ = α × β (multiplicatively)

### Example

**Tropical vs. Temperate Forests:**
- Tropical: High α (200 spp/ha), moderate β, very high γ
- Temperate: Moderate α (20 spp/ha), high β, moderate γ

**Conservation Implication:**
- High β requires protecting multiple sites to capture total diversity
- Complementarity: Choose reserves that share fewest species

## Species-Area Relationships

### Mechanisms Explaining Species-Area

**1. Habitat Heterogeneity**
- Larger areas contain more habitat types
- More niches → More species

**2. Passive Sampling**
- Larger areas sample more individuals
- More likely to encounter rare species

**3. Extinction-Immigration Balance**
- (Island biogeography theory)

### Extinction Debt

**Concept**: Time lag between habitat loss and species extinctions

**Process:**
1. Habitat destroyed → Reduced to smaller area
2. Species richness initially exceeds new equilibrium
3. Gradual extinctions over decades as "pay" debt

**Implications:**
- Past habitat loss → Future extinctions committed
- Conservation urgency: Protect now to prevent future loss

**Example:**
- Atlantic Forest (Brazil): Fragmented 1800s-1900s
- Bird extinctions still occurring in fragments
- Predicted additional losses based on species-area curves

## Endemism

### Patterns of Endemism

**Endemic species**: Found nowhere else

**Island Endemism:**
- High on isolated islands (Galápagos, Hawaii, Madagascar)
- Evolution in isolation → Unique species

**Continental Endemism:**
- Mountains (Sky islands): Isolated peaks = habitat islands
- Ancient lakes (Baikal, Tanganyika): Long isolation → endemic fish, invertebrates
- Mediterranean regions: Stable climate refugia

### Hotspots of Endemism

**Galápagos Islands:**
- ~80% of land birds endemic
- ~97% of reptiles endemic
- Darwin's finches (15 species), giant tortoises (15 species), marine iguanas

**Lake Baikal (Russia):**
- World's oldest, deepest lake (~25 million years)
- ~1,700 species, ~66% endemic
- Unique amphipods, sculpins, nerpa seal

**Hawaii:**
- Most isolated island group
- All native birds from single colonization events → Adaptive radiations
- Honeycreepers: 50+ species from one finch ancestor

## Human Impacts on Biogeography

### Biotic Homogenization

**Process**: Replacement of native species by widespread invasives

**Result**: Regions become more similar (reduced beta diversity)

**Examples:**
- European starlings, house sparrows now globally distributed
- Same invasive plants on islands worldwide

### Range Shifts from Climate Change

**Observed Patterns:**
- Poleward shifts: 6.1 km/decade average
- Upslope shifts: 11 m/decade average
- Marine species shifting faster than terrestrial

**Mismatches:**
- Species move at different rates → Community disassembly
- Interactions disrupted (pollinators, predators)

**Future Projections:**
- 15-37% of species "committed to extinction" by 2050 under mid-range warming
- Highland species "nowhere to go"

## Conservation Biogeography

### Prioritization Frameworks

**Biodiversity Hotspots:**
- Focus on high endemism + high threat
- Cost-effective: Protect 2.5% land → Save 50% species

**Wilderness Areas:**
- Low human impact, intact ecosystems
- Examples: Amazon, Congo Basin, boreal forests
- Complement hotspots (different strengths)

**Complementarity:**
- Select reserves that collectively maximize species coverage
- Avoid redundancy (protecting same species multiple times)

### Climate Refugia

**Definition**: Areas maintaining suitable climate despite regional change

**Importance:**
- Persistence during unfavorable periods
- Source for recolonization after disturbance

**Examples:**
- Cool, moist microclimates (north slopes, riparian zones)
- High elevational range (species can shift up/down)

**Conservation Strategy:**
- Protect refugia to maintain biodiversity through climate change
- Establish corridors for movement to refugia

## Summary

- Species richness increases toward equator (latitudinal gradient) due to energy, time, area, and niche specialization
- Biodiversity hotspots (Tropical Andes, Madagascar, Sundaland) contain >50% of species on ~2.5% of land
- Island biogeography theory predicts species richness from area and isolation; applies to habitat fragments
- Continental drift explains disjunct distributions (Gondwana breakup → ratites, southern beech)
- Species-area relationships universal; habitat loss creates extinction debt (future losses committed)
- Endemic species concentrated on islands (Hawaii, Galápagos) and isolated habitats (ancient lakes, mountains)
- Human impacts include biotic homogenization (invasives replacing natives) and climate-driven range shifts
- Conservation prioritizes hotspots (high endemism + threat) and climate refugia (persistence sites)
