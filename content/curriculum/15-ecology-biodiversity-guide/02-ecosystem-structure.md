# Ecosystem Structure and Function

## Overview

Ecosystems consist of biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components interacting through energy flows and nutrient cycles. Understanding ecosystem structure - who's present and in what abundance - helps explain ecosystem function - how energy and materials move through the system.

---

## Ecosystem Components

### Biotic Components

**Producers (Autotrophs)**
- Convert inorganic materials into organic compounds
- **Photoautotrophs**: Use sunlight (plants, algae, cyanobacteria)
- **Chemoautotrophs**: Use chemical energy (sulfur bacteria, deep-sea vent organisms)
- Form base of food webs

**Consumers (Heterotrophs)**
- Obtain energy from organic compounds
- **Primary consumers** (herbivores): Eat producers
- **Secondary consumers** (carnivores): Eat herbivores
- **Tertiary consumers**: Eat other carnivores
- **Omnivores**: Eat multiple trophic levels

**Decomposers and Detritivores**
- Break down dead organic matter
- **Decomposers**: Bacteria and fungi that chemically break down material
- **Detritivores**: Animals that physically consume detritus (earthworms, millipedes, vultures)
- Return nutrients to soil/water for reuse

### Abiotic Components

**Physical Factors**
- Temperature, light, water availability
- Determine what organisms can survive

**Chemical Factors**
- pH, salinity, nutrient concentrations (N, P, K, etc.)
- Affect productivity and species composition

**Substrate**
- Soil, rock, water - provides habitat and nutrients

## Energy Flow

### Solar Energy Capture

**Gross Primary Production (GPP)**
- Total energy captured by photosynthesis
- Varies by biome: Tropical forests (~2,200 g/m²/yr) vs. Deserts (~90 g/m²/yr)

**Net Primary Production (NPP)**
- GPP minus plant respiration
- Energy available to consumers
- NPP = GPP - R (respiration)

**Factors Affecting NPP**
- Sunlight availability
- Water availability
- Temperature (affects metabolic rates)
- Nutrient availability (especially N and P)

### Trophic Efficiency

**10% Rule** (Lindeman, 1942)
- Only ~10% of energy transferred between trophic levels
- Rest lost to:
  - Respiration (heat)
  - Incomplete consumption (not all biomass eaten)
  - Incomplete digestion (some energy in feces)

**Implications:**
- Limits food chain length (usually 4-5 levels max)
- More biomass at lower trophic levels (ecological pyramids)
- Eating lower on food chain more efficient for human food systems

### Energy Flow Models

**Trophic Pyramid Structure:**

```
Tertiary Consumers (0.1 kg)
         ↑
Secondary Consumers (1 kg)
         ↑
Primary Consumers (10 kg)
         ↑
Producers (100 kg)
```

### Inverted Pyramids

Some ecosystems show inverted biomass pyramids:
- **Aquatic systems**: Phytoplankton reproduce rapidly but have low standing biomass
- Consumers can have more biomass than producers at any moment
- Productivity (rate) matters more than standing stock (amount)

## Nutrient Cycling

### Biogeochemical Cycles

Unlike energy, nutrients cycle repeatedly through ecosystems

**General Pattern:**
1. Nutrients in abiotic pool (atmosphere, soil, water)
2. Uptake by producers
3. Transfer through consumers
4. Return via excretion and decomposition
5. Repeat

### Major Nutrient Cycles

**Carbon Cycle**
- Atmosphere ↔ Biosphere via photosynthesis/respiration
- Long-term storage in fossil fuels, sediments, ocean
- Human impact: Fossil fuel burning adds CO₂ faster than removal

**Nitrogen Cycle**
- N₂ in atmosphere → fixation → NH₃/NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻ → organisms
- **Fixation**: Lightning or bacteria (Rhizobium in legume roots)
- **Nitrification**: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (by bacteria)
- **Denitrification**: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (returns to atmosphere)
- Human impact: Haber-Bosch process, fertilizer runoff → eutrophication

**Phosphorus Cycle**
- No atmospheric component (unlike C and N)
- Weathering of rocks → soil → uptake by plants
- Returns via decomposition
- Leaches into sediments → ocean → uplifted over millions of years
- Often limiting nutrient in freshwater
- Human impact: Mining, fertilizer, detergents → eutrophication

**Water (Hydrological) Cycle**
- Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → runoff/infiltration
- Transpiration by plants moves water from soil to atmosphere
- Forests generate ~40% of terrestrial rainfall through recycling

## Ecosystem Productivity Patterns

### Terrestrial NPP Patterns

| Biome | NPP (g/m²/yr) | Limiting Factors |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Tropical rainforest | 2,200 | Light (dense canopy) |
| Temperate forest | 1,200 | Growing season length |
| Grassland | 600 | Water |
| Desert | 90 | Water, nutrients |
| Tundra | 140 | Temperature, nutrients |

### Aquatic NPP Patterns

| Ecosystem | NPP (g/m²/yr) | Characteristics |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Coral reefs | 2,500 | High diversity, nutrient recycling |
| Estuaries | 1,500 | Nutrient-rich, high turnover |
| Open ocean | 125 | Nutrient-poor, vast area |
| Deep ocean | <1 | No light for photosynthesis |

**Ocean Productivity Paradox**: Open ocean is nutrient-poor but supports large fish populations
- Solution: High turnover rate compensates for low standing biomass
- Upwelling zones bring nutrients → high productivity

## Decomposition and Nutrient Regeneration

### Decomposition Process

**Factors Affecting Decomposition Rate:**
- **Temperature**: Faster in warm conditions (bacterial activity)
- **Moisture**: Need water, but waterlogging slows it (anaerobic)
- **Oxygen**: Aerobic decomposition faster than anaerobic
- **Substrate quality**: High lignin/C:N ratio = slower decay
- **Organism community**: Bacteria, fungi, invertebrates

**Decomposition Rates by Biome:**
- Tropical rainforest: Leaf litter decomposes in ~6 months
- Temperate forest: 1-2 years
- Boreal forest/tundra: Decades (cold, acidic conditions)

### Nutrient Limitation

**Liebig's Law of the Minimum**: Growth limited by scarcest resource

**Common Limiting Nutrients:**
- **Terrestrial**: Often nitrogen (sometimes phosphorus)
- **Freshwater**: Usually phosphorus
- **Marine**: Often nitrogen (sometimes iron in open ocean)

**Redfield Ratio (C:N:P = 106:16:1)**
- Typical ratio in marine phytoplankton
- Helps predict nutrient limitation

## Ecosystem Engineering

### Niche Construction

Some organisms significantly modify their environment, affecting other species

**Examples:**

**Beavers**
- Build dams → create wetlands
- Increase habitat diversity
- Alter hydrology and nutrient flows
- Support dozens of other species

**Corals**
- Secrete calcium carbonate skeletons → build reef structure
- Create 3D habitat for thousands of species
- Protect coastlines from erosion

**Elephants**
- Knock down trees → maintain savanna openings
- Dig waterholes → benefit other species
- Disperse seeds long distances

**Termites**
- Build mounds → alter soil structure and nutrients
- Process dead wood → accelerate nutrient cycling
- Mounds used by other species for nesting

## Spatial Heterogeneity

### Patch Dynamics

Ecosystems are mosaics of patches in different successional stages

**Disturbance Creates Patchiness:**
- Fire, windthrow, grazing, floods
- Different patches at different stages
- Increases overall diversity (different species favor different stages)

**Edge Effects**
- Transition zones between patches have unique conditions
- Often higher diversity but also vulnerability to invasives
- Fragmentation creates more edge relative to interior

### Landscape Ecology

**Connectivity Matters:**
- Corridors allow movement between patches
- Gene flow, recolonization after local extinction
- Critical for large predators and migratory species

**Matrix Quality:**
- Surrounding "sea" affects patches
- Agricultural matrix more hostile than forest matrix

## Real-World Applications

### Eutrophication

**Process:**
1. Excess nutrients (N, P) enter water from agriculture, sewage
2. Algal bloom → high productivity
3. Algae die → decomposition consumes oxygen
4. Hypoxia (low oxygen) → fish kills
5. Ecosystem shift to low-diversity, degraded state

**Examples:**
- Gulf of Mexico "dead zone" (~20,000 km² hypoxic area from Mississippi River nutrients)
- Lake Erie algal blooms (cyanobacteria produce toxins)

**Solutions:**
- Reduce fertilizer use, buffer strips, wetland restoration
- Sewage treatment upgrades

### Carbon Sequestration

**Natural Carbon Sinks:**
- **Forests**: Store ~45% of terrestrial carbon
- **Wetlands**: High carbon storage despite small area
- **Oceans**: Largest carbon reservoir (phytoplankton, dissolved CO₂)
- **Soil**: 2-3× more carbon than atmosphere

**Management Implications:**
- Protect existing carbon stores (avoid deforestation, wetland drainage)
- Restore degraded ecosystems (reforestation, wetland creation)
- Soil management (no-till agriculture, cover crops)

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **NPP** | Net primary production - energy available to consumers |
| **Trophic efficiency** | Proportion of energy transferred between levels (~10%) |
| **Biogeochemical cycle** | Movement of elements through biotic and abiotic components |
| **Limiting nutrient** | Nutrient in shortest supply relative to demand |
| **Decomposition** | Breakdown of organic matter by decomposers |
| **Ecosystem engineer** | Species that physically modifies environment |
| **Eutrophication** | Excessive nutrient enrichment causing ecological degradation |

---

## Summary

- Ecosystems contain producers, consumers, and decomposers interacting with abiotic factors
- Energy flows unidirectionally with ~10% transfer efficiency between trophic levels
- Nutrients cycle through biotic and abiotic pools via biogeochemical cycles
- NPP varies by biome based on temperature, water, and nutrient availability
- Decomposition regenerates nutrients at rates depending on temperature, moisture, and substrate quality
- Ecosystem engineers like beavers and corals create habitat structure affecting many other species
- Understanding ecosystem structure and function essential for managing eutrophication, carbon sequestration, and other environmental challenges
