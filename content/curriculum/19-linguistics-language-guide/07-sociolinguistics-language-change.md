# Sociolinguistics & Language Change: Language in Society and Time

## Overview

Sociolinguistics examines how language varies and changes based on social factors—region, class, ethnicity, gender, age, context. Historical linguistics studies language change over time—how Latin became French, how Old English became Modern English.

Together, these fields reveal language as a dynamic, socially embedded system. Variation is not random error but systematic patterning shaped by identity, power, and interaction. Change is constant, driven by social forces and linguistic constraints.

## Part I: Sociolinguistics

### What is Sociolinguistics?

**Definition:** Study of language in social context; how linguistic variation correlates with social structure

**Core Insight:** Language variation is **systematic**, not chaotic
- Different ways to say the same thing (variants)
- Variants correlate with social factors (variables)

### Linguistic Variation

#### Regional Variation (Dialects)

**Dialect:** Variety of language associated with a geographic region

**Example: American English**

| Feature | Northern | Southern | West |
|---------|----------|----------|------|
| */r/* after vowels | Not pronounced (Boston) | Pronounced | Pronounced |
| *pin* vs. *pen* | Distinguished | Same (pin-pen merger) | Distinguished |
| *you* plural | *you guys* | *y'all* | *you guys* |
| *caught* vs. *cot* | Distinguished | Merged (some areas) | Merged |

**Isogloss:** Geographic boundary of linguistic feature
- Bundle of isoglosses → dialect boundary

**Dialect continuum:** Gradual change across space
- Neighboring villages mutually intelligible
- Distant villages not mutually intelligible
- No sharp boundary (where does Dutch end, German begin?)

**Mutual Intelligibility:**
- Traditionally defines language vs. dialect
- But: Political/cultural factors override
  - Norwegian/Swedish/Danish: Separate languages (mutual intelligibility high)
  - Mandarin/Cantonese: "Dialects" of Chinese (not mutually intelligible)
- **"A language is a dialect with an army and navy"** (Max Weinreich)

#### Social Class Variation

**Labov's New York City Study (1966):**
- /r/ pronunciation after vowels (*car, fourth*)
- Three department stores: High-end (Saks), Mid (Macy's), Low (S. Klein)

**Findings:**
- Higher-class stores: More /r/ pronunciation
- Casual speech: Less /r/
- Careful speech: More /r/ (style-shifting)

**Interpretation:**
- /r/ = prestige variant (associated with higher status)
- Speakers adjust speech based on context (audience design)

**Norwich Study (Trudgill):**
- *-ing* vs. *-in'* (*running* vs. *runnin'*)
- Working class: More *-in'*
- Middle class: More *-ing*
- Men: More *-in'* than women (same class)

#### Gender Variation

**General patterns:**
- Women use more **standard/prestige** forms
- Men use more **vernacular/non-standard** forms

**Explanations:**
- **Social pressure**: Women held to higher linguistic norms
- **Covert prestige**: Non-standard forms signal masculinity, toughness
- **Social networks**: Women's networks more diverse (crossover to prestige norms)

**Examples:**
- *-ing* vs. *-in'*: Women say *running*, men *runnin'*
- Multiple negation (*I didn't do nothing*): More common among men

**Caution:** Generalizations don't apply universally; context-dependent

#### Ethnicity and Language

**African American Vernacular English (AAVE):**
- Systematic grammatical differences from Standard American English (SAE)
- Not "broken English"; rule-governed dialect

**Features:**
| AAVE | SAE | Feature |
|------|-----|---------|
| He walking | He is walking | Copula deletion |
| She be running | She is usually running | Habitual *be* |
| She done finished | She has finished | Completive *done* |
| I been knew that | I've known that for a long time | Remote past *been* |
| He don't be here | He isn't usually here | Negative habitual |

**Copula deletion:**
- "She ∅ happy" (AAVE) vs. "She is happy" (SAE)
- Systematic: Deleted where SAE contracts ("She's happy" → "She ∅ happy")
- Not deleted where SAE doesn't contract ("I know where she is" → *not* "I know where she ∅")

**Social significance:**
- Stigmatized, wrongly viewed as "uneducated"
- Identity marker for African American community
- Code-switching: AAVE in-group, SAE in formal contexts

**Chicano English:**
- English variety of Mexican Americans
- Distinct from Spanish-accented English
- Features: Prosodic patterns, vowel changes

#### Age Variation

**Apparent-time hypothesis:** Compare age groups at one point in time to infer change
- If young people use variant X more than old people → change in progress
- Assumes: People's language stable after adolescence

**Real-time study:** Track same speakers over years
- Tests apparent-time hypothesis
- Martha's Vineyard (Labov): Centralization of diphthongs increased over decades

**Age-grading:** Features associated with age, not historical change
- Teen slang adopted, then dropped as adults (each generation)

### Diglossia and Bilingualism

**Diglossia (Ferguson):** Two varieties of a language in complementary distribution

**Example: Arabic**
- **High (H) variety:** Classical/Standard Arabic (education, media, religion)
- **Low (L) variety:** Colloquial dialects (home, conversation)
- Functional separation: Use H in formal contexts, L in informal

**Bilingualism:** Use of two (or more) languages
- **Individual bilingualism**: Person speaks multiple languages
- **Societal bilingualism**: Community uses multiple languages

**Code-Switching:**
- Alternating between languages within conversation
- **Intersentential**: Between sentences ("Let's go. Ya vámonos.")
- **Intrasentential**: Within sentences ("I'm gonna ir al store")
- Rule-governed, not random
- Marks identity, group membership

**Example (Spanish-English):**
- "Estaba training para la competencia"
- "I was training for the competition"
- English verb *training* inserted into Spanish sentence

### Language and Identity

**Linguistic identity markers:**
- **Regional**: "I'm from Boston" → /r/-lessness
- **Ethnic**: "I'm Black" → AAVE features
- **Class**: "I'm working-class" → non-standard forms
- **Gender**: Femininity/masculinity performance

**Accommodation Theory (Giles):**
- **Convergence**: Adjust speech toward interlocutor (build rapport)
- **Divergence**: Adjust speech away from interlocutor (assert difference)

**Example:**
- Job interview: Converge to interviewer's standard speech
- In-group conversation: Diverge from out-group norms

### Language Attitudes and Prestige

**Overt prestige:** Openly valued variety (standard language)
- Associated with education, success, formality
- Used in official contexts

**Covert prestige:** Valued within group but stigmatized externally
- Non-standard forms signal solidarity, authenticity
- "Talking proper" seen as snobbish in some communities

**Matched-guise technique (Lambert):**
- Same speaker records passage in two dialects/accents
- Listeners rate speakers on intelligence, friendliness, etc.
- Reveals attitudes toward varieties

**Findings:**
- Standard varieties rated higher on competence (intelligence, education)
- Non-standard varieties rated higher on solidarity (friendliness, trustworthiness)

### Language Planning and Policy

**Corpus Planning:** What form should the language take?
- Standardization: Grammar, spelling
- Modernization: New vocabulary (technology terms)
- Example: Academie Française (French), Modern Hebrew revival

**Status Planning:** What role should the language play?
- Official language designation
- Language of education, government, media
- Example: English in India, French in Canada

**Acquisition Planning:** Who should learn the language?
- Language education policy
- Minority language instruction
- Example: Irish in Ireland, Welsh in Wales

**Language Rights:**
- Indigenous language protection
- Minority language education
- Linguistic discrimination laws

### Language Endangerment

**Statistics:**
- ~7,000 languages worldwide
- 40% endangered (< 1,000 speakers)
- One language dies every two weeks

**Causes:**
- **Language shift**: Community abandons heritage language for dominant language
- **Lack of intergenerational transmission**: Children not learning parents' language
- **Economic pressure**: Dominant language needed for jobs, education
- **Globalization**: English, Mandarin, Spanish spreading

**Stages (UNESCO):**
1. **Vulnerable**: Some children learning
2. **Definitely endangered**: Children not learning as mother tongue
3. **Severely endangered**: Only grandparents speak fluently
4. **Critically endangered**: Few elderly speakers
5. **Extinct**: No speakers

**Revitalization efforts:**
- **Hebrew**: Successfully revived (was liturgical, now native language of millions)
- **Hawaiian**: Immersion schools, media, official status (partial success)
- **Maori**: Education programs, official status (ongoing)
- **Navajo**: Largest Native American language (still threatened)

## Part II: Historical Linguistics

### What is Language Change?

**Fundamental insight:** All languages change constantly
- No "pure" or "unchanging" languages
- Change is normal, not decay

**Types of change:**
- **Phonological**: Sound changes
- **Morphological**: Word structure changes
- **Syntactic**: Grammar changes
- **Semantic**: Meaning changes
- **Lexical**: Vocabulary changes

### Sound Change

#### Regular Sound Change

**Grimm's Law (1822):** Systematic consonant shifts in Germanic languages

| Proto-Indo-European | Germanic | Example |
|---------------------|----------|---------|
| *p | *f | *pater → *father* |
| *t | *θ | *tres → *three* |
| *k | *h | *centum → *hundred* (h from earlier x) |
| *b | *p | — |
| *d | *t | *decem → *ten* |
| *g | *k | *genus → *kin* |

**Great Vowel Shift (English, 1400-1700):**
- Long vowels raised and/or diphthongized

| Middle English | Modern English | Example |
|----------------|----------------|---------|
| /iː/ | /aɪ/ | *time* [tiːm] → [taɪm] |
| /eː/ | /iː/ | *seek* [seːk] → [siːk] |
| /aː/ | /eɪ/ | *name* [naːm] → [neɪm] |
| /oː/ | /uː/ | *boot* [boːt] → [buːt] |
| /uː/ | /aʊ/ | *house* [huːs] → [haʊs] |

**Result:** English spelling frozen before shift → spelling-sound mismatch

#### Types of Sound Change

**Assimilation:** Sound becomes like neighbor
- Latin *impossibilis* → Italian *impossibile* (m before p)

**Dissimilation:** Sound becomes unlike neighbor
- Latin *peregrinus* → *pelegrino* (r...r → l...r)

**Deletion:**
- *knight* /knixt/ → /naɪt/ (lost k, x)

**Insertion:**
- *something* often pronounced *somethink* (p inserted)

**Metathesis:** Reordering
- Old English *brid* → *bird*
- Old English *þridda* → *third*

**Weakening/Lenition:**
- Voiced stops → fricatives: Spanish *vida* (life) from Latin *vita* (t → d)

### Morphological and Syntactic Change

**Loss of inflections (English):**

**Old English (1000 CE):**
- Rich case system (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative)
- *stan* (stone): *stan* (nom), *stanes* (gen), *stane* (dat), *stan* (acc)

**Modern English:**
- Minimal inflection
- *stone, stone's*
- Word order rigidified to compensate (SVO mandatory)

**Grammaticalization:** Content words → grammar words

**Examples:**
- *going to* → *gonna* (future marker)
  - "I am going to see him" (motion) → "It's gonna rain" (future, no motion)
- French *pas*: step → negation
  - *Je ne marche pas* (I don't walk step) → *Je ne marche pas* (I don't walk)

**Reanalysis:**
- *a napron* → *an apron* (boundary shifted)
- *an ewt* → *a newt*

### Semantic Change

**Broadening:** Meaning becomes more general
- *dog*: Specific breed → any canine

**Narrowing:** Meaning becomes more specific
- *meat*: Any food → animal flesh
- *deer*: Any animal → specific animal

**Amelioration:** Meaning improves
- *nice*: Foolish → pleasant

**Pejoration:** Meaning worsens
- *silly*: Happy/blessed → foolish
- *villain*: Farm laborer → wicked person

**Metaphor:**
- *mouse*: Animal → computer device
- *web*: Spider's web → internet

**Semantic bleaching:**
- *very*: Truly → intensifier (lost original force)
- *literally*: Actually → intensifier (*literally dying* = very)

### Comparative Method

**Reconstructing proto-languages** from descendants

**Steps:**
1. **Assemble cognates** (words from common ancestor)
2. **Identify sound correspondences**
3. **Reconstruct proto-form**

**Example: Romance languages from Latin**

| Latin | Spanish | French | Italian | Portuguese |
|-------|---------|--------|---------|------------|
| *cantare* | cantar | chanter | cantare | cantar |
| *mater* | madre | mère | madre | mãe |

**Indo-European Family:**
- **Germanic**: English, German, Dutch, Swedish
- **Romance**: Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian
- **Slavic**: Russian, Polish, Czech, Serbo-Croatian
- **Indo-Iranian**: Hindi, Persian, Bengali
- **Celtic**: Irish, Welsh, Breton
- **Greek**, **Albanian**, **Armenian**, **Baltic**

**Proto-Indo-European (PIE):**
- Reconstructed (no written records)
- Spoken ~4000-2500 BCE
- Homeland debated (Pontic Steppe vs. Anatolia)

### Language Families

**Major families:**
- **Niger-Congo** (Africa): Swahili, Yoruba, Zulu (~1,500 languages)
- **Austronesian** (Pacific, Southeast Asia): Malay, Tagalog, Hawaiian (~1,200 languages)
- **Trans-New Guinea**: ~300 languages
- **Sino-Tibetan**: Mandarin, Cantonese, Burmese, Tibetan (~450 languages)
- **Indo-European**: ~450 languages (most speakers: 3+ billion)
- **Afro-Asiatic**: Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic (~350 languages)
- **Turkic**: Turkish, Uzbek, Kazakh (~35 languages)

**Language isolates:** No known relatives
- **Basque** (Spain/France)
- **Korean** (debated, possibly Koreanic family)
- **Japanese** (debated, possibly Japonic family)

### Mechanisms of Change

**Language contact:**
- **Borrowing**: English borrowed ~10,000 French words (Norman Conquest)
- **Creolization**: Pidgin becomes native language (Haitian Creole from French)
- **Convergence**: Languages become structurally similar (Balkan Sprachbund)

**Social factors:**
- **Prestige**: Adopt features of high-status variety
- **Identity**: Maintain/exaggerate features to signal group membership

**Internal factors:**
- **Analogy**: Regularize irregular forms (*dived* replacing *dove*)
- **Phonetic ease**: Simplify difficult articulations

**Transmission errors:**
- Children's imperfect learning → change across generations

## Real-World Applications

**Forensic linguistics:**
- Author identification (writing style, dialect)
- Speaker identification (voiceprint, accent)
- Legal disputes (trademark, defamation)

**Language revitalization:**
- Documentation of endangered languages
- Creation of educational materials
- Policy advocacy

**Language teaching:**
- Understanding dialect interference in L2 learning
- Appropriate target variety selection

**Social policy:**
- Combating linguistic discrimination
- Bilingual education design
- Official language designation

**Marketing and media:**
- Dialect coaching for actors
- Accent selection for advertisements (trust, sophistication)

**Historical research:**
- Dating texts via linguistic features
- Reconstructing migration patterns
- Understanding cultural contact

## Key Terms

**Sociolinguistics:**
- **Sociolinguistics**: Study of language in social context
- **Dialect**: Regional/social language variety
- **Idiolect**: Individual's unique language variety
- **Prestige**: Social value of language variety
- **Code-Switching**: Alternating between languages/dialects
- **Diglossia**: High and low varieties in complementary distribution
- **Language Shift**: Community abandons one language for another
- **Language Endangerment**: Language losing speakers

**Historical Linguistics:**
- **Sound Change**: Systematic phonological evolution
- **Grimm's Law**: Consonant shift in Germanic languages
- **Great Vowel Shift**: English long vowel changes (1400-1700)
- **Grammaticalization**: Content word becomes grammatical marker
- **Semantic Change**: Shift in word meaning
- **Comparative Method**: Reconstructing proto-languages
- **Cognates**: Words from common ancestor
- **Language Family**: Related languages from common origin

## Summary

Sociolinguistics reveals language variation as systematic, shaped by region, class, ethnicity, gender, and context. Dialects are rule-governed varieties, not errors. Speakers shift styles based on audience and purpose, balancing prestige and identity.

Language endangerment threatens ~40% of the world's 7,000 languages. Revitalization efforts face challenges but offer hope (Hebrew, Hawaiian). Language planning decisions—corpus, status, acquisition—shape linguistic futures.

Historical linguistics shows all languages change constantly through sound, morphological, syntactic, and semantic shifts. The comparative method reconstructs proto-languages, revealing language families like Indo-European. Contact, social factors, and transmission errors drive change.

Together, sociolinguistics and historical linguistics demonstrate that language is dynamic, socially embedded, and constantly evolving. Variation is not deviation but inherent systematicity. Change is not decay but natural transformation. Understanding these processes illuminates human society, cognition, and history.
