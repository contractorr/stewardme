# Glossary of Linguistic Terms

## A

**Abjad**
- Writing system representing only consonants; vowels are optional or marked with diacritics. Examples: Arabic, Hebrew.

**Abugida (Alphasyllabary)**
- Writing system where consonant symbols have an inherent vowel that can be modified with diacritics. Examples: Devanagari, Ethiopic.

**Acoustic Phonetics**
- Study of physical properties of speech sounds as sound waves (frequency, amplitude, duration).

**Acquisition**
- Process of learning a language, either as a first language (L1) or additional language (L2+).

**Affricate**
- Consonant combining a stop and fricative (e.g., /tʃ/ in *church*, /dʒ/ in *judge*).

**Agent**
- Semantic role of the doer of an action (e.g., *John* in "John kicked the ball").

**Allophone**
- Phonetic variant of a phoneme that doesn't distinguish meaning (e.g., aspirated [pʰ] in *pit* vs. unaspirated [p] in *spit*, both /p/).

**Alphabet**
- Writing system with separate symbols for both consonants and vowels. Examples: Latin, Greek, Cyrillic.

**Amelioration**
- Semantic change where a word's meaning improves (e.g., *nice*: foolish → pleasant).

**Analogy**
- Process where irregular forms become regular based on patterns (e.g., *dived* replacing *dove*).

**Antonymy**
- Relationship between words with opposite meanings (e.g., *hot*/*cold*).

**Arbitrariness**
- Principle that the connection between linguistic form and meaning is arbitrary, not natural (except onomatopoeia).

**Argument**
- In philosophy and logic, a set of premises offered in support of a conclusion.

**Articulation**
- Production of speech sounds using vocal organs.

**Assimilation**
- Phonological process where a sound becomes more like a neighboring sound (e.g., *input* → [ɪmpʊt]).

**Auditory Phonetics**
- Study of how speech sounds are perceived by the ear and brain.

## B

**Babbling**
- Pre-linguistic vocalization stage (6-12 months) where infants produce syllable-like sounds.

**Behaviorism**
- Theory that language is learned through imitation and reinforcement (challenged by Chomsky).

**Bilingualism**
- Ability to use two languages; can be individual or societal.

**Binding Theory**
- Theory governing relationships between pronouns, reflexives, and their antecedents.

**Broadening**
- Semantic change where a word's meaning becomes more general (e.g., *dog*: specific breed → any canine).

## C

**Categorical Perception**
- Phenomenon where continuous acoustic variation is perceived as discrete categories.

**C-command**
- Structural relationship in syntax defining binding domains.

**Code-Switching**
- Alternating between two or more languages or dialects within a conversation.

**Cognates**
- Words in different languages that descended from the same ancestor (e.g., English *father*, German *Vater*).

**Cognitive Linguistics**
- Approach emphasizing language as shaped by general cognitive processes and embodied experience.

**Comparative Method**
- Technique for reconstructing proto-languages by comparing cognates across related languages.

**Competence**
- Speaker's implicit knowledge of language rules (Chomsky), contrasted with performance.

**Complement**
- Element required by a head to complete its meaning (e.g., *the book* in "read the book").

**Complementary Distribution**
- Situation where allophones appear in mutually exclusive environments, never contrasting.

**Compositionality**
- Principle that the meaning of a complex expression is derived from the meanings of its parts plus combination rules.

**Consonant**
- Speech sound produced with constriction in the vocal tract (e.g., /p/, /s/, /l/).

**Constituent**
- Group of words that functions as a single unit in hierarchical structure.

**Construction Grammar**
- Theory viewing language as a collection of form-meaning pairings (constructions).

**Contrastive Distribution**
- Situation where sounds can appear in the same environment and create meaning differences (phonemes).

**Convergence**
- Adjusting one's speech toward an interlocutor's style to build rapport.

**Conversational Implicature**
- Meaning implied beyond literal content, derived from Gricean maxims and context.

**Cooperative Principle**
- Grice's principle that conversational participants are expected to contribute appropriately to the exchange.

**Copula**
- Linking verb (typically *be*) connecting subject and predicate (e.g., "She *is* happy").

**Covert Prestige**
- Social value of non-standard forms within a community, though stigmatized externally.

**Creolization**
- Process by which a pidgin becomes a native language with full grammatical structure.

**Critical Period Hypothesis**
- Theory that language acquisition is most successful before puberty due to biological constraints.

## D

**Deep Orthography**
- Writing system with poor sound-spelling correspondence (e.g., English).

**Deixis**
- Reference that depends on context (e.g., *I*, *here*, *now*).

**Deletion (Elision)**
- Phonological process where a sound is removed (e.g., /t/ in *exactly* → [ɪgˈzækli]).

**Derivation**
- Process of forming new words from existing ones using affixes (e.g., *teach* → *teacher*).

**Descriptive Linguistics**
- Approach that describes how language is actually used, without prescribing rules.

**Diachronic**
- Studying language change across time (contrasted with synchronic).

**Dialect**
- Regional or social variety of a language with systematic differences in pronunciation, grammar, or vocabulary.

**Dialect Continuum**
- Gradual variation across geographic space where adjacent varieties are mutually intelligible but distant ones are not.

**Diglossia**
- Situation where a community uses two varieties of a language in complementary functional domains (high vs. low).

**Diphthong**
- Vowel sound that glides from one quality to another within a single syllable (e.g., /aɪ/ in *buy*).

**Displacement**
- Language's ability to refer to things not present in time or space.

**Dissimilation**
- Phonological process where a sound becomes unlike a neighboring sound.

**Divergence**
- Adjusting one's speech away from an interlocutor's style to assert difference.

**Double Dissociation**
- Pattern where two brain lesions produce opposite deficits, suggesting distinct systems.

**Duality of Patterning**
- Property where meaningless sounds combine to form meaningful words, which combine to form sentences.

## E

**Empiricism**
- View that knowledge comes from sensory experience; in linguistics, that language is learned from input.

**Entailment**
- Logical relationship where if A is true, B must be true (e.g., "killed" → "dead").

**Epenthesis**
- Phonological process of inserting a sound (e.g., *something* → *somethink*).

**Epistemology**
- Philosophical study of knowledge and justified belief.

**Etymology**
- Study of word origins and historical development.

## F

**Face**
- Public self-image that people want to maintain; central to politeness theory (positive and negative face).

**Felicity Conditions**
- Conditions that must hold for a speech act to succeed (e.g., promises require ability to perform action).

**Formant**
- Resonant frequency of the vocal tract; formants distinguish vowels.

**Fossilization**
- Phenomenon where L2 learner's errors become permanent, stabilizing below native competence.

**Fricative**
- Consonant produced with turbulent airflow through narrow constriction (e.g., /f/, /s/, /ʃ/).

## G

**Glide**
- Vowel-like consonant (e.g., /w/, /j/ in *wet*, *yes*).

**Glottal**
- Sound produced at the glottis (vocal folds), like /h/ or glottal stop /ʔ/.

**Government and Binding (GB)**
- Chomskyan syntactic framework from the 1980s.

**Gradable Antonyms**
- Opposite words on a scale (e.g., *hot*/*cold* with degrees in between).

**Grammaticalization**
- Process where content words evolve into grammatical markers (e.g., *going to* → *gonna*).

**Great Vowel Shift**
- Systematic change in English long vowels (1400-1700) causing spelling-sound mismatch.

**Gricean Maxims**
- Cooperative principles for conversation: Quantity, Quality, Relevance, Manner.

**Grimm's Law**
- Systematic consonant shift characterizing Germanic languages' divergence from Proto-Indo-European.

## H

**Head**
- Central element of a phrase that determines its category (e.g., noun in NP, verb in VP).

**Hieroglyphs**
- Ancient Egyptian writing system combining logograms, phonograms, and determinatives.

**Holophrastic Speech**
- One-word stage of child language where single words express complex ideas.

**Homonymy**
- Relationship between words with the same form but unrelated meanings (e.g., *bank*: financial institution vs. river edge).

**Hyponymy**
- Relationship where one word's meaning is included in another's (e.g., *rose* is a hyponym of *flower*).

## I

**Idiolect**
- Individual's unique language variety.

**Illocutionary Force**
- Social action performed by an utterance (e.g., promising, requesting).

**Implicature**
- See Conversational Implicature.

**Inflection**
- Grammatical modification of words to express tense, number, case, etc. (e.g., *walk* → *walked*, *cat* → *cats*).

**Insertion**
- See Epenthesis.

**Interlanguage**
- L2 learner's developing linguistic system, distinct from both L1 and target L2.

**International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)**
- Standardized system for representing speech sounds of all languages.

**Intonation**
- Pitch patterns across phrases and sentences signaling meaning (e.g., question vs. statement).

**Isogloss**
- Geographic boundary of a linguistic feature.

## L

**L1 Acquisition**
- First language acquisition; learning one's native language as a child.

**L2 Acquisition**
- Second or additional language acquisition, typically in adolescence or adulthood.

**Language Family**
- Group of genetically related languages descended from a common ancestor.

**Language Isolate**
- Language with no known relatives (e.g., Basque, possibly Korean).

**Language Shift**
- Process where a community abandons one language in favor of another.

**Langue**
- Saussure's term for the abstract language system shared by a community (vs. parole).

**Lenition**
- Weakening of consonant articulation (e.g., voiceless → voiced).

**Lexicon**
- Mental dictionary; inventory of words and their meanings.

**Linguistic Relativity (Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis)**
- Theory that language influences thought and perception.

**Liquid**
- Consonant with partial closure, allowing relatively free airflow (/l/, /r/).

**Localization**
- Principle that specific cognitive functions map to specific brain regions.

**Logographic**
- Writing system where symbols represent words or morphemes (e.g., Chinese characters).

## M

**Manner of Articulation**
- How airflow is modified to produce consonants (stop, fricative, nasal, etc.).

**Marginal Analysis**
- In philosophy/economics, comparing additional benefits to additional costs.

**Mean Length of Utterance (MLU)**
- Average number of morphemes per utterance; measure of child language development.

**Meronymy**
- Part-whole relationship (e.g., *wheel* is a meronym of *car*).

**Metaphysics**
- Philosophical study of the nature of reality.

**Metathesis**
- Reordering of sounds (e.g., *ask* → [æks]).

**Minimal Pair**
- Two words differing in only one phoneme, demonstrating phonemic contrast (e.g., *pat*/*bat*).

**Minimalist Program**
- Chomskyan framework emphasizing simplicity and economy in syntactic theory.

**Modularity**
- View that language (or mind) consists of specialized, independent processing systems.

**Morpheme**
- Minimal meaningful unit (*un-happy-ness* = 3 morphemes).

**Morphology**
- Study of word structure and formation.

**Motherese (Child-Directed Speech)**
- Simplified, exaggerated speech style used with young children.

**Movement**
- Syntactic operation displacing constituents (e.g., wh-movement in questions).

**Mutual Intelligibility**
- Ability of speakers of different varieties to understand each other; traditional criterion for dialect vs. language.

## N

**Narrowing**
- Semantic change where a word's meaning becomes more specific (e.g., *meat*: any food → animal flesh).

**Nasal**
- Sound produced with airflow through the nose (/m/, /n/, /ŋ/).

**Nativism**
- View that humans have innate linguistic knowledge (Universal Grammar).

**Negation**
- Grammatical expression of denial or falsity.

**Neural Correlate**
- Brain activity associated with a mental state or cognitive process.

**Neurolinguistics**
- Study of neural mechanisms underlying language.

**NLP (Natural Language Processing)**
- Computational processing and analysis of human language.

**Normative**
- Prescriptive statements about what ought to be (vs. descriptive).

**Noun Phrase (NP)**
- Phrase headed by a noun (e.g., *the big house*).

## O

**Object**
- Grammatical relation; typically the entity acted upon (e.g., *the ball* in "kicked the ball").

**Optimality Theory (OT)**
- Phonological framework based on ranked, violable constraints.

**Orthography**
- Conventional spelling system of a language.

**Orthographic Depth**
- Degree of sound-spelling correspondence (shallow = regular, deep = irregular).

**Overregularization**
- Applying grammatical rules too broadly (e.g., *goed*, *foots*); evidence of rule learning.

**Overt Prestige**
- Openly valued linguistic variety, typically the standard dialect.

## P

**Palatal**
- Sound produced with tongue body against hard palate (e.g., /j/ in *yes*).

**Parameter**
- Variable property of language set during acquisition (e.g., pro-drop, head direction).

**Parole**
- Saussure's term for actual speech acts (vs. langue).

**Patient**
- Semantic role of entity undergoing action (e.g., *the ball* in "John kicked the ball").

**Pejoration**
- Semantic change where a word's meaning worsens (e.g., *silly*: happy → foolish).

**Performance**
- Actual language use, including errors and limitations (vs. competence).

**Phoneme**
- Minimal sound unit that distinguishes meaning in a language (e.g., /p/ vs. /b/ in English).

**Phonetics**
- Study of physical properties of speech sounds (articulatory, acoustic, auditory).

**Phonology**
- Study of sound systems and patterns within languages.

**Phonotactics**
- Language-specific constraints on permissible sound sequences.

**Phrase Structure**
- Hierarchical organization of words into constituents.

**Pidgin**
- Simplified language arising from contact between groups with no common language; not a native language.

**Place of Articulation**
- Where in the vocal tract a consonant constriction occurs (bilabial, alveolar, velar, etc.).

**Plasticity**
- Brain's ability to change and reorganize, especially important for language acquisition.

**Politeness Theory**
- Framework analyzing strategies for managing face in interaction.

**Polysemy**
- Single word with multiple related meanings (e.g., *head*: body part, leader, top).

**Positive**
- In philosophy/economics, descriptive statements about what is (vs. normative).

**Poverty of the Stimulus**
- Argument that linguistic input is insufficient to explain acquisition, supporting Universal Grammar.

**Pragmatics**
- Study of meaning in context; how speakers convey more than literal content.

**Prescriptive Linguistics**
- Approach that prescribes how language should be used (vs. descriptive).

**Prestige**
- Social value attributed to a linguistic variety.

**Presupposition**
- Background assumption that remains under negation (e.g., "John stopped smoking" presupposes he used to smoke).

**Productivity**
- Language's ability to generate infinite novel sentences from finite rules.

**Prosody**
- Suprasegmental features like stress, tone, and intonation.

**Prototype Theory**
- Theory that categories have central, typical members and graded boundaries.

**Psycholinguistics**
- Study of psychological and cognitive processes underlying language production and comprehension.

## Q

**Quantifier**
- Element specifying quantity (e.g., *all*, *some*, *no*).

**Quantification**
- Logical expression of quantity in semantics.

## R

**Reanalysis**
- Process where linguistic structure is reinterpreted (e.g., *a napron* → *an apron*).

**Rebus Principle**
- Using a symbol for its sound rather than its meaning in writing.

**Recursion**
- Embedding linguistic structures within structures of the same type, enabling infinite expressiveness.

**Reference**
- What a linguistic expression picks out in the world (Frege's Bedeutung).

**Reflexive**
- Pronoun referring back to the subject (e.g., *himself*, *herself*).

**Relevance Theory**
- Alternative to Gricean pragmatics emphasizing cognitive relevance.

**Retroflex**
- Sound produced with tongue tip curled back (e.g., American English /r/).

## S

**Scalar Implicature**
- Implicature arising from choosing a weaker term on a scale (e.g., "some" implicates "not all").

**Scope**
- Range over which a logical operator has effect (e.g., quantifier, negation).

**Selectional Restriction**
- Constraint on what arguments a word can take (e.g., *eat* requires edible object).

**Semantic Feature**
- Component of word meaning (e.g., [+HUMAN], [+ANIMATE]).

**Semantic Role (Thematic Role)**
- Role an entity plays in the event described (Agent, Patient, Theme, etc.).

**Semantics**
- Study of literal, truth-conditional meaning.

**Sense**
- Mode of presentation of a referent; cognitive content (Frege's Sinn).

**Shallow Orthography**
- Writing system with close sound-spelling correspondence (e.g., Spanish, Finnish).

**Sign**
- Linguistic unit consisting of signifier (form) and signified (meaning) (Saussure).

**SLA (Second Language Acquisition)**
- Process and study of learning additional languages.

**Sociolinguistics**
- Study of language variation and use in social context.

**Specifier**
- Constituent in phrase structure, sister to intermediate projection.

**Speech Act**
- Action performed through utterance (asserting, requesting, promising, etc.).

**Stop (Plosive)**
- Consonant produced with complete closure then release (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/).

**Stress**
- Prominence on a syllable through increased loudness, duration, or pitch.

**Structuralism**
- Linguistic approach emphasizing language as a system of interrelated elements (Saussure, Bloomfield).

**Structure Dependence**
- Principle that linguistic rules operate on hierarchical structures, not linear sequences.

**Subject**
- Grammatical relation; typically the entity performing action in active voice.

**Subjacency**
- Constraint on movement operations in syntax.

**Syllabary**
- Writing system where symbols represent syllables (e.g., Japanese kana, Cherokee).

**Syllable**
- Organizational unit of sound consisting of onset, nucleus, and coda.

**Synchronic**
- Studying language at a single point in time (vs. diachronic).

**Synonymy**
- Relationship between words with the same or very similar meaning (e.g., *big*/*large*).

**Syntax**
- Study of sentence structure; rules governing how words combine into phrases and sentences.

## T

**Telegraphic Speech**
- Two-word stage of child language omitting function words.

**Thematic Role**
- See Semantic Role.

**Tone**
- Use of pitch to distinguish word meanings (e.g., Mandarin, Yoruba).

**Transfer**
- Influence of L1 on L2 acquisition (positive or negative).

**Transformation**
- Operation that derives one structure from another (e.g., active to passive).

**Truth Condition**
- Conditions under which a sentence is true; central to truth-conditional semantics.

**Typology**
- Classification and comparison of languages based on structural features.

## U

**Universal Grammar (UG)**
- Chomsky's theory of innate linguistic knowledge shared by all humans.

**Universals**
- Properties or patterns found across all or most languages.

**Usage-Based Linguistics**
- Approach emphasizing language learning from frequency patterns and usage events.

**Utterance**
- Instance of language use; what is actually said in context.

## V

**Velar**
- Sound produced with tongue body against soft palate (e.g., /k/, /g/, /ŋ/).

**Verb Phrase (VP)**
- Phrase headed by a verb (e.g., *ate the apple*).

**VOT (Voice Onset Time)**
- Delay between consonant release and onset of voicing; distinguishes voiced/voiceless stops.

**Voicing**
- Vibration of vocal folds during speech sound production.

**Vowel**
- Speech sound produced with relatively open vocal tract (e.g., /i/, /a/, /u/).

## W

**Wh-Movement**
- Movement of question words (who, what, where) to sentence-initial position.

**Word Order**
- Sequence of major constituents (SVO, SOV, VSO, etc.); parametric variation across languages.

**Writing System**
- Systematic method of representing language visually.

## X

**X-Bar Theory**
- Framework for phrase structure with uniform schema (XP → Spec + X' → X + Complement).

---

## Summary

This glossary covers fundamental terms across all subfields of linguistics—phonetics, phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, psycholinguistics, sociolinguistics, historical linguistics, and writing systems.

Understanding these terms enables precise discussion of linguistic phenomena and provides the foundation for deeper exploration of how language works as a cognitive system, social tool, and evolving technology. From phonemes to presuppositions, from dialects to diphthongs, these concepts reveal the systematic nature of human language in all its complexity and diversity.
