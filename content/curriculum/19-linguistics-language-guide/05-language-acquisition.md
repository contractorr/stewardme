# Language Acquisition: How We Learn Language

## Overview

Language acquisition examines how humans learn language—both as children acquiring their first language (L1) and as learners acquiring additional languages (L2+). The speed and universality of child language acquisition, despite limited and noisy input, poses one of the most fascinating puzzles in cognitive science.

Children master complex grammar by age 4-5 without explicit instruction, suggesting specialized learning mechanisms. Understanding acquisition illuminates the nature of language itself and informs education, speech pathology, and theories of mind.

## First Language Acquisition

### The Poverty of the Stimulus

**Chomsky's Argument:** Children acquire language despite:

1. **Underdetermination**: Input doesn't explicitly show all grammatical rules
   - Children hear "What did you eat?" not "*What you did eat?"
   - But they know second is wrong without being told

2. **Degenerate input**: Speech contains errors, false starts, incomplete sentences
   - Yet children extract perfect grammar

3. **No negative evidence**: Children rarely corrected for grammar (only meaning)
   - Parents correct "I goed store" → "You went to the store" (meaning), not "You went to the store" (grammar)

**Conclusion:** Must have innate linguistic knowledge (Universal Grammar) guiding acquisition

### Stages of First Language Acquisition

#### Pre-linguistic Stage (0-12 months)

**0-2 months: Crying, cooing**
- Reflexive vocalizations

**2-6 months: Babbling**
- **Reduplicated babbling**: [bababa], [mamama]
- Practicing articulatory control

**6-12 months: Canonical babbling**
- **Variegated babbling**: [badaga], [maniba]
- Sound inventory narrows to native language
- **Perceptual narrowing**: Lose ability to distinguish non-native contrasts
  - Japanese infants can distinguish /r/ vs. /l/ at 6 months, not at 12 months

**Language-specific listening preference emerges by 6 months**

#### One-Word Stage (12-18 months)

**Characteristics:**
- Single words express whole ideas (holophrastic speech)
- "Milk!" = "I want milk" or "That is milk" or "Where is milk?"

**Vocabulary:**
- First words: *mama, dada, ball, dog, more*
- ~50 words by 18 months

**Comprehension exceeds production** (understand more than they say)

#### Two-Word Stage (18-24 months)

**Characteristics:**
- Word combinations show grammatical relations
- "Daddy shoe" (possession), "More cookie" (recurrence), "Sit chair" (action-location)

**Telegraphic speech**: Content words, few function words
- "Want cookie" not "I want a cookie"
- Omit articles, auxiliaries, inflections

**Examples:**
- "Baby sleep"
- "Throw ball"
- "Mommy sock" (could mean "Mommy's sock" or "Mommy, put on my sock")

#### Multi-Word Stage (24+ months)

**24-30 months:**
- 3+ word utterances
- Mean Length of Utterance (MLU) increases (~2.5 morphemes)
- Grammatical morphemes emerge: *-ing, -ed, plural -s, possessive 's*

**Example progression:**
- "Baby sleeping" (present progressive)
- "Two cookies" (plural)
- "Daddy's car" (possessive)

**30-36 months:**
- MLU ~3-4 morphemes
- Complex sentences with coordination: "I want cookie and milk"
- Questions: "Where Daddy go?"

**36+ months:**
- Embedding and recursion: "I think that he went home"
- Passive voice (later, ~4-5 years)
- Full auxiliary system

**By age 5:**
- Most core grammar acquired
- Vocabulary 10,000+ words (learning ~10 words/day from 18 months)

### Order of Morpheme Acquisition

**Brown's 14 morphemes** (acquired in predictable order, regardless of input frequency):

| Order | Morpheme | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 1-2 | Present progressive -*ing* | "Baby sleeping" |
| 1-2 | Plural -*s* | "Two dogs" |
| 3 | Irregular past | "Went, came, fell" |
| 4 | Possessive *'s* | "Daddy's hat" |
| 5 | Copula *be* | "He is tall" |
| 6 | Articles *a, the* | "A dog, the cat" |
| 7 | Regular past -*ed* | "Walked, played" |
| 8 | Third person -*s* | "She runs" |
| 9 | Auxiliary *be* | "He is running" |

**Why this order?**
- Complexity (syntactic, semantic)
- Perceptual salience
- Frequency interacts but doesn't determine order

### Overregularization

**Definition:** Applying rules too broadly

**Classic example:**
- Stage 1: *went* (correct, memorized irregular)
- Stage 2: *goed* (overregularization after learning -*ed* rule)
- Stage 3: *went* (correct, learns exception)

**Other examples:**
- *foots* (plural)
- *mans*
- *eated*

**Significance:** Shows rule learning, not just imitation
- Children say forms they've never heard
- Demonstrates active grammar construction

### Critical Period Hypothesis

**Lenneberg (1967):** Language acquisition most successful before puberty

**Evidence:**

**1. Deaf children and sign language:**
- Exposed to ASL from birth: Native-like fluency
- Exposed after age 12: Never achieve native fluency (grammar especially impaired)

**2. Genie (feral child):**
- Discovered age 13, had no language exposure
- Learned vocabulary but never mastered grammar
- Critical period passed

**3. Second language acquisition:**
- Younger learners achieve better pronunciation, grammar
- Adults can learn languages but rarely achieve native-like competence

**Mechanisms:**
- Neural plasticity decreases with age
- Maturational changes in brain
- Sensitive period for phonology (early) vs. syntax (later)

**Debate:** Hard critical period vs. gradual decline?

### Nature vs. Nurture

#### Nativist View (Chomsky)

**Universal Grammar (UG):** Innate linguistic knowledge
- Children born with language faculty
- UG provides principles (structure-dependence) + parameters (word order)
- Input triggers parameter-setting

**Evidence:**
- Poverty of stimulus
- Universal acquisition stages
- Creolization (children create grammar where none existed)

#### Empiricist/Usage-Based View (Tomasello)

**Language learned from input** through general cognitive mechanisms
- Pattern recognition
- Analogy
- Statistical learning

**Evidence:**
- Input frequency affects acquisition
- Item-based learning before abstract rules
- Children make errors UG should prevent

#### Interactionist View

**Both innate predispositions and input critical**
- Social interaction scaffolds learning
- Joint attention, motherese (child-directed speech)
- Cognitive development (Piaget) interacts with language

**Motherese/Child-Directed Speech (CDS):**
- Higher pitch, exaggerated intonation
- Slower, clearer articulation
- Simplified syntax, repetition
- Facilitates learning (debated whether necessary)

### Special Cases

#### Nicaraguan Sign Language (NSL)

**1970s:** Deaf children brought together in schools (no shared sign language)
- Cohort 1: Created pidgin-like system (no consistent grammar)
- Cohort 2 (younger children): Transformed pidgin into full language with grammar
- **Creolization** in one generation

**Significance:** Children spontaneously create grammar

#### Williams Syndrome

- Genetic disorder; intellectual disability (IQ ~60)
- **Relatively preserved language** (fluent speech, rich vocabulary)
- Dissociation of language from general cognition
- Suggests language as separate module

#### Specific Language Impairment (SLI)

- Normal intelligence
- **Selective language deficits** (especially grammar)
- Opposite of Williams Syndrome
- Further evidence for modularity

## Second Language Acquisition (L2)

### L1 vs. L2 Acquisition

| Factor | L1 | L2 |
|--------|----|----|
| **Age** | Childhood | Variable |
| **Success** | Universal success | Variable outcomes |
| **Ultimate attainment** | Native competence | Rare for late learners |
| **Fossilization** | N/A | Common (errors become permanent) |
| **Critical period** | Before puberty | Decreasing facility with age |
| **Explicit instruction** | Not needed | Can help |

### Transfer and Interlanguage

**Transfer:** L1 influences L2

**Positive Transfer:**
- Spanish *hospital* → English *hospital* (similar)
- French grammar helps with Spanish

**Negative Transfer (Interference):**
- Spanish speaker: "*He no like it*" (Spanish negation pattern)
- Mandarin speaker: "*Yesterday I go store*" (no past tense in Mandarin)
- French speaker: "*I am reading book*" (French doesn't require article)

**Interlanguage (Selinker):** Learner's developing L2 system
- Not just errors; systematic grammar
- Evolves over time toward (but often not reaching) native norms
- Fossilization: Interlanguage stabilizes below native level

### Acquisition Orders and Sequences

**Morpheme order in L2 similar to L1:**
- *-ing* and plural -*s* acquired early
- Third person -*s* acquired late (even for advanced learners)

**Developmental sequences:** Fixed order regardless of L1
- English negation:
  1. External negation: "*No want*"
  2. Internal negation: "*I no want*"
  3. Auxiliary negation: "*I don't want*"

**Implication:** Universal acquisition processes beyond L1 transfer

### Input, Output, Interaction

**Comprehensible Input (Krashen):**
- Input slightly above current level (i+1)
- Acquisition through understanding messages
- Controversial: Is input sufficient?

**Output Hypothesis (Swain):**
- Producing language (not just understanding) drives learning
- Output reveals gaps in knowledge
- Forces syntactic processing

**Interaction Hypothesis (Long):**
- Negotiation of meaning during conversation crucial
- Clarification requests, recasts highlight errors
- Combines input and output

### Individual Differences

**Age:**
- Younger learners: Better ultimate attainment (especially phonology)
- Older learners: Faster initial progress (metalinguistic awareness)

**Motivation:**
- Integrative (join community) vs. instrumental (practical goals)
- Higher motivation → better outcomes

**Aptitude:**
- Phonetic coding ability
- Grammatical sensitivity
- Inductive learning ability
- Memory (working memory, vocabulary)

**Personality:**
- Extroverts: More interaction practice
- Low anxiety: Willing to take risks

**Learning Context:**
- Naturalistic (immersion) vs. instructed (classroom)
- Study abroad: Gains in fluency, colloquial language

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Behaviorism (Skinner)

**Idea:** Language learned through imitation, reinforcement

**Mechanism:**
- Child says "milk"
- Parent gives milk (reinforcement)
- Behavior strengthened

**Critique (Chomsky):**
- Children produce novel sentences never heard
- Overregularizations show rule-learning, not imitation
- Errors often not corrected
- **Poverty of stimulus** unexplained

### Universal Grammar (Chomsky)

**Idea:** Innate linguistic principles + parameters

**Parameters:**
- **Pro-drop**: Spanish allows *Hablo* (speak-1SG), English requires *I speak*
- **Head direction**: English head-initial (VO), Japanese head-final (OV)

**Acquisition:** Exposure sets parameters (like switches)

**Evidence:** Poverty of stimulus, universals, critical period

**Challenge:** Variability in acquisition timing, input effects

### Usage-Based / Emergentist (Tomasello, Bybee)

**Idea:** Grammar emerges from usage patterns

**Mechanisms:**
- **Frequency**: Common patterns entrenched
- **Analogy**: Extend patterns to new items
- **Item-based learning**: Specific constructions before abstractions

**Example:**
- "*I wanna X*" learned as chunk
- Later, abstract "*want to X*" rule

**Evidence:** Input frequency matters, gradual abstraction, prototype effects

**Challenge:** Doesn't explain acquisition despite limited input

### Connectionist Models

**Neural networks** learn patterns from input
- No explicit rules; weights adjusted by experience
- Captures frequency effects, gradual learning
- Past tense learning: *Rumelhart & McClelland* model learns irregular and regular forms

**Critique:** Needs more data than children get (still poverty of stimulus)

## Real-World Applications

### Language Teaching

**Insights from acquisition research:**
- **Comprehensible input**: Ensure materials at right level
- **Interaction**: Conversation practice, not just grammar drills
- **Natural order**: Don't expect mastery of late-acquired forms early
- **Critical period**: Early exposure for better outcomes

**Methods informed by SLA:**
- **Communicative Language Teaching**: Focus on meaning, not just form
- **Task-Based Learning**: Learn through doing (real-world tasks)
- **Immersion**: Content taught in L2 (successful for children)

### Speech-Language Pathology

**Assessment:**
- Distinguish language disorder from normal variation
- MLU, morpheme acquisition stage

**Intervention:**
- Facilitate development along normal trajectory
- Augmentative communication for severe impairments

### Bilingualism and Multilingualism

**Simultaneous bilingualism** (both from birth):
- Separate phonological, syntactic systems from early on
- Code-switching follows grammatical rules (not random mixing)
- Cognitive benefits: Executive function, metalinguistic awareness

**Sequential bilingualism** (L2 after L1 established):
- L1 influences L2 (transfer)
- Outcomes depend on age, input, motivation

**Myths debunked:**
- Bilingualism doesn't delay language development
- Doesn't confuse children
- No evidence for "one language at a time" rule

### Technology

**Language learning apps** (Duolingo, Babbel):
- Spaced repetition (memory research)
- Immediate feedback
- Limitations: Less conversational practice

**Speech recognition for children:**
- Adapted to child speech characteristics
- Reading tutors, language assessment

## Key Terms

- **L1 Acquisition**: First language learning (native language)
- **L2 Acquisition**: Second/additional language learning
- **Universal Grammar (UG)**: Innate linguistic knowledge
- **Poverty of Stimulus**: Input insufficient to explain acquisition
- **Critical Period**: Optimal window for language learning (before puberty)
- **Holophrastic Speech**: One-word utterances
- **Telegraphic Speech**: Multi-word utterances omitting function words
- **Overregularization**: Overapplying rules (*goed*)
- **Mean Length of Utterance (MLU)**: Average morphemes per utterance
- **Motherese/CDS**: Child-directed speech
- **Transfer**: L1 influence on L2
- **Interlanguage**: Learner's developing L2 grammar
- **Fossilization**: Errors becoming permanent in L2

## Summary

First language acquisition is remarkably uniform and rapid, with children mastering core grammar by age 5 despite limited input. Predictable stages—from babbling to one-word to multi-word speech—and systematic errors like overregularization reveal active grammar construction, not mere imitation.

The poverty of stimulus argument suggests innate Universal Grammar, though usage-based approaches emphasize learning from input through general cognitive mechanisms. The critical period hypothesis explains why early exposure yields better outcomes, particularly for phonology and syntax.

Second language acquisition differs from L1 in ultimate attainment, variability, and the role of transfer. Input, output, and interaction all contribute, with individual differences in age, motivation, and aptitude affecting outcomes.

Language acquisition research informs teaching methods (comprehensible input, communicative approaches), clinical practice (diagnosing and treating language disorders), and our understanding of human cognition (innate vs. learned, modularity, critical periods). The fact that all typically developing children acquire language, while achieving native L2 fluency is rare for adult learners, highlights the special status of language in human development.
