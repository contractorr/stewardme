# Phonetics & Phonology: The Sound Systems of Language

## Overview

Phonetics studies speech sounds as physical events—how they're produced, transmitted, and perceived. Phonology studies sound patterns and mental representations—how sounds function within a particular language's system. Together, they explain the sonic dimension of language, from articulatory mechanics to abstract sound categories.

**Key Distinction:**
- **Phonetics**: Physical properties of sounds (universal, applies to all languages)
- **Phonology**: Mental organization of sounds (language-specific patterns and rules)

## Part I: Phonetics

### What is Phonetics?

The scientific study of speech sounds, divided into three subfields:

| Subfield | Focus | Methods |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Articulatory** | How sounds are produced in vocal tract | Anatomy, imaging (MRI, ultrasound) |
| **Acoustic** | Physical properties of sound waves | Spectrograms, frequency analysis |
| **Auditory** | How sounds are perceived | Psychoacoustics, neuroimaging |

### Articulatory Phonetics

#### The Vocal Tract

**Sound Production Process:**
1. **Lungs** generate airflow
2. **Larynx** (voice box) houses vocal folds
3. **Pharynx, Oral cavity, Nasal cavity** shape sound

**Key Structures:**
- **Vocal folds**: Vibrate for voiced sounds (/b/, /z/), open for voiceless (/p/, /s/)
- **Velum** (soft palate): Raised for oral sounds, lowered for nasal (/m/, /n/)
- **Tongue**: Most flexible articulator (tip, blade, body, root)
- **Lips**: Rounded, spread, closed
- **Teeth, Alveolar ridge, Hard palate**: Places of articulation

#### Describing Consonants

Consonants characterized by three features:

##### 1. Voicing
- **Voiced**: Vocal folds vibrate (/b/, /d/, /g/, /v/, /z/)
- **Voiceless**: Vocal folds open (/p/, /t/, /k/, /f/, /s/)

*Test: Place hand on throat; feel vibration for voiced sounds*

##### 2. Place of Articulation

| Place | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Bilabial** | Two lips | /p/, /b/, /m/ (*p*at, *b*at, *m*at) |
| **Labiodental** | Lower lip + upper teeth | /f/, /v/ (*f*at, *v*at) |
| **Dental** | Tongue tip + teeth | /θ/, /ð/ (*th*in, *th*is) |
| **Alveolar** | Tongue tip + alveolar ridge | /t/, /d/, /s/, /z/, /n/ (*t*ip, *d*ip, *s*ip, *z*ip, *n*ip) |
| **Post-alveolar** | Tongue blade + behind alveolar | /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /tʃ/, /dʒ/ (*sh*ip, a*s*ure, *ch*ip, *j*ump) |
| **Retroflex** | Tongue tip curled back | /ɻ/ (American English *r*) |
| **Palatal** | Tongue body + hard palate | /j/ (*y*es) |
| **Velar** | Tongue body + soft palate | /k/, /g/, /ŋ/ (*k*ey, *g*o, si*ng*) |
| **Uvular** | Tongue root + uvula | French /ʁ/ |
| **Glottal** | Vocal folds | /h/, /ʔ/ (*h*at, uh-oh) |

##### 3. Manner of Articulation

| Manner | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Stop (Plosive)** | Complete closure, air released | /p/, /b/, /t/, /d/, /k/, /g/ |
| **Fricative** | Narrow constriction, turbulent airflow | /f/, /v/, /s/, /z/, /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /θ/, /ð/, /h/ |
| **Affricate** | Stop + fricative | /tʃ/ (church), /dʒ/ (judge) |
| **Nasal** | Oral closure, air through nose | /m/, /n/, /ŋ/ |
| **Liquid** | Partial closure | /l/ (lateral), /r/ |
| **Glide** | Vowel-like, but function as consonants | /w/, /j/ |

#### Describing Vowels

Vowels characterized by:

##### 1. Height (Tongue Position)
- **High**: /i/ (beet), /u/ (boot)
- **Mid**: /e/ (bait), /o/ (boat)
- **Low**: /æ/ (bat), /ɑ/ (bot)

##### 2. Backness
- **Front**: /i/ (beet), /ɪ/ (bit), /e/ (bait), /æ/ (bat)
- **Central**: /ə/ (about), /ʌ/ (but)
- **Back**: /u/ (boot), /ʊ/ (book), /o/ (boat), /ɑ/ (bot)

##### 3. Rounding
- **Rounded**: /u/ (boot), /o/ (boat)
- **Unrounded**: /i/ (beet), /æ/ (bat)

**Vowel Chart (English):**

```
         Front    Central    Back
High     i  ɪ                u  ʊ
Mid      e         ə          o
Low      æ         ʌ          ɑ
```

##### 4. Tenseness
- **Tense**: /i/, /e/, /u/, /o/ (longer, can end syllables)
- **Lax**: /ɪ/, /ɛ/, /ʊ/, /ɑ/ (shorter, typically need consonant after)

##### 5. Diphthongs
Vowels that glide from one quality to another:
- /aɪ/ (buy), /aʊ/ (cow), /ɔɪ/ (boy)

### Acoustic Phonetics

#### Sound Waves

**Frequency (Hz):** Pitch (high vs. low)
- Fundamental frequency (F0): Vocal fold vibration rate
- Men: ~120 Hz, Women: ~220 Hz, Children: ~300 Hz

**Amplitude (dB):** Loudness

**Duration (ms):** Length

#### Spectrograms

Visual representation of speech:
- **X-axis**: Time
- **Y-axis**: Frequency
- **Darkness**: Amplitude (energy)

**Formants:** Resonant frequencies of vocal tract
- **F1**: Correlates with vowel height (higher F1 = lower vowel)
- **F2**: Correlates with vowel backness (higher F2 = fronter vowel)
- **F3, F4...**: Additional resonances

**Example:**
- /i/ (beet): Low F1 (high vowel), high F2 (front vowel)
- /ɑ/ (bot): High F1 (low vowel), low F2 (back vowel)

**Consonant Cues:**
- **Voice Onset Time (VOT)**: Delay between consonant release and voicing
  - /p/: Long VOT (~60ms)
  - /b/: Short VOT (~0-20ms)
- **Formant transitions**: Frequency movement into/out of consonant

### Auditory Phonetics

**Categorical Perception:** Continuous acoustic variation perceived as discrete categories

**Example:**
- VOT continuum: 0ms → 60ms
- Speakers perceive sharp boundary (~25ms) between /b/ and /p/, not gradual change
- Within-category differences harder to distinguish than across-category

**McGurk Effect:** Visual input affects auditory perception
- Hear /ba/ + see /ga/ → perceive /da/
- Demonstrates multimodal speech perception

### Suprasegmentals (Prosody)

Features extending beyond single segments:

#### Stress
- **Lexical stress**: Word-level prominence
  - "REcord" (noun) vs. "reCORD" (verb)
  - "DEsert" (noun) vs. "deSERT" (verb)

#### Tone
- Pitch used to distinguish word meaning (40-70% of world languages)

**Mandarin Chinese:**
- mā (妈, high level tone) "mother"
- má (麻, rising tone) "hemp"
- mǎ (马, falling-rising tone) "horse"
- mà (骂, falling tone) "scold"

**Tone Types:**
- **Level**: High, mid, low
- **Contour**: Rising, falling, falling-rising

#### Intonation
- Sentence-level pitch patterns

**English:**
- Falling: Statements "I'm going home ↓"
- Rising: Yes/no questions "Are you going home ↑?"
- Fall-rise: Uncertainty "Maybe ↓↑"

## Part II: Phonology

### What is Phonology?

Study of sound patterns and mental representations

**Key Insight:** Sounds (phones) vs. mental categories (phonemes)

### Phonemes and Allophones

**Phoneme:** Minimal sound unit distinguishing meaning
- /p/ and /b/ are different phonemes in English (*pat* vs. *bat*)

**Allophone:** Variant of a phoneme
- [pʰ] (aspirated p in *pit*) vs. [p] (unaspirated p in *spit*) — same phoneme /p/

**Minimal Pairs:** Words differing in one phoneme
- *pat* /pæt/ vs. *bat* /bæt/ → /p/ and /b/ are phonemes
- *cat* /kæt/ vs. *kit* /kɪt/ → /æ/ and /ɪ/ are phonemes

**Distribution:**
- **Complementary Distribution**: Allophones appear in non-overlapping environments
  - [pʰ] appears word-initially (*pat*)
  - [p] appears after /s/ (*spat*)
  - Never contrast → allophones of /p/

- **Contrastive Distribution**: Phonemes can appear in same environment, creating different words
  - /p/ and /b/ both word-initial (*pat*, *bat*) → different phonemes

### Phonological Rules

**Format:** A → B / C __ D
- "A becomes B in the context of C before and D after"

#### Common Processes

**Assimilation:** Segment becomes more like neighboring sound

**Examples:**
- **Nasal place assimilation**: /n/ → [m] before bilabials
  - *in*possible → [ɪmpɑsəbəl] (n → m before p)
  - *in*credible → [ɪnkrɛdəbəl] (n → ŋ before k)

- **Vowel nasalization**: Vowels become nasal before nasal consonants
  - *pan* [pʰæ̃n] (nasal vowel)

**Palatalization:** Consonants become palatal before front vowels
- *did you* → [dɪdʒu] (d → dʒ before j)

**Deletion (Elision):**
- /t/ deletion: *exactly* [ɪgˈzækli] (t deleted)
- Vowel reduction: *photography* [fəˈtɑgrəfi] (o → ə)

**Insertion (Epenthesis):**
- Prince → *prince[ə]s* (some speakers)

**Metathesis:** Sound reordering
- *ask* → [æks] (some dialects)

#### Feature-Based Rules

Sounds characterized by binary features:

| Feature | + | - |
|---------|---|---|
| [voice] | Voiced (b, d, g) | Voiceless (p, t, k) |
| [nasal] | Nasal (m, n) | Oral (b, d) |
| [continuant] | Fricatives (f, s) | Stops (p, t) |
| [anterior] | Front (p, t, s) | Back (k, ʃ) |

**Advantage:** Capture natural classes
- /p, t, k/ = [-voice, -continuant] (voiceless stops)
- Rules apply to classes, not individual sounds

### Syllable Structure

**Syllable Parts:**
- **Onset**: Initial consonant(s) — *pl* in *play*
- **Nucleus**: Vowel (required) — *a* in *play*
- **Coda**: Final consonant(s) — *st* in *fast*
- **Rhyme**: Nucleus + Coda

**Structure:**
```
   Syllable
   /      \
 Onset   Rhyme
         /    \
     Nucleus  Coda
```

**Syllable Types:**
- **CV**: *me* (open syllable)
- **CVC**: *met* (closed syllable)
- **CCVC**: *stop*
- **V**: *I*

**Phonotactic Constraints:** Language-specific rules on syllable structure

**English:**
- Onset: Up to 3 consonants (*str*eet), but not any 3 (*ptak*)
- Coda: Up to 4 consonants (te*xts* /tɛksts/)
- /ŋ/ cannot begin words (*ngoc* impossible)

**Japanese:**
- Simple syllables: (C)V
- No codas except /n/: *Honda* [hon.da], *Sakura* [sa.ku.ɾa]

### Phonological Typology

**Consonant Inventories:**
- Small: Hawaiian (~13 phonemes)
- Large: !Xóõ (Khoisan language, ~120 phonemes with clicks)

**Vowel Inventories:**
- Small: Abkhaz (2 vowels: /ə, a/)
- Large: English (11-15 depending on dialect)

**Tonal Systems:**
- Non-tonal: English, Spanish
- 2-tone: Zulu (high, low)
- 4-tone: Mandarin
- 5-tone: Cantonese, Thai
- 9-tone: Vietnamese (including contours)

**Syllable Structure:**
- Simple: Japanese, Hawaiian (mostly CV)
- Complex: English, Russian, Georgian (many consonant clusters)

### Stress and Metrical Phonology

**Stress Patterns:**
- **Fixed**: Always same position
  - Czech: First syllable
  - French: Final syllable
  - Polish: Penultimate syllable

- **Weight-sensitive**: Depends on syllable structure
  - Heavy syllables (CVV, CVC) attract stress
  - Light syllables (CV) avoid stress
  - Latin: Stress penultimate if heavy, else antepenultimate

- **Free**: Unpredictable (English, Russian)
  - Must be learned per word

### Autosegmental Phonology

**Tone as separate tier:**

```
Mandarin "ma":
Syllable tier:    m   a
Tone tier:        [H] [R] [FR] [F]
                  ↓   ↓   ↓    ↓
                  mā  má  mǎ   mà
```

**Explains:**
- Tone spreading
- Stability: Tone persists even when vowel deleted

### Optimality Theory (OT)

**Approach:** Phonology as constraint interaction, not rules

**Constraints:**
- **Faithfulness**: Output matches input (preserve contrasts)
- **Markedness**: Avoid complex structures

**Key Idea:** Constraints ranked; higher-ranked constraint violations avoided

**Example (English epenthesis):**
- Input: /pæt + z/ "pats"
- Candidates: [pæts], [pætz], [pætəz]
- Winner: [pæts] (satisfies high-ranked constraint against complex codas)

## Real-World Applications

**Speech Recognition:**
- Acoustic models map sound waves to phonemes
- Phonological rules handle variation (*got you* → *gotcha*)

**Speech Synthesis:**
- Phonological rules generate natural-sounding speech
- Prosody (stress, intonation) for intelligibility

**Language Teaching:**
- Phonetics trains learners to produce/perceive non-native sounds
- Minimal pair drills exploit phonemic contrasts

**Speech Pathology:**
- Diagnose articulation disorders (phonetic) vs. phonological disorders
- Target intervention appropriately

**Writing Systems:**
- Alphabets based on phonemic principle (one letter ≈ one phoneme)
- Deep orthographies (English) vs. shallow (Spanish, Finnish)

**Forensic Phonetics:**
- Speaker identification via voice prints
- Dialect analysis in legal cases

## Key Terms

**Phonetics:**
- **Phonetics**: Study of physical speech sounds
- **Articulation**: Production of sounds in vocal tract
- **Voicing**: Vocal fold vibration
- **Place of Articulation**: Where constriction occurs
- **Manner of Articulation**: How airflow is modified
- **Formant**: Resonant frequency of vocal tract
- **VOT**: Voice Onset Time
- **Prosody**: Suprasegmental features (stress, tone, intonation)

**Phonology:**
- **Phoneme**: Mental category distinguishing meaning
- **Allophone**: Phonetic variant of a phoneme
- **Minimal Pair**: Words differing in one phoneme
- **Phonological Rule**: Pattern describing sound changes
- **Syllable**: Organizational unit (onset + nucleus + coda)
- **Phonotactics**: Constraints on sound combinations
- **Assimilation**: Sound becomes like neighbor

## Summary

Phonetics provides the physical foundation of language through articulatory, acoustic, and auditory perspectives. Consonants are described by voicing, place, and manner; vowels by height, backness, and rounding. Spectrograms reveal acoustic properties, while categorical perception shows how continuous signals map to discrete categories.

Phonology abstracts from phonetics to mental representations and patterns. Phonemes contrast to distinguish meanings, while allophones vary predictably by context. Phonological rules capture systematic sound patterns through processes like assimilation and deletion. Syllable structure and phonotactics constrain possible sound sequences.

Cross-linguistic variation is immense—from Hawaiian's 13 phonemes to !Xóõ's 120, from Japanese's simple syllables to Georgian's complex clusters, from non-tonal languages to 9-tone systems. Yet universal tendencies emerge, suggesting constraints from articulatory ease and perceptual distinctness.

Phonetics and phonology underpin practical applications from speech technology to language teaching to forensic analysis, revealing the intricate sound systems that carry linguistic information from speaker to hearer.
