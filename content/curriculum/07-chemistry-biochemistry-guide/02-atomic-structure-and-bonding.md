# Atomic Structure and Chemical Bonding

## Overview

Understanding atoms and how they bond is fundamental to all chemistry. This chapter explores atomic structure, the periodic table's organization, and the forces that hold molecules together. Chemical properties emerge from electron configuration, and molecular function depends on bond types and geometries.

## Atomic Structure

### Components of Atoms

**Protons (p⁺)**:
- Positive charge (+1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
- Mass: 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (1 atomic mass unit, amu)
- Located in nucleus
- Number of protons = atomic number (Z)
- Defines element identity

**Neutrons (n⁰)**:
- No charge (neutral)
- Mass: 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (≈1 amu)
- Located in nucleus
- Isotopes differ by neutron number
- Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons

**Electrons (e⁻)**:
- Negative charge (-1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
- Mass: 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg (≈1/1836 amu, negligible)
- Occupy orbitals around nucleus
- Determine chemical properties
- Neutral atom: # protons = # electrons

### Isotopes

Atoms of the same element with different neutron numbers:

- **Carbon-12 (¹²C)**: 6 protons, 6 neutrons (98.9% abundance)
- **Carbon-13 (¹³C)**: 6 protons, 7 neutrons (1.1% abundance)
- **Carbon-14 (¹⁴C)**: 6 protons, 8 neutrons (radioactive, trace amounts)

**Applications**:
- Radiocarbon dating uses ¹⁴C decay
- Medical imaging uses radioactive isotopes (PET scans)
- NMR spectroscopy uses ¹H, ¹³C to determine molecular structure

## Electron Configuration

### Quantum Numbers

Four quantum numbers describe each electron:

1. **Principal (n)**: Energy level; n = 1, 2, 3...
   - Higher n = farther from nucleus, higher energy

2. **Angular momentum (l)**: Orbital shape; l = 0 to (n-1)
   - l = 0: s orbital (spherical)
   - l = 1: p orbital (dumbbell)
   - l = 2: d orbital (complex)
   - l = 3: f orbital (very complex)

3. **Magnetic (mₗ)**: Orbital orientation; mₗ = -l to +l
   - p orbitals: three orientations (px, py, pz)
   - d orbitals: five orientations

4. **Spin (mₛ)**: Electron spin; mₛ = +½ or -½
   - Two electrons per orbital (opposite spins)

### Aufbau Principle & Electron Filling

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy:

**Filling Order**: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p...

**Rules**:
1. **Aufbau**: Fill lowest energy orbitals first
2. **Pauli Exclusion**: Maximum two electrons per orbital, opposite spins
3. **Hund's Rule**: Within a subshell, maximize unpaired electrons

**Examples**:

| Element | Atomic # | Electron Configuration | Shorthand |
|---------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| H | 1 | 1s¹ | [H] |
| C | 6 | 1s² 2s² 2p² | [He] 2s² 2p² |
| N | 7 | 1s² 2s² 2p³ | [He] 2s² 2p³ |
| O | 8 | 1s² 2s² 2p⁴ | [He] 2s² 2p⁴ |
| Ne | 10 | 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ | [He] 2s² 2p⁶ |
| Na | 11 | 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹ | [Ne] 3s¹ |
| Fe | 26 | 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶ | [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶ |

### Valence Electrons

**Valence electrons**: Outermost shell electrons; determine chemical reactivity

- **Group 1** (Li, Na, K): 1 valence electron
- **Group 17** (F, Cl, Br): 7 valence electrons
- **Group 18** (He, Ne, Ar): 8 valence electrons (stable, noble gases)

**Octet Rule**: Atoms gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons (stable noble gas configuration)

## The Periodic Table

### Organization

- **Periods** (rows): Elements in same period have same number of electron shells
- **Groups** (columns): Elements in same group have same valence electron configuration, similar properties

### Key Groups

| Group | Name | Valence e⁻ | Properties |
|-------|------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Alkali metals | 1 | Highly reactive; soft metals; +1 ions |
| 2 | Alkaline earth metals | 2 | Reactive; +2 ions |
| 13-16 | Main group | 3-6 | Varied properties |
| 17 | Halogens | 7 | Highly reactive nonmetals; -1 ions |
| 18 | Noble gases | 8 (2 for He) | Inert; minimal reactivity |
| 3-12 | Transition metals | Variable | Form colored compounds; multiple oxidation states |

### Periodic Trends

**Atomic Radius**:
- Increases down a group (more electron shells)
- Decreases across a period (more protons pull electrons closer)

**Ionization Energy** (energy to remove electron):
- Decreases down a group (electrons farther from nucleus)
- Increases across a period (stronger nuclear attraction)

**Electronegativity** (tendency to attract electrons):
- Decreases down a group
- Increases across a period
- Fluorine most electronegative (4.0); cesium/francium least (0.7)

**Electron Affinity** (energy change adding electron):
- Generally increases across period
- Halogens have high electron affinity (want to complete octet)

## Chemical Bonding

### Ionic Bonding

**Mechanism**: Transfer of electrons from metal to nonmetal

**Example**: Sodium chloride (NaCl)
- Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (loses 1 electron)
- Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (gains 1 electron)
- Electrostatic attraction holds Na⁺ and Cl⁻ together

**Properties**:
- High melting/boiling points
- Brittle crystals
- Conduct electricity when dissolved (ions mobile)
- Soluble in polar solvents (water)

**Other Examples**:
- MgO (magnesium oxide)
- CaF₂ (calcium fluoride)
- K₂S (potassium sulfide)

### Covalent Bonding

**Mechanism**: Sharing of electrons between nonmetals

**Types**:
- **Single bond**: One shared electron pair (H-H)
- **Double bond**: Two shared electron pairs (O=O)
- **Triple bond**: Three shared electron pairs (N≡N)

**Example**: Water (H₂O)
- Oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogens
- Each O-H bond is one shared pair

**Polar vs Nonpolar Covalent**:

**Nonpolar covalent**: Equal sharing (ΔEN < 0.5)
- H₂, Cl₂, CH₄ (methane)
- Symmetrical electron distribution

**Polar covalent**: Unequal sharing (0.5 < ΔEN < 2.0)
- H₂O, HCl, NH₃
- Partial charges: δ+ and δ-
- More electronegative atom attracts electrons more

**Properties**:
- Lower melting/boiling points than ionic compounds
- Poor electrical conductivity
- Soluble in nonpolar solvents (like dissolves like)

### Metallic Bonding

**Mechanism**: Metal atoms share a "sea" of delocalized electrons

**Properties**:
- Conductivity (electricity and heat)
- Malleability (can be hammered into sheets)
- Ductility (can be drawn into wires)
- Luster (shiny)

### Bond Polarity and Electronegativity

**Electronegativity difference** (ΔEN) predicts bond type:

| ΔEN | Bond Type | Example |
|-----|-----------|---------|
| < 0.5 | Nonpolar covalent | H-H (0.0), C-H (0.4) |
| 0.5-2.0 | Polar covalent | O-H (1.4), C-O (1.0) |
| > 2.0 | Ionic | Na-Cl (2.1), Ca-F (3.0) |

## Lewis Structures

### Drawing Lewis Structures

**Steps**:
1. Count total valence electrons
2. Connect atoms with single bonds
3. Distribute remaining electrons to complete octets
4. Form multiple bonds if needed
5. Check formal charges

**Example: Carbon dioxide (CO₂)**
- Total valence electrons: 4 (C) + 2×6 (O) = 16
- Structure: O=C=O
- Each atom has octet (8 electrons)

**Example: Ammonia (NH₃)**
- Total valence electrons: 5 (N) + 3×1 (H) = 8
- Structure: N with three single bonds to H, one lone pair
- N has octet; each H has 2 electrons (duet)

### Resonance Structures

Multiple valid Lewis structures for same molecule:

**Example: Benzene (C₆H₆)**
- Two structures with alternating single/double bonds
- Reality: electrons delocalized, all C-C bonds equivalent
- Resonance stabilizes molecule

**Example: Carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻)**
- Three resonance structures
- Each C-O bond has partial double bond character

### Formal Charge

**Formula**: Formal charge = (valence e⁻) - (lone pair e⁻) - ½(bonding e⁻)

Best Lewis structure:
- Minimize formal charges
- Negative charges on more electronegative atoms
- Positive charges on less electronegative atoms

## Molecular Geometry (VSEPR Theory)

**VSEPR**: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion

Electron pairs repel; molecular shape minimizes repulsion.

### Common Geometries

| Electron Pairs | Bonding | Lone Pairs | Geometry | Bond Angle | Example |
|----------------|---------|------------|----------|------------|---------|
| 2 | 2 | 0 | Linear | 180° | CO₂ |
| 3 | 3 | 0 | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃ |
| 3 | 2 | 1 | Bent | <120° | SO₂ |
| 4 | 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄ |
| 4 | 3 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | <109.5° | NH₃ |
| 4 | 2 | 2 | Bent | <109.5° | H₂O |
| 5 | 5 | 0 | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120° | PCl₅ |
| 6 | 6 | 0 | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

**Key Insight**: Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles

## Intermolecular Forces

Forces between molecules (not within molecules):

### Types (Weakest to Strongest)

**1. London Dispersion Forces (Van der Waals)**
- Present in all molecules
- Temporary dipoles from electron movement
- Strength increases with molecular size/surface area
- Example: I₂ (solid), CH₄ (gas)

**2. Dipole-Dipole Interactions**
- Between polar molecules
- Permanent partial charges attract
- Stronger than London forces
- Example: HCl, acetone

**3. Hydrogen Bonding**
- Special dipole-dipole interaction
- H bonded to N, O, or F interacts with lone pair on another N, O, or F
- Unusually strong (10-40 kJ/mol)
- Examples: H₂O, NH₃, DNA base pairs, protein folding

**Comparison**:
- Covalent bond: ~400 kJ/mol
- Hydrogen bond: ~20 kJ/mol
- Dipole-dipole: ~5 kJ/mol
- London forces: ~1 kJ/mol

### Effect on Properties

**Boiling Points**:
- Stronger intermolecular forces → higher boiling point
- H₂O boils at 100°C (hydrogen bonding)
- H₂S boils at -60°C (only dipole-dipole, no hydrogen bonding)

**Solubility**:
- "Like dissolves like"
- Polar solutes dissolve in polar solvents (water)
- Nonpolar solutes dissolve in nonpolar solvents (hexane)

## Biochemical Relevance

### Protein Structure
- **Covalent bonds**: Peptide bonds link amino acids
- **Hydrogen bonds**: Stabilize α-helices and β-sheets
- **Disulfide bonds**: Covalent S-S bonds between cysteines
- **Ionic interactions**: Between charged residues
- **Hydrophobic effect**: Nonpolar residues cluster away from water

### DNA Structure
- **Phosphodiester bonds**: Covalent backbone
- **Hydrogen bonds**: A-T (2 bonds), G-C (3 bonds) base pairing
- **Base stacking**: London forces between aromatic rings stabilize helix

### Membrane Structure
- **Phospholipids**: Polar heads, nonpolar tails
- **Bilayer formation**: Driven by hydrophobic effect
- **Fluidity**: London forces between lipid tails

### Enzyme Catalysis
- **Active site binding**: Hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, hydrophobic effects position substrate
- **Transition state stabilization**: Multiple weak interactions lower activation energy

## Key Terms

- **Atom**: Smallest unit of element; nucleus plus electrons
- **Ion**: Atom with unequal protons and electrons
- **Isotope**: Same element, different neutron count
- **Valence electrons**: Outermost electrons; determine reactivity
- **Ionic bond**: Electron transfer; electrostatic attraction
- **Covalent bond**: Electron sharing between atoms
- **Electronegativity**: Tendency to attract electrons in bond
- **Polar molecule**: Unequal charge distribution
- **Hydrogen bond**: Strong dipole interaction involving H-N/O/F
- **VSEPR**: Theory predicting molecular geometry from electron repulsion

## Summary

Atoms consist of protons, neutrons, and electrons, with electrons determining chemical behavior. Electron configurations follow quantum mechanical rules, filling orbitals in specific order. The periodic table organizes elements by electron configuration, with trends in atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity. Chemical bonds form as atoms achieve stable electron configurations: ionic bonds transfer electrons, covalent bonds share electrons, and metallic bonds involve delocalized electrons. Bond polarity depends on electronegativity differences. Lewis structures represent bonding, while VSEPR theory predicts three-dimensional molecular geometry. Intermolecular forces—London dispersion, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding—determine physical properties and are crucial in biochemistry, stabilizing proteins, DNA, and membranes. Understanding atomic structure and bonding is essential for predicting chemical reactivity and biological function.
