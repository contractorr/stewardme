# Glossary

## A

**Acetyl-CoA**: Coenzyme A carrying a two-carbon acetyl group; central metabolite linking glycolysis to citric acid cycle; precursor for fatty acid synthesis.

**Acid**: Proton (H⁺) donor; substance that increases [H⁺] in solution; pH < 7.

**Activation Energy (Eₐ)**: Minimum energy required for reactants to reach transition state and undergo reaction; lowered by catalysts.

**Active Site**: Specific region of enzyme where substrate binds and catalysis occurs; contains catalytic residues.

**Active Transport**: Movement of molecules across membrane against concentration gradient using energy (ATP or electrochemical gradient).

**Adenine (A)**: Purine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with thymine (DNA) or uracil (RNA).

**ADP (Adenosine Diphosphate)**: Nucleotide with two phosphate groups; product of ATP hydrolysis; phosphorylated to regenerate ATP.

**Aerobic Respiration**: Cellular respiration requiring oxygen; produces ~32 ATP per glucose through glycolysis, citric acid cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.

**Aldehyde**: Organic compound with carbonyl group (C=O) at end of carbon chain; general structure R-CHO.

**Alkane**: Saturated hydrocarbon with only single C-C bonds; general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂; relatively unreactive.

**Alkene**: Unsaturated hydrocarbon with at least one C=C double bond; general formula CₙH₂ₙ; more reactive than alkanes.

**Allosteric Regulation**: Regulation of enzyme activity by binding of molecule at site other than active site, causing conformational change.

**Alpha Helix (α-helix)**: Common secondary protein structure; right-handed coil stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms; 3.6 residues per turn.

**Amino Acid**: Building block of proteins; contains amino group (NH₂), carboxyl group (COOH), and variable R group (side chain); 20 standard types.

**AMPK (AMP-Activated Protein Kinase)**: Energy sensor activated when cellular energy (ATP) low; activates catabolic pathways and inhibits anabolic pathways.

**Amphipathic**: Molecule having both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions; characteristic of phospholipids and detergents.

**Anabolism**: Biosynthetic metabolic pathways that build complex molecules from simpler precursors; requires energy input.

**Anaerobic**: Process occurring without oxygen; fermentation is anaerobic.

**Anticodon**: Three-nucleotide sequence on tRNA complementary to mRNA codon; ensures correct amino acid incorporation during translation.

**Apoptosis**: Programmed cell death; controlled process removing damaged or unnecessary cells without inflammation.

**Aromatic**: Cyclic, planar molecule with delocalized π electrons providing unusual stability; benzene is prototypical aromatic compound.

**Atom**: Smallest unit of element retaining chemical properties; consists of nucleus (protons, neutrons) and electrons.

**ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)**: Universal energy currency of cells; hydrolysis to ADP + Pᵢ releases ~7.3 kcal/mol (standard conditions); regenerated continuously.

**ATP Synthase**: Enzyme complex synthesizing ATP from ADP + Pᵢ; driven by proton gradient across membrane; rotary mechanism; Complex V of oxidative phosphorylation.

**Avogadro's Number (Nₐ)**: 6.022 × 10²³; number of particles (atoms, molecules) in one mole.

## B

**Base**: Proton (H⁺) acceptor; substance that decreases [H⁺] in solution; pH > 7.

**Base Pairing**: Hydrogen bonding between complementary nitrogenous bases; A-T (or A-U) and G-C in nucleic acids.

**Beta Oxidation (β-Oxidation)**: Catabolic pathway breaking fatty acids into two-carbon acetyl-CoA units; occurs in mitochondria; produces NADH and FADH₂.

**Beta Sheet (β-sheet)**: Common secondary protein structure; extended strands connected by hydrogen bonds; can be parallel or antiparallel.

**Buffer**: Solution resisting pH change when acid or base added; consists of weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and conjugate acid).

## C

**Carbohydrate**: Biomolecule composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (approximate formula (CH₂O)ₙ); includes sugars, starches, cellulose; functions in energy storage and structure.

**Carboxyl Group**: Functional group -COOH; acidic; characteristic of carboxylic acids and amino acids.

**Catalyst**: Substance increasing reaction rate without being consumed; lowers activation energy; enzymes are biological catalysts.

**Catabolism**: Metabolic pathways breaking down complex molecules into simpler ones; releases energy.

**Cation**: Positively charged ion; formed by loss of electrons (e.g., Na⁺, Ca²⁺).

**Cell Membrane**: Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins surrounding cell; selectively permeable; controls molecular passage.

**Cellulose**: Polysaccharide of β-1,4-linked glucose; structural component of plant cell walls; most abundant organic molecule on Earth; humans cannot digest.

**Chemiosmotic Coupling**: Mechanism linking electron transport to ATP synthesis via proton gradient across membrane; proposed by Peter Mitchell.

**Chiral**: Molecule with non-superimposable mirror image; has handedness; occurs at carbon with four different substituents.

**Cholesterol**: Steroid lipid with four fused rings; component of animal cell membranes; precursor for steroid hormones and bile acids.

**Chromosome**: DNA molecule complexed with proteins; contains genes; humans have 46 (23 pairs).

**Citric Acid Cycle**: Central metabolic pathway oxidizing acetyl-CoA to CO₂; produces NADH, FADH₂, and GTP; occurs in mitochondrial matrix; also called Krebs cycle or TCA cycle.

**Codon**: Three-nucleotide sequence on mRNA specifying particular amino acid or stop signal during translation.

**Coenzyme**: Organic cofactor required for enzyme activity; often derived from vitamins; examples include NAD⁺, FAD, Coenzyme A.

**Cofactor**: Non-protein component required for enzyme activity; can be metal ion or coenzyme.

**Competitive Inhibition**: Enzyme inhibition where inhibitor binds active site, competing with substrate; increases apparent Kₘ; Vₘₐₓ unchanged.

**Complementary Base Pairing**: Specific hydrogen bonding between nucleotide bases; A pairs with T (DNA) or U (RNA); G pairs with C.

**Compound**: Substance composed of two or more elements in fixed ratio; has properties distinct from constituent elements.

**Concentration Gradient**: Difference in solute concentration across space; molecules tend to diffuse down gradient (high to low concentration).

**Condensation Reaction**: Chemical reaction joining two molecules with removal of small molecule (usually water); forms peptide bonds, ester bonds, glycosidic bonds.

**Covalent Bond**: Chemical bond formed by sharing electron pair(s) between atoms; can be single, double, or triple bond.

**Cytosine (C)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with guanine.

## D

**Denaturation**: Loss of protein or nucleic acid structure without breaking covalent bonds; caused by heat, pH changes, chemicals; often reversible.

**Deoxyribose**: Five-carbon sugar in DNA; lacks hydroxyl group at 2' position compared to ribose.

**Diffusion**: Net movement of molecules from region of high concentration to low concentration; driven by entropy increase.

**Dipeptide**: Molecule consisting of two amino acids linked by peptide bond.

**Disaccharide**: Carbohydrate composed of two monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bond; examples include sucrose, lactose, maltose.

**Disulfide Bond**: Covalent S-S bond between two cysteine residues; stabilizes protein tertiary structure.

**DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)**: Double-stranded nucleic acid storing genetic information; composed of nucleotides with deoxyribose sugar and bases A, T, G, C.

**Double Helix**: Three-dimensional structure of DNA; two antiparallel strands wound around common axis; proposed by Watson and Crick.

## E

**Electronegativity**: Tendency of atom to attract electrons in chemical bond; fluorine most electronegative (4.0); affects bond polarity.

**Electron**: Negatively charged subatomic particle orbiting atomic nucleus; determines chemical properties; mass ~1/1836 of proton.

**Electron Transport Chain**: Series of protein complexes in inner mitochondrial membrane transferring electrons from NADH and FADH₂ to oxygen; pumps protons creating gradient driving ATP synthesis.

**Element**: Pure substance consisting of only one type of atom; cannot be broken down by chemical means; organized in periodic table.

**Enantiomer**: One of two non-superimposable mirror image molecules; optical isomer; often has different biological activity.

**Endergonic**: Reaction requiring energy input; ΔG > 0; non-spontaneous; characteristic of anabolic pathways.

**Endocytosis**: Process by which cell takes in material by forming vesicles from plasma membrane; types include phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated.

**Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)**: Membrane network in eukaryotic cells; rough ER has ribosomes (protein synthesis); smooth ER lacks ribosomes (lipid synthesis, detoxification).

**Endothermic**: Reaction absorbing heat; ΔH > 0; surroundings cool.

**Enthalpy (H)**: Heat content of system; change in enthalpy (ΔH) reflects heat absorbed or released at constant pressure.

**Entropy (S)**: Measure of disorder or randomness; second law of thermodynamics states total entropy increases in spontaneous processes.

**Enzyme**: Biological catalyst, typically protein, that accelerates chemical reaction without being consumed; highly specific and efficient.

**Equilibrium**: State where forward and reverse reaction rates equal; concentrations constant; ΔG = 0.

**Ester**: Organic compound formed by condensation of carboxylic acid and alcohol; general structure R-COO-R'; found in fats and oils.

**Exergonic**: Reaction releasing energy; ΔG < 0; spontaneous; characteristic of catabolic pathways.

**Exocytosis**: Process by which cell expels material via fusion of vesicle with plasma membrane; releases contents to extracellular space.

**Exon**: Coding sequence in eukaryotic gene; transcribed and retained in mature mRNA; contrasts with intron.

**Exothermic**: Reaction releasing heat; ΔH < 0; surroundings warm.

## F

**FAD/FADH₂**: Flavin adenine dinucleotide; electron carrier in oxidation-reduction reactions; derived from riboflavin (vitamin B₂).

**Fatty Acid**: Long hydrocarbon chain with carboxyl group; building block of lipids; saturated (no double bonds) or unsaturated (one or more double bonds).

**Fermentation**: Anaerobic catabolism regenerating NAD⁺ for glycolysis; produces lactate or ethanol + CO₂; yields only 2 ATP per glucose.

**First Law of Thermodynamics**: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted between forms; ΔE = q + w.

**Functional Group**: Specific atom arrangement determining chemical reactivity and properties; examples include hydroxyl (-OH), carboxyl (-COOH), amino (-NH₂).

## G

**Gene**: Segment of DNA encoding functional product (protein or RNA); unit of heredity.

**Gene Expression**: Process by which genetic information converted to functional product; involves transcription and translation.

**Genome**: Complete set of genetic material (DNA) in organism.

**Gibbs Free Energy (G)**: Thermodynamic potential predicting spontaneity; ΔG = ΔH - TΔS; ΔG < 0 indicates spontaneous reaction.

**Gluconeogenesis**: Anabolic pathway synthesizing glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (lactate, amino acids, glycerol); primarily in liver.

**Glucose**: Six-carbon monosaccharide (C₆H₁₂O₆); most abundant sugar; primary energy source for cells.

**Glycerol**: Three-carbon alcohol with three hydroxyl groups; component of triacylglycerols and phospholipids.

**Glycogen**: Highly branched polysaccharide of α-1,4 and α-1,6-linked glucose; primary carbohydrate storage in animals; found in liver and muscle.

**Glycolysis**: Metabolic pathway converting glucose to two pyruvate molecules; produces 2 ATP and 2 NADH; occurs in cytoplasm.

**Glycosidic Bond**: Covalent bond linking monosaccharides in di- and polysaccharides; formed by condensation reaction.

**GPCR (G Protein-Coupled Receptor)**: Large family (~800 in humans) of cell surface receptors; seven transmembrane helices; activate G proteins upon ligand binding.

**Guanine (G)**: Purine nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA; pairs with cytosine.

## H

**Half-Life**: Time required for half of substance to decay or be eliminated; applies to radioactive isotopes, proteins, mRNAs.

**Helix**: Spiral structure; α-helix is common protein secondary structure; DNA forms double helix.

**Hemoglobin**: Oxygen-transport protein in red blood cells; contains four subunits, each with heme group; exhibits cooperative binding.

**Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation**: pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA]); relates pH to ratio of conjugate base to acid; used for buffer calculations.

**Heterogeneous Catalyst**: Catalyst in different phase than reactants; example: solid platinum catalyzing gas-phase reactions.

**Hexose**: Six-carbon monosaccharide; examples include glucose, fructose, galactose.

**Homogeneous Catalyst**: Catalyst in same phase as reactants; example: H₂SO₄ catalyzing liquid-phase esterification.

**Hormone**: Signaling molecule produced in one location, transported via bloodstream, affects target cells elsewhere; types include peptides, steroids, amino acid derivatives.

**Hybridization**: Mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals; sp³ (tetrahedral), sp² (trigonal planar), sp (linear).

**Hydrocarbon**: Organic compound containing only carbon and hydrogen; includes alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, aromatics.

**Hydrogen Bond**: Strong dipole-dipole interaction between H bonded to N, O, or F and lone pair on another N, O, or F; ~20 kJ/mol; crucial for protein folding and DNA base pairing.

**Hydrolysis**: Cleavage of bond using water; reverse of condensation; breaks peptide bonds, ester bonds, glycosidic bonds.

**Hydrophilic**: "Water-loving"; polar or charged molecules that interact favorably with water.

**Hydrophobic**: "Water-fearing"; nonpolar molecules that do not interact favorably with water; tend to aggregate.

**Hydrophobic Effect**: Tendency of nonpolar molecules to aggregate in aqueous solution, minimizing contact with water; drives protein folding and membrane formation.

## I

**Induced Fit**: Model of enzyme-substrate binding where active site changes conformation upon substrate binding, optimizing catalysis.

**Inhibitor**: Molecule that decreases enzyme activity; can be competitive, noncompetitive, or uncompetitive.

**Integral Protein**: Membrane protein embedded in lipid bilayer; often spans membrane; hydrophobic regions interact with lipid tails.

**Intermolecular Force**: Attractive force between molecules; includes London dispersion, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding; weaker than covalent bonds.

**Intron**: Non-coding sequence in eukaryotic gene; transcribed but removed from pre-mRNA during splicing.

**Ion**: Atom or molecule with unequal number of protons and electrons; net charge; cation (positive) or anion (negative).

**Ionic Bond**: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions; formed by electron transfer; characteristic of salts.

**Ionization Energy**: Energy required to remove electron from atom; increases across period (left to right), decreases down group.

**Isomer**: Molecules with same molecular formula but different structures; types include constitutional, geometric, and enantiomers.

**Isotope**: Atoms of same element with different number of neutrons; same atomic number, different mass number.

## K

**Ketone**: Organic compound with carbonyl group (C=O) bonded to two carbon atoms; general structure R-CO-R'; less reactive than aldehydes.

**Kinase**: Enzyme catalyzing phosphorylation; transfers phosphate from ATP to substrate; key regulators of cell signaling and metabolism.

**Kinetics**: Study of reaction rates and mechanisms; enzyme kinetics described by Michaelis-Menten equation.

## L

**Le Châtelier's Principle**: System at equilibrium responds to stress by shifting to counteract change; applies to concentration, temperature, pressure changes.

**Ligand**: Molecule that binds to receptor or other protein; triggers biological response.

**Limiting Reactant**: Reactant consumed first in reaction; determines maximum product yield.

**Lipid**: Hydrophobic or amphipathic biomolecule; includes fatty acids, triacylglycerols, phospholipids, steroids; functions in energy storage, membranes, signaling.

**Lipid Bilayer**: Double layer of phospholipids forming cell membrane; hydrophobic tails face inward, hydrophilic heads face outward.

**Lock-and-Key Model**: Early model of enzyme-substrate binding proposing rigid active site complementary to substrate shape; largely superseded by induced fit model.

## M

**Macromolecule**: Large molecule composed of many repeating units; includes proteins, nucleic acids, polysaccharides; also called biopolymer.

**Metabolism**: Sum of all chemical reactions in organism; includes catabolism (breakdown) and anabolism (synthesis).

**Michaelis Constant (Kₘ)**: Substrate concentration at which reaction velocity equals half Vₘₐₓ; inverse measure of enzyme-substrate affinity.

**Mitochondria**: Organelles in eukaryotic cells performing oxidative phosphorylation; contain own DNA; "powerhouses of cell."

**Mole**: Amount of substance containing Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³) of particles; SI unit for amount.

**Molecular Formula**: Chemical formula showing exact number of each atom type in molecule; e.g., C₆H₁₂O₆ for glucose.

**Monomer**: Small molecule that can join with others to form polymer; amino acids are monomers of proteins.

**Monosaccharide**: Simple sugar that cannot be hydrolyzed to simpler carbohydrates; examples include glucose, fructose, ribose.

**mRNA (Messenger RNA)**: RNA molecule carrying genetic information from DNA to ribosome for protein synthesis; template for translation.

**Mutation**: Change in DNA sequence; can result from replication errors, radiation, chemicals; may affect protein function.

## N

**NAD⁺/NADH**: Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide; electron carrier in oxidation-reduction reactions; derived from niacin (vitamin B₃).

**Neutron**: Electrically neutral subatomic particle in atomic nucleus; mass ~1 amu; isotopes differ in neutron number.

**Noncompetitive Inhibition**: Enzyme inhibition where inhibitor binds site other than active site; decreases Vₘₐₓ; Kₘ unchanged.

**Nonpolar**: Molecule or bond with symmetrical electron distribution; no partial charges; hydrophobic; dissolves in nonpolar solvents.

**Nucleic Acid**: Biopolymer composed of nucleotides; includes DNA and RNA; stores and transmits genetic information.

**Nucleotide**: Building block of nucleic acids; consists of nitrogenous base, five-carbon sugar, and phosphate group(s); also functions as energy carrier (ATP) and signaling molecule (cAMP).

**Nucleus**: 1) Central dense core of atom containing protons and neutrons. 2) Membrane-enclosed organelle in eukaryotes containing chromosomes.

## O

**Octet Rule**: Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve eight valence electrons (noble gas configuration).

**Orbital**: Region around atomic nucleus where electron likely to be found; types include s (spherical), p (dumbbell), d, f.

**Organic Chemistry**: Branch of chemistry studying carbon-containing compounds.

**Oxidation**: Loss of electrons; increase in oxidation state; often involves loss of hydrogen or gain of oxygen.

**Oxidative Phosphorylation**: Process synthesizing ATP using energy from electron transport chain; occurs in inner mitochondrial membrane; produces ~28 ATP per glucose.

**Oxidation State**: Hypothetical charge on atom if all bonds were ionic; used to track electron transfer in redox reactions.

## P

**Peptide Bond**: Covalent amide bond linking amino acids in proteins; formed by condensation between carboxyl and amino groups; planar with partial double bond character.

**Periodic Table**: Arrangement of elements by increasing atomic number; elements in same group (column) have similar properties.

**pH**: Measure of acidity; pH = -log[H⁺]; pH < 7 acidic, pH = 7 neutral, pH > 7 basic.

**Phenotype**: Observable characteristics of organism; result from genotype and environmental interactions.

**Phosphate Group**: PO₄³⁻; important functional group in biological molecules; found in nucleotides, ATP, phospholipids.

**Phospholipid**: Amphipathic lipid with glycerol backbone, two fatty acids, and phosphate group; primary component of biological membranes.

**Phosphorylation**: Addition of phosphate group; often regulates protein activity; catalyzed by kinases; reversed by phosphatases.

**Photosynthesis**: Process by which plants and some bacteria convert light energy, CO₂, and H₂O into glucose and O₂.

**Polar**: Molecule or bond with asymmetric electron distribution; has partial charges (δ+ and δ-); hydrophilic; dissolves in polar solvents.

**Polymer**: Large molecule composed of many similar or identical subunits (monomers); examples include proteins, nucleic acids, polysaccharides.

**Polypeptide**: Polymer of amino acids linked by peptide bonds; another term for protein, especially referring to single chain.

**Polysaccharide**: Carbohydrate polymer of many monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds; examples include starch, glycogen, cellulose.

**Primary Structure**: Linear sequence of monomers in polymer; amino acid sequence in proteins; nucleotide sequence in nucleic acids.

**Product**: Substance formed in chemical reaction; appears on right side of reaction equation.

**Protein**: Macromolecule composed of one or more polypeptides; diverse functions include catalysis, structure, transport, defense, signaling.

**Proton**: Positively charged subatomic particle in atomic nucleus; mass ~1 amu; number of protons defines element (atomic number).

**Purine**: Double-ring nitrogenous base; adenine and guanine in DNA/RNA; larger than pyrimidines.

**Pyrimidine**: Single-ring nitrogenous base; cytosine, thymine (DNA), uracil (RNA); smaller than purines.

**Pyruvate**: Three-carbon product of glycolysis; converted to acetyl-CoA (aerobic) or lactate/ethanol (anaerobic).

## Q

**Quaternary Structure**: Arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits in multi-subunit protein; stabilized by same forces as tertiary structure.

**Quantum Number**: Number specifying property of atomic orbital or electron; includes principal (n), angular momentum (l), magnetic (mₗ), and spin (mₛ).

## R

**Reactant**: Starting substance in chemical reaction; appears on left side of reaction equation; consumed during reaction.

**Receptor**: Protein that binds specific signaling molecule (ligand) and initiates cellular response; types include GPCRs, RTKs, ion channels, intracellular.

**Redox Reaction**: Reaction involving transfer of electrons; includes oxidation (electron loss) and reduction (electron gain); mnemonic: OIL RIG.

**Reduction**: Gain of electrons; decrease in oxidation state; often involves gain of hydrogen or loss of oxygen.

**Resonance**: Delocalization of electrons across multiple atoms; molecule is hybrid of multiple Lewis structures; provides extra stability.

**Ribose**: Five-carbon sugar in RNA; has hydroxyl group at 2' position (unlike deoxyribose in DNA).

**Ribosome**: Molecular machine synthesizing proteins by translating mRNA; composed of rRNA and proteins; site of translation.

**RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)**: Single-stranded nucleic acid; contains ribose sugar and bases A, U, G, C; types include mRNA, tRNA, rRNA; roles in protein synthesis and regulation.

## S

**Saturated**: Molecule with no double or triple bonds; alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons; saturated fats lack double bonds in fatty acid chains.

**Second Law of Thermodynamics**: Total entropy of universe increases in spontaneous processes; energy transformations not 100% efficient.

**Secondary Structure**: Local folding patterns in protein or nucleic acid; α-helix and β-sheet in proteins; double helix in DNA.

**Signal Transduction**: Process converting extracellular signal into cellular response; involves receptors, second messengers, kinase cascades.

**Spontaneous**: Process that occurs without continuous external energy input; ΔG < 0; does not imply fast (thermodynamics vs kinetics).

**Starch**: Plant polysaccharide of α-1,4 and α-1,6-linked glucose; includes amylose (linear) and amylopectin (branched); energy storage.

**Steroid**: Lipid with four fused carbon rings; includes cholesterol and steroid hormones (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol).

**Stoichiometry**: Quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions; based on balanced equations and mole ratios.

**Substrate**: Reactant molecule binding to enzyme active site; undergoes chemical transformation.

**Synthesis**: Chemical reaction combining simpler substances into more complex product; anabolic process; A + B → AB.

## T

**Tertiary Structure**: Overall three-dimensional shape of single polypeptide chain; stabilized by hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, disulfide bonds, hydrophobic effect.

**Thermodynamics**: Study of energy and its transformations; first law (conservation), second law (entropy increases), concepts of enthalpy, entropy, free energy.

**Thymine (T)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base found only in DNA; pairs with adenine.

**Transcription**: Synthesis of RNA from DNA template; first step in gene expression; catalyzed by RNA polymerase.

**Transcription Factor**: Protein regulating gene transcription by binding DNA; includes general factors (required for all genes) and specific factors (regulate particular genes).

**Translation**: Synthesis of protein from mRNA template; occurs at ribosome; tRNA delivers amino acids specified by mRNA codons.

**Triacylglycerol**: Lipid consisting of glycerol esterified to three fatty acids; also called triglyceride; primary form of fat storage; 9 kcal/g.

**tRNA (Transfer RNA)**: RNA molecule delivering amino acids to ribosome during translation; contains anticodon complementary to mRNA codon.

## U

**Unsaturated**: Molecule containing double or triple bonds; alkenes and alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons; unsaturated fats have double bonds in fatty acid chains.

**Uracil (U)**: Pyrimidine nitrogenous base found only in RNA; replaces thymine; pairs with adenine.

## V

**Valence Electron**: Electron in outermost shell; determines chemical reactivity and bonding behavior.

**Van der Waals Forces**: Weak intermolecular attractions; includes London dispersion forces; contribute to protein folding and DNA stability.

**VSEPR Theory**: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion; predicts molecular geometry based on minimizing repulsion between electron pairs.

## W

**Water (H₂O)**: Polar molecule with unique properties crucial for life; solvent for biochemical reactions; participates in hydrolysis and condensation; high heat capacity and heat of vaporization; maximum density at 4°C.

## Z

**Zwitterion**: Molecule with both positive and negative charges but no net charge; amino acids exist as zwitterions at physiological pH (NH₃⁺-CHR-COO⁻).

**Zymogen**: Inactive enzyme precursor; activated by proteolytic cleavage; prevents premature activity; examples include digestive enzymes (pepsinogen, trypsinogen).

---

## Summary

This glossary provides concise definitions of key terms in chemistry and biochemistry covered throughout this guide. From fundamental concepts like atoms and bonds to complex processes like signal transduction and metabolism, these terms form the vocabulary necessary for understanding the molecular basis of life. Mastering this terminology enables clearer communication about chemical and biochemical phenomena and provides the foundation for more advanced study in medicine, pharmacology, biotechnology, and related fields. When encountering unfamiliar concepts, refer to this glossary as a quick reference to reinforce understanding and make connections across the diverse topics spanning chemistry and biochemistry.
