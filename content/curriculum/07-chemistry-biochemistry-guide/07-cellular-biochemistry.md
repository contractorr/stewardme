# Cellular Biochemistry

## Overview

Cellular biochemistry explores how biomolecules interact within cells to enable life processes. This chapter examines enzymes and catalysis, membrane structure and transport, signal transduction, gene expression, and cellular regulation—integrating chemistry with cell biology to understand how molecular interactions produce living systems.

## Enzymes and Catalysis

### Enzyme Properties

**Enzymes**: Biological catalysts, typically proteins (some RNAs)

**Characteristics**:
- **Specificity**: Recognize specific substrates
- **Efficiency**: Rate enhancement 10⁶ to 10¹⁷-fold
- **Regulation**: Activity controlled by cellular conditions
- **Unchanged**: Not consumed; can catalyze many reactions

**Enzyme vs Non-Enzyme Catalysts**:
- Higher specificity (substrate and reaction type)
- Operate under mild conditions (37°C, pH 7, aqueous)
- Regulated by cellular signals

### How Enzymes Work

**Active Site**: Specific region where substrate binds
- Small portion of total enzyme
- 3D cleft or crevice
- Complementary to substrate shape
- Contains catalytic residues

**Binding Models**:

**Lock-and-Key** (Fischer):
- Rigid active site
- Substrate fits like key in lock

**Induced Fit** (Koshland):
- Active site changes shape upon substrate binding
- More accurate model
- Conformational change optimizes catalysis

**Catalytic Mechanisms**:

1. **Proximity and orientation**: Brings reactants together in proper alignment
2. **Transition state stabilization**: Binds transition state more tightly than substrate
3. **Acid-base catalysis**: Donates/accepts protons
4. **Covalent catalysis**: Forms transient covalent intermediate
5. **Metal ion catalysis**: Metal cofactor participates directly
6. **Strain**: Distorts substrate toward transition state

### Enzyme Kinetics

**Michaelis-Menten Equation**:

**v = (Vₘₐₓ[S]) / (Kₘ + [S])**

- v: Reaction velocity
- Vₘₐₓ: Maximum velocity (all enzyme saturated)
- [S]: Substrate concentration
- Kₘ: Michaelis constant (substrate concentration at ½ Vₘₐₓ)

**Interpreting Kₘ**:
- **Low Kₘ**: High affinity (enzyme saturated at low [S])
- **High Kₘ**: Low affinity (requires high [S] to saturate)

**kcat (Turnover Number)**:
- Catalytic rate constant
- Reactions per second per enzyme molecule when saturated
- Ranges from 0.5 (lysozyme) to 40,000,000 (catalase)

**Catalytic Efficiency**: kcat/Kₘ
- Measure of enzyme performance
- Diffusion limit: ~10⁸ to 10⁹ M⁻¹s⁻¹
- Some enzymes reach this limit ("catalytically perfect")

### Enzyme Inhibition

**Irreversible Inhibition**:
- Covalent modification of enzyme
- Permanent inactivation (unless new enzyme synthesized)
- Example: Aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX enzymes

**Reversible Inhibition**:

**Competitive**:
- Inhibitor binds active site
- Competes with substrate
- Increases apparent Kₘ; Vₘₐₓ unchanged
- Overcome by increasing [S]

**Noncompetitive**:
- Inhibitor binds elsewhere (allosteric site)
- Changes enzyme shape, reducing activity
- Decreases Vₘₐₓ; Kₘ unchanged
- Cannot be overcome by increasing [S]

**Uncompetitive**:
- Inhibitor binds enzyme-substrate complex
- Decreases both Vₘₐₓ and Kₘ

**Examples**:
- **Competitive**: Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (cholesterol synthesis)
- **Noncompetitive**: Heavy metals (Pb, Hg) bind cysteines
- **Suicide inhibitors**: Substrate analogs that irreversibly inactivate after initial binding

### Enzyme Regulation

**Allosteric Regulation**:
- Binding at one site affects another site
- **Positive cooperativity**: Binding of one substrate facilitates binding of others
  - Example: Hemoglobin O₂ binding (sigmoidal curve)
- **Negative cooperativity**: Binding inhibits further binding

**Covalent Modification**:
- **Phosphorylation**: Kinases add phosphate; phosphatases remove
  - Can activate or inhibit depending on enzyme
  - Rapid, reversible
- **Acetylation, methylation, ubiquitination**: Other modifications

**Proteolytic Activation**:
- Zymogens (inactive precursors) cleaved to active form
- Example: Digestive enzymes (pepsinogen → pepsin, trypsinogen → trypsin)
- Irreversible activation

**Compartmentalization**:
- Separating enzymes and substrates
- Example: Lysosomal hydrolases active only at low pH in lysosomes

**Cofactors and Coenzymes**:
- **Cofactors**: Metal ions (Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cu²⁺)
- **Coenzymes**: Organic molecules (NAD⁺, FAD, Coenzyme A)
- Often derived from vitamins
- Required for activity (holoenzyme = apoenzyme + cofactor)

## Membrane Structure and Function

### Lipid Bilayer

**Structure**:
- Two leaflets of phospholipids
- Hydrophobic tails face inward
- Hydrophilic heads face aqueous environments
- Thickness: ~5 nm

**Properties**:
- **Selective permeability**: Barrier to charged/polar molecules
- **Fluidity**: Lateral movement of lipids
- **Asymmetry**: Inner and outer leaflets differ in composition

**Factors Affecting Fluidity**:

**Temperature**: Higher temperature → more fluid

**Saturation**:
- Saturated fatty acids: Pack tightly, less fluid
- Unsaturated fatty acids: Kinks prevent packing, more fluid

**Cholesterol**:
- At high temperature: Restrains movement (reduces fluidity)
- At low temperature: Prevents tight packing (increases fluidity)
- Buffer effect

### Membrane Proteins

**Types**:

**Integral (Intrinsic)**:
- Embedded in bilayer
- Transmembrane or embedded in one leaflet
- Hydrophobic regions interact with lipid tails
- Difficult to remove (require detergents)

**Peripheral (Extrinsic)**:
- Attached to surface
- Interact with lipid heads or integral proteins
- Easily removed (salt solutions)

**Functions**:
1. **Transport**: Channels, carriers
2. **Enzymatic**: Catalysis at membrane
3. **Signal transduction**: Receptors
4. **Cell recognition**: Glycoproteins
5. **Intercellular joining**: Adhesion proteins
6. **Attachment**: To cytoskeleton or extracellular matrix

**Fluid Mosaic Model** (Singer & Nicolson):
- Membrane is dynamic
- Proteins float in lipid bilayer
- Lateral movement common; flip-flop rare

### Membrane Transport

**Passive Transport** (No energy required):

**Simple Diffusion**:
- Small, nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂, N₂)
- Down concentration gradient
- Direct through bilayer

**Facilitated Diffusion**:
- Polar molecules, ions
- Through protein channels or carriers
- Down concentration gradient
- Faster than simple diffusion

**Channel Proteins**:
- Form pores (aquaporins for water, ion channels)
- Gated (open/close in response to signals)

**Carrier Proteins**:
- Bind substrate, undergo conformational change
- Example: GLUT transporters (glucose)

**Active Transport** (Requires energy):

**Primary Active Transport**:
- Uses ATP directly
- Example: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump
  - Exports 3 Na⁺, imports 2 K⁺ per ATP
  - Maintains resting membrane potential
  - ~30% of cell's ATP

**Secondary Active Transport**:
- Uses electrochemical gradient established by primary transport
- **Symport**: Both molecules same direction
  - Example: Na⁺-glucose cotransporter (intestine)
- **Antiport**: Molecules opposite directions
  - Example: Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger

**Bulk Transport**:
- **Endocytosis**: Material enters cell in vesicles
  - Phagocytosis (large particles)
  - Pinocytosis (fluids)
  - Receptor-mediated (specific molecules)
- **Exocytosis**: Vesicles fuse with membrane, release contents

## Signal Transduction

### Overview

Cells respond to external signals (hormones, neurotransmitters, light) through cascades that amplify signals and produce responses.

**General Pathway**:
1. **Signal molecule** binds receptor
2. **Receptor activation** triggers intracellular cascade
3. **Amplification** through second messengers and kinase cascades
4. **Response**: Gene expression, enzyme activation, cytoskeletal changes

### Receptor Types

**G Protein-Coupled Receptors (GPCRs)**:
- Largest receptor family (~800 in humans)
- Seven transmembrane helices
- Activate G proteins upon ligand binding
- Examples: β-adrenergic receptors, odorant receptors

**Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs)**:
- Single transmembrane helix
- Ligand binding causes dimerization
- Autophosphorylation of tyrosine residues
- Recruits signaling proteins
- Examples: Insulin receptor, growth factor receptors

**Ion Channel Receptors**:
- Ligand-gated channels
- Binding opens channel
- Example: Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (neuromuscular junction)

**Intracellular Receptors**:
- Cytoplasmic or nuclear
- Lipophilic ligands cross membrane
- Directly regulate gene expression
- Examples: Steroid hormone receptors, thyroid hormone receptors

### Second Messengers

**cAMP (Cyclic AMP)**:
- Synthesized from ATP by adenylyl cyclase
- Degraded by phosphodiesterase
- Activates protein kinase A (PKA)
- Example: Epinephrine → β-receptor → G protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → glycogen breakdown

**Ca²⁺**:
- Released from endoplasmic reticulum
- Binds calmodulin → activates calmodulin-dependent kinases
- Example: Muscle contraction triggered by Ca²⁺ release

**IP₃ (Inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate)** and **DAG (Diacylglycerol)**:
- Produced from PIP₂ by phospholipase C
- IP₃ releases Ca²⁺ from ER
- DAG activates protein kinase C (PKC)

**cGMP (Cyclic GMP)**:
- Synthesized from GTP by guanylyl cyclase
- Example: Nitric oxide (NO) activates guanylyl cyclase → cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation (Viagra mechanism)

### Signal Amplification

**Cascade Effect**:
- One receptor activates many G proteins
- One G protein activates many adenylyl cyclase molecules
- One adenylyl cyclase produces many cAMP molecules
- One cAMP activates many PKA molecules
- Overall amplification: 10⁶-fold or more

### Termination Mechanisms

- **Receptor desensitization**: Phosphorylation reduces responsiveness
- **Second messenger degradation**: Phosphodiesterases, phosphatases
- **GTPase activity**: G proteins self-inactivate (hydrolyze GTP → GDP)
- **Endocytosis**: Receptor internalization

## Gene Expression Regulation

### Transcriptional Control

**Prokaryotes** (E. coli example):

**Lac Operon**:
- Genes for lactose metabolism
- **Repressor protein**: Binds operator, blocks transcription
- Lactose binds repressor → inactivates it → transcription proceeds
- Negative control

**Trp Operon**:
- Genes for tryptophan synthesis
- **Repressor** inactive without tryptophan
- Tryptophan (corepressor) binds repressor → activates it → blocks transcription
- Negative feedback

**Eukaryotes**:

More complex; chromatin structure, enhancers, multiple transcription factors.

**Transcription Factors**:
- **General**: Required for all genes (TFIID, etc.)
- **Specific**: Regulate particular genes (responsive to signals)

**Chromatin Remodeling**:
- **Histone acetylation**: Loosens chromatin; increases transcription
  - Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) add acetyl groups
- **Histone deacetylation**: Tightens chromatin; decreases transcription
  - Histone deacetylases (HDACs) remove acetyl groups
- **Methylation**: Can activate or repress depending on location

**Enhancers and Silencers**:
- Distant regulatory sequences
- Interact with promoters via DNA looping
- Tissue-specific and developmental regulation

### Post-Transcriptional Control

**mRNA Processing**:
- **Alternative splicing**: Different exon combinations → different proteins
  - Example: Tropomyosin (40+ isoforms from one gene)
- **5' cap and 3' poly-A tail**: Affect stability and translation

**mRNA Stability**:
- Half-life varies (minutes to days)
- RNA-binding proteins and sequences (AU-rich elements) affect degradation

**microRNAs (miRNAs)**:
- Small non-coding RNAs (~22 nucleotides)
- Bind complementary mRNA sequences
- Block translation or trigger degradation
- Regulate >60% of human genes

### Translational Control

**Initiation Factors**:
- Phosphorylation can activate or inhibit translation
- Example: eIF2 phosphorylation reduces global translation (stress response)

**Riboswitches**:
- mRNA structures that bind metabolites
- Conformational change affects translation or transcription

**Localization**:
- mRNAs transported to specific cellular locations
- Local translation (e.g., at synapses in neurons)

### Post-Translational Control

**Protein Modifications**:
- **Phosphorylation**: Adds/removes regulatory signals
- **Glycosylation**: Adds carbohydrates; affects folding, stability, localization
- **Ubiquitination**: Marks for degradation by proteasome
- **Acetylation, methylation, SUMOylation**: Various regulatory roles

**Protein Degradation**:
- **Proteasome**: Degrades ubiquitin-tagged proteins
- **Lysosomes**: Degrade membrane proteins, extracellular material
- Regulated half-lives control protein abundance

## Cellular Energy Sensors

### AMP-Activated Protein Kinase (AMPK)

**Function**: Energy sensor; activated when ATP low (AMP/ATP ratio high)

**Effects**:
- Activates catabolic pathways (glucose uptake, fatty acid oxidation)
- Inhibits anabolic pathways (fatty acid synthesis, protein synthesis)
- Mimicked by metformin (diabetes drug) and exercise

### mTOR (Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin)

**Function**: Nutrient sensor; activated by amino acids, insulin, growth factors

**Effects**:
- Promotes anabolism (protein synthesis, lipid synthesis)
- Promotes cell growth and proliferation
- Inhibited by rapamycin (immunosuppressant, potential anti-aging drug)

**mTOR vs AMPK**:
- mTOR: "Plenty of nutrients, grow!"
- AMPK: "Low energy, conserve!"
- Reciprocal regulation

## Cell Cycle and Checkpoints

**Phases**:
- **G1**: Growth, preparation for DNA synthesis
- **S**: DNA synthesis (replication)
- **G2**: Growth, preparation for mitosis
- **M**: Mitosis (cell division)
- **G0**: Quiescent (non-dividing)

**Cyclins and CDKs** (Cyclin-Dependent Kinases):
- Cyclins: Regulatory proteins (levels oscillate)
- CDKs: Catalytic kinases (levels constant)
- Cyclin binds CDK → activates it → phosphorylates targets → drives cell cycle progression

**Checkpoints**:
- **G1 checkpoint**: DNA damage? Growth signals present?
- **G2 checkpoint**: DNA fully replicated? DNA damage repaired?
- **M checkpoint**: All chromosomes attached to spindle?

**p53**: "Guardian of the genome"
- Activated by DNA damage
- Arrests cell cycle (allow repair) or triggers apoptosis
- Mutated in >50% of cancers

## Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death)

**Function**: Remove damaged, infected, or unnecessary cells

**Intrinsic Pathway** (mitochondrial):
- Triggered by internal stress (DNA damage, oxidative stress)
- Mitochondria release cytochrome c
- Activates caspases (proteases)

**Extrinsic Pathway** (death receptor):
- Triggered by external signals (immune cells)
- Death ligands (FasL, TNF) bind receptors
- Directly activates caspases

**Execution**:
- Caspases cleave cellular proteins
- DNA fragmentation
- Cell shrinks, forms apoptotic bodies
- Phagocytes engulf (no inflammation)

**vs Necrosis**:
- Apoptosis: Controlled, no inflammation
- Necrosis: Uncontrolled cell death, triggers inflammation

## Key Terms

- **Enzyme**: Biological catalyst (typically protein)
- **Active site**: Region where substrate binds and reaction occurs
- **Michaelis-Menten kinetics**: Describes enzyme reaction rates
- **Allosteric regulation**: Binding at one site affects another
- **Lipid bilayer**: Double layer of phospholipids forming membrane
- **Active transport**: Movement against gradient using energy
- **GPCR**: G protein-coupled receptor; large receptor family
- **Second messenger**: Intracellular signaling molecule (cAMP, Ca²⁺, IP₃)
- **Signal transduction**: Converting extracellular signal to cellular response
- **Transcription factor**: Protein regulating gene expression
- **Apoptosis**: Programmed cell death

## Summary

Cellular biochemistry integrates molecular interactions into living processes. Enzymes catalyze reactions with extraordinary specificity and efficiency, lowering activation energies through multiple mechanisms; their activity is regulated allosterically, by covalent modification, and through compartmentalization. Biological membranes, composed of lipid bilayers and embedded proteins, control molecular passage through passive diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport. Signal transduction pathways, utilizing GPCRs, RTKs, and other receptors, amplify external signals through second messengers and kinase cascades to produce cellular responses. Gene expression is regulated at transcriptional, post-transcriptional, translational, and post-translational levels, allowing cells to respond dynamically to environmental changes. Energy sensors like AMPK and mTOR coordinate metabolism with nutrient availability. Cell cycle checkpoints and p53 ensure genomic integrity, while apoptosis eliminates damaged cells without inflammation. Understanding cellular biochemistry reveals how the chemistry of life produces coordinated, responsive, self-regulating systems that maintain organization, respond to stimuli, grow, reproduce, and die—the fundamental characteristics distinguishing living from non-living matter.
