# Organic Chemistry Foundations

## Overview

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's unique bonding versatility creates millions of molecules essential to life and modern society. This chapter covers hydrocarbons, functional groups, isomerism, nomenclature, and reaction types that form the foundation for understanding biochemistry.

## Why Carbon?

### Carbon's Special Properties

**Tetravalent**: Four valence electrons allow four covalent bonds
- Forms single, double, and triple bonds
- Bonds with itself, creating chains and rings of unlimited length
- Forms stable bonds with H, O, N, S, P, halogens

**Catenation**: Ability to form long carbon chains
- More extensive than any other element
- Creates structural diversity

**Intermediate Electronegativity**: 2.5
- Forms polar and nonpolar bonds
- Enables diverse reactivity

Isomers: Same molecular formula, different structures

**2. Stereoisomers**

Same connectivity, different spatial arrangement.

**A. Geometric Isomers**
- Cis-trans isomers around double bonds
- Example: maleic acid (cis) vs fumaric acid (trans)

**B. Enantiomers (Optical Isomers)**
- Non-superimposable mirror images
- Occur at chiral centers (4 different groups on carbon)
- Rotate plane-polarized light opposite directions
- Often have different biological activity

**Example**: Amino acids (except glycine) are chiral
- L-amino acids in proteins
- D-amino acids rare in nature

**Chirality Importance**:
- Thalidomide: One enantiomer therapeutic, other caused birth defects
- Ibuprofen: Only S-enantiomer active
- Limonene: One smells like oranges, mirror image smells like lemons

## Nomenclature (IUPAC)

### Alkane Naming Rules

1. Find longest carbon chain (parent name)
2. Number chain from end nearest substituent
3. Name substituents with position numbers
4. Alphabetize substituents (ignore di-, tri-, etc.)
5. Use commas between numbers, hyphens between numbers and words

**Example**: 2,3-dimethylpentane
- 5-carbon chain (pentane)
- Methyl groups at positions 2 and 3

### Functional Group Naming

1. Identify functional group (determines suffix)
2. Number to give functional group lowest number
3. Name substituents

**Example**: 3-methyl-2-butanone
- 4-carbon chain (butane)
- Ketone at position 2 (-one suffix)
- Methyl group at position 3

## Biochemical Relevance

### Fatty Acids
- Long-chain carboxylic acids
- Saturated (no double bonds): Solid at room temperature
- Unsaturated (double bonds): Liquid at room temperature
- Cis double bonds create kinks (prevent tight packing)
- Trans fats: Artificially created, linked to heart disease

### Steroids
- Four fused carbon rings
- Cholesterol, testosterone, estrogen, cortisol
- Different functional groups create diverse functions

### Neurotransmitters
- Many are amines or amino acids
- Dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine
- Structure determines receptor binding

### Drug Design
- Functional groups determine:
  - Solubility (polar vs nonpolar)
  - Membrane permeability
  - Receptor binding
  - Metabolism and excretion

### Metabolic Reactions
- Many are organic reactions:
  - Oxidation of alcohols â†’ aldehydes/ketones
  - Ester formation and hydrolysis
  - Decarboxylation (removing COâ‚‚)
  - Transamination (transferring -NHâ‚‚ groups)

## Key Terms

- **Hydrocarbon**: Molecule containing only C and H
- **Alkane**: Saturated hydrocarbon (C-C single bonds)
- **Alkene**: Unsaturated hydrocarbon (C=C double bond)
- **Functional group**: Atom arrangement determining reactivity
- **Isomer**: Same formula, different structure
- **Enantiomer**: Non-superimposable mirror image molecules
- **Chiral**: Has non-superimposable mirror image
- **Addition reaction**: Adding atoms to multiple bond
- **Substitution reaction**: Replacing atom/group
- **Condensation**: Joining molecules with loss of water
- **Polymer**: Large molecule of repeating units
