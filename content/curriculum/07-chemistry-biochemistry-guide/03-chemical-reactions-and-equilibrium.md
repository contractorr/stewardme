# Chemical Reactions and Equilibrium

## Overview

Chemical reactions transform substances into new materials. Understanding reaction types, stoichiometry, thermodynamics, kinetics, and equilibrium is essential for predicting what happens when molecules interact. These principles govern everything from industrial processes to metabolic pathways.

## Types of Chemical Reactions

### Synthesis (Combination)
**A + B → AB**

Multiple reactants combine into one product.

**Examples**:
- 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (water formation)
- N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (Haber-Bosch process)
- Ca + O₂ → CaO (calcium oxide formation)

### Decomposition
**AB → A + B**

Single reactant breaks into multiple products.

**Examples**:
- 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (hydrogen peroxide decomposition)
- CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (limestone decomposition, requires heat)
- 2HgO → 2Hg + O₂ (mercury oxide decomposition)

### Single Replacement (Displacement)
**A + BC → AC + B**

One element replaces another in a compound.

**Examples**:
- Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ (zinc displaces hydrogen)
- Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag (copper displaces silver)
- 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ (sodium displaces hydrogen)

### Double Replacement (Metathesis)
**AB + CD → AD + CB**

Two compounds exchange components.

**Examples**:
- AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃ (precipitation)
- HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (neutralization)
- BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl (precipitation)

### Combustion
**Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy**

Rapid reaction with oxygen releasing energy.

**Examples**:
- CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (methane combustion)
- C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (glucose combustion/cellular respiration)
- 2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O (octane combustion in engines)

### Redox (Oxidation-Reduction)

**Oxidation**: Loss of electrons (increase in oxidation state)
**Reduction**: Gain of electrons (decrease in oxidation state)

**Mnemonic**: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain)

**Example**: Rusting of iron
- 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
- Fe oxidized: 0 → +3
- O reduced: 0 → -2

**Example**: Cellular respiration (glucose oxidation)
- C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O
- Glucose oxidized (loses electrons/hydrogen)
- Oxygen reduced (gains electrons)

## Stoichiometry

### Balancing Equations

**Law of Conservation of Mass**: Atoms cannot be created or destroyed.

**Example**: Combustion of propane
- Unbalanced: C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
- Balanced: C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

**Steps**:
1. Write unbalanced equation
2. Balance atoms that appear in fewest compounds first
3. Balance hydrogen and oxygen last
4. Check that all atoms balance

### Mole Calculations

**Mole**: 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)

**Example**: How many moles of CO₂ from 2 moles C₃H₈?
- C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
- Ratio: 1 mole C₃H₈ produces 3 moles CO₂
- 2 moles C₃H₈ × (3 moles CO₂ / 1 mole C₃H₈) = 6 moles CO₂

### Limiting Reactant

**Limiting reactant**: Reactant consumed first, limits product formation

**Example**:
- Reaction: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
- Available: 1 mole N₂, 2 moles H₂
- Ratio needed: 1:3
- Ratio available: 1:2 (insufficient H₂)
- H₂ is limiting reactant
- Maximum NH₃ = 2 moles H₂ × (2 moles NH₃ / 3 moles H₂) = 1.33 moles NH₃

### Percent Yield

**Theoretical yield**: Maximum product from stoichiometry
**Actual yield**: What's actually obtained
**Percent yield** = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%

## Thermodynamics

### First Law: Energy Conservation

**ΔE = q + w**

- ΔE: Change in internal energy
- q: Heat absorbed by system
- w: Work done on system

**In chemistry**: ΔH (enthalpy change) often used at constant pressure

### Enthalpy (H)

**Exothermic reaction**: ΔH < 0 (releases heat)
- Combustion reactions
- Formation of bonds
- Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O, ΔH = -890 kJ/mol

**Endothermic reaction**: ΔH > 0 (absorbs heat)
- Photosynthesis
- Breaking bonds
- Example: N₂ + O₂ → 2NO, ΔH = +181 kJ/mol

**Hess's Law**: Total enthalpy change is independent of pathway

### Entropy (S)

**Entropy**: Measure of disorder/randomness

**Second Law**: Total entropy of universe increases in spontaneous processes

**ΔS > 0**: Increase in disorder (favorable)
- Solid → liquid → gas
- Dissolution
- Increasing number of particles

**ΔS < 0**: Decrease in disorder (unfavorable)
- Gas → liquid → solid
- Precipitation
- Decreasing number of particles

### Gibbs Free Energy (G)

**ΔG = ΔH - TΔS**

Predicts spontaneity at constant temperature and pressure.

**ΔG < 0**: Spontaneous (exergonic)
- Reaction proceeds forward without input
- Examples: Combustion, cellular respiration

**ΔG > 0**: Non-spontaneous (endergonic)
- Requires energy input
- Examples: Photosynthesis, protein synthesis

**ΔG = 0**: Equilibrium

### Temperature Effects

| ΔH | ΔS | ΔG | Spontaneity |
|----|----|----|-------------|
| - | + | Always - | Always spontaneous |
| + | - | Always + | Never spontaneous |
| - | - | - at low T, + at high T | Spontaneous at low T |
| + | + | + at low T, - at high T | Spontaneous at high T |

**Example**: Melting ice
- ΔH > 0 (endothermic)
- ΔS > 0 (solid → liquid)
- Spontaneous above 0°C (TΔS overcomes ΔH)

## Chemical Kinetics

### Reaction Rate

**Rate**: Change in concentration over time

**Rate = Δ[concentration] / Δtime**

**Factors Affecting Rate**:
1. **Concentration**: Higher concentration → more collisions → faster rate
2. **Temperature**: Higher temperature → more energetic collisions → faster rate
3. **Surface area**: Larger area → more collisions → faster rate
4. **Catalysts**: Lower activation energy → faster rate

### Collision Theory

For reaction to occur:
1. Molecules must collide
2. Collision must have sufficient energy (≥ activation energy, Eₐ)
3. Collision must have proper orientation

**Activation Energy (Eₐ)**: Minimum energy needed for reaction

### Reaction Rate Laws

**For reaction**: aA + bB → products

**Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ**

- k: Rate constant (temperature dependent)
- m, n: Reaction orders (determined experimentally, not from stoichiometry)
- Overall order = m + n

**Example**: 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂
- Experimental rate law: Rate = k[NO]²[O₂]
- Second order in NO, first order in O₂
- Overall third order

### Catalysts

**Catalyst**: Substance that speeds reaction without being consumed

**Mechanism**:
- Provides alternative pathway
- Lowers activation energy
- Does not change ΔG (thermodynamics)
- Speeds forward and reverse reactions equally
- Reaches equilibrium faster

**Types**:
- **Homogeneous**: Same phase as reactants (H₂SO₄ in esterification)
- **Heterogeneous**: Different phase (solid Pt in catalytic converters)
- **Enzymes**: Biological catalysts (discussed in biochemistry chapters)

## Chemical Equilibrium

### Equilibrium Concept

**Equilibrium**: Forward and reverse reaction rates equal; concentrations constant

**A + B ⇌ C + D**

At equilibrium:
- Reactions still occur (dynamic equilibrium)
- No net change in concentrations
- Microscopic: constant molecular interconversion
- Macroscopic: appears static

### Equilibrium Constant (K)

**For**: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

**K = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ**

- Concentrations at equilibrium
- Products in numerator
- Reactants in denominator
- Coefficients become exponents
- Pure solids and liquids omitted

**Interpreting K**:
- K >> 1: Products favored; equilibrium far right
- K << 1: Reactants favored; equilibrium far left
- K ≈ 1: Comparable amounts of both

**Example**: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃
- K = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³)

### Le Châtelier's Principle

**Statement**: System at equilibrium responds to stress by shifting to counteract it

**Stress Types**:

**1. Concentration Change**
- Add reactant → shift right (toward products)
- Add product → shift left (toward reactants)
- Remove reactant → shift left
- Remove product → shift right

**2. Temperature Change**
- Increase temperature → shift toward endothermic direction
- Decrease temperature → shift toward exothermic direction

**3. Pressure Change** (gases only)
- Increase pressure → shift toward fewer moles of gas
- Decrease pressure → shift toward more moles of gas

**4. Catalyst**
- No shift in equilibrium position
- Reaches equilibrium faster

**Example**: Haber-Bosch process
- N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ + heat (exothermic)
- To increase NH₃ yield:
  - Increase pressure (4 moles gas → 2 moles gas)
  - Decrease temperature (exothermic favored)
  - Remove NH₃ as formed
  - Use iron catalyst (speeds equilibrium attainment)

## Acids and Bases

### Definitions

**Arrhenius**:
- Acid: Produces H⁺ in water
- Base: Produces OH⁻ in water

**Brønsted-Lowry** (more general):
- Acid: Proton (H⁺) donor
- Base: Proton (H⁺) acceptor

**Lewis** (most general):
- Acid: Electron pair acceptor
- Base: Electron pair donor

### pH Scale

**pH = -log[H⁺]**

**pOH = -log[OH⁻]**

**pH + pOH = 14** (at 25°C)

| pH | [H⁺] (M) | Type |
|----|----------|------|
| 0 | 10⁰ = 1 | Strongly acidic |
| 1-3 | 10⁻¹ to 10⁻³ | Acidic |
| 4-6 | 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁶ | Weakly acidic |
| 7 | 10⁻⁷ | Neutral |
| 8-10 | 10⁻⁸ to 10⁻¹⁰ | Weakly basic |
| 11-13 | 10⁻¹¹ to 10⁻¹³ | Basic |
| 14 | 10⁻¹⁴ | Strongly basic |

**Examples**:
- Stomach acid: pH ~2
- Lemon juice: pH ~2.3
- Blood: pH ~7.4
- Seawater: pH ~8.1
- Bleach: pH ~12.5

### Strong vs Weak Acids/Bases

**Strong acids** (completely dissociate):
- HCl, HBr, HI (hydrohalic acids)
- HNO₃ (nitric acid)
- H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid)
- HClO₄ (perchloric acid)

**Strong bases** (completely dissociate):
- Group 1 hydroxides: LiOH, NaOH, KOH
- Group 2 hydroxides: Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂

**Weak acids** (partially dissociate):
- CH₃COOH (acetic acid)
- H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
- H₃PO₄ (phosphoric acid)
- Most organic acids

**Weak bases** (partially ionize):
- NH₃ (ammonia)
- Amines (R-NH₂)

### Buffers

**Buffer**: Solution resisting pH change when acid or base added

**Composition**: Weak acid + its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid)

**Common Buffers**:
- Acetate: CH₃COOH / CH₃COO⁻
- Phosphate: H₂PO₄⁻ / HPO₄²⁻
- Carbonate: H₂CO₃ / HCO₃⁻

**Biological Importance**:
- Blood pH maintained at 7.4 by carbonate/bicarbonate buffer
- Small pH changes can denature proteins, disrupt metabolism
- Acidosis (pH < 7.35) or alkalosis (pH > 7.45) are medical emergencies

**Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation**:
**pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA])**

Used to calculate buffer pH or required concentrations.

## Oxidation States

**Rules for Assigning**:
1. Free element: 0 (O₂, Na, Fe)
2. Monatomic ion: Charge (Na⁺ = +1, Cl⁻ = -1)
3. Oxygen: Usually -2 (except peroxides: -1)
4. Hydrogen: +1 with nonmetals, -1 with metals
5. Sum equals overall charge

**Example**: MnO₄⁻
- O = -2 each, total -8
- Overall charge = -1
- Mn = +7

## Biochemical Applications

### Metabolic Reactions
- Redox reactions central to energy metabolism
- NAD⁺/NADH and FAD/FADH₂ are electron carriers
- Glycolysis, citric acid cycle, electron transport all involve redox

### Enzyme Kinetics
- Michaelis-Menten kinetics describe enzyme rates
- Enzymes don't change equilibrium, only speed to equilibrium
- Competitive/non-competitive inhibition affects rate

### Acid-Base Homeostasis
- Blood pH buffered primarily by H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻
- Respiratory system regulates CO₂ (acid)
- Kidneys regulate HCO₃⁻ (base)
- Amino acid ionization states depend on pH

### Le Châtelier in Physiology
- High altitude → low O₂ → hemoglobin equilibrium shifts → body produces more red blood cells
- Exercise → muscle produces CO₂ → blood pH drops → breathing rate increases to expel CO₂

## Key Terms

- **Stoichiometry**: Quantitative relationships in reactions
- **Limiting reactant**: Reactant consumed first
- **Enthalpy (ΔH)**: Heat content change
- **Entropy (ΔS)**: Disorder change
- **Gibbs free energy (ΔG)**: Predicts spontaneity
- **Activation energy (Eₐ)**: Energy barrier to reaction
- **Catalyst**: Speeds reaction without being consumed
- **Equilibrium**: Forward and reverse rates equal
- **Le Châtelier's principle**: Equilibrium shifts to oppose stress
- **pH**: Measure of acidity (-log[H⁺])
- **Buffer**: Resists pH change

## Summary

Chemical reactions transform substances through synthesis, decomposition, replacement, combustion, and redox processes. Stoichiometry quantifies these transformations using balanced equations and mole ratios. Thermodynamics—via enthalpy, entropy, and Gibbs free energy—predicts whether reactions are spontaneous. Kinetics describes reaction rates, influenced by concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts that lower activation energy. At equilibrium, forward and reverse rates balance, described by equilibrium constants; Le Châtelier's principle predicts shifts from perturbations. Acids donate protons, bases accept them; pH quantifies acidity, and buffers resist pH changes—critical for biological systems. These principles govern industrial processes like the Haber-Bosch synthesis, environmental phenomena like acid rain, and all biochemical pathways from glycolysis to DNA replication. Understanding reactions and equilibrium is essential for predicting chemical behavior in both laboratory and living systems.
