# Biochemical Molecules

## Overview

Four major classes of biomolecules—carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids—form the molecular machinery of life. Each class has distinctive structures and functions, from energy storage to genetic information to catalysis. Understanding their chemistry is essential for comprehending metabolism, genetics, and cell biology.

## Carbohydrates

### Structure and Classification

**General Formula**: (CH₂O)ₙ

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones.

**Classification by Size**:

**1. Monosaccharides** (simple sugars)
- Cannot be hydrolyzed to simpler carbohydrates
- General formula: CₙH₂ₙOₙ
- Usually 3-7 carbons

**2. Disaccharides**
- Two monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bond
- Can be hydrolyzed to monosaccharides

**3. Oligosaccharides**
- 3-10 monosaccharide units
- Often on cell surface as recognition markers

**4. Polysaccharides**
- Many monosaccharides (hundreds to thousands)
- Storage (starch, glycogen) or structural (cellulose, chitin)

### Monosaccharides

**Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)**:
- Most abundant monosaccharide
- Primary energy source for cells
- Aldohexose (6 carbons, aldehyde group)
- Exists as ring structure (pyranose, 6-membered)
- α-glucose vs β-glucose (OH orientation at C1)

**Other Hexoses**:
- **Fructose**: Ketohexose; fruit sugar; sweeter than glucose
- **Galactose**: Component of lactose; differs from glucose at C4
- **Mannose**: Differs from glucose at C2

**Pentoses**:
- **Ribose**: Component of RNA
- **Deoxyribose**: Component of DNA (lacks OH at C2)

### Disaccharides

| Disaccharide | Components | Linkage | Source |
|--------------|------------|---------|--------|
| **Sucrose** (table sugar) | Glucose + Fructose | α1→2 | Sugarcane, beets |
| **Lactose** (milk sugar) | Glucose + Galactose | β1→4 | Milk |
| **Maltose** (malt sugar) | Glucose + Glucose | α1→4 | Germinating grains |

**Glycosidic Bond**: Covalent bond between sugar molecules via condensation (loss of H₂O)

**Lactose Intolerance**: Insufficient lactase enzyme to hydrolyze lactose

### Polysaccharides

**Storage Polysaccharides**:

**Starch** (plants):
- **Amylose**: Linear α1→4 glucose polymer; helical structure
- **Amylopectin**: Branched; α1→4 main chain, α1→6 branches every ~25 residues
- Easily digested by α-amylase

**Glycogen** (animals):
- Similar to amylopectin but more branched (branches every ~10 residues)
- Stored in liver and muscle
- Rapid mobilization for energy

**Structural Polysaccharides**:

**Cellulose**:
- Linear β1→4 glucose polymer
- Straight chains held by hydrogen bonds (crystalline sheets)
- Most abundant organic molecule on Earth
- Humans cannot digest (lack β-glucosidase)
- Dietary fiber

**Chitin**:
- Modified glucose with N-acetyl group
- Structural component in arthropod exoskeletons, fungal cell walls
- β1→4 linkage like cellulose

### Carbohydrate Functions

1. **Energy**: Quick energy source (4 kcal/g)
2. **Storage**: Starch (plants), glycogen (animals)
3. **Structure**: Cellulose (plant cell walls), chitin (exoskeletons)
4. **Cell recognition**: Glycoproteins and glycolipids on cell surface
5. **Nucleic acids**: Ribose and deoxyribose in RNA/DNA

## Lipids

### Characteristics

- Hydrophobic or amphipathic (both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions)
- Mostly nonpolar; insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents
- Diverse structures (not polymers like other biomolecules)

### Fatty Acids

**Structure**: Long hydrocarbon chain with carboxylic acid group

**General Formula**: CH₃(CH₂)ₙCOOH

**Types**:

**Saturated**: No double bonds
- Palmitic acid (C16:0)
- Stearic acid (C18:0)
- Solid at room temperature (straight chains pack tightly)
- Found in animal fats, coconut oil

**Unsaturated**: One or more double bonds
- **Monounsaturated**: One double bond
  - Oleic acid (C18:1, ω-9)
- **Polyunsaturated**: Multiple double bonds
  - Linoleic acid (C18:2, ω-6) — essential fatty acid
  - α-Linolenic acid (C18:3, ω-3) — essential fatty acid

**Cis vs Trans**:
- **Cis**: Natural form; creates kink in chain
- **Trans**: Artificial (hydrogenation); straight chain; associated with cardiovascular disease

**Essential Fatty Acids**:
- ω-3 (EPA, DHA): Anti-inflammatory; brain development
- ω-6 (linoleic acid): Precursor to signaling molecules

### Triacylglycerols (Triglycerides)

**Structure**: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids (ester bonds)

**Function**:
- Energy storage (9 kcal/g — more than 2× carbohydrates/proteins)
- Anhydrous (no water needed for storage)
- Adipose tissue in animals
- Seeds and oils in plants

**Hydrolysis** (digestion):
- Lipases break ester bonds
- Products: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids

### Phospholipids

**Structure**: Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group + additional group

**Amphipathic**:
- Hydrophobic tails (fatty acids)
- Hydrophilic head (phosphate group)

**Examples**:
- **Phosphatidylcholine** (lecithin)
- **Phosphatidylethanolamine**
- **Phosphatidylserine**

**Function**:
- Primary component of cell membranes
- Form lipid bilayers (hydrophobic interior, hydrophilic surfaces)
- Membrane fluidity depends on saturation and cholesterol content

### Steroids

**Structure**: Four fused carbon rings (three 6-membered, one 5-membered)

**Cholesterol**:
- Precursor for all steroid hormones
- Component of animal cell membranes (modulates fluidity)
- Synthesized in liver
- Dietary sources: Animal products

**Steroid Hormones**:
- **Sex hormones**: Testosterone, estrogen, progesterone
- **Corticosteroids**: Cortisol (stress response), aldosterone (electrolyte balance)
- **Vitamin D**: Synthesized from cholesterol via UV light

### Other Lipids

**Waxes**: Long-chain fatty acid + long-chain alcohol
- Protective coatings (plant leaves, insect cuticles)

**Terpenes**: Built from isoprene units (C₅H₈)
- Vitamin A, carotenoids, menthol

**Prostaglandins**: 20-carbon fatty acid derivatives
- Signaling molecules; inflammation, pain, fever

## Proteins

### Amino Acid Building Blocks

**General Structure**: H₂N-CHR-COOH
- Amino group (NH₂)
- Carboxyl group (COOH)
- R group (side chain) — determines identity
- Central carbon (α-carbon)

**20 Standard Amino Acids**:

| Type | Properties | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Nonpolar, hydrophobic** | Repel water | Gly, Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Met, Phe, Trp, Pro |
| **Polar, uncharged** | Hydrophilic; H-bonding | Ser, Thr, Cys, Tyr, Asn, Gln |
| **Positively charged** (basic) | +charge at pH 7 | Lys, Arg, His |
| **Negatively charged** (acidic) | -charge at pH 7 | Asp, Glu |

**Special Amino Acids**:
- **Glycine**: Smallest; R = H; flexible
- **Proline**: Cyclic structure; creates kinks
- **Cysteine**: Contains thiol (-SH); forms disulfide bonds
- **Methionine**: Start codon (AUG) in protein synthesis

**Chirality**: All amino acids (except glycine) are L-isomers in proteins

### Peptide Bonds

**Formation**: Condensation between carboxyl of one amino acid and amino of another

**Peptide Bond**: C-N bond (partial double bond character due to resonance)
- Planar (no rotation)
- Trans configuration (R groups on opposite sides)

**Nomenclature**:
- Dipeptide: 2 amino acids
- Tripeptide: 3 amino acids
- Oligopeptide: Few amino acids
- Polypeptide/Protein: Many amino acids (typically >50)

**Directionality**: Written N-terminus → C-terminus

### Protein Structure Hierarchy

**Primary Structure (1°)**:
- Linear sequence of amino acids
- Determined by gene sequence
- Peptide bonds only

**Secondary Structure (2°)**:
- Local folding patterns
- Stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms

**α-Helix**:
- Right-handed spiral
- 3.6 residues per turn
- H-bonds between C=O of residue n and N-H of residue n+4
- Common in membrane-spanning regions

**β-Sheet**:
- Extended strands
- Hydrogen bonds between adjacent strands
- Parallel (same direction) or antiparallel (opposite directions)
- Pleated structure

**β-Turn**: Reverses chain direction; often at protein surface

**Tertiary Structure (3°)**:
- Overall 3D shape of single polypeptide
- Stabilized by:
  - **Hydrophobic interactions**: Nonpolar residues cluster in interior
  - **Hydrogen bonds**: Between R groups
  - **Ionic bonds** (salt bridges): Between charged R groups
  - **Disulfide bonds**: Covalent S-S bonds between cysteines
  - **Van der Waals forces**

**Quaternary Structure (4°)**:
- Assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits
- Same stabilizing forces as tertiary structure
- Example: Hemoglobin (4 subunits: 2α, 2β)

### Protein Folding

**Anfinsen's Principle**: Primary sequence determines tertiary structure

**Chaperones**: Proteins assisting proper folding
- Prevent aggregation
- Example: Heat shock proteins (HSPs)

**Denaturation**: Loss of structure without breaking peptide bonds
- Causes: Heat, pH changes, chemicals (urea, detergents)
- Often reversible if conditions restored

**Misfolding Diseases**:
- Alzheimer's: Amyloid-β plaques
- Prion diseases (Mad Cow): Misfolded PrP protein
- Many genetic diseases result from mutations causing misfolding

### Protein Functions

| Function | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Catalysis** | Enzymes (digestive enzymes, kinases) |
| **Structure** | Collagen, keratin, elastin |
| **Transport** | Hemoglobin (O₂), transferrin (iron) |
| **Storage** | Ferritin (iron), casein (milk protein) |
| **Defense** | Antibodies, complement proteins |
| **Movement** | Actin, myosin (muscle contraction) |
| **Signaling** | Hormones (insulin), receptors |
| **Regulation** | Transcription factors, cyclins |

## Nucleic Acids

### Nucleotide Building Blocks

**Components**:
1. **Nitrogenous base**
2. **Five-carbon sugar** (pentose)
3. **Phosphate group(s)**

**Nitrogenous Bases**:

**Purines** (double ring):
- **Adenine (A)**
- **Guanine (G)**

**Pyrimidines** (single ring):
- **Cytosine (C)** — DNA and RNA
- **Thymine (T)** — DNA only
- **Uracil (U)** — RNA only (replaces thymine)

**Mnemonic**: Purines are "PURe As Gold" (larger, double ring)

### DNA vs RNA

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Sugar** | Deoxyribose (no OH at C2') | Ribose (OH at C2') |
| **Bases** | A, T, G, C | A, U, G, C |
| **Structure** | Double helix | Usually single strand |
| **Stability** | Very stable | Less stable (2' OH reactive) |
| **Location** | Nucleus (eukaryotes) | Nucleus and cytoplasm |
| **Function** | Genetic information storage | Protein synthesis, regulation |

### DNA Structure

**Primary Structure**: Sequence of nucleotides
- Phosphodiester bonds link nucleotides
- 5' to 3' directionality (5' phosphate, 3' OH)
- Sugar-phosphate backbone

**Secondary Structure** (Watson-Crick):
- Double helix
- Antiparallel strands (one 5'→3', other 3'→5')
- Base pairing rules:
  - A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds)
  - G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)
- Complementary strands
- Major and minor grooves (protein binding sites)

**Stability**:
- Hydrogen bonds between bases
- Base stacking (hydrophobic interactions, Van der Waals)
- G-C rich regions more stable than A-T rich (3 vs 2 H-bonds)

**Tertiary Structure**:
- Supercoiling (underwound or overwound helix)
- Packaging with histones (eukaryotes)
- Circular DNA (prokaryotes, mitochondria, chloroplasts)

### RNA Types

**mRNA (messenger RNA)**:
- Carries genetic information from DNA to ribosome
- Codons (3-nucleotide sequences) specify amino acids
- 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail (eukaryotes)

**tRNA (transfer RNA)**:
- Delivers amino acids to ribosome
- Cloverleaf structure (2D), L-shape (3D)
- Anticodon pairs with mRNA codon
- Amino acid attached at 3' end

**rRNA (ribosomal RNA)**:
- Structural and catalytic component of ribosomes
- Peptidyl transferase activity (forms peptide bonds)
- Most abundant RNA

**Other RNAs**:
- **snRNA**: Splicing pre-mRNA
- **miRNA, siRNA**: Gene regulation
- **lncRNA**: Long non-coding RNA; diverse regulatory roles

### Nucleotide Functions Beyond Genetics

**Energy Currency**:
- **ATP** (adenosine triphosphate): Primary energy carrier
- Hydrolysis of phosphate bonds releases energy (ΔG = -7.3 kcal/mol)

**Electron Carriers**:
- **NAD⁺/NADH**: Oxidized/reduced forms
- **FAD/FADH₂**: Oxidized/reduced forms
- Critical in cellular respiration

**Signaling**:
- **cAMP** (cyclic AMP): Second messenger in signal transduction
- **cGMP** (cyclic GMP): Vision, smooth muscle relaxation

**Coenzymes**:
- **Coenzyme A**: Carries acetyl groups in metabolism
- **NAD⁺, FAD**: Derived from niacin and riboflavin (vitamins)

## Biomolecule Interactions

### Enzyme-Substrate Binding
- Lock-and-key or induced-fit models
- Multiple weak interactions (H-bonds, ionic, hydrophobic)
- Specificity from complementary shapes

### Protein-DNA Interactions
- Transcription factors bind DNA via major/minor grooves
- Helix-turn-helix, zinc fingers, leucine zippers
- Specific base recognition through hydrogen bonding

### Lipid-Protein Interactions
- Membrane proteins embedded in lipid bilayer
- Hydrophobic residues interact with lipid tails
- Lipid rafts: Cholesterol-rich microdomains affect protein localization

### Carbohydrate Recognition
- Lectins bind specific carbohydrate structures
- Blood type determined by carbohydrate antigens
- Viral entry often requires carbohydrate recognition

## Key Terms

- **Monosaccharide**: Simple sugar (glucose, fructose)
- **Polysaccharide**: Many sugar units (starch, cellulose, glycogen)
- **Glycosidic bond**: Covalent bond linking sugars
- **Fatty acid**: Long hydrocarbon chain with carboxyl group
- **Phospholipid**: Lipid with phosphate; forms membranes
- **Amino acid**: Building block of proteins
- **Peptide bond**: Covalent bond linking amino acids
- **Primary structure**: Amino acid sequence
- **Denaturation**: Loss of protein structure
- **Nucleotide**: Building block of nucleic acids (base + sugar + phosphate)
- **Complementary base pairing**: A-T/U and G-C pairing in nucleic acids

## Summary

The four major classes of biomolecules each have distinctive chemistry and functions. Carbohydrates—from simple monosaccharides like glucose to complex polysaccharides like starch and cellulose—provide energy and structure. Lipids, predominantly hydrophobic, include fatty acids, triacylglycerols for energy storage, phospholipids forming membrane bilayers, and cholesterol-derived steroids. Proteins, built from 20 amino acids linked by peptide bonds, fold into complex three-dimensional structures determined by their primary sequence and stabilized by various interactions; they serve as enzymes, structural components, transporters, and signaling molecules. Nucleic acids—DNA and RNA—store and transmit genetic information through specific base pairing, with nucleotides also functioning as energy carriers (ATP) and signaling molecules (cAMP). Understanding the structure, properties, and interactions of these biomolecules is fundamental to comprehending metabolism, genetics, cell biology, and ultimately how life works at the molecular level.
