# Genetics and Heredity

## Overview

Genetics provides the mechanistic basis for evolution. DNA encodes heritable information, genes are the units of inheritance, and population genetics describes how allele frequencies change over time.

---

## DNA: The Molecule of Heredity

### Structure

- **Double helix** (Watson & Crick, 1953; Franklin's X-ray crystallography)
- **Nucleotides:** Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)
- **Base pairing:** A-T, G-C
- **Antiparallel strands:** 5' to 3' direction

### Central Dogma

```
DNA → RNA → Protein
    (transcription) (translation)
```

- **Replication:** DNA copies itself
- **Transcription:** DNA → mRNA
- **Translation:** mRNA → protein (at ribosomes)

### The Genetic Code

- **Codons:** 3-nucleotide sequences
- **64 codons** code for 20 amino acids + stop signals
- **Degenerate:** multiple codons per amino acid
- **Universal:** same code in almost all organisms (evidence of common ancestry)

## Genes and Chromosomes

### Gene Structure

**Eukaryotic gene:**
- Promoter (regulatory region)
- Exons (coding sequences)
- Introns (non-coding, spliced out)
- Regulatory elements (enhancers, silencers)

### Chromosomes

| Organism | Chromosome # | Genome Size |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Human | 46 (23 pairs) | 3.2 billion bp |
| Fruit fly | 8 | 140 million bp |
| E. coli | 1 (circular) | 4.6 million bp |

**Ploidy:**
- Haploid (n) - one set (gametes)
- Diploid (2n) - two sets (most animals)
- Polyploid - multiple sets (common in plants)

## Mendelian Inheritance

### Mendel's Laws

**1. Law of Segregation**
- Two alleles separate during gamete formation
- Each gamete gets one allele

**2. Law of Independent Assortment**
- Genes on different chromosomes sort independently
- (Modified by linkage)

### Inheritance Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Dominant/recessive | One allele masks the other | Pea shape (round/wrinkled) |
| Codominance | Both alleles expressed | ABO blood type |
| Incomplete dominance | Intermediate phenotype | Snapdragon flower color |
| X-linked | Gene on X chromosome | Hemophilia, color blindness |

### Punnett Squares

Cross: Aa × Aa

|   | A | a |
|---|---|---|
| A | AA | Aa |
| a | Aa | aa |

**Genotype ratio:** 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa
**Phenotype ratio:** 3 dominant : 1 recessive

## Beyond Mendel

### Polygenic Traits

- Multiple genes contribute to one trait
- Continuous variation (bell curve)
- Examples: height, skin color, intelligence

### Pleiotropy

- One gene affects multiple traits
- Example: Sickle cell gene affects blood cells, spleen, bones

### Epistasis

- One gene masks/modifies another gene's effect
- Example: Coat color in mice (agouti/non-agouti and pigment genes)

### Linked Genes

- Genes on same chromosome tend to be inherited together
- **Recombination** (crossing over) breaks up linkage
- Recombination frequency → genetic mapping

## Population Genetics

### Allele Frequencies

**Calculating allele frequency:**

For diploid population with genotypes AA, Aa, aa:
- p (frequency of A) = (2×AA + Aa) / (2×total)
- q (frequency of a) = (2×aa + Aa) / (2×total)
- p + q = 1

### Genetic Variation Measures

| Measure | What it tells us |
|---------|------------------|
| Heterozygosity (H) | Proportion of heterozygotes |
| Nucleotide diversity (π) | Average pairwise differences |
| Number of alleles | Raw diversity count |

### Sources of Genetic Variation

1. **Mutation** - ultimate source
2. **Recombination** - shuffles existing variation
3. **Gene flow** - imports variation from other populations

## Molecular Evolution

### Neutral Theory (Kimura, 1968)

Most molecular evolution is due to:
- Neutral mutations (no fitness effect)
- Genetic drift

**Not** most evolution is neutral—most *molecular* changes are.

### Molecular Clock

- Neutral mutations accumulate at roughly constant rate
- More differences = more time since divergence
- Calibrate with fossils to date divergences

**Limitations:**
- Rate varies among genes and lineages
- Selection affects rates
- Need careful calibration

### Detecting Selection

| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| dN/dS < 1 | Purifying selection (conserved) |
| dN/dS = 1 | Neutral evolution |
| dN/dS > 1 | Positive selection |

Where dN = nonsynonymous substitution rate, dS = synonymous substitution rate

### Selective Sweep

When beneficial allele spreads:
- Linked neutral variants "hitchhike"
- Reduces variation near selected site
- Detectable in genome scans

---

## Genetic Variation and Evolution

### Standing Variation

Pre-existing genetic variation in population allows rapid response to:
- Environmental change
- New selective pressures
- Example: Pesticide resistance alleles often pre-exist at low frequency

### Quantitative Genetics

**Heritability (h²):** proportion of phenotypic variance due to genetic variance

- h² = 0: trait not heritable, selection can't change it
- h² = 1: trait fully heritable, selection very effective

**Response to selection:**
R = h² × S

Where R = response, S = selection differential

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Allele** | Variant form of a gene |
| **Genotype** | Genetic makeup (e.g., Aa) |
| **Phenotype** | Observable characteristics |

| **Homozygous** | Two identical alleles (AA or aa) |
| **Recombination** | Shuffling of alleles during meiosis |
| **Heritability** | Proportion of variation due to genetics |
| **dN/dS** | Ratio detecting selection at molecular level |

---

## Summary

- DNA stores genetic information; genes are functional units
- Mendelian inheritance explains discrete trait patterns
- Most traits are polygenic with complex inheritance
- Population genetics tracks allele frequency changes
- Neutral theory: most molecular changes are non-adaptive
- Molecular clocks date evolutionary divergences
- Standing genetic variation enables rapid evolutionary response
