# Evolution in Action

## Overview

Evolution isn't just ancient history—it's happening all around us in real time. From antibiotic-resistant bacteria to selective breeding, this chapter explores observable evolution with practical implications for medicine, agriculture, and conservation.

---

## Antibiotic Resistance

### The Problem

- Antibiotic resistance kills ~1.3 million people annually
- May kill 10 million/year by 2050
- "Superbugs" resist multiple antibiotics
- Pipeline of new antibiotics has slowed

### Evolutionary Mechanism

1. **Variation:** Random mutations create resistance
2. **Selection:** Antibiotic kills susceptible bacteria
3. **Reproduction:** Resistant bacteria survive and multiply
4. **Spread:** Resistance genes spread (even between species)

### How Resistance Evolves

| Mechanism | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Target modification | Drug target changes shape | MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staph) |
| Drug inactivation | Enzyme destroys antibiotic | Beta-lactamase breaks penicillins |
| Efflux pumps | Pump drug out of cell | Tetracycline resistance |
| Reduced uptake | Cell membrane changes | Aminoglycoside resistance |

### Horizontal Gene Transfer

Bacteria share resistance genes via:
- **Conjugation:** Direct cell-to-cell transfer
- **Transformation:** Uptake of free DNA
- **Transduction:** Viral-mediated transfer

**Result:** Resistance spreads across species, rapidly

### Fighting Resistance

Evolutionary strategies:
- **Cycling antibiotics** - rotate to reduce selection
- **Combination therapy** - harder to evolve resistance to multiple drugs
- **Narrow spectrum** - reduce collateral selection
- **Reduce overuse** - agricultural antibiotics, unnecessary prescriptions

## Pesticide and Herbicide Resistance

### Same Evolutionary Pattern

| Resistance type | # Resistant species | Key examples |
|-----------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Insecticide | 500+ | Mosquitoes, agricultural pests |
| Herbicide | 260+ | Palmer amaranth, ryegrass |
| Rodenticide | Several | Warfarin-resistant rats |

### Case Study: Bt Resistance

- Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) toxin in GM crops
- Evolved resistance in 5+ insect species
- **Refuge strategy:** Non-Bt crops maintain susceptible population

### Integrated Pest Management

Evolutionary thinking in agriculture:
- Rotate modes of action
- Use multiple mechanisms
- Maintain refugia
- Monitor resistance allele frequencies

## Viral Evolution

### Why Viruses Evolve Fast

- **Short generation time** (minutes to hours)
- **High mutation rate** (especially RNA viruses)
- **Large population sizes**
- **Strong selection** (immune system, drugs)

### Influenza: Annual Evolution

- **Antigenic drift:** Gradual mutations evade immunity
- **Antigenic shift:** Reassortment creates novel strains (pandemic risk)
- Why we need annual flu vaccines

### HIV: Evolution Within a Patient

- Mutates ~1 per genome per replication
- Evolves drug resistance within months
- **HAART** (combination therapy) works because resistance to all drugs simultaneously is unlikely

### COVID-19 Variants

Demonstrated evolution in real-time:
- Alpha, Delta, Omicron: increased transmissibility
- Mutations in spike protein affect antibody binding
- Ongoing evolution as virus becomes endemic

## Artificial Selection

### Definition

Human-directed selection for desired traits.

### Domestication Examples

| Organism | Wild ancestor | Selected traits |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Dog | Wolf | Tameness, varied morphology |
| Corn | Teosinte | Large ears, many kernels |
| Cattle | Aurochs | Milk production, docility |
| Wheat | Wild grasses | Non-shattering, large grains |
| Chicken | Jungle fowl | Egg production, meat |

### Speed of Change

Darwin used artificial selection as evidence:
- Dramatic change in few generations
- Shows heritability of variation
- Demonstrates selection's power

**Example:** Russian fox experiment (1959-present)
- Selected for tameness
- 50 generations → dog-like behavior
- Pleiotropy: also changed coat color, ear shape

### Modern Applications

- **Crop breeding:** Yield, disease resistance, nutrition
- **Livestock:** Growth rate, feed efficiency
- **Laboratory organisms:** Model systems for research

## Coevolution

### Definition

Reciprocal evolutionary change in interacting species.

### Types of Coevolution

**Antagonistic (arms race):**
- Predator-prey: speed, camouflage, toxins
- Host-parasite: immune evasion, resistance
- Herbivore-plant: toxins vs. detoxification

**Mutualistic:**
- Plant-pollinator: flower shape matches pollinator
- Cleaner fish and hosts
- Mycorrhizal fungi and plants

### Red Queen Hypothesis

"It takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place"

Species must constantly evolve to:
- Keep up with coevolving parasites
- Maintain competitive ability
- Explains maintenance of sexual reproduction

### Case Study: Garter Snakes and Newts

- Rough-skinned newts produce tetrodotoxin
- Garter snakes evolved resistance
- Escalating arms race: more toxic newts, more resistant snakes
- Geographic mosaic of coevolution

## Industrial Melanism

### The Peppered Moth

**Classic example of natural selection observed:**

| Period | Environment | Favored form |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Pre-1850 | Lichens on trees | Light (camouflage) |
| Industrial era | Soot-blackened trees | Dark (melanic) |
| Post-Clean Air Act | Lichens returned | Light |

**Evidence:**
- Kettlewell's experiments showed differential bird predation
- Genetic basis identified (cortex gene)
- Frequency tracked with pollution levels

## Evolution and Conservation

### Evolutionary Rescue

Can populations evolve fast enough to survive environmental change?

**Requirements:**
- Sufficient genetic variation
- Selection not too strong
- Population not too small (avoid drift)

### Conservation Applications

| Application | Evolutionary principle |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Captive breeding | Maintain genetic diversity |
| Assisted migration | Move adaptive variation |
| Genetic rescue | Introduce genes from other populations |
| Climate adaptation | Preserve evolutionary potential |

### Invasive Species

Rapid evolution in new environments:
- Cane toads in Australia (faster, morphology change)
- European house sparrows in North America
- Evolution of increased competitive ability (EICA hypothesis)

## Experimental Evolution

### Laboratory Evolution Studies

**E. coli Long-Term Evolution Experiment** (Lenski, 1988-present)
- 80,000+ generations
- Observed evolution of citrate metabolism
- Parallel evolution across replicate populations
- Ongoing molecular adaptation

### What We've Learned

- Evolution is repeatable (but not identical)
- Contingency matters (history affects outcomes)
- Epistasis constrains some paths
- Beneficial mutations often involve trade-offs

## Human-Induced Evolution

### Unintended Consequences

| Human activity | Evolutionary response |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Fishing (size-selective) | Smaller fish at maturity |
| Hunting (trophy selection) | Smaller horns/tusks |
| Urbanization | Urban heat tolerance, behavior changes |
| Climate change | Shifted timing, ranges, body size |

### Example: Trophy Hunting and Horns

- Bighorn sheep: 30% decrease in horn size over 30 years
- Elephants: Increased tusklessness in heavily poached populations
- Selecting against the traits we value

## Summary

- Antibiotic resistance is evolution in action—a medical crisis
- Same principles cause pesticide, herbicide, and antiviral resistance
- Artificial selection demonstrates the power of selection
- Coevolution shapes interacting species (arms races, mutualisms)
- Industrial melanism provided textbook example of natural selection
- Conservation requires considering evolutionary processes
- Humans are now a major evolutionary force, often with unintended consequences
- Understanding evolution is crucial for medicine, agriculture, and environmental management
