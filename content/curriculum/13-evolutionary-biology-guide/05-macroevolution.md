# Macroevolution

## Overview

Macroevolution refers to evolutionary patterns and processes above the species level—the origin of major groups, mass extinctions, adaptive radiations, and the tree of life across deep time.

## Major Transitions in Evolution

### The Origin of Life (~4 BYA)

- Abiogenesis: life from non-life
- RNA World hypothesis: RNA as first replicator
- LUCA (Last Universal Common Ancestor)

### Key Transitions

| Transition | When (MYA) | Significance |
|------------|------------|--------------|
| Prokaryotes → Eukaryotes | ~2000 | Endosymbiosis (mitochondria, chloroplasts) |
| Single-cell → Multicellular | ~1000-600 | Division of labor |
| Water → Land (plants) | ~470 | Terrestrial ecosystems |
| Water → Land (animals) | ~375 | Tetrapod evolution |
| Ectothermy → Endothermy | ~250-150 | Warm-bloodedness |
| Dinosaurs → Birds | ~150 | Only surviving dinosaur lineage |

### The Sixth Extinction?

- Current extinction rate 100-1000× background
- Human causes: habitat loss, climate change, pollution, invasive species
- May constitute mass extinction in progress

### Aftermath of Extinction

- Opens ecological opportunities
- Surviving lineages diversify
- Recovery takes millions of years
- Example: Mammal radiation after dinosaur extinction

## Adaptive Radiation

### Definition

Rapid diversification of a lineage into many species filling diverse ecological niches.

### Conditions Favoring Radiation

1. **Ecological opportunity** - empty niches available
2. **Key innovation** - novel trait opens new possibilities
3. **Geographic isolation** - reduces competition, allows specialization

### Classic Examples

| Radiation | Location | Diversity | Key Features |
|-----------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Darwin's finches | Galápagos | 14 species | Beak diversity |
| Hawaiian honeycreepers | Hawaii | ~50 species | Bill shapes, diets |
| Cichlid fishes | African lakes | 500+ species | Jaw morphology |
| Anole lizards | Caribbean | 400+ species | Ecomorphs |
| Mammals | Global | 6,400 species | Post-dinosaur radiation |

### Key Innovations

Traits that enable exploitation of new resources/habitats:
- Wings (flight in insects, birds, bats)
- Flowers (angiosperm success)
- Jaws (gnathostome radiation)
- Amniotic egg (land colonization)

## Phylogenetics

### Building the Tree of Life

**Phylogeny:** evolutionary relationships depicted as branching tree

**Methods:**
- **Cladistics:** group by shared derived characters (synapomorphies)
- **Maximum likelihood:** find tree with highest probability given data
- **Bayesian:** incorporate prior probabilities

### Reading Phylogenetic Trees

```
        ┌── Species A
    ┌───┤
    │   └── Species B
────┤
    │   ┌── Species C
    └───┤
        └── Species D
```

- **Node:** common ancestor (divergence point)
- **Branch:** lineage through time
- **Tips:** existing species or taxa
- **Sister groups:** A+B and C+D

### Important Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Monophyletic** | Ancestor + all descendants (clade) |
| **Paraphyletic** | Ancestor + some descendants ("reptiles" excluding birds) |
| **Polyphyletic** | Group from multiple ancestors (warm-blooded animals) |
| **Outgroup** | Related taxon outside group of interest |

### Molecular Phylogenetics

- Uses DNA/protein sequences
- More characters than morphology
- Can resolve relationships morphology cannot
- Revolutionized understanding of tree of life

## Patterns in Macroevolution

### Punctuated Equilibrium vs. Gradualism

| Pattern | Description | Evidence |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Gradualism** | Slow, constant change | Some lineages show gradual trends |
| **Punctuated equilibrium** | Stasis + rapid change | Fossil record often shows sudden appearances |

Both patterns occur; not mutually exclusive.

### Convergent Evolution

Independent evolution of similar features in unrelated lineages.

**Examples:**
- Eyes (evolved 40+ times)
- Wings (insects, birds, bats, pterosaurs)
- Echolocation (bats, dolphins)
- Camera eyes (vertebrates, cephalopods)
- C4 photosynthesis (60+ times in plants)

### Evolutionary Constraints

**Developmental constraints:**
- Existing body plan limits possibilities
- Example: Tetrapod limbs always from same bones

**Phylogenetic inertia:**
- Ancestral traits persist despite selection
- Historical contingency matters

**Trade-offs:**
- Can't optimize everything simultaneously
- Example: Speed vs. endurance

### Coevolution

Reciprocal evolutionary change in interacting species:
- Predator-prey arms races
- Plant-pollinator mutualisms
- Host-parasite dynamics

---

## Macroevolution and Microevolution

### Are They Different Processes?

**Same mechanisms:**
- Selection, drift, mutation, gene flow
- Extrapolation over long time

**Different emphases:**
- Macroevolution: extinction, species sorting, developmental evolution
- Contingency plays larger role over deep time

### Species Selection

- Differential speciation/extinction rates
- Species as units of selection
- Example: Lineages with sexual selection may speciate faster

## Summary

- Earth is 4.6 billion years old; life appeared ~4 BYA
- Five major mass extinctions shaped biodiversity; we may be in sixth
- Adaptive radiation follows extinction or colonization of new habitats
- Phylogenetics reveals evolutionary relationships using morphology and molecules
- Convergent evolution shows selection finding similar solutions independently
- Macroevolution uses same mechanisms as microevolution but over vast timescales
