# Mechanisms of Evolution

## Overview

Evolution occurs through four main mechanisms: natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and mutation. Only natural selection consistently produces adaptation; the others introduce variation or cause random changes.

---

## The Four Forces of Evolution

| Mechanism | Effect on Variation | Adaptive? | Strength depends on... |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Natural selection | Reduces (favors beneficial) | Yes | Fitness differences |
| Genetic drift | Reduces (random loss) | No | Population size |
| Gene flow | Increases (imports alleles) | Sometimes | Migration rate |
| Mutation | Increases (creates alleles) | Rarely | Mutation rate |

---

## 1. Natural Selection

### How It Works

1. **Variation exists** in population (heritable differences)
2. **Struggle for existence** - more offspring than can survive
3. **Differential survival** - some variants survive/reproduce better
4. **Inheritance** - survivors pass traits to offspring
5. **Result:** Beneficial alleles increase in frequency

### Requirements for Natural Selection

- **Variation** - individuals differ
- **Heritability** - differences are genetic
- **Differential fitness** - differences affect survival/reproduction

### Types of Selection

**Directional Selection**
- Favors one extreme phenotype
- Shifts population mean
- Example: Larger body size in colder climates

**Stabilizing Selection**
- Favors intermediate phenotype
- Reduces variation
- Example: Human birth weight (~7-8 lbs optimal)

**Disruptive Selection**
- Favors both extremes
- Increases variation
- Example: Beak sizes in seedcracker finches

**Sexual Selection**
- Based on mating success, not survival
- **Intrasexual:** Competition within sex (antlers, fighting)
- **Intersexual:** Mate choice (peacock tails, bird songs)

### Fitness

**Fitness (w)** = relative reproductive success

Components:
- Survival to reproductive age
- Mating success
- Fecundity (offspring number)
- Offspring survival

> Fitness is always relative to other genotypes in the population and to the environment

## 2. Genetic Drift

### Definition

Random changes in allele frequencies due to chance sampling in finite populations.

### Key Characteristics

- **Stronger in small populations**
- **Non-adaptive** - doesn't improve fitness
- **Reduces genetic variation** over time
- **Unpredictable direction**

### Types of Drift Events

**Founder Effect**
- Small group colonizes new area
- Carries only subset of original genetic diversity
- Example: Amish populations (Ellis-van Creveld syndrome)

**Bottleneck Effect**
- Population crashes, then recovers
- Surviving individuals have reduced diversity
- Example: Cheetahs (very low genetic diversity), Northern elephant seals

### Drift vs. Selection

| Small population | Large population |
|------------------|------------------|
| Drift dominates | Selection dominates |
| Random allele changes | Adaptive allele changes |
| Diversity lost quickly | Diversity maintained |

**Effective population size (Ne)** - genetic population size, often much smaller than census size due to:
- Unequal sex ratios
- Variance in reproductive success
- Population fluctuations

---

## 3. Gene Flow

### Definition

Movement of alleles between populations through migration and interbreeding.

### Effects

- **Homogenizes** populations (makes them similar)
- **Introduces new alleles** to populations
- Can **spread beneficial alleles** across populations
- Can **swamp local adaptation** if too strong

### Examples

- Pollen dispersal between plant populations
- Bird migration connecting breeding populations
- Human migration patterns affecting genetic diversity

### Gene Flow and Speciation

- High gene flow → prevents divergence → one species
- Low gene flow → allows divergence → speciation possible

---

## 4. Mutation

### Definition

Random, heritable changes in DNA sequence.

### Types of Mutations

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Point mutation | Single nucleotide change | Sickle cell (A→T) |
| Insertion/deletion | Added/removed bases | Frameshift mutations |
| Duplication | Gene/region copied | Gene family expansion |
| Inversion | Segment flipped | Chromosome rearrangements |
| Translocation | Segment moves to different chromosome | Cancer-causing fusions |

### Mutation Effects

- **Neutral** - no fitness effect (most common)
- **Deleterious** - reduces fitness (common)
- **Beneficial** - increases fitness (rare)

### Mutation as Evolutionary Force

- **Only source of new genetic variation**
- Mutation rate typically low (10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁹ per base pair per generation in humans)
- Provides raw material for other forces
- Alone, mutation changes allele frequencies very slowly

---

## Interactions Between Forces

### Selection vs. Drift

- In small populations, drift can override selection
- Mildly deleterious alleles can fix by drift
- Strongly beneficial alleles usually spread even in small populations

### Selection vs. Gene Flow

- Gene flow can prevent local adaptation
- Selection must be strong to overcome high gene flow
- Example: Heavy metal tolerance in plants near mines maintained despite gene flow from non-tolerant populations

### Mutation-Selection Balance

- Deleterious mutations continually arise by mutation
- Selection removes them
- Equilibrium frequency depends on mutation rate and selection strength

---

## Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

### The Null Model

If **no evolution** is occurring, allele frequencies stay constant.

**Conditions:**
1. No selection
2. No drift (infinite population)
3. No gene flow
4. No mutation
5. Random mating

**Equations:**
- Allele frequencies: p + q = 1
- Genotype frequencies: p² + 2pq + q² = 1

### Using Hardy-Weinberg

- Deviations indicate evolution is occurring
- Useful for calculating carrier frequencies
- Example: If q² (aa genotype) = 0.01, then q = 0.1 and carrier frequency (2pq) = 0.18

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Natural selection** | Differential survival/reproduction based on heritable traits |
| **Genetic drift** | Random changes in allele frequencies |
| **Founder effect** | Drift occurring when small group colonizes new area |
| **Bottleneck** | Drift occurring after population crash |
| **Gene flow** | Movement of alleles between populations |
| **Mutation** | Heritable change in DNA sequence |
| **Hardy-Weinberg** | Null model predicting stable allele frequencies without evolution |

---

## Summary

- Four mechanisms drive evolution: selection, drift, gene flow, mutation
- Only natural selection produces adaptation
- Drift is stronger in small populations; selection in large
- Gene flow connects populations and can spread or swamp adaptation
- Mutation is the ultimate source of all genetic variation
- Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium serves as null model to detect evolutionary change
