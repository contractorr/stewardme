# Soil, Water, and Agricultural Inputs

## Overview

Agricultural productivity depends fundamentally on soil health, water availability, and external inputs (fertilizers, pesticides, seeds). These three pillars determine what can grow, where, and how much. Managing them wisely means the difference between sustainable abundance and degraded ecosystems, between farmer prosperity and failed harvests.

This chapter explores the science of soils, the challenge of water scarcity, and the double-edged nature of chemical and biological inputs that boosted yields but created environmental consequences.

---

## Soil Science

### Soil Formation

**Soil** = weathered rock + organic matter + water + air + organisms

**Formation factors (Hans Jenny's equation):**
- **Parent material**: Bedrock type
- **Climate**: Temperature, rainfall
- **Organisms**: Plants, microbes, animals
- **Topography**: Slope, drainage
- **Time**: Hundreds to thousands of years

**Result**: 1 cm of topsoil takes 200-1,000 years to form

### Soil Composition

**Volume breakdown of healthy agricultural soil:**
- 45% minerals (sand, silt, clay)
- 25% air
- 25% water
- 5% organic matter (plant/animal residues, humus, organisms)

### Soil Texture

**Particle sizes:**
- **Sand**: 0.05-2 mm (gritty, drains fast, low nutrients)
- **Silt**: 0.002-0.05 mm (smooth, moderate drainage/nutrients)
- **Clay**: < 0.002 mm (sticky, drains slow, holds nutrients)

**Loam**: Balanced mix of sand-silt-clay; ideal for most crops

**Implications**:
- Sandy soils need frequent irrigation, fertilization
- Clay soils risk waterlogging, compaction
- Texture cannot be easily changed

### Soil Structure

**Definition**: How particles clump together into aggregates

**Good structure**:
- Pore space for air/water movement
- Root penetration
- Microbial habitat

**Degradation causes**:
- Tillage breaks apart aggregates
- Compaction from heavy machinery
- Loss of organic matter "glue"

### Soil Fertility

**Essential Nutrients:**

**Macronutrients (large quantities):**
- **Nitrogen (N)**: Proteins, chlorophyll; often limiting
- **Phosphorus (P)**: DNA, ATP, root growth
- **Potassium (K)**: Water regulation, disease resistance
- Calcium, magnesium, sulfur

**Micronutrients (trace amounts):**
- Iron, zinc, boron, copper, manganese, molybdenum

**Liebig's Law of the Minimum**: Yield limited by scarcest nutrient, not total nutrients

### Soil pH

**Scale**: 0-14 (7 = neutral)

**Agricultural implications:**
| pH | Condition | Crops | Issues |
|----|-----------|-------|--------|
| < 5.5 | Acidic | Blueberries, potatoes | Aluminum toxicity, nutrient lockup |
| 6-7 | Slightly acidic | Most crops | Optimal nutrient availability |
| 7-8 | Slightly alkaline | Wheat, barley | Some micronutrient deficiency |
| > 8 | Alkaline | Few crops | Iron, zinc deficiency |

**Management**:
- Lime raises pH (acidic soils)
- Sulfur lowers pH (alkaline soils)

### Soil Organic Matter (SOM)

**Composition**: Decomposed plant/animal material, living microbes, humus

**Benefits:**
- Nutrient reservoir (mineralization releases N, P)
- Water retention (SOM holds 20x its weight in water)
- Aggregation (improves structure)
- Carbon storage

**Typical levels**:
- Temperate cropland: 2-6%
- Degraded tropical: < 1%
- Prairie soils: 4-8%

**Building SOM**: Cover crops, compost, reduced tillage, crop residues

**Loss**: Tillage, erosion, tropical heat (rapid decomposition)

### Soil Organisms

**Diversity**: 1 teaspoon of soil contains:
- 1 billion bacteria
- Several meters of fungal hyphae
- Protozoa, nematodes, arthropods, earthworms

**Functions**:
- **Decomposition**: Break down organic matter → nutrients
- **Nitrogen fixation**: Rhizobium bacteria (legume roots) convert N₂ gas → ammonia
- **Mycorrhizal fungi**: Extend root reach for water/nutrients; receive sugars from plant
- **Soil structure**: Fungi hyphae bind aggregates; earthworm tunnels aerate soil

**Threats**: Heavy tillage, pesticides, compaction

## Soil Degradation

### Erosion

**Types:**
- **Water erosion**: Rainfall detaches soil; runoff carries it away
  - Sheet erosion (uniform removal)
  - Rill erosion (small channels)
  - Gully erosion (large channels)
- **Wind erosion**: Exposed soil blown away (Dust Bowl 1930s)

**Global scale**:
- 75 billion tons soil lost annually
- Erosion rate: 10-40x soil formation rate
- 1/3 of cropland degraded

**Causes**:
- Bare soil (no plant cover)
- Tillage (breaks aggregates)
- Deforestation
- Overgrazing

**Consequences**:
- Lost productivity (topsoil = most fertile)
- Sedimentation (rivers, reservoirs)
- Carbon emissions (oxidation of SOM)

**Prevention**:
- No-till/conservation tillage
- Cover crops
- Terracing
- Contour plowing
- Windbreaks

### Salinization

**Process**: Salt accumulates in soil (from irrigation water, poor drainage)

**Mechanism**:
1. Irrigation water contains dissolved salts
2. Evaporation leaves salts behind
3. Without flushing (drainage), salts concentrate

**Impact**: 20% of irrigated land salinized; yields decline as salinity increases

**Regions**: Indus Valley (Pakistan), Central Asia, California, Australia

**Solutions**:
- Improved drainage
- High-quality water
- Salt-tolerant crops
- Drip irrigation (less water applied)

### Compaction

**Cause**: Heavy machinery compresses soil, squeezing out air pores

**Effect**:
- Restricted root growth
- Poor water infiltration → runoff
- Reduced microbial activity

**Prevention**:
- Limit field traffic
- Avoid wet soil conditions
- Controlled traffic farming (fixed wheel paths)

### Nutrient Depletion

**Cause**: Continuous cropping exports nutrients faster than replenishment

**Sub-Saharan Africa**: "Mining" soils (negative nutrient balance) due to low fertilizer use

**Solution**: Fertilizers, manure, crop rotation, legumes

### Acidification

**Causes**:
- Nitrogen fertilizer use → acidifies over time
- Acid rain
- Crop removal of base cations

**Impact**: Yield declines below pH 5.5

**Solution**: Lime application

## Water in Agriculture

### Global Water Use

**Distribution of freshwater withdrawals:**
- Agriculture: 70%
- Industry: 19%
- Municipal: 11%

**Irrigation**: 20% of cropland (irrigated), produces 40% of food

### Irrigation Systems

**(Covered in detail in previous chapter; summary here)**

**Methods ranked by efficiency:**
1. **Drip/micro**: 85-95% - water delivered directly to roots
2. **Sprinkler**: 70-85% - water sprayed over crops
3. **Furrow**: 50-70% - water flows between crop rows
4. **Flood**: 40-60% - field submerged

**Adoption barriers to efficient systems**: Upfront cost, technical knowledge, crop suitability

### Water Scarcity

**Definitions:**
- **Physical scarcity**: Insufficient water resources to meet demand
- **Economic scarcity**: Water available but lack of infrastructure/investment

**Regions facing scarcity:**
- Middle East, North Africa
- Northern China
- Western US
- Northwestern India

**Groundwater depletion:**

| Aquifer | Location | Depletion Rate | Impact |
|---------|----------|----------------|--------|
| Ogallala | US Great Plains | 1-2 ft/year | Switching to dryland farming |
| North China Plain | China | 2-3 m/year | Beijing subsidence, food security risk |
| Northwest India | India | 1-2 m/year | Energy crisis (deeper pumping) |

**Consequences**:
- Increased pumping costs
- Land subsidence
- Saltwater intrusion (coastal aquifers)
- Farming cessation

### Virtual Water Trade

**Concept**: Water embedded in traded goods

**Example**: 1 kg beef requires 15,000 liters water (mostly for feed crops)

**Implication**: Water-scarce countries import water-intensive products (wheat, meat) from water-abundant regions

**Top virtual water exporters**: US, Brazil, Argentina, Australia (grain, meat)

**Top importers**: Japan, Mexico, China, Middle East

## Fertilizers

### Nutrient Needs

**Typical crop removal (kg/ton grain):**
- Wheat: 20 kg N, 4 kg P, 5 kg K
- Maize: 15 kg N, 3 kg P, 6 kg K
- Rice: 16 kg N, 3 kg P, 4 kg K

**Without replenishment**: Soil depletes → yields decline

### Synthetic Fertilizers

**Nitrogen (N):**
- **Source**: Haber-Bosch process (N₂ + H₂ → NH₃) using natural gas
- **Forms**: Urea, ammonium nitrate, anhydrous ammonia
- **Use**: 110 million tons N/year globally
- **Impact**: Enabled population growth from 1.6B (1900) to 8B (2024)

**Phosphorus (P):**
- **Source**: Mined phosphate rock
- **Peak phosphorus concern**: Finite resource; high-quality deposits depleting
- **Major producers**: Morocco (70% of reserves), China, US

**Potassium (K):**
- **Source**: Mined potash deposits
- **Major producers**: Canada, Russia, Belarus

### Organic Fertilizers

**Sources:**
- **Manure**: Livestock waste (N, P, K + organic matter)
- **Compost**: Decomposed plant/animal material
- **Crop residues**: Left on field after harvest
- **Green manure**: Cover crops plowed under

**Advantages**:
- Slow-release nutrients
- Build soil organic matter
- Improve soil structure

**Disadvantages**:
- Lower nutrient concentration (need large volumes)
- Slower crop response
- Labor-intensive application
- Potential pathogens (manure)

### Fertilizer Efficiency

**Nitrogen Use Efficiency (NUE)**: Crop uptake / N applied

**Global average NUE**: 40-50% (50-60% lost to environment)

**N losses:**
- **Volatilization**: NH₃ evaporates to atmosphere
- **Leaching**: Nitrate (NO₃⁻) washes into groundwater
- **Runoff**: Reaches surface water → algal blooms
- **Denitrification**: Microbes convert to N₂O (greenhouse gas)

**Improving efficiency:**
- **Right rate**: Soil testing, crop needs
- **Right time**: Apply when crop actively growing
- **Right place**: Subsurface placement, near roots
- **Right source**: Slow-release formulations, nitrification inhibitors

### Environmental Impacts

**Dead zones**: Excess N/P → algal blooms → decomposition depletes oxygen → fish die
- Gulf of Mexico: 15,000 km² (Mississippi River runoff)
- Baltic Sea, Chesapeake Bay, Lake Erie

**Greenhouse gases**:
- N₂O from fertilizers (298x CO₂ warming potential)
- Agriculture contributes 60% of global N₂O

**Groundwater contamination**: Nitrate above safe limits in agricultural regions

## Pesticides

### Categories

**By target:**
- **Herbicides**: Kill weeds (70% of pesticide use)
- **Insecticides**: Kill insects
- **Fungicides**: Kill fungi
- **Others**: Rodenticides, nematicides

**By chemistry:**
- **Organophosphates**: Nerve agents (malathion, chlorpyrifos)
- **Carbamates**: Nerve disruption (carbaryl, aldicarb)
- **Pyrethroids**: Synthetic versions of natural chrysanthemum compounds
- **Neonicotinoids**: Insect neurotoxins (imidacloprid) - implicated in bee decline
- **Glyphosate**: Herbicide (Roundup) - most widely used

### Benefits

- **Crop protection**: Without pesticides, yield losses would increase from 35% to 70%
- **Economic**: Higher farm profitability
- **Food safety**: Reduce mycotoxins (fungal toxins in grain)
- **Public health**: Control disease vectors (malaria mosquitoes)

### Risks

**Human health:**
- Acute poisoning (3 million cases/year globally, 250,000 deaths)
- Chronic exposure: Cancer, endocrine disruption, developmental effects
- Farmworker exposure disproportionately high

**Environmental:**
- **Non-target species**: Bees, butterflies, birds, aquatic life
- **Bioaccumulation**: DDT in eagles, orcas (banned 1972 US)
- **Resistance**: 500+ weed species resistant to herbicides; insect pests evolve resistance

**Regulation:**
- US EPA registration
- EU stricter standards (precautionary principle)
- Many developing countries lack enforcement

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

**Philosophy**: Use pesticides as last resort; combine multiple tactics

**Components:**
1. **Monitoring**: Scout fields, identify pests, assess damage
2. **Thresholds**: Treat only if pest population exceeds economic damage level
3. **Cultural practices**: Crop rotation, timing, resistant varieties
4. **Biological control**: Natural predators (ladybugs eat aphids)
5. **Mechanical**: Traps, barriers
6. **Chemical**: Targeted, lower-toxicity pesticides when needed

**Results**: 50-70% pesticide reduction with maintained yields

**Adoption**: Widespread in developed countries; less in developing (knowledge barriers)

## Seeds and Biotechnology

### Seed Systems

**Farmer-saved seed**:
- Traditional practice
- Free, adapted to local conditions
- Genetic diversity
- Variable quality

**Commercial seed**:
- Hybrid vigor (F1 generation high yields; F2 loses advantage → must repurchase)
- Quality control
- Cost

### Green Revolution Varieties

**Traits**:
- **Semi-dwarf**: Short stems → less lodging (falling over), more energy to grain
- **High-yield**: With fertilizer/irrigation
- **Photoperiod insensitivity**: Not dependent on day length → multiple growing regions

**Adoption**: 75% of wheat area in developing countries (1990s)

### Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs)

**Definition**: Direct gene transfer using recombinant DNA technology

**Major GMO crops (2020):**
- Soybeans (most widespread)
- Maize
- Cotton
- Canola

**Traits:**
| Trait | Mechanism | Benefit |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Bt crops** | Bacillus thuringiensis gene produces insecticidal protein | Reduces insecticide spraying |
| **Herbicide tolerance** | Glyphosate (Roundup Ready) or glufosinate tolerance | Simplifies weed control |
| **Drought tolerance** | Various genes | Maintains yield under water stress |
| **Golden Rice** | Beta-carotene synthesis (vitamin A precursor) | Addresses deficiency (not yet deployed) |

**Adoption**: 190 million hectares (2019), ~13% of global cropland
- Dominated by US, Brazil, Argentina
- Banned/restricted: EU, many African countries

**Benefits**:
- Reduced insecticide use (Bt cotton: 25% reduction in India)
- Yield gains (5-25% depending on pest pressure)
- Farmer income increases

**Concerns**:
- Corporate control (Monsanto/Bayer dominance)
- Herbicide-resistant weeds
- Non-target impacts (Bt effects on monarch butterflies - disputed)
- Gene flow to wild relatives
- Farmer dependency (can't save seed with patents)

**Scientific consensus**: GMOs no riskier than conventional breeding; each trait needs case-by-case evaluation

### CRISPR and Gene Editing

**Difference from GMOs**: Editing existing genes vs. inserting foreign genes

**Regulatory status**:
- US: Not regulated as GMOs (if no foreign DNA)
- EU: Classified as GMOs (2018 ruling)

**Potential**: Drought tolerance, disease resistance, nutritional enhancement, reduced allergens

## Key Terms

- **Soil organic matter**: Decomposed plant/animal material in soil
- **Erosion**: Removal of topsoil by water or wind
- **Salinization**: Salt accumulation in soil
- **Irrigation efficiency**: Fraction of applied water used by crops
- **Aquifer depletion**: Groundwater withdrawal exceeding recharge
- **Haber-Bosch process**: Industrial nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃)
- **Nitrogen use efficiency**: Crop N uptake / N fertilizer applied
- **Pesticide**: Chemical controlling pests, weeds, or diseases
- **Integrated Pest Management**: Multi-tactic pest control minimizing pesticides
- **GMO**: Genetically modified organism with foreign gene insertion

## Summary

Soil, water, and inputs form the physical foundation of agriculture. Soil health—determined by texture, structure, organic matter, and organisms—dictates productivity. Yet 1/3 of global soils are degraded from erosion, salinization, and nutrient depletion, with topsoil forming 100x slower than it's lost.

Water scarcity threatens agriculture as 70% of freshwater withdrawals irrigate 20% of cropland producing 40% of food. Aquifers deplete faster than they recharge across major farming regions, creating an unsustainable trajectory.

Synthetic fertilizers enabled population growth from 1.6 to 8 billion by overcoming nutrient limits, but half the applied nitrogen escapes to pollute water and air. Pesticides protect crops but harm non-target species, human health, and create resistant pests.

Seeds embody agricultural potential—from Green Revolution semi-dwarfs to GMO Bt crops to CRISPR gene editing. Each technology promises higher yields and resilience but raises questions about corporate control, environmental impacts, and equity.

Managing these inputs wisely—building soil health, conserving water, optimizing fertilizer efficiency, adopting IPM—determines whether agriculture can sustainably feed 10 billion people or whether degradation undermines the foundation of food systems.
