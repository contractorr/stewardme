# Nutrition & Food Security

## Overview

Nutrition and food security represent the ultimate purpose of food systems: ensuring all people have access to sufficient, safe, nutritious food to live healthy, active lives. Yet paradoxes define our current reality: 820 million undernourished coexist with 2 billion overweight/obese; we produce enough food globally but distribute it unequally; micronutrient deficiencies afflict billions despite agricultural abundance.

This chapter explores nutritional requirements, the multidimensional nature of food security, causes of hunger amid plenty, and the double burden of malnutrition facing developing countries.

## Human Nutritional Requirements

### Energy (Calories)

**Measurement**: Kilocalories (kcal) or kilojoules (kJ)
- 1 kcal = 4.184 kJ

**Average adult requirements**: 2,000-2,500 kcal/day
- Varies by age, sex, body size, activity level
- Sedentary office worker: 1,800-2,200 kcal
- Construction worker: 3,000-4,000 kcal

**Macronutrient energy**:
- Carbohydrates: 4 kcal/gram
- Protein: 4 kcal/gram
- Fat: 9 kcal/gram
- Alcohol: 7 kcal/gram

### Macronutrients

**Carbohydrates**
- **Function**: Primary energy source, especially for brain
- **Recommended**: 45-65% of calories
- **Sources**: Grains, tubers, fruits, vegetables
- **Types**:
  - Simple (sugars): Quick energy, blood sugar spike
  - Complex (starches): Sustained energy
  - Fiber: Indigestible, aids digestion, heart health

**Protein**
- **Function**: Tissue building/repair, enzymes, hormones, immune function
- **Recommended**: 10-35% of calories; 0.8g/kg body weight minimum
- **Sources**:
  - Complete (all essential amino acids): Meat, fish, eggs, dairy, soy
  - Incomplete: Legumes, grains, nuts (combine for complete profile)
- **Deficiency**: Kwashiorkor (severe protein malnutrition), stunting in children

**Fats**
- **Function**: Energy storage, cell membranes, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), hormone production
- **Recommended**: 20-35% of calories
- **Types**:
  - Unsaturated (healthy): Olive oil, avocados, nuts, fish
  - Saturated (moderate): Butter, meat, coconut oil
  - Trans fats (avoid): Partially hydrogenated oils, linked to heart disease
- **Essential fatty acids**: Omega-3 (fish, flaxseed), omega-6 (vegetable oils)

### Micronutrients

**Vitamins** (organic compounds)

| Vitamin | Function | Deficiency | Sources |
|---------|----------|------------|---------|
| **A (retinol)** | Vision, immune function | Night blindness, child mortality | Liver, carrots, sweet potatoes, leafy greens |
| **B1 (thiamine)** | Energy metabolism | Beriberi (nerve damage) | Whole grains, pork, legumes |
| **B9 (folate)** | DNA synthesis, cell division | Neural tube defects (pregnancy) | Leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains |
| **B12 (cobalamin)** | Nerve function, red blood cells | Anemia, nerve damage | Meat, fish, dairy (not in plants) |
| **C (ascorbic acid)** | Collagen, immune function | Scurvy (bleeding gums, wounds) | Citrus, peppers, tomatoes, leafy greens |
| **D (calciferol)** | Calcium absorption, bone health | Rickets (children), osteomalacia | Sunlight, fatty fish, fortified milk |
| **E (tocopherol)** | Antioxidant | Rare; nerve damage | Nuts, seeds, vegetable oils |
| **K** | Blood clotting | Bleeding disorders | Leafy greens, intestinal bacteria |

**Minerals** (inorganic elements)

| Mineral | Function | Deficiency | Sources |
|---------|----------|------------|---------|
| **Iron** | Hemoglobin (oxygen transport) | Anemia (fatigue, impaired cognition) | Red meat, beans, fortified cereals |
| **Calcium** | Bone structure, muscle contraction | Osteoporosis | Dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods |
| **Iodine** | Thyroid hormones | Goiter, cognitive impairment, cretinism | Iodized salt, seafood |
| **Zinc** | Immune function, growth | Stunting, impaired immunity | Meat, shellfish, legumes, seeds |
| **Magnesium** | Enzyme function, muscle/nerve | Rare; muscle cramps | Nuts, whole grains, leafy greens |
| **Selenium** | Antioxidant | Keshan disease (heart) | Brazil nuts, seafood, grains |

### Water

**Requirement**: 2-3 liters/day (varies by climate, activity)

**Functions**: Temperature regulation, nutrient transport, waste removal

**Deficiency**: Dehydration → impaired cognition, kidney stones, death (severe)

## Food Security Framework

### FAO Definition (1996)

"Food security exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life."

### Four Pillars

**1. Availability**
- **Definition**: Sufficient food supply in region/country
- **Measures**: Production levels, stock levels, imports
- **Example**: Wheat harvest failure → reduced availability

**2. Access**
- **Definition**: Ability to obtain food (economic, physical, social)
- **Economic**: Purchasing power (income vs. food prices)
- **Physical**: Proximity to markets/stores (food deserts)
- **Social**: Gender, caste, ethnicity discrimination
- **Example**: Food abundant in markets but urban poor lack income to buy

**3. Utilization**
- **Definition**: Proper nutritional use of food
- **Components**:
  - Nutritional quality (diverse diet)
  - Food safety (free from pathogens, toxins)
  - Clean water (hydration, food preparation)
  - Healthcare (treat infections that impair nutrient absorption)
- **Example**: Child receives food but diarrhea prevents nutrient absorption

**4. Stability**
- **Definition**: Consistent access over time (no sudden shocks)
- **Threats**: Climate events, price spikes, conflict, economic crises
- **Example**: Seasonal hunger (pre-harvest "lean season" in rural areas)

### Additional Dimension: Agency

**Recent addition (HLPE 2020)**: Capacity of individuals to make own choices about food

**Aspects**:
- Food preferences respected
- Control over resources
- Autonomy in food decisions

## Global Hunger and Malnutrition

### Undernourishment (Hunger)

**Definition**: Caloric intake insufficient for active, healthy life

**Prevalence**: 820 million people (10% of global population, 2023)

**Regional distribution**:
| Region | Number (millions) | Prevalence |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| Sub-Saharan Africa | 280 | 23% |
| Southern Asia | 305 | 15% |
| Eastern Asia | 50 | 3% |
| Latin America | 47 | 7% |

**Trend**: Declined from 947 million (2005) to 785 million (2015), then rose due to conflict, climate, COVID

### Micronutrient Deficiencies ("Hidden Hunger")

**Iron deficiency (anemia)**
- Prevalence: 1.6 billion people (25% globally)
- Impacts: Fatigue, impaired cognitive development, maternal mortality
- High-risk groups: Children, pregnant women
- Solutions: Iron supplementation, food fortification (flour), dietary diversity

**Vitamin A deficiency**
- Prevalence: 190 million preschool children
- Impacts: Blindness (leading preventable cause in children), increased mortality from infections
- Solutions: Supplementation (vitamin A capsules), fortification, biofortification (orange sweet potatoes, Golden Rice)

**Iodine deficiency**
- Prevalence: 2 billion at risk
- Impacts: Goiter, cognitive impairment (IQ loss), cretinism (severe)
- Solution: Iodized salt (major public health success; 88% of households now use)

**Zinc deficiency**
- Prevalence: 17% globally
- Impacts: Stunting, impaired immunity, diarrhea
- Solutions: Supplementation, fortification, biofortification (zinc wheat)

### Child Malnutrition

**Stunting**: Low height for age (chronic malnutrition)
- Prevalence: 149 million children under 5 (22%)
- Causes: Inadequate nutrition during first 1,000 days (pregnancy to age 2)
- Impacts: Irreversible cognitive impairment, lower lifetime earnings, increased disease risk
- Hotspots: Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia

**Wasting**: Low weight for height (acute malnutrition)
- Prevalence: 45 million children under 5
- Causes: Recent severe illness, food shortage
- Impacts: High mortality risk (12x higher than healthy children)
- Treatment: Therapeutic foods (Plumpy'Nut - peanut-based ready-to-use food)

**Underweight**: Low weight for age
- Prevalence: 149 million children

## The Double Burden of Malnutrition

### Overweight and Obesity

**Definition**:
- **Overweight**: BMI 25-30
- **Obesity**: BMI ≥ 30

**Prevalence**: 2 billion adults overweight, 650 million obese (2023)

**Trends**:
- US: 42% obese (2020)
- Middle East: 30-40% obese
- China: Obesity tripled since 1990

**Health consequences**:
- Type 2 diabetes (10% global prevalence)
- Cardiovascular disease (leading cause of death globally)
- Certain cancers
- Joint problems

**Economic costs**: $2 trillion annually (obesity + related diseases)

### Nutrition Transition

**Process**: As countries develop, diets shift

**Stages**:
1. **Pre-transition**: Starchy staples, minimal variety, undernutrition
2. **Transition**: Increased fats, sugar, processed foods, animal products
3. **Post-transition**: Excess calories, low micronutrients, obesity

**Drivers**:
- Urbanization (away-from-home eating)
- Income growth (afford meat, processed foods)
- Globalization (Western fast food chains)
- Marketing (ultra-processed foods)
- Time scarcity (convenience foods)

**Examples**:
- **Mexico**: Coca-Cola consumption highest globally; 73% overweight/obese; Type 2 diabetes epidemic
- **China**: Meat consumption increased 15x (1960-2020); childhood obesity rose from 1% to 20%

### Triple Burden

**Definition**: Undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, and overweight coexisting

**Context**: Developing countries face all three simultaneously
- Stunted children in households with obese adults
- Individuals both overweight and micronutrient-deficient (eating calorie-dense, nutrient-poor foods)

**Example**: India has high child stunting (35%) alongside rising adult obesity (20%)

## Causes of Food Insecurity

### Poverty

**Primary driver**: Cannot afford food despite availability

**Evidence**:
- Ultra-poor (< $1.90/day) spend 50-70% of income on food
- Food price spikes drive millions into poverty (2007-08 crisis: 105 million)

**Poverty-hunger cycle**:
- Malnutrition → reduced productivity → lower income → less food access

### Conflict

**Impact**: 60% of hungry people live in conflict zones

**Mechanisms**:
- Destruction of farms, markets, infrastructure
- Displacement (refugees, internally displaced)
- Livelihood loss
- Aid access blocked

**Examples**:
- Yemen: 17 million food insecure (2/3 population) due to civil war
- Syria: Agricultural production halved (2011-18)
- South Sudan: Famine conditions (2017, 2021)

### Climate Variability and Extremes

**Droughts**:
- 83% of agriculture damage/loss from droughts (2005-15)
- East Africa (2011, 2017, 2022): Repeated droughts → livestock deaths, crop failures → hunger

**Floods**: Destroy crops, contaminate water

**Extreme weather**: Hurricanes, typhoons disrupt production, infrastructure

**Long-term climate change**:
- Yield declines (wheat -6%, maize -7.4% per 1°C warming)
- Shifting growing zones
- Increased pests/diseases

### Inequality

**Within countries**: Marginalized groups face greater food insecurity
- Gender: Women-headed households more vulnerable
- Caste/ethnicity: Discrimination limits access
- Rural-urban: Rural poor less access to diverse foods

**Between countries**: Low-income countries lack resources for social protection, infrastructure

### Poor Infrastructure

**Roads**: Rural isolation → high transport costs → low farm prices, high consumer prices

**Storage**: Lack of silos → post-harvest losses → local gluts (low prices) and scarcity (high prices)

**Markets**: Inefficient, monopolistic structures → farmers receive low share of consumer price

## Solutions and Interventions

### Agricultural Productivity

**Approach**: Increase supply → lower prices → improved access

**Methods**:
- Improved seeds (higher yields, drought tolerance)
- Fertilizer/irrigation access
- Extension services (training farmers)
- R&D investment

**Evidence**: Green Revolution doubled yields; poverty fell

**Limits**: Doesn't address access inequality; environmental sustainability concerns

### Social Protection

**Cash transfers**: Direct payments to poor households
- **Brazil Bolsa Família**: Conditional cash (school attendance, health visits) → reduced hunger
- **Kenya**: Mobile money transfers to drought-affected pastoralists

**Food assistance**:
- **In-kind**: Distribute food directly (WFP, SNAP/food stamps)
- **Vouchers**: Redeemable for food (local market support)
- **School feeding**: 388 million children receive meals (attendance incentive)

**Public works**: Food/cash for labor on infrastructure projects

### Nutrition-Specific Interventions

**Supplementation**:
- Vitamin A capsules (every 6 months for children)
- Iron-folate tablets (pregnant women)
- Micronutrient powders (sprinkle on food)

**Fortification**:
- Iodized salt (near-universal)
- Fortified flour (iron, folic acid)
- Golden Rice (vitamin A) - controversial, limited adoption

**Biofortification**:
- Breeding crops for higher micronutrients
- Orange sweet potato (vitamin A) - 6x regular sweet potato
- Iron beans, zinc wheat

**Breastfeeding promotion**:
- Exclusive breastfeeding to 6 months (reduces infant mortality 13x)
- Continued breastfeeding to 2 years

**Treatment of severe acute malnutrition**:
- Ready-to-use therapeutic foods (RUTF) - Plumpy'Nut
- Community-based management (no hospitalization for most cases)

### Nutrition-Sensitive Agriculture

**Approach**: Improve nutrition through agriculture beyond calorie production

**Strategies**:
- **Diverse production**: Home gardens (vegetables, fruits), small livestock (chickens, goats)
- **Nutrient-dense crops**: Promote legumes, indigenous vegetables
- **Livestock**: Small animals provide protein, income for food purchase
- **Aquaculture**: Fish ponds (protein, micronutrients)

**Evidence**: Home gardens improve child vitamin A intake, reduce anemia

### Governance and Policy

**Food policy councils**: Multi-stakeholder coordination (agriculture, health, trade, environment)

**National nutrition plans**: Targets, accountability mechanisms

**Trade policies**: Balance self-sufficiency and imports; food reserves for price stabilization

**Women's empowerment**: Land rights, credit access, decision-making → improved child nutrition

### Emergency Response

**Early warning systems**: Monitor rainfall, prices, conflict → trigger assistance before crisis

**Humanitarian aid**: WFP feeds 100+ million annually in emergencies

**Resilience building**: Asset protection (livestock insurance), livelihood diversification

## Food Deserts and Access Inequality (Developed Countries)

**Definition**: Areas with limited access to affordable, nutritious food

**Characteristics**:
- Low-income neighborhoods
- Few supermarkets (convenience stores dominate)
- Limited public transport

**Prevalence**: 19 million Americans in food deserts

**Health impacts**: Higher obesity, diabetes rates

**Causes**:
- Supermarket redlining (avoid low-income areas)
- Lack of car ownership
- Small stores charge higher prices

**Solutions**:
- Incentivize supermarket entry (tax breaks)
- Mobile markets
- Online delivery subsidies
- Community gardens

## Key Terms

- **Undernourishment**: Insufficient caloric intake
- **Malnutrition**: Poor nutrition (includes undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, overweight)
- **Stunting**: Low height for age (chronic malnutrition)
- **Wasting**: Low weight for height (acute malnutrition)
- **Micronutrient deficiency**: Lack of vitamins/minerals ("hidden hunger")
- **Food security**: Access to sufficient, safe, nutritious food
- **Nutrition transition**: Shift from undernutrition to overweight with development
- **Biofortification**: Breeding crops for higher micronutrient content
- **Fortification**: Adding nutrients to processed foods
- **Food desert**: Area with limited access to affordable, nutritious food

## Summary

Nutrition and food security represent the core purpose of agriculture: nourishing humanity. Yet 820 million people remain hungry despite global food production exceeding needs, while 2 billion are overweight. This paradox reveals that food security depends on more than production—access, utilization, and stability matter equally.

Micronutrient deficiencies afflict billions, causing blindness, cognitive impairment, and mortality. Childhood stunting affects 149 million, permanently impairing potential. Meanwhile, developing countries face a triple burden: coexisting undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, and rising obesity from ultra-processed food consumption.

Causes span poverty (inability to afford food), conflict (60% of hungry in conflict zones), climate extremes (droughts, floods), and inequality (marginalized groups disproportionately affected). Solutions require multi-sectoral approaches: boosting agricultural productivity while ensuring equitable access through social protection, nutrition interventions (supplementation, fortification, therapeutic feeding), and governance addressing structural inequalities.

Feeding 10 billion nutritiously by 2050 demands not just producing more calories but distributing them equitably, diversifying diets beyond staple grains, and addressing the double burden of malnutrition. The challenge lies in transforming food systems to deliver nutrition, not just food.
