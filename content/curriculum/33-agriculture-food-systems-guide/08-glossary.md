# Glossary of Agricultural and Food Systems Terms

## A

**Adaptation** - Adjustments in agricultural practices to cope with climate change impacts (e.g., drought-tolerant crops, changed planting dates).

**Agroecology** - Application of ecological principles to agricultural system design; emphasizes biodiversity, synergy, and nutrient recycling.

**Aggregation** - Collecting agricultural products from multiple small producers for collective marketing or processing.

**Anaerobic digester** - System that decomposes organic waste (manure) in oxygen-free conditions to produce biogas (methane) for energy.

**Aquaculture** - Farming of aquatic organisms (fish, shellfish, seaweed) in controlled environments.

**Aquifer** - Underground layer of water-bearing rock from which groundwater can be extracted.

**Agroforestry** - Integration of trees with crops or livestock; provides shade, erosion control, and diversified income.

## B

**Biofortification** - Breeding crops for higher micronutrient content (e.g., orange sweet potatoes with more vitamin A).

**Biodiversity** - Variety of life forms including species diversity, genetic diversity within species, and ecosystem diversity.

**Biological pest control** - Use of natural predators or parasites to control agricultural pests instead of chemical pesticides.

**Biomass** - Organic material from plants or animals used for energy production or as agricultural input.

## C

**CAFO (Confined Animal Feeding Operation)** - Large-scale livestock facility with concentrated animals in confined spaces (1,000+ cattle, 2,500+ pigs, 125,000+ chickens).

**Carbon sequestration** - Process of capturing atmospheric CO₂ and storing it in soil organic matter or plant biomass.

**Climate-smart agriculture** - Farming approach that increases productivity, builds climate resilience, and reduces greenhouse gas emissions.

**Cold chain** - Temperature-controlled supply chain maintaining refrigeration from production through retail for perishable foods.

**Commodity** - Standardized, undifferentiated agricultural product traded in bulk (corn, wheat, soybeans, cotton).

**Comparative advantage** - Economic principle that countries benefit from specializing in products they produce relatively more efficiently.

**Compaction** - Compression of soil by heavy machinery, reducing pore space and restricting root growth and water infiltration.

**Cover crops** - Plants grown between cash crop seasons to prevent erosion, add organic matter, and suppress weeds.

**Crop rotation** - Sequential planting of different crops on the same land to break pest cycles and restore soil nutrients.

## D

**Deforestation** - Clearing of forests for agricultural expansion; major source of CO₂ emissions.

**Drip irrigation** - Highly efficient irrigation method delivering water directly to plant roots through tubes; 85-95% efficiency.

**Drought tolerance** - Ability of crops to maintain yields under water stress; trait developed through breeding or genetic modification.

## E

**Economies of scale** - Decrease in average cost per unit as production volume increases; favors large farms.

**Ecosystem services** - Benefits nature provides to agriculture and society (pollination, pest control, water purification, climate regulation).

**Enteric fermentation** - Digestive process in ruminant animals (cattle, sheep, goats) that produces methane as byproduct.

**Erosion** - Removal of topsoil by water or wind; occurring 10-40x faster than soil formation globally.

**Extensive agriculture** - Low-input farming over large land areas; lower yields per hectare but less intensive resource use.

## F

**Fallow** - Leaving land unplanted for a season or longer to restore soil fertility.

**Fertilizer** - Substance added to soil to supply nutrients; can be synthetic (chemical) or organic (manure, compost).

**Food desert** - Area with limited access to affordable, nutritious food, typically low-income neighborhoods lacking supermarkets.

**Food loss** - Reduction in food quantity or quality from farm to retail (excludes consumer waste).

**Food security** - Condition when all people have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, nutritious food.

**Food system** - Entire network from agricultural production through consumption, including all actors and processes.

**Food waste** - Food discarded at retail or consumer level; combined with food loss, represents 1/3 of global production.

**Fortification** - Adding nutrients to processed foods (e.g., iodized salt, iron-fortified flour).

**Futures contract** - Agreement to buy or sell a commodity at a specified future date for a predetermined price; used for hedging and speculation.

## G

**Genetically Modified Organism (GMO)** - Organism with DNA altered through genetic engineering; major GMO crops include Bt corn, Roundup Ready soybeans.

**Greenhouse gas (GHG)** - Gas that traps heat in atmosphere; from agriculture includes CO₂, methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O).

**Green manure** - Cover crops grown specifically to be plowed under to add organic matter and nutrients to soil.

**Green Revolution** - Period (1950s-1980s) of dramatically increased agricultural productivity through high-yield varieties, fertilizers, and irrigation; saved ~1 billion from starvation but had environmental costs.

## H

**Haber-Bosch process** - Industrial method of synthesizing ammonia (NH₃) from nitrogen gas and hydrogen; enabled mass production of nitrogen fertilizer; foundation of modern agriculture.

**Hedging** - Use of futures contracts to reduce price risk; farmers lock in selling price before harvest.

**Herbicide** - Chemical that kills weeds; most common type of pesticide (70% of use).

**Hidden hunger** - Micronutrient deficiencies (iron, vitamin A, iodine, zinc) affecting billions despite adequate caloric intake.

**Humus** - Stable, decomposed organic matter in soil; improves structure, water retention, and nutrient availability.

## I

**Insecticide** - Chemical that kills insect pests.

**Intensive agriculture** - High inputs (capital, labor, fertilizers) per unit land area; high yields but environmental pressure.

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM)** - Pest control strategy combining multiple tactics (monitoring, biological control, cultural practices, targeted chemical use) to minimize pesticide use.

**Intercropping** - Growing two or more crops simultaneously in same field; form of polyculture.

**Irrigation** - Artificial application of water to crops; uses 70% of global freshwater withdrawals; enables farming in arid regions.

**Irrigation efficiency** - Proportion of applied water actually used by crops; ranges from 40-60% (flood) to 85-95% (drip).

## L

**Land tenure** - System of land ownership and rights; includes private ownership, communal, state ownership, and open access.

**Leaching** - Downward movement of nutrients (especially nitrate) through soil into groundwater; pollutes water supplies and represents nutrient loss.

**Liebig's Law of the Minimum** - Crop yield limited by scarcest nutrient, not total nutrients; named after Justus von Liebig.

**Loam** - Ideal soil texture with balanced proportions of sand, silt, and clay; excellent for most crops.

## M

**Macronutrients** - Nutrients required in large quantities: nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), potassium (K), also carbohydrates, proteins, fats for human nutrition.

**Malnutrition** - Poor nutrition including undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, and overweight/obesity.

**Micronutrients** - Elements required in small amounts: iron, zinc, iodine, vitamins; deficiencies affect billions globally.

**Mitigation** - Actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from agriculture (e.g., methane-reducing feed additives, optimized fertilizer use).

**Monoculture** - Growing single crop species over large continuous area; enables mechanization but increases pest/disease vulnerability and reduces biodiversity.

**Monopsony** - Market with single buyer; gives buyer power to dictate low prices to sellers (e.g., meatpackers buying from farmers).

## N

**Nitrogen cycle** - Movement of nitrogen through environment; critical for agriculture as nitrogen is often limiting nutrient.

**Nitrogen fixation** - Conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to ammonia (NH₃) by bacteria; occurs in legume roots and industrial Haber-Bosch process.

**Nitrogen Use Efficiency (NUE)** - Proportion of applied nitrogen fertilizer taken up by crops; globally 40-50%, meaning 50-60% lost to environment.

**Nitrous oxide (N₂O)** - Greenhouse gas with 298x warming potential of CO₂; 60% of emissions from agriculture (fertilizers, manure).

**No-till** - Planting crops without plowing; reduces erosion, builds soil carbon, conserves moisture.

**Nutrition transition** - Shift from traditional diets (staples, minimal variety) to diets high in fats, sugar, processed foods, and animal products as countries develop; associated with rising obesity.

## O

**Organic agriculture** - Farming without synthetic pesticides/fertilizers or GMOs; emphasizes soil health and biodiversity; yields typically 20% lower but commands premium prices.

**Organic matter (soil)** - Decomposed plant and animal material in soil; improves fertility, water retention, and structure; 2-6% in temperate cropland.

**Overgrazing** - Livestock stocking density exceeds land's carrying capacity; causes erosion, soil compaction, vegetation loss.

## P

**Pastoralism** - Livestock-based livelihood with mobile herds following seasonal grazing; practiced by Maasai, Mongolian herders, others.

**Pesticide** - Chemical used to control pests, weeds, or diseases; includes herbicides (most common), insecticides, fungicides.

**pH (soil)** - Measure of soil acidity/alkalinity; most crops prefer 6-7; affects nutrient availability.

**Pollination** - Transfer of pollen enabling plant reproduction; 75% of crop species depend on animal pollinators; contributes $235-577 billion annually.

**Polyculture** - Growing multiple crop species together; increases biodiversity, reduces pest risk, but harder to mechanize.

**Precision agriculture** - Use of GPS, sensors, and data to optimize inputs (fertilizer, water) spatially and temporally; improves efficiency.

**Price elasticity** - Responsiveness of demand or supply to price changes; food demand relatively inelastic (necessity).

## R

**Regenerative agriculture** - Farming approach focused on restoring soil health through no-till, cover crops, diverse rotations, and integrated livestock.

**Resilience** - Ability of agricultural systems to withstand and recover from shocks (drought, price spikes, pests).

## S

**Salinization** - Accumulation of salts in soil from irrigation; 20% of irrigated land affected; reduces yields and can make land uncultivable.

**Shifting cultivation** - Agricultural system where plots are cleared, farmed for 2-3 years, then abandoned to regenerate; sustainable with low population density and long fallows; also called slash-and-burn.

**Smallholder** - Farm less than 2 hectares; 500 million globally (80% of farms); produce 35% of food but face challenges accessing markets, credit, technology.

**Soil organic matter (SOM)** - Living and dead plant/animal material in soil; critical for fertility, structure, water retention, carbon storage.

**Soil structure** - Arrangement of soil particles into aggregates; good structure allows air/water movement and root penetration.

**Soil texture** - Relative proportions of sand, silt, and clay; determines drainage, nutrient retention, workability.

**Specialty crops** - Fruits, vegetables, nuts, nursery products (not commodity grains); more differentiated markets where quality and variety matter.

**Stunting** - Low height for age in children; indicates chronic malnutrition during first 1,000 days (pregnancy to age 2); affects 149 million children; causes irreversible cognitive impairment.

**Subsidy** - Government payment supporting farmers or agriculture; includes direct payments, price supports, input subsidies, crop insurance; US/EU spend billions annually.

**Subsistence farming** - Growing food primarily for own consumption with minimal market sales; common in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.

**Supply chain** - Network of actors and activities moving products from production to consumption; includes aggregation, processing, distribution, retail.

**Sustainable agriculture** - Farming that maintains long-term productivity while protecting environmental and social well-being; balances economic, environmental, and social sustainability.

## T

**Tariff** - Tax on imports; protects domestic farmers but raises consumer prices; agricultural tariffs average 62% in developing countries, 39% in developed.

**Terracing** - Creating flat platforms on hillsides for farming; reduces erosion and water runoff.

**Tillage** - Mechanical manipulation of soil (plowing); prepares seedbed but breaks aggregates, increases erosion, and releases soil carbon.

**Total Factor Productivity (TFP)** - Output per unit of all inputs combined; measure of agricultural efficiency; growing 1.5% annually globally but regional disparities persist.

**Traceability** - Ability to track product through supply chain from origin to consumer; enabled by blockchain, QR codes; facilitates rapid food safety recalls.

## U

**Undernourishment** - Caloric intake insufficient for active, healthy life; affects 820 million people (10% of global population).

**Underweight** - Low weight for age in children; indicates malnutrition; affects 149 million children under 5.

## V

**Vertical farming** - Multi-story indoor farming with artificial lighting; achieves 390x land productivity and 95% water savings but high energy costs limit to leafy greens.

**Vertical integration** - Single company controlling multiple supply chain stages (e.g., Tyson owns hatcheries, feed mills, processing); increases efficiency but reduces competition.

**Virtual water** - Water embedded in traded products; 1 kg beef requires 15,000 liters water; water-scarce countries import water-intensive products.

## W

**Wasting** - Low weight for height in children; indicates acute malnutrition from recent illness or food shortage; affects 45 million children; high mortality risk.

**Water scarcity** - Physical (insufficient water resources) or economic (lack of infrastructure) inability to meet water demand; affects agriculture in Middle East, North Africa, Western US, Northern India.

**Waterlogging** - Soil saturation from poor drainage or over-irrigation; restricts root oxygen, reduces yields.

**Wholesale** - Sale of goods in large quantities to retailers or distributors rather than consumers; intermediary stage in supply chain.

## Y

**Yield** - Agricultural output per unit land area (e.g., tons per hectare); primary productivity measure; varies dramatically by region, crop, and inputs.

---

## Summary

This glossary covers core terms spanning agricultural production systems, soil and water science, inputs and technologies, economics and markets, nutrition and food security, climate change and sustainability, and supply chains. Understanding these concepts provides the foundation for analyzing how agriculture feeds humanity while shaping landscapes, economies, and the environment.
