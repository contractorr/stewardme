# Earth Systems & Climate

## Geology's Role in Climate

Climate and geology are intimately connected over timescales from years to billions of years. Geological processes regulate long-term climate, create resources, and record climate history. Understanding these connections bridges geology to Earth's climate system (covered more deeply in the Climate & Earth Systems guide).

### The Long-Term Carbon Cycle

**Atmospheric CO₂** is controlled on million-year timescales by the geological carbon cycle—a negative feedback that stabilizes climate.

**Silicate weathering** (CO₂ sink):

**Chemical reaction**:
```
CaSiO₃ + 2CO₂ + 3H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ + H₄SiO₄
(silicate rock + CO₂ + water → dissolved ions)
```

**Process**:
1. **Rainfall**: Atmospheric CO₂ dissolves → carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)
2. **Weathering**: Acid reacts with silicate minerals (feldspars, pyroxenes) in rocks
3. **River transport**: Dissolved calcium (Ca²⁺) and bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) flow to ocean
4. **Carbonate precipitation**: Marine organisms (foraminifera, coccolithophytes, corals) build shells (CaCO₃)
5. **Burial**: Shells accumulate on seafloor → limestone
6. **Net result**: Atmospheric CO₂ → rock-bound carbon (removed for millions of years)

**Rate depends on**:
- **Temperature**: Warmer → faster weathering (chemical reactions accelerate)
- **Rainfall**: More rain → more weathering
- **Exposed rock area**: Mountain uplift → fresh rock surface → faster weathering

**Negative feedback** (climate stabilization):
- **If Earth warms**: Weathering accelerates → CO₂ drawdown → cooling
- **If Earth cools**: Weathering slows → CO₂ accumulates (volcanism continues) → warming
- **Timescale**: 100,000 to millions of years (too slow for human-caused warming)

**Volcanism** (CO₂ source):

**Processes**:
1. **Seafloor spreading**: Mid-ocean ridges outgas CO₂ from mantle (~70 million tonnes CO₂/year)
2. **Subduction zone volcanism**: Arcs release CO₂ from subducted carbonates + sediments (~80 million tonnes/year)
3. **Hotspot volcanism**: Plumes outgas deep CO₂ (~30 million tonnes/year)
4. **Continental rifts**: Volcanism releases CO₂ (~10 million tonnes/year)
5. **Metamorphism**: Heating carbonate rocks (limestone) releases CO₂

**Total natural outgassing**: ~200 million tonnes CO₂/year
**Human emissions** (2020s): ~37,000 million tonnes CO₂/year (185× natural rate)

**Long-term balance**:
- Volcanic outgassing = silicate weathering (roughly balanced over 10-100 Ma)
- Perturbations (supercontinent assembly/breakup, flood basalts, asteroid impacts) cause climate shifts

### Tectonics and Climate

**Mountain building affects climate**:

**Himalayas-Tibet** (example):
1. **Uplift exposes rock**: India-Asia collision raises 70 km thick crust
2. **Increased weathering**: High topography → more rainfall, physical erosion exposes fresh rock → accelerated CO₂ drawdown
3. **Asian monsoon intensification**: Plateau blocks atmospheric circulation → stronger monsoons → more rainfall → more weathering
4. **Global cooling**: Enhanced weathering drew down CO₂ → cooling over last 50 million years
5. **Ice ages begin**: Sufficient cooling by ~3 Ma → Northern Hemisphere glaciation

**Debated**: How much Himalayan uplift contributed to Cenozoic cooling? (vs other factors: opening of Drake Passage, closure of Panama Isthmus)

**Seafloor spreading rates**:
- **Fast spreading** → more mid-ocean ridge → more volcanism → more CO₂ → warming
- **Slow spreading** → less volcanism → less CO₂ → cooling
- **Cretaceous** (145-66 Ma): Very fast spreading → high CO₂ (1,000-2,000 ppm) → hot climate (no polar ice, palm trees in Arctic)

**Continental positions**:
- **Pangea** (300-200 Ma): Supercontinent assembly → reduced weathering (less coastline, arid interior) → CO₂ accumulation → warming
- **Breakup** → more coastline, tropical landmasses → increased weathering → cooling
- **Ice ages require land at poles**: Antarctica over South Pole (since 35 Ma) enables ice sheet; Arctic Ocean mostly enclosed → Northern Hemisphere ice sheets (since 2.7 Ma)

### Large Igneous Provinces (LIPs)

**Definition**: Massive volcanic eruptions (>100,000 km² area, >500,000 km³ basalt) over ~1 million years.

**Mechanism**:
- Mantle plume head reaches surface
- Decompression melting → flood basalts
- Eruptions episodic (individual eruptions can be weeks-decades, quiet periods between)

**Climate impacts**:

**Short-term** (years-decades): **Cooling**
- SO₂ → sulfate aerosols → reflect sunlight
- Volcanic winter (like Tambora 1815, Pinatubo 1991)

**Long-term** (thousands-millions of years): **Warming**
- CO₂ outgassing → greenhouse effect
- Basalt weathering also releases CO₂ (metamorphosed organic carbon in flood plain sediments)

**Associated mass extinctions**:

| LIP | Age (Ma) | Volume (km³) | Extinction Event | Mechanism |
|-----|----------|-------------|------------------|-----------|
| **Siberian Traps** | 252 | ~4,000,000 | Permian-Triassic (96% marine species) | Warming (~5-8°C), ocean anoxia, acidification, Hg poisoning |
| **CAMP** (Central Atlantic Magmatic Province) | 201 | ~2,000,000 | Triassic-Jurassic (50-80% species) | Warming, acidification |
| **Deccan Traps** | 66 | ~1,000,000 | Cretaceous-Paleogene (75% species)* | Warming, stressed ecosystems pre-impact |

*Deccan likely contributed but asteroid (Chicxulub) was primary driver

**Siberian Traps** (worst extinction):
- Erupted through coal-bearing sediments → released CO₂, CH₄, Hg
- Global warming ~5-8°C
- Ocean anoxia (oxygen-depleted waters)
- Ocean acidification (dissolved CO₂)
- H₂S oceans (anaerobic bacteria produce hydrogen sulfide → poisonous atmosphere)
- **Recovery**: 10 million years

### Snowball Earth Episodes

**Definition**: Global glaciation events—ice sheets reach equator, possibly complete coverage.

**Evidence**:
1. **Glacial deposits at paleoequator**: Dropstones, tillites in rocks deposited at low latitudes (paleomagnetism confirms)
2. **Cap carbonates**: Thick carbonate layers immediately above glacial deposits (rapid deposition from CO₂-rich post-glacial oceans)
3. **Banded Iron Formations**: Returned during Sturtian glaciation (ocean anoxia)

**Major Snowball events**:
- **Huronian** (2.4-2.1 Ga): Triggered by Great Oxidation Event (O₂ destroyed CH₄ greenhouse)
- **Sturtian** (720-660 Ma): ~60 million years
- **Marinoan** (650-635 Ma): Possibly most severe

**Runaway ice-albedo feedback**:

**Initiation**:
1. Climate cools (trigger: reduced CO₂ from enhanced weathering, orbital variations, reduced solar input)
2. Ice sheets expand
3. **Ice reflects sunlight** (albedo ~0.8 vs ocean ~0.06)
4. Less solar absorption → more cooling → more ice → runaway

**Frozen state**:
- Oceans frozen 1-2 km thick (possibly open water at equator—debated)
- Mean temperature: -40 to -50°C
- **No hydrological cycle**: No rain → weathering stops → no CO₂ removal
- **Volcanism continues**: CO₂ accumulates (200 million tonnes/year)

**Exit**:
1. Volcanic CO₂ builds to ~350× pre-industrial (~100,000 ppm) over ~10 million years
2. Super-greenhouse effect → ice melts rapidly (~1,000 years)
3. Dark ocean absorbs sunlight → further melting
4. **Intense weathering**: Exposed rock + warm + CO₂-rich atmosphere → massive carbonate precipitation → cap carbonates

**Biological consequences**:
- **Sturtian-Marinoan**: Eukaryotes survived (low-light photosynthesizers, chemotrophs near volcanic vents?)
- **Aftermath**: Ediacaran biota flourished (~575 Ma) → Cambrian explosion (541 Ma)
- **Hypothesis**: Snowball Earth environmental stress drove evolutionary innovations

**Why hasn't it happened since**:
- **Life**: Vegetation and microbes stabilize climate (weathering feedbacks)
- **Continental positions**: Less conducive to runaway glaciation
- **Sun brighter**: 6% more luminous than 600 Ma

### Orbital (Milankovitch) Cycles

**Milutin Milankovitch** (1920s-1940s): Serbian geophysicist calculated how Earth's orbital variations affect climate.

**Three cycles**:

**1. Eccentricity** (shape of orbit):
- **Period**: ~100,000 years (also 405,000 years)
- **Effect**: Circular orbit (eccentricity ~0) → elliptical (eccentricity ~0.06)
- More elliptical → greater seasonal contrast (larger difference in solar energy between closest/farthest from Sun)
- **Current**: Eccentricity = 0.0167 (nearly circular)

**2. Obliquity** (axial tilt):
- **Period**: ~41,000 years
- **Range**: 22.1° to 24.5°
- Greater tilt → stronger seasons (more extreme summer/winter)
- **Current**: 23.44° (decreasing)

**3. Precession** (wobble of axis):
- **Period**: ~26,000 years (precession of equinoxes)
- Changes which hemisphere has summer when Earth is closest to Sun
- **Current**: Northern Hemisphere summer during aphelion (farthest from Sun) → cooler summers
- **In ~13,000 years**: Northern Hemisphere summer during perihelion → warmer summers

**Combined effect on ice ages**:
- Cycles modulate solar radiation (insolation) received at high latitudes
- **Critical**: High-latitude summer insolation determines if ice persists year-round
- **Weak summer sun** → snow survives summer → ice sheets grow
- **Strong summer sun** → snow melts → ice sheets shrink

**Pleistocene ice age cycles** (last 2.7 Ma):
- **Dominant period**: ~100 ka (eccentricity) for last 900 ka
- Before ~900 ka: ~41 ka cycles (obliquity dominant)
- **Transitions**:
  - Glacial periods: 80-90 ka (slow ice buildup)
  - Interglacial periods: 10-20 ka (rapid warming)
- **Asymmetry**: Gradual cooling, abrupt warming ("sawtooth" pattern)

**Evidence**:
- Ice cores (δ¹⁸O temperature proxy, CO₂ in bubbles)
- Ocean sediments (foram δ¹⁸O)
- Coral reef terraces (sea level)

**Why Milankovitch cycles trigger ice ages now but not during Cretaceous?**
- **Prerequisite conditions**:
  - Continents near poles (Antarctica, Arctic Ocean)
  - Atmospheric CO₂ low enough (~280 ppm during pre-industrial interglacials, ~180 ppm during glacials)
  - Mountain ice sheets (Himalayas, Rockies, Andes) seed glaciation
- **Cretaceous**: CO₂ too high (~1,000-2,000 ppm), no polar landmasses → Milankovitch cycles had minimal effect

### Earth's Deep Time Climate Record

**Archean** (4.0-2.5 Ga):
- **Faint Young Sun Paradox**: Sun 20-30% dimmer, yet Earth not frozen
- **Solution**: Greenhouse gases (CO₂ ~10-100× present, CH₄)
- **Temperature**: Possibly 40-70°C (evidence: thermophilic bacteria fossils, oxygen isotopes)

**Proterozoic** (2.5-0.541 Ga):
- **Early Proterozoic** (2.5-1.8 Ga): Warmer, high CO₂
- **Mid-Proterozoic** (1.8-0.8 Ga): "Boring billion"—stable, moderate temperatures, low O₂
- **Neoproterozoic** (0.8-0.541 Ga): Snowball Earth events (720, 650 Ma)

**Paleozoic** (541-252 Ma):
- **Cambrian-Ordovician** (541-444 Ma): Warm, high CO₂ (~4,000-5,000 ppm)
- **Late Ordovician** (445 Ma): Brief ice age (Gondwana over South Pole)
- **Silurian-Devonian** (444-359 Ma): Warm again
- **Carboniferous-Permian** (359-252 Ma): Major ice age (Gondwana glaciation), then warming
  - CO₂ drawdown from coal-forming forests → ice age
  - Pangea formation → arid interior, reduced weathering → CO₂ accumulation → warming

**Mesozoic** (252-66 Ma):
- **Warmest sustained period in Phanerozoic**
- **Peak warmth**: Cretaceous (~100 Ma)
- CO₂: 1,000-2,000 ppm (4-7× pre-industrial)
- **No polar ice**: Palm trees in Arctic, crocodiles near poles
- Sea level: +200 m higher than present (thermal expansion + no ice sheets)
- Ocean temps: 30-35°C tropics, 15°C poles (vs 28°C and 0°C today)

**Cenozoic** (66 Ma-present):
- **Long-term cooling**:
  - Peak warmth: PETM (56 Ma, global temps +5-8°C)
  - Gradual cooling: CO₂ decline from 1,000+ ppm (Paleocene) → 280 ppm (pre-industrial)
- **Ice sheet formation**:
  - Antarctica (35 Ma): Circumpolar current isolates Antarctica → ice sheet forms
  - Northern Hemisphere (2.7 Ma): Ice sheets in North America, Europe
- **Quaternary** (2.6 Ma-present): Glacial-interglacial cycles (Milankovitch)

**Current interglacial** (Holocene, 11,700 years-present):
- Relatively stable until Industrial Revolution (~1750)
- **Anthropocene**: CO₂ 280 ppm (1750) → 420 ppm (2024) in 274 years
  - Rate ~100× faster than natural transitions (deglaciations: 80 ppm in 10,000 years)
  - Temperature: +1.2°C (2024 vs 1850-1900 baseline)

### Paleoclimate Proxies (Geological)

**1. Oxygen isotopes (δ¹⁸O)**:

**Principle**:
- ¹⁶O (lighter) evaporates preferentially → rain enriched in ¹⁶O
- During ice ages: ¹⁶O locked in ice sheets → ocean enriched in ¹⁸O
- Marine organisms (foraminifera) record ocean δ¹⁸O in shells (CaCO₃)

**Applications**:
- Ice core δ¹⁸O: Temperature proxy (polar regions)
- Ocean sediment foram δ¹⁸O: Ice volume + temperature

**2. Carbon isotopes (δ¹³C)**:
- Plants prefer ¹²C during photosynthesis
- Organic-rich sediments depleted in ¹³C
- Sudden negative δ¹³C excursions → massive organic carbon release or burial
  - PETM (56 Ma): -3‰ shift → methane hydrate release hypothesis

**3. Glacial deposits**:
- **Tillites**: Lithified till (unsorted sediment from glaciers)
- **Dropstones**: Large rocks dropped by melting icebergs into fine sediment (indicates nearby glaciers)
- **Striations**: Scratches on bedrock from moving ice

**4. Evaporites** (gypsum, halite):
- Form in arid, evaporative conditions
- Indicate hot, dry climate

**5. Coal deposits**:
- Require warm, wet swamps
- Abundant in Carboniferous-Permian (tropical climate)

**6. Reefs**:
- Coral reefs: Warm (<30°C), shallow, clear seas
- Distribution through time indicates tropical belts

**7. Paleosoils** (ancient soils):
- **Laterites**: Deep weathering in tropics
- **Calcretes** (calcium carbonate): Arid climates
- **Clay mineralogy**: Kaolinite (wet tropics), smectite (seasonal), illite (temperate)

**8. Leaf morphology**:
- **Entire margins** (smooth edges): Warm, wet
- **Toothed margins**: Cooler, seasonal
- **Size**: Larger leaves = warmer, wetter

**9. Banded Iron Formations** (BIFs):
- Occur mostly 2.5-1.8 Ga
- Require anoxic oceans + dissolved iron + periodic oxygen pulses
- Climate connection: Glaciations (Snowball Earth) may have enhanced BIF deposition

### Geological Carbon Sequestration

**Concept**: Capture anthropogenic CO₂ and inject into geological formations for permanent storage.

**Target formations**:

**1. Deep saline aquifers** (most capacity):
- Porous sandstone/limestone >800 m depth
- Filled with brine (too salty for use)
- CO₂ injected as supercritical fluid (liquid-like density, gas-like viscosity)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - **Structural trapping**: Cap rock (shale, anhydrite) prevents upward migration
  - **Residual trapping**: CO₂ droplets immobilized in pores
  - **Dissolution trapping**: CO₂ dissolves in brine (denser → sinks)
  - **Mineral trapping**: CO₂ reacts with rock → carbonates (permanent, but slow: millennia)
- **Examples**:
  - Sleipner (Norway): 1 Mt CO₂/year since 1996 into North Sea saline aquifer
  - Gorgon (Australia): 4 Mt/year into Dupuy Formation

**2. Depleted oil/gas fields**:
- Already proven seals (held hydrocarbons for millions of years)
- Existing infrastructure (wells, monitoring)
- **Enhanced oil recovery** (EOR): Inject CO₂ → pushes remaining oil out → stores CO₂
  - Economic incentive (oil revenue)
  - But: Some CO₂ returns to surface with oil (net storage ~70-80%)

**3. Basalt formations**:
- CO₂ reacts with basalt → carbonate minerals (calcite, magnesite)
- **Fast**: 95% mineralized in 2 years (vs 1,000s of years in sedimentary rocks)
- **CarbFix** (Iceland): Injects CO₂ + H₂O into basalt (Hellisheidi geothermal plant)
- **Limitation**: Requires large volumes of water, specific geology (reactive basalt)

**4. Unmineable coal seams**:
- CO₂ adsorbs onto coal
- Displaces methane (can be recovered)
- **Limited capacity**, complex

**Total global storage capacity**: Estimates 2,000-20,000 Gt CO₂ (highly uncertain)
- Current annual emissions: ~37 Gt CO₂
- **Scale challenge**: 50-500 years of emissions can be stored (but need to deploy at scale)

**Risks**:
- **Leakage**: CO₂ escapes through faults, abandoned wells, cap rock fractures
  - Slow leakage (1% per century) defeats purpose
  - Rapid leakage: Asphyxiation risk (CO₂ heavier than air, accumulates in valleys)
    - Lake Nyos (1986): Natural CO₂ release killed 1,700 people
- **Induced seismicity**: Injection increases pore pressure → can reactivate faults
  - Mostly small (<M3), but public concern
- **Monitoring**: Requires long-term (century-scale) verification

**Current deployment**: ~40 Mt CO₂/year captured globally (2023)
- **Needs**: >5,000 Mt/year by 2050 for net-zero pathways (IEA)
- **Challenge**: 125× scale-up in 27 years

### Volcanism and Short-Term Climate

**Sulfate aerosols** (primary cooling mechanism):
- Explosive eruptions inject SO₂ into stratosphere (>10 km altitude)
- SO₂ + OH• + H₂O → H₂SO₄ droplets (sulfate aerosols)
- Aerosols reflect sunlight (albedo effect) → cooling
- **Persistence**: 1-3 years in stratosphere (tropospheric aerosols rain out in weeks)

**Cooling magnitude**:
```
ΔT (°C) ≈ -0.003 × SO₂ (megatonnes)
```
- Rough approximation; actual depends on latitude, season, eruption height

**Historic examples**:
- **Tambora 1815**: 60 Mt SO₂ → -0.5 to -0.7°C global → "Year Without Summer" (1816)
- **Krakatoa 1883**: 20 Mt SO₂ → -0.3°C → 1 year cooling
- **Pinatubo 1991**: 20 Mt SO₂ → -0.5°C → 2 years cooling

**VEI-6 threshold**: Eruptions injecting >10 Mt SO₂ cause measurable global cooling

**CO₂ from volcanoes**:
- Eruptions release CO₂ but **amounts trivial vs human emissions**
- Pinatubo: ~50 Mt CO₂ (vs 23,000 Mt from humans in 1991)
- **Long-term**: Volcanic CO₂ (~200 Mt/year) ≪ human CO₂ (~37,000 Mt/year)
- **Cooling dominates**: Sulfate effect outweighs CO₂ warming for first 3-5 years

**Super-eruptions**:
- VEI-7/8 (>100 km³): Decade-long volcanic winter
- Toba (74 ka): -3 to -5°C for 10-15 years (models vary)
- Agricultural collapse, mass starvation likely

## Key Terms

- **Silicate weathering**: Chemical breakdown of silicate rocks by carbonic acid, removes atmospheric CO₂
- **Long-term carbon cycle**: Geological processes (weathering, volcanism, sedimentation) regulating CO₂ over millions of years
- **Negative feedback**: Self-regulating process (warming → faster weathering → CO₂ drawdown → cooling)
- **Large Igneous Province (LIP)**: Massive volcanic event (>500,000 km³ basalt over ~1 Ma)
- **Snowball Earth**: Global glaciation events (~720, 650 Ma) where ice reached equator
- **Cap carbonates**: Thick carbonate layers deposited after Snowball Earth (from super-greenhouse)
- **Milankovitch cycles**: Orbital variations (eccentricity, obliquity, precession) modulating ice age cycles
- **Eccentricity**: Orbital shape variation (circular ↔ elliptical), ~100 ka period
- **Obliquity**: Axial tilt variation (22-24.5°), ~41 ka period
- **Precession**: Axial wobble, ~26 ka period
- **PETM**: Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (56 Ma), rapid warming event (+5-8°C)
- **δ¹⁸O (delta-O-18)**: Oxygen isotope ratio, proxy for temperature and ice volume
- **Paleoclimate proxies**: Geological indicators of past climate (isotopes, fossils, sediments)
- **Geological carbon sequestration**: Capturing CO₂ and injecting into subsurface formations
- **Sulfate aerosols**: Stratospheric H₂SO₄ droplets from volcanic SO₂, reflect sunlight → cooling

## Summary

Geology and climate are coupled over timescales from years to billions of years. The long-term carbon cycle regulates atmospheric CO₂ via silicate weathering (removes CO₂) and volcanism (adds CO₂), creating negative feedback that stabilizes climate over millions of years. Enhanced weathering from Himalayan uplift (~50 Ma-present) may have contributed to Cenozoic cooling and Quaternary ice ages. Continental positions affect ocean circulation and ice sheet formation; Antarctica over South Pole enabled ice sheet growth 35 Ma.

Large Igneous Provinces (Siberian Traps 252 Ma, CAMP 201 Ma, Deccan 66 Ma) cause mass extinctions via CO₂ outgassing → warming, ocean anoxia, acidification. Snowball Earth events (720, 650 Ma) involved runaway ice-albedo feedback → global glaciation → volcanic CO₂ accumulation (~100,000 ppm) → super-greenhouse melting. Cap carbonates record rapid post-Snowball carbonate deposition.

Milankovitch cycles (eccentricity ~100 ka, obliquity ~41 ka, precession ~26 ka) modulate solar insolation at high latitudes, driving Pleistocene glacial-interglacial cycles. Ice ages require prerequisite conditions: continents near poles, low CO₂ (~180-280 ppm). Earth's deep-time record shows warmest sustained period in Cretaceous (1,000-2,000 ppm CO₂, no polar ice, +200 m sea level), followed by Cenozoic cooling.

Paleoclimate proxies—oxygen isotopes (ice cores, foraminifera), carbon isotopes, glacial deposits, evaporites, coal, reefs, paleosoils, leaf morphology—reconstruct ancient climates. Geological carbon sequestration (injecting CO₂ into saline aquifers, depleted oil fields, basalts) offers potential storage of 2,000-20,000 Gt CO₂, but current deployment (~40 Mt/year) needs 125× scale-up by 2050. Risks include leakage and induced seismicity.

Volcanic eruptions cause short-term cooling via sulfate aerosols (Tambora 1815: -0.5°C, Pinatubo 1991: -0.5°C for 1-3 years). Super-eruptions (VEI-7/8) could cause decade-long volcanic winter. Volcanic CO₂ emissions (~200 Mt/year) are trivial compared to human emissions (~37,000 Mt/year = 185× natural rate). Understanding geological climate regulation provides context for current anthropogenic disruption—human CO₂ rise (280→420 ppm in 274 years) is ~100× faster than natural deglaciation rates, overwhelming geological negative feedbacks that operate over 100,000+ year timescales.
