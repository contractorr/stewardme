# Rocks, Minerals & Resources

## Minerals

### Definition and Properties

**Mineral**: Naturally occurring, inorganic, crystalline solid with definite chemical composition and ordered internal structure.

**Five criteria**:
1. **Naturally occurring**: Formed by geological processes (excludes synthetic crystals)
2. **Inorganic**: Not produced by organisms (excludes shells, coal, pearls as minerals—though they are geological materials)
3. **Solid**: Definite shape and volume (excludes water, mercury, natural gases)
4. **Crystalline**: Atoms arranged in ordered, repeating pattern
5. **Definite chemical composition**: Specific formula, often with limited substitution allowed

**~5,400 known minerals**, but ~20 "rock-forming minerals" comprise >95% of Earth's crust.

### Crystal Structure

**Crystal**: Solid with atoms arranged in repeating 3D pattern (lattice).

**Crystal systems** (7 types based on symmetry and axis lengths/angles):

| System | Axes | Angles | Example Minerals |
|--------|------|--------|------------------|
| **Cubic** (Isometric) | a=b=c | 90°,90°,90° | Halite (NaCl), pyrite, garnet, diamond |
| **Tetragonal** | a=b≠c | 90°,90°,90° | Zircon, rutile |
| **Orthorhombic** | a≠b≠c | 90°,90°,90° | Olivine, aragonite |
| **Monoclinic** | a≠b≠c | 90°,α,90° | Gypsum, orthoclase feldspar |
| **Triclinic** | a≠b≠c | α≠β≠γ | Plagioclase feldspar, kyanite |
| **Hexagonal** | a=b≠c | 90°,90°,120° | Quartz (high-T form), beryl |
| **Trigonal** | a=b=c | α=β=γ≠90° | Calcite, dolomite, quartz (low-T) |

### Physical Properties

**Color**: Often diagnostic but can be variable (impurities change color)
- Quartz: Clear (pure), purple (amethyst, Fe³⁺), pink (rose quartz), smoky (irradiated), yellow (citrine)

**Streak**: Color of powdered mineral (more reliable than color)
- Hematite: Metallic gray/black appearance, reddish-brown streak

**Luster**: Appearance in reflected light
- Metallic (galena, pyrite), vitreous/glassy (quartz), pearly (talc), greasy (jade), dull/earthy (kaolinite)

**Hardness**: Resistance to scratching (Mohs scale, 1-10)

| Mohs Hardness | Mineral | Can be scratched by | Absolute Hardness |
|---------------|---------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Talc** | Fingernail | 1 |
| 2 | **Gypsum** | Fingernail (2.5) | 3 |
| 3 | **Calcite** | Copper coin | 9 |
| 4 | **Fluorite** | Iron nail | 21 |
| 5 | **Apatite** | Steel knife (5.5) | 48 |
| 6 | **Orthoclase feldspar** | Quartz | 72 |
| 7 | **Quartz** | Steel file | 100 |
| 8 | **Topaz** | Sapphire | 200 |
| 9 | **Corundum** (sapphire, ruby) | Diamond | 400 |
| 10 | **Diamond** | Nothing natural | 1,500 |

Note: Scale is logarithmic in absolute hardness. Diamond is not 10× harder than talc—it's 1,500× harder.

**Cleavage**: Tendency to break along flat planes (related to crystal structure)
- **Perfect cleavage**: Muscovite (mica)—splits into thin sheets
- **Good cleavage**: Halite—breaks into cubes (3 perpendicular cleavage planes)
- **Poor/no cleavage**: Quartz—fractures irregularly (conchoidal fracture)

**Fracture**: Break pattern when no cleavage
- Conchoidal (curved, glass-like): Quartz, obsidian
- Fibrous: Asbestos
- Hackly (jagged): Native metals

**Specific gravity**: Density relative to water
- Light minerals (<2.5): Halite (2.2), gypsum (2.3)
- Average silicates (2.5-3.5): Quartz (2.65), feldspar (2.6-2.7), calcite (2.7)
- Mafic minerals (3.0-4.0): Olivine (3.3-4.4), pyroxene (3.2-3.6)
- Heavy minerals (>4.0): Magnetite (5.2), pyrite (5.0), galena (7.5)
- Native metals: Copper (8.9), silver (10.5), gold (19.3)

### Major Rock-Forming Mineral Groups

**Silicates** (90% of crust):
- Silicon-oxygen tetrahedra (SiO₄⁴⁻) linked in various arrangements
- **Framework silicates**: Quartz (SiO₂), feldspars
- **Sheet silicates**: Micas (muscovite, biotite), clay minerals
- **Chain silicates**: Pyroxenes, amphiboles
- **Isolated silicates**: Olivine, garnet

**Feldspars** (50-60% of crust):
- **K-feldspar** (orthoclase, microcline): KAlSi₃O₈
- **Plagioclase**: (Na,Ca)(Al,Si)₄O₈ — complete solid solution from albite (NaAlSi₃O₈) to anorthite (CaAl₂Si₂O₈)
- Forms at high T, major component of granite, basalt, gneiss

**Quartz** (12% of crust):
- SiO₂
- Second most abundant mineral after feldspars
- Very hard (7), no cleavage, resistant to weathering
- Found in granite, sandstone, quartzite

**Micas**:
- **Muscovite** (white mica): KAl₂(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂ — in granite, schist
- **Biotite** (black mica): K(Mg,Fe)₃(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂ — in granite, gneiss, schist
- Perfect cleavage (sheet structure)

**Pyroxenes**:
- Single-chain silicates (Si₂O₆)
- **Augite**: (Ca,Mg,Fe)₂Si₂O₆ — common in basalt, gabbro
- Dark color, 90° cleavage angles

**Amphiboles**:
- Double-chain silicates
- **Hornblende**: (Ca,Na,K)₂(Mg,Fe,Al)₅(Si,Al)₈O₂₂(OH)₂ — in andesite, diorite, amphibolite
- Dark color, 120° cleavage angles

**Olivine**:
- (Mg,Fe)₂SiO₄
- Olive-green color
- Major component of mantle peridotite, basalt

**Carbonates**:
- **Calcite**: CaCO₃ — limestone, marble
- **Dolomite**: CaMg(CO₃)₂ — dolostone
- Reacts with acid (fizzes): CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

**Oxides**:
- **Magnetite**: Fe₃O₄ — iron ore, strongly magnetic
- **Hematite**: Fe₂O₃ — major iron ore, red color
- **Ilmenite**: FeTiO₃ — titanium ore

**Sulfides**:
- **Pyrite**: FeS₂ — "fool's gold," golden metallic luster
- **Galena**: PbS — lead ore, high density, cubic cleavage
- **Chalcopyrite**: CuFeS₂ — copper ore

**Halides**:
- **Halite**: NaCl — rock salt, cubic cleavage, tastes salty
- **Fluorite**: CaF₂ — flux in steelmaking, fluorine source

## Rocks

**Rock**: Aggregate of one or more minerals (or organic matter). Three main types based on formation process.

### Igneous Rocks

**Definition**: Formed from cooling and solidification of magma (below surface) or lava (above surface).

**Texture** (grain size indicates cooling rate):
- **Intrusive** (plutonic): Slow cooling → large crystals (coarse-grained, phaneritic)
- **Extrusive** (volcanic): Fast cooling → small crystals (fine-grained, aphanitic) or glass
- **Porphyritic**: Two stages—large crystals (phenocrysts) in fine-grained matrix (groundmass)

**Composition** (silica content SiO₂):

| Type | SiO₂ % | Color | Minerals | Intrusive | Extrusive | Plate Setting |
|------|--------|-------|----------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Felsic** | 65-75% | Light (white, pink, gray) | Quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, muscovite | **Granite** | **Rhyolite** | Continental crust, arcs |
| **Intermediate** | 55-65% | Medium gray | Plagioclase, amphibole, biotite, minor quartz | **Diorite** | **Andesite** | Subduction zones (arcs) |
| **Mafic** | 45-55% | Dark gray to black | Plagioclase, pyroxene, olivine | **Gabbro** | **Basalt** | Ocean floor, rifts, hotspots |
| **Ultramafic** | <45% | Green-black | Olivine, pyroxene | **Peridotite** | **Komatiite** (rare) | Mantle |

**Volcanic glass**:
- **Obsidian**: Felsic composition, black, conchoidal fracture (no time for crystals)
- **Pumice**: Felsic, highly vesicular (gas bubbles), floats on water
- **Scoria**: Mafic, vesicular, dark, heavier than pumice

**Bowen's Reaction Series** (crystallization order from magma):

**Discontinuous branch** (isolated → chain → sheet):
1. Olivine (highest T, ~1,200°C)
2. Pyroxene (~1,100°C)
3. Amphibole (~1,000°C)
4. Biotite (~850°C)

**Continuous branch** (plagioclase solid solution):
- Ca-rich (anorthite) → Na-rich (albite) as temperature drops

**Final crystallization** (~650-700°C):
- K-feldspar
- Muscovite
- Quartz

**Applications**:
- **Magmatic differentiation**: Single magma can produce multiple rock types (fractional crystallization removes early-formed minerals)
- **Partial melting**: Only some minerals melt → changes composition (mafic source → felsic melt if only low-T minerals melt)

### Sedimentary Rocks

**Definition**: Formed from lithification (compaction + cementation) of sediments.

**Formation steps**:
1. **Weathering**: Breakdown of rock (mechanical, chemical)
2. **Erosion**: Removal of weathered material
3. **Transportation**: Movement by water, wind, ice, gravity
4. **Deposition**: Settling in layers (strata)
5. **Burial**: Overlying sediment accumulates
6. **Lithification**: Compaction (squeezes out water) + cementation (minerals precipitate in pores)

**Types**:

**A. Clastic** (detrital)—fragments of pre-existing rock:

| Rock | Grain Size | Composition | Depositional Environment |
|------|-----------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Conglomerate** | >2 mm (rounded) | Mixed rock fragments | High-energy rivers, beaches |
| **Breccia** | >2 mm (angular) | Mixed rock fragments | Landslides, fault zones |
| **Sandstone** | 0.0625-2 mm | Quartz-rich (usually) | Rivers, beaches, deserts, shallow seas |
| **Siltstone** | 0.004-0.0625 mm | Quartz, clays | Low-energy rivers, lakes, deep ocean |
| **Shale/Mudstone** | <0.004 mm | Clay minerals | Lakes, lagoons, deep ocean |

**B. Chemical**—precipitation from solution:

| Rock | Composition | Formation | Uses |
|------|-------------|-----------|------|
| **Limestone** | CaCO₃ (calcite) | Warm, shallow seas; CaCO₃ precipitation | Cement, building stone, flux |
| **Dolostone** | CaMg(CO₃)₂ (dolomite) | Mg-rich water alters limestone | Building stone, magnesium source |
| **Evaporite** (halite, gypsum, anhydrite) | NaCl, CaSO₄·2H₂O | Evaporation of seawater or saline lakes | Salt, plaster, drywall |
| **Chert** (flint, jasper) | SiO₂ (microcrystalline quartz) | Silica precipitation or diagenesis | Tools (prehistoric), abrasives |
| **Iron formation** (banded iron, BIF) | Fe₂O₃, Fe₃O₄, SiO₂ | Precambrian oxygen buildup | Major iron ore |

**C. Biochemical/Organic**—biological origin:

| Rock | Composition | Formation | Uses |
|------|-------------|-----------|------|
| **Limestone** (fossiliferous) | CaCO₃ (shell fragments) | Accumulation of marine organisms | Building stone, cement |
| **Chalk** | CaCO₃ (coccolith plates) | Deep-sea plankton accumulation | Blackboard chalk, lime |
| **Coal** | Carbon from plant matter | Buried swamp vegetation, compressed | Fuel (declining), steel production |
| **Diatomite** | SiO₂ (diatom frustules) | Freshwater/marine diatom accumulation | Filters, absorbents, insulation |

**Sedimentary structures**:
- **Bedding/stratification**: Layering (different grain sizes, compositions, or conditions)
- **Cross-bedding**: Inclined layers within main bed (indicates current direction—wind or water)
- **Graded bedding**: Grain size decreases upward (turbidity currents)
- **Ripple marks**: Small waves preserved in sand (water or wind currents)
- **Mud cracks**: Polygonal cracks from drying (indicates subaerial exposure)
- **Fossils**: Preserved remains or traces of organisms

**Importance**:
- **Fossil record**: Only sedimentary rocks preserve fossils (heat destroys organic material)
- **Paleoenvironments**: Sedimentary features indicate ancient climates, water depths, currents
- **Resources**: Oil, gas, coal, uranium, groundwater aquifers

### Metamorphic Rocks

**Definition**: Pre-existing rocks transformed by heat and/or pressure (without melting).

**Metamorphic agents**:
1. **Heat**: Increased temperature (200-800°C) increases atomic mobility → mineral recrystallization
2. **Pressure**: Directed (stress) causes alignment; confining (burial) increases density
3. **Fluids**: Hot water/steam transport ions, enable chemical reactions

**Types**:

**A. Foliated** (layered, aligned minerals):

| Rock | Foliation Type | Parent Rock | Metamorphic Grade | Minerals |
|------|----------------|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Slate** | Slaty cleavage (microscopic) | Shale/mudstone | Low | Clay minerals, fine micas |
| **Phyllite** | Lustrous sheen | Slate | Low-medium | Fine micas (sericite) |
| **Schist** | Schistosity (visible mica flakes) | Phyllite, shale, basalt | Medium | Muscovite, biotite, garnet, staurolite |
| **Gneiss** | Banding (light/dark layers) | Granite, schist | High | Feldspar, quartz, biotite, amphibole |
| **Migmatite** | Partial melting | Gneiss | Very high (near melting) | Felsic melt veins + solid remnants |

**B. Non-foliated** (no preferred orientation):

| Rock | Parent Rock | Metamorphic Conditions | Characteristics |
|------|-------------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Marble** | Limestone/dolostone | Contact or regional | Interlocking calcite/dolomite crystals, reacts with acid |
| **Quartzite** | Quartz sandstone | Contact or regional | Fused quartz grains, very hard, glassy luster |
| **Hornfels** | Any fine-grained rock | Contact (intrusion heat) | Fine-grained, tough, no foliation |
| **Granulite** | Various | Very high T, deep burial | Granular texture, high-grade minerals |

**Metamorphic facies** (P-T conditions):
- Groups of minerals that form together under specific pressure-temperature conditions
- **Index minerals**: Indicate metamorphic grade
  - Chlorite (low-grade) → Biotite → Garnet → Staurolite → Kyanite → Sillimanite (high-grade)
- **Geothermometry/geobarometry**: Estimate ancient T and P from mineral assemblages

**Settings**:
- **Contact metamorphism**: Heat from magma intrusion affects surrounding rock (localized, hornfels)
- **Regional metamorphism**: Large-scale burial + tectonic compression (slate → schist → gneiss)
- **Hydrothermal**: Hot fluids alter rock (mid-ocean ridges, ore deposits)
- **Dynamic**: Fault zones (intense shearing, mylonite)

### The Rock Cycle

**Continuous transformation** between rock types:

**Igneous → Sedimentary**:
- Weathering + erosion → sediment → lithification

**Sedimentary → Metamorphic**:
- Burial + heat/pressure → metamorphism

**Metamorphic → Igneous**:
- Melting → magma → crystallization

**Shortcuts**:
- Igneous → Metamorphic: Heat/pressure without melting
- Sedimentary → Sedimentary: Uplift, re-weathering, re-deposition
- Metamorphic → Sedimentary: Uplift, weathering, erosion

**No inherent directionality**: Any rock type can transform into any other. Timescale: millions of years typically.

## Ore Deposits and Mineral Resources

**Ore**: Rock containing valuable mineral(s) in economically extractable concentration.

**Concentration factor**: How much more concentrated than average crust?
- **Gold**: Average crust 0.004 ppm; ore needs ~5 ppm → 1,250× concentration
- **Copper**: Average crust 50 ppm; ore needs ~5,000 ppm (0.5%) → 100× concentration
- **Iron**: Average crust 5%; ore needs ~30-60% → 6-12× concentration
- **Aluminum**: Average crust 8%; ore (bauxite) needs ~30% → 4× concentration

### Ore-Forming Processes

**1. Magmatic Segregation**:
- Dense minerals (chromite, magnetite, platinum group metals) sink in magma chamber
- Accumulate as layers at bottom
- **Example**: Bushveld Complex (South Africa)—90% of world's platinum, 80% of chromium reserves
  - 2 billion tonnes Cr₂O₃, 30,000 tonnes platinum group metals

**2. Hydrothermal Deposits**:
- Hot water (200-400°C) circulates through cracks
- Dissolves metals from surrounding rock
- Precipitates when conditions change (T, P, pH)

**Types**:
- **Porphyry copper**: Large intrusion → fractures → copper sulfide deposition (chalcopyrite)
  - Chile (Escondida, Chuquicamata), Arizona (Morenci)
  - 60% of world copper
- **Vein deposits**: Metals (gold, silver, lead, zinc) in quartz veins
  - Comstock Lode (Nevada): Silver/gold
- **Massive sulfide**: Seafloor hydrothermal vents deposit copper-zinc-lead sulfides
  - Cyprus-type (ophiolites), Kuroko-type (back-arc basins)

**3. Sedimentary Concentration**:
- **Placer deposits**: Dense minerals (gold, diamonds, tin) concentrated in rivers/beaches by water sorting
  - California Gold Rush placers
  - Diamond beaches (Namibia)
- **Evaporites**: Salt (NaCl), gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O), potash (K-salts) from evaporating seawater
- **Banded Iron Formations** (BIFs): Precambrian (2.5-1.8 Ga) oxygen buildup precipitated dissolved iron
  - >60% Fe (hematite, magnetite)
  - Largest iron ore source globally

**4. Weathering and Enrichment**:
- **Bauxite** (aluminum ore): Tropical weathering removes silica, concentrates aluminum hydroxides
  - Requires millions of years of tropical conditions
  - Guinea, Australia, Brazil
- **Laterite**: Iron/nickel enrichment in tropical soils
  - New Caledonia (25% of world nickel reserves)
- **Secondary enrichment**: Surface oxidation + groundwater leaching → metals dissolve → reprecipitate at depth (supergene enrichment)

### Critical Minerals and Geopolitics

**Definition**: Minerals essential for economy/security, with supply risk.

**Rare Earth Elements (REEs)**:
- 17 elements: Lanthanides + scandium + yttrium
- **Uses**: Magnets (Nd, Dy), batteries (La), phosphors (Eu, Tb), catalysts (Ce)
- **Supply**: 70% mined in China (Bayan Obo deposit)
  - Processing: 85-90% in China (toxic, expensive separation)
- **Geology**: Carbonatites (igneous carbonate rocks), ion-adsorption clays
- **Geopolitics**: China restricted exports 2010 → price spike → diversification efforts (Mountain Pass, CA; Nolans Bore, Australia)

**Lithium**:
- **Uses**: Batteries (EVs, electronics), ceramics, pharmaceuticals
- **Supply**: "Lithium triangle"—Chile, Argentina, Bolivia (60% of reserves)
  - **Brine deposits**: Salar de Atacama (Chile), Salar de Uyuni (Bolivia)
    - Evaporate brine (months-years), extract LiCl
  - **Hard rock** (spodumene, LiAlSi₂O₆): Australia (50% of production), faster extraction
- **Demand**: Growing ~20%/year (EV adoption)
- **Geopolitics**: Bolivia nationalized lithium (underdeveloped), China controls processing

**Cobalt**:
- **Uses**: Batteries (cathodes), superalloys, magnets
- **Supply**: 70% from Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC)
  - Artisanal mining (child labor concerns)
  - Chinese companies control most DRC production
- **Geology**: Copper-cobalt deposits in Central African Copperbelt
- **Strategy**: Substitution research (cobalt-free batteries), recycling

**Copper**:
- **Uses**: Wiring, electronics, plumbing, motors, renewables (wind/solar use 4-5× more Cu than fossil plants)
- **Supply**: Chile (28%), Peru (12%), China (9%)
- **Reserves**: 880 million tonnes (40-50 years at current use)
- **Challenge**: Grade declining (1% today vs 3-5% historically) → more energy, waste per tonne

**Platinum Group Metals (PGMs)**:
- **Uses**: Catalytic converters, hydrogen fuel cells, electronics, jewelry
- **Supply**: South Africa (70%), Russia (13%)
- **Geology**: Bushveld Complex (layered mafic intrusion)
- **Risk**: Concentrated supply, strike vulnerability

**Tantalum**:
- **Uses**: Capacitors (electronics), superalloys, surgical implants
- **Supply**: DRC (40%), Rwanda, Brazil
- **Conflict mineral**: Funded armed groups in DRC ("blood tantalum")
- **Geology**: Pegmatites (very coarse granite), coltan (columbite-tantalite)

**Strategic reserves**:
- US National Defense Stockpile: Established WWII, contains ~40 minerals (Pt, Co, rare earths, etc.)
- China stockpiling: Copper, rare earths, oil, grain
- Goal: Ensure supply during disruptions (war, embargo, natural disaster)

## Fossil Fuels

### Oil and Natural Gas

**Formation** (over ~10-100 million years):

**Stage 1: Source rock formation**
- Marine microorganisms (phytoplankton, algae) die, sink
- Oxygen-poor seafloor preserves organic matter (>1% TOC—total organic carbon)
- Burial by sediments → fine-grained organic-rich shale or limestone

**Stage 2: Diagenesis → Catagenesis** (oil/gas generation)
- **Depth 0-2 km, <50°C**: Biogenic gas (methane from bacteria)
- **Depth 2-5 km, 60-120°C** ("oil window"): Kerogen → oil + gas
  - Kerogen: Solid organic matter (Type I, II, III depending on source)
  - Type I (algal): Highest oil yield
  - Type II (mixed marine): Good oil yield
  - Type III (terrestrial plants): Mostly gas
- **Depth >5 km, >150°C** ("gas window"): Oil → natural gas (thermal cracking)
- **Depth >7-8 km, >200°C**: All hydrocarbons destroyed (graphite remains)

**Stage 3: Migration**
- Oil/gas less dense than water → buoyancy drives upward
- Moves through permeable rocks (sandstone, fractured limestone)
- **Primary migration**: Out of source rock
- **Secondary migration**: Through carrier beds to trap

**Stage 4: Accumulation in traps**
- **Trap**: Geometry that prevents further migration
  - **Reservoir rock**: Porous + permeable (sandstone, carbonate reefs)
  - **Seal/cap rock**: Impermeable (shale, salt, anhydrite)
- **Structural traps**: Folds (anticlines), faults, salt domes
- **Stratigraphic traps**: Pinch-outs, unconformities, reefs

**World oil reserves** (~1.7 trillion barrels proven, 2023):
- Venezuela (18%), Saudi Arabia (17%), Iran (9%), Iraq (8%), Kuwait (6%)
- ~50 years at current consumption (100 million barrels/day)
- **Peak oil debate**: When will production peak? Conventional peaked ~2005-2010; unconventional (shale, tight oil) extended plateau

**Natural gas reserves** (~200 trillion cubic meters):
- Russia (19%), Iran (17%), Qatar (13%), Turkmenistan (7%)
- ~50 years at current consumption
- Cleaner than oil/coal (less CO₂ per energy, minimal SO₂/particulates)

**Shale revolution** (US, 2000s):
- Horizontal drilling + hydraulic fracturing ("fracking") unlocked tight shale oil/gas
- Bakken (North Dakota), Eagle Ford (Texas), Permian Basin (Texas/New Mexico)
- US became net oil exporter 2019 (first time since 1949)
- EROEI lower (~5-15:1 vs 20-100:1 for conventional)

### Coal

**Formation**:
- **Peat** (surface): Partially decayed plant matter in swamps
- **Burial**: Sediment covers peat → temperature + pressure increase
- **Coalification**: Progressive carbon concentration
  - Peat (60% C) → Lignite (70% C) → Bituminous (80% C) → Anthracite (90% C)
- **Time**: Typically 50-100 Ma minimum; most coal from Carboniferous (359-299 Ma) and Permian (299-252 Ma)

**Why Carboniferous was coal age**:
1. Vast swamp forests (tree ferns, lycopods, horsetails)
2. Warm, wet climate (tropical conditions widespread)
3. Lignin-degrading fungi hadn't evolved → plant material didn't decompose
4. Repeated sea level changes → burial cycles

**World coal reserves** (~1.1 trillion tonnes):
- US (23%), Russia (15%), Australia (14%), China (13%)
- ~140 years at current consumption
- Declining in developed nations (emissions, economics), growing in Asia (cheap baseload power)

**Environmental issues**:
- **CO₂**: ~1,000 g/kWh (vs 500 g/kWh gas, 0 g/kWh renewables)
- **Air pollution**: SO₂, NOₓ, Hg, particulates
- **Mining impacts**: Mountaintop removal, land subsidence, water contamination (acid mine drainage)
- **Ash disposal**: Coal ash contains arsenic, selenium, mercury

## Key Terms

- **Mineral**: Naturally occurring, inorganic, crystalline solid with definite composition
- **Rock**: Aggregate of minerals (or organic matter)
- **Crystal**: Solid with ordered, repeating atomic structure
- **Mohs hardness**: Scale 1-10 measuring scratch resistance
- **Cleavage**: Tendency to break along flat planes (crystal structure)
- **Igneous rock**: Formed from cooling magma/lava
- **Intrusive/plutonic**: Igneous rock formed below surface (slow cooling, coarse grains)
- **Extrusive/volcanic**: Igneous rock formed at surface (fast cooling, fine grains)
- **Felsic**: Silica-rich (>65% SiO₂), light-colored igneous rock (granite, rhyolite)
- **Mafic**: Silica-poor (<55% SiO₂), dark igneous rock (basalt, gabbro)
- **Sedimentary rock**: Formed from lithification of sediments
- **Clastic**: Sedimentary rock from rock fragments (sandstone, shale)
- **Chemical**: Sedimentary rock from mineral precipitation (limestone, evaporite)
- **Metamorphic rock**: Pre-existing rock transformed by heat/pressure without melting
- **Foliation**: Layered texture in metamorphic rocks from aligned minerals
- **Ore**: Rock containing valuable minerals in economically extractable concentration
- **Hydrothermal**: Hot water deposits minerals in fractures/pores
- **Placer deposit**: Dense minerals concentrated by water/wind sorting
- **Rare Earth Elements (REEs)**: 17 elements critical for technology, 70% mined in China
- **Lithium**: Alkali metal essential for batteries, 60% reserves in Chile-Argentina-Bolivia
- **Kerogen**: Solid organic matter in source rocks that generates oil/gas when heated
- **Oil window**: Depth/temperature range (2-5 km, 60-120°C) where oil forms from kerogen
- **Reservoir rock**: Porous, permeable rock that stores oil/gas
- **Seal/cap rock**: Impermeable rock that traps oil/gas below it

## Summary

Minerals are naturally occurring, inorganic, crystalline solids with definite composition. ~5,400 known minerals, but ~20 rock-forming minerals dominate Earth's crust (feldspars 50-60%, quartz 12%, micas, pyroxenes, amphiboles, olivine). Physical properties—hardness (Mohs scale 1-10), cleavage, luster, density—enable identification. Silicates comprise 90% of crust due to silicon-oxygen abundance.

Rocks form via three processes. Igneous: magma/lava cooling (texture depends on cooling rate; composition ranges from felsic/granite to mafic/basalt). Sedimentary: weathering → erosion → deposition → lithification (clastic like sandstone, chemical like limestone, organic like coal; only type preserving fossils). Metamorphic: heat/pressure transformation without melting (foliated like schist/gneiss, non-foliated like marble/quartzite). The rock cycle continuously transforms rocks between types over millions of years.

Ore deposits form via magmatic segregation (platinum in Bushveld), hydrothermal circulation (porphyry copper, gold veins), sedimentary concentration (placer gold, BIFs), and weathering enrichment (bauxite). Critical minerals—rare earths (70% China), lithium (60% Chile-Argentina-Bolivia), cobalt (70% DRC)—concentrate in specific geological settings, creating geopolitical dependencies. Declining ore grades require more energy/waste per tonne.

Fossil fuels form over tens of millions of years. Oil/gas: marine microorganisms → organic-rich source rock → burial/heating (60-120°C oil window) → migration to reservoir trap (sealed by impermeable cap rock). Coal: swamp vegetation → burial → coalification (peat → lignite → bituminous → anthracite). Reserves: ~1.7 trillion barrels oil, ~1.1 trillion tonnes coal (~50-140 years at current use). Understanding geological processes locating these resources is essential for resource security, energy transitions, and managing environmental impacts.
