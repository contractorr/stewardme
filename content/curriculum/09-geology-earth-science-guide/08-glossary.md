# Glossary

## A

**Aa (lava)**: Blocky, rough-surfaced basaltic lava flow with jagged, clinkery appearance. Compare pahoehoe (smooth, ropy).

**Absolute age**: Numerical age in years (determined by radiometric dating). Compare relative age.

**Abyssal plain**: Flat, sediment-covered ocean floor at 4,000-6,000 m depth. Flattest regions on Earth.

**Accretion**: Process of growth by gradual accumulation of material. Applied to: (1) Planet formation (planetesimal collisions), (2) Tectonic plates (sediments added at margins).

**Accretionary wedge**: Sediments scraped off subducting oceanic plate and accreted to overriding plate at trench. Forms topographic ridge on landward side of trench.

**Acid mine drainage**: Sulfuric acid runoff from oxidized sulfide minerals (pyrite) in mine waste. pH <3, high dissolved metals (Fe, Cu, Zn), toxic to aquatic life.

**Aftershock**: Earthquake following main shock on same fault segment. Frequency decreases with time (Omori's Law). Can continue for months to years.

**Albedo**: Fraction of solar radiation reflected by a surface. Ice: ~0.8 (80% reflected), ocean: ~0.06, Earth average: ~0.3.

**Alluvium**: Unconsolidated sediment deposited by rivers (sand, silt, gravel). Forms floodplains, deltas, alluvial fans.

**Amphibole**: Double-chain silicate mineral group. Example: hornblende. Dark color, 120° cleavage angles. Common in intermediate igneous rocks (andesite, diorite).

**Andesite**: Intermediate volcanic rock (55-65% SiO₂). Fine-grained, gray. Typical of subduction zone volcanism (named for Andes Mountains).

**Angle of repose**: Maximum stable slope angle for loose material. Dry sand: ~32-34°. Exceeding angle triggers landslide.

**Angstrom (Å)**: Unit of length, 10⁻¹⁰ m. Used for atomic-scale measurements (atomic radii, crystal structures).

**Anoxia**: Absence of dissolved oxygen in water. Kills aerobic organisms. Caused by stratification, eutrophication, or stagnation. Common in some ancient oceans (preserved organic-rich source rocks).

**Anthracite**: Highest rank coal (86-97% carbon). Hard, lustrous, black. Highest energy content, lowest volatiles. Formed by deep burial metamorphism.

**Anthropocene**: Proposed epoch defined by significant human impact on Earth's geology and ecosystems. Proposed start dates: Industrial Revolution (~1800), nuclear age (1945), or Great Acceleration (1950s).

**Anticline**: Upward-arching fold (arch shape: ∩). Oldest rocks at core. Common petroleum trap structure.

**Aphanitic**: Fine-grained igneous texture where individual crystals invisible to naked eye. Results from rapid cooling (volcanic rocks).

**Aquifer**: Permeable rock unit saturated with groundwater that yields usable quantities to wells. Examples: sandstone, fractured limestone, gravel.

**Archean**: Eon spanning 4.0-2.5 Ga. First continents, origin of life, stromatolites, anoxic atmosphere.

**Arkose**: Sandstone rich in feldspar (>25%). Indicates nearby granitic source, limited transport/weathering.

**Artesian**: Groundwater under enough pressure that water rises above aquifer top without pumping. Can flow naturally at surface if pressure sufficient.

**Ash (volcanic)**: Fine volcanic fragments <2 mm diameter. Glassy or crystal fragments explosively ejected. Can travel thousands of km in atmosphere.

**Asthenosphere**: Ductile, partially molten upper mantle layer (100-350 km depth). Low seismic velocity zone. Allows tectonic plate motion (plates slide on asthenosphere).

**Atmosphere**: Gaseous envelope surrounding Earth. Composition: 78% N₂, 21% O₂, 0.04% CO₂, trace gases. Total mass: 5.15 × 10¹⁸ kg.

**Atoll**: Ring-shaped coral reef enclosing lagoon. Forms as volcanic island subsides while reef grows upward.

## B

**Banded Iron Formation (BIF)**: Precambrian sedimentary rock with alternating iron-rich (hematite, magnetite) and silica-rich layers. Formed 2.5-1.8 Ga during Great Oxidation Event. Major iron ore.

**Basalt**: Mafic volcanic rock (<52% SiO₂). Fine-grained, dark gray to black. Most common volcanic rock (ocean floor, Hawaii, Iceland, flood basalts).

**Baseload power**: Continuous minimum electricity demand; met by always-on generation (nuclear, coal, hydro, geothermal).

**Basin**: (1) Structural: Downward-warping area where sediments accumulate (sedimentary basin). (2) Topographic: Low-lying area draining to common outlet (river basin).

**Batholith**: Massive intrusive igneous body >100 km² surface area. Typically granitic. Forms mountain range cores (Sierra Nevada, Idaho batholith).

**Bauxite**: Aluminum ore; rock rich in aluminum hydroxides (gibbsite, boehmite). Forms by tropical weathering (removes silica, concentrates Al). Top producers: Guinea, Australia, Brazil.

**Bedding**: Layering in sedimentary rocks reflecting deposition in discrete events. Individual layer = bed.

**Bedrock**: Solid rock underlying loose surface materials (soil, alluvium).

**Benthic**: Relating to ocean floor. Benthic organisms live on/in seafloor sediments.

**Biochemical**: Rock or sediment formed by biological precipitation of minerals. Examples: limestone (shell fragments), diatomite (diatom skeletons).

**Biosphere**: All living organisms and organic matter on Earth. Mass: ~550 Gt carbon (mostly plants). Extends from deep ocean to stratosphere.

**Bitumen**: Thick, sticky petroleum (tar). Too viscous to flow naturally. Requires heating or dilution for extraction (Alberta oil sands).

**Bituminous coal**: Medium-rank coal (76-86% carbon). Black, sub-metallic luster. Most abundant coal type globally. Used for power generation, steelmaking (coking coal).

**Blood diamonds**: Diamonds mined in conflict zones, sold to fund armed groups. Prominent in 1990s-2000s West African civil wars (Sierra Leone, Angola, DRC).

**Blood cobalt**: Cobalt mined under exploitative/dangerous conditions, often using child labor. Primarily DRC artisonal mines. Funds armed groups.

**Borehole**: Narrow hole drilled into ground for exploration, monitoring, or extraction (water, oil, minerals, geothermal).

**Bowen's Reaction Series**: Sequence in which minerals crystallize from cooling magma. High-T: olivine, pyroxene; low-T: quartz, muscovite. Explains magmatic differentiation.

**Breccia**: Clastic sedimentary rock with angular fragments >2 mm. Indicates minimal transport (landslide, fault zone, volcanic).

**Brine**: Water with high dissolved salts (>5%). Seawater: ~3.5%. Hypersaline brines (salars): 10-30%. Source of lithium, potassium, bromine.

**Brittle**: Material that fractures rather than bends under stress. Shallow crust is brittle (earthquakes). Compare ductile.

## C

**Caldera**: Large volcanic depression (>1 km diameter) formed by collapse after magma chamber evacuation. Examples: Yellowstone, Crater Lake.

**Cap carbonate**: Thick carbonate layer deposited immediately after Snowball Earth glaciation. Records super-greenhouse conditions during rapid deglaciation.

**Cap rock**: Impermeable rock layer preventing upward migration of fluids (oil, gas, groundwater, CO₂). Essential component of hydrocarbon/CO₂ traps. Examples: shale, salt, anhydrite.

**Carbonatite**: Rare igneous rock composed mainly of carbonate minerals (calcite, dolomite). Forms from mantle plumes. Major source of rare earth elements (Bayan Obo, China).

**Carbonate rock**: Sedimentary rock composed of carbonate minerals (CaCO₃, CaMg(CO₃)₂). Examples: limestone, dolostone. Forms in warm, shallow seas or freshwater lakes.

**Carbon-14 dating**: Radiometric dating method using ¹⁴C (half-life 5,730 years). Applicable to organic materials <50,000 years old. Revolutionized archaeology.

**Carnot efficiency**: Maximum theoretical efficiency of heat engine. η = 1 - T_cold/T_hot (temperatures in Kelvin). Real engines achieve 30-65% of Carnot limit.

**Catagenesis**: Thermal alteration of organic matter to oil and gas (60-150°C, 2-5 km depth). Part of petroleum formation process.

**Cement (sedimentary)**: Mineral precipitated in pore spaces, binding sediment grains together. Common cements: calcite, silica, iron oxides.

**Cenozoic**: Era spanning 66 Ma-present ("recent life"). Age of mammals. Includes present. Subdivided into Paleogene, Neogene, Quaternary.

**Chert**: Sedimentary rock composed of microcrystalline quartz (SiO₂). Very hard, conchoidal fracture. Forms by silica precipitation or replacement. Varieties: flint, jasper.

**Clast**: Individual fragment in sedimentary rock. Size classification: clay <0.004 mm, silt 0.004-0.0625 mm, sand 0.0625-2 mm, gravel/pebble >2 mm.

**Clastic**: Sedimentary rock composed of fragments (clasts) of pre-existing rocks. Examples: sandstone, shale, conglomerate.

**Clay**: (1) Particle size: <0.004 mm. (2) Mineral group: Sheet silicates (kaolinite, illite, smectite). Expands when wet, plastic behavior.

**Cleavage**: (1) Mineral property: Tendency to break along flat planes related to crystal structure. (2) Rock property: Planar fabric in metamorphic rocks (slaty cleavage).

**Coal**: Sedimentary rock composed of compressed, altered plant material. Ranks (increasing carbon): peat → lignite → bituminous → anthracite. Forms in swamps. Major fossil fuel.

**Cobalt**: Transition metal (Co). Uses: Li-ion batteries (50%), superalloys (20%), catalysts, magnets. DRC produces 70% (ethical concerns: child labor, conflict funding).

**Collision boundary**: Convergent plate boundary where two continental plates collide. Neither subducts (too buoyant). Forms major mountain ranges (Himalayas, Alps).

**Compaction**: Reduction of sediment volume by expulsion of water under burial weight. Increases density. First stage of lithification.

**Conchoidal fracture**: Curved, shell-like breakage surface. Typical of quartz, obsidian (volcanic glass). Fracture type when no cleavage present.

**Conglomerate**: Clastic sedimentary rock with rounded fragments >2 mm. Indicates high-energy transport (rivers, beaches).

**Contact metamorphism**: Metamorphism by heat from magma intrusion. Localized (meters to km), no regional deformation. Forms hornfels.

**Continental crust**: Thick (35-70 km), old (up to 4.0 Ga), felsic (60-70% SiO₂), low density (2.7 g/cm³). Comprises continents. Cannot subduct (too buoyant).

**Continental drift**: Theory (Alfred Wegener, 1912) that continents move across Earth's surface. Precursor to plate tectonics. Initially rejected, vindicated 1960s.

**Convergent boundary**: Plate boundary where plates move toward each other. Types: ocean-ocean (island arcs), ocean-continent (Andes), continent-continent (Himalayas).

**Copper**: Transition metal (Cu). Best electrical conductor (except Ag). Uses: wiring (60%), construction, transport. Chile produces 28%. EVs use 4× more Cu than ICE vehicles.

**Core**: Earth's center (2,900-6,371 km depth). Composed of iron-nickel. Outer core: liquid, generates magnetic field. Inner core: solid (crystallized by pressure).

**Craton**: Stable, ancient part of continental crust (typically >2 Ga). Not deformed since Precambrian. Examples: Canadian Shield, Kaapvaal Craton (South Africa).

**Creep (soil)**: Very slow (mm-year) downslope movement of soil. Driven by freeze-thaw cycles, wetting-drying, gravity.

**Cross-bedding**: Inclined sedimentary layers within main bed. Indicates current direction (wind or water). Preserved in ancient sandstones.

**Cross-cutting relationships**: Principle: Geological features (faults, intrusions) cutting through rocks are younger than the rocks they cut.

**Crust**: Outermost solid layer of Earth. Continental: 35-70 km thick. Oceanic: ~7 km thick. Separated from mantle by Moho discontinuity.

**Crystal**: Solid with ordered, repeating atomic arrangement (lattice). External shape (habit) reflects internal structure.

**Crystallization**: Formation of crystals from magma, solution, or vapor. Minerals crystallize in sequence (Bowen's Series) as magma cools.

## D

**Dating**: Determining age of rocks/fossils. Types: Relative (older/younger) vs Absolute (numerical age in years, via radiometric dating).

**Debris avalanche**: Very rapid (10-100+ m/s) flow of fragmented rock, soil, vegetation. Typically volcanic or earthquake-triggered. Extremely destructive.

**Debris flow**: Rapid (1-10 m/s) flow of water-saturated debris (rock, soil, vegetation). Consistency of wet concrete. Triggered by heavy rain, snowmelt, volcanic eruption.

**Decompression melting**: Melting caused by pressure decrease (at constant temperature). Occurs when mantle rises (mid-ocean ridges, hotspots, rifts).

**Deep time**: Vast timescales of geological processes (millions to billions of years). Concept difficult for humans to intuitively grasp.

**Delta**: Sediment deposit at river mouth where flow enters standing water (ocean, lake). Shaped like Greek letter delta (Δ). Examples: Mississippi, Nile.

**Denudation**: Overall lowering of land surface by weathering, erosion, transport. Erosion: removal; denudation: net lowering.

**Deposition**: Settling and accumulation of sediment. Occurs when transport energy (wind, water) decreases below threshold for grain size.

**Diagenesis**: Physical/chemical changes in sediment during burial before metamorphism. Includes compaction, cementation, recrystallization. T <200°C.

**Diamond**: Allotrope of carbon. Hardest natural material (Mohs 10). Forms at high P-T (>150 km depth) in mantle. Brought to surface rapidly in kimberlite pipes.

**Differentiation (planetary)**: Density-driven separation of materials. Dense iron sank to form core; light silicates rose to form crust/mantle. Occurred when Earth molten (~4.5 Ga).

**Dike**: Tabular intrusive igneous body cutting across existing rock layers. Often vertical or steeply inclined. Younger than rocks it cuts.

**Diorite**: Intermediate intrusive igneous rock (55-65% SiO₂). Coarse-grained, gray. Intrusive equivalent of andesite.

**Dip**: Angle between horizontal plane and inclined geological surface (bedding, fault, etc.). Measured perpendicular to strike.

**Discharge**: Volume of water flowing past a point per time. River discharge in cubic meters per second (m³/s or cms). Amazon: ~200,000 m³/s average.

**Divergent boundary**: Plate boundary where plates move apart. New lithosphere forms (seafloor spreading at mid-ocean ridges, continental rifting).

**Dolomite**: (1) Mineral: CaMg(CO₃)₂. (2) Rock (dolostone): Sedimentary rock composed of dolomite mineral. Forms by Mg-rich water altering limestone.

**Ductile**: Material that deforms by bending/flowing rather than fracturing. Deep crust/mantle behaves ductilely (high T, P). Compare brittle.

**Dutch Disease**: Economic phenomenon where resource exports appreciate currency, making other sectors uncompetitive. Named for Netherlands (North Sea gas discovery 1960s).

## E

**Earthquake**: Sudden slip on fault releasing stored elastic energy as seismic waves. Caused by tectonic stress exceeding frictional strength.

**Eccentricity**: Measure of orbital ellipticity. Circle: e=0; more elliptical: e approaches 1. Earth's orbit: e=0.0167 (nearly circular), varies 0.0-0.06 over 100 ka cycles (Milankovitch).

**Economic geology**: Study of mineral deposits and processes forming ore concentrations. Applied to exploration and extraction.

**Elastic rebound**: Earthquake mechanism. Rock deforms elastically under stress → fault ruptures → rock snaps back to unstrained state → seismic waves released.

**Energy density**: Energy per unit mass (gravimetric, MJ/kg) or volume (volumetric, MJ/L). Gasoline: 46 MJ/kg; Li-ion battery: 0.9 MJ/kg (50× difference).

**Eon**: Largest division of geologic time. Four eons: Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, Phanerozoic.

**Epicenter**: Point on Earth's surface directly above earthquake focus (hypocenter). Location reported in earthquake alerts.

**Epoch**: Subdivision of period in geologic time scale. Examples: Pleistocene, Holocene (within Quaternary Period).

**Era**: Subdivision of eon in geologic time scale. Phanerozoic Eon has three eras: Paleozoic, Mesozoic, Cenozoic.

**EROEI**: Energy Return on Energy Invested. Net energy delivered to society = Energy Out / Energy In. Conventional oil ~30:1; shale oil ~10:1; corn ethanol ~1.3:1.

**Erosion**: Physical removal and transport of weathered material by water, wind, ice, gravity.

**Eruption**: Explosive or effusive ejection of magma/lava from volcano. Measured by VEI (Volcanic Explosivity Index, 0-8).

**Evaporite**: Sedimentary rock formed by evaporation of saline water. Minerals: halite (NaCl), gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O), anhydrite (CaSO₄), sylvite (KCl).

**Evolution**: Change in heritable traits of populations over generations. Mechanism: natural selection acting on genetic variation (Darwin, 1859).

**Extrusive**: Igneous rock formed at Earth's surface from lava. Fine-grained (rapid cooling). Examples: basalt, andesite, rhyolite. Also called volcanic.

## F

**Factor of Safety**: Ratio of resisting forces to driving forces for slope stability. FS >1: stable; FS <1: failure (landslide).

**Fault**: Fracture in rock with displacement. Types: normal (extension), reverse/thrust (compression), strike-slip (horizontal).

**Faunal succession**: Principle that fossil species succeed one another in definite order. Enables correlation of distant rock units (William Smith, 1790s).

**Feldspar**: Most abundant mineral group in crust (50-60%). Framework silicates. Types: K-feldspar (KAlSi₃O₈), plagioclase (Na-Ca series). Essential component of igneous, metamorphic rocks.

**Felsic**: Silica-rich igneous rock (>65% SiO₂). Light-colored (white, pink, gray). High viscosity magma → explosive eruptions. Examples: granite, rhyolite.

**Ferromagnesian**: Minerals rich in iron (Fe) and magnesium (Mg). Dark-colored, high density. Examples: olivine, pyroxene, amphibole, biotite.

**Fission track dating**: Dating method counting radiation damage tracks in minerals (zircon, apatite). Useful range: 10 ka to 1 Ga.

**Fissure eruption**: Lava eruption from linear crack rather than central vent. Forms flood basalts (Deccan Traps, Columbia River Basalts).

**Flood basalt**: Massive outpouring of basaltic lava from fissure eruptions. Forms flat, extensive plateaus. Associated with LIPs (Large Igneous Provinces) and mass extinctions.

**Focus (hypocenter)**: Point within Earth where earthquake rupture initiates. Depth: shallow (<70 km), intermediate (70-300 km), deep (300-700 km).

**Fold**: Bend in rock layers (strata). Types: anticline (upward arch), syncline (downward trough). Result of compressional stress.

**Foliation**: Layered texture in metamorphic rocks from alignment of platy minerals (mica, chlorite) perpendicular to stress. Examples: slaty cleavage, schistosity, gneissic banding.

**Foraminifera (forams)**: Single-celled protists with calcium carbonate shells. Abundant in ocean sediments. Used for: dating, paleoclimate (δ¹⁸O), paleoecology.

**Formation**: Fundamental rock unit in stratigraphy. Distinct lithology, mappable at surface, named for type locality (e.g., Morrison Formation).

**Fossil**: Preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms. Found only in sedimentary rocks (heat destroys organics in igneous/metamorphic).

**Fossil fuel**: Hydrocarbon formed from ancient organic matter. Types: oil, natural gas, coal. Provides ~82% of global primary energy (2023).

**Fractional crystallization**: Process where early-formed crystals are removed from magma (sink, float, or flow out). Remaining melt becomes enriched in excluded elements. Drives magmatic differentiation.

**Fracture**: (1) Break in rock without displacement (compare fault). (2) Mineral property: Irregular breaking pattern when no cleavage (e.g., conchoidal, fibrous).

**Frost line (solar system)**: Distance from Sun (~5 AU) where temperature low enough for water ice to condense. Inside: rocky planets; outside: gas/ice giants.

## G

**Gabbro**: Mafic intrusive igneous rock (<52% SiO₂). Coarse-grained, dark. Intrusive equivalent of basalt. Major component of oceanic crust (layer 3).

**Galena**: Lead sulfide (PbS). Main lead ore. High density (7.5 g/cm³), metallic luster, cubic cleavage.

**Gemstone**: Mineral valued for beauty, rarity, durability. Cut and polished for jewelry. Examples: diamond, ruby, sapphire, emerald.

**Geochemistry**: Study of chemical composition and processes of Earth materials. Applications: ore genesis, climate reconstruction, planetary formation.

**Geochronology**: Science of dating rocks and geological events. Methods: radiometric dating, biostratigraphy, magnetostratigraphy.

**Geologic time scale**: Chronological framework of Earth's 4.54-billion-year history. Hierarchical: eons → eras → periods → epochs.

**Geology**: Scientific study of Earth—composition, structure, processes, 4.54-billion-year history.

**Geomorphology**: Study of landforms and processes shaping Earth's surface (erosion, deposition, tectonics, weathering).

**Geophysics**: Study of Earth's physical properties using physics principles. Methods: seismology, gravity, magnetics, electrical, electromagnetic.

**Geosphere**: Solid Earth (crust, mantle, core). One of four spheres (with hydrosphere, atmosphere, biosphere).

**Geothermal energy**: Heat from Earth's interior. Used for electricity generation, direct heating. Sources: magma chambers (volcanoes), hot dry rock, geopressured brines.

**Geothermal gradient**: Rate of temperature increase with depth. Average: 25-30°C/km. Higher near magma (~100°C/km), lower in stable cratons (~15°C/km).

**Geyser**: Hot spring that intermittently erupts jets of water/steam. Requires: heat source, water supply, plumbing system. Examples: Old Faithful (Yellowstone), Strokkur (Iceland).

**Glaciation**: Period when ice sheets and glaciers expand, covering larger areas. Most recent glacial maximum: 26.5-19 ka (ice to 40°N latitude).

**Glacier**: Persistent body of ice moving downslope under its own weight. Forms where snow accumulation exceeds melting. Erosional power shapes landscapes (U-valleys, fjords, cirques).

**Gneiss**: High-grade metamorphic rock with compositional banding (light/dark layers). Foliated. From granite, schist, or sediments. Common in continental shields.

**Gondwana**: Southern supercontinent during Paleozoic-Mesozoic (550-180 Ma). Included South America, Africa, Antarctica, Australia, India, Arabia. Broke up starting ~180 Ma.

**Graben**: Downthrown block between two normal faults (valley structure). Forms during crustal extension. Example: East African Rift.

**Graded bedding**: Sedimentary layer with grain size decreasing upward. Indicates waning current energy. Typical of turbidites (underwater landslide deposits).

**Grain**: Individual crystal or fragment in rock.

**Granite**: Felsic intrusive igneous rock (>70% SiO₂). Coarse-grained, light-colored (white, pink, gray). Minerals: quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, biotite. Abundant in continental crust.

**Granulite**: High-grade metamorphic rock with granular texture. Forms at T>700°C. No hydrous minerals (dehydration reactions).

**Graphite**: Allotrope of carbon. Soft (Mohs 1-2), sheet structure. Forms by metamorphism of organic carbon. Uses: pencils, lubricants, batteries.

**Gravimeter**: Instrument measuring local gravitational field strength. Used for: ore exploration (density anomalies), crustal structure.

**Gravity anomaly**: Difference between measured and theoretical gravity at location. Positive: excess mass (dense rock, ore body); negative: mass deficit (low-density sediments, magma chamber).

**Great Oxidation Event (GOE)**: Rise in atmospheric oxygen ~2.45-2.0 Ga from cyanobacterial oxygenic photosynthesis. Transformed atmosphere, oceans, enabled aerobic life.

**Greenhouse effect**: Warming of surface by atmospheric absorption/re-radiation of infrared. Greenhouse gases (H₂O, CO₂, CH₄) absorb IR, trap heat.

**Groundwater**: Water occupying pore spaces and fractures in subsurface rock/sediment below water table. ~30× more freshwater than all lakes/rivers.

**Gypsum**: Calcium sulfate dihydrate (CaSO₄·2H₂O). Soft (Mohs 2). Evaporite mineral. Uses: drywall (plaster), cement retarder, soil amendment.

## H

**Habit (crystal)**: External shape of crystal reflecting internal atomic structure. Examples: cubic (halite), prismatic (quartz), tabular (feldspar), fibrous (asbestos).

**Hadean**: Eon spanning 4.54-4.0 Ga. Hellish conditions: molten surface, heavy bombardment, Moon-forming impact. No surviving rock record (oldest: 4.03 Ga Acasta Gneiss).

**Half-life (t₁/₂)**: Time for half of radioactive isotope to decay. Constant for each isotope. Examples: ¹⁴C: 5,730 years; ⁴⁰K: 1.25 billion years; ²³⁸U: 4.47 billion years.

**Halite**: Sodium chloride (NaCl). Evaporite mineral (rock salt). Cubic cleavage, salty taste, soft (Mohs 2.5). Uses: food, de-icing, chemical feedstock.

**Hardness**: Resistance to scratching. Mohs scale (1-10): talc (1) → diamond (10). Relative scale (not linear in absolute hardness).

**Heat flow**: Rate of heat transfer from Earth's interior to surface. Average: 47 TW globally. Higher at mid-ocean ridges, lower in cratons.

**Hematite**: Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃). Major iron ore. Reddish-brown streak (diagnostic), metallic to earthy luster. ~60% Fe content.

**Holocene**: Current epoch (11,700 years ago-present). Interglacial period. Human civilization developed. Proposed end: Anthropocene begins (~1800 or 1950).

**Hornblende**: Common amphibole mineral. Dark green to black, 120° cleavage. Found in intermediate igneous/metamorphic rocks.

**Hornfels**: Fine-grained, non-foliated metamorphic rock. Forms by contact metamorphism (baking by intrusion). Tough, massive texture.

**Horst**: Upthrown block between two normal faults (ridge structure). Forms during crustal extension. Opposite of graben.

**Hot spot**: Volcanic region above mantle plume. Fixed (or slow-moving) relative to moving plates. Creates age-progressive volcanic chains (Hawaii, Yellowstone).

**Hydraulic fracturing (fracking)**: Injecting high-pressure fluid (water, sand, chemicals) to fracture rock, releasing trapped oil/gas. Enables shale oil/gas extraction. Controversial (water use, induced seismicity, methane emissions).

**Hydrocarbon**: Organic compound of hydrogen and carbon. Fossil fuels (oil, natural gas) are hydrocarbon mixtures. Methane (CH₄), octane (C₈H₁₈), etc.

**Hydrogeology**: Study of groundwater—occurrence, movement, quality. Critical for water resources, contamination tracking, waste disposal.

**Hydrosphere**: All water on/near Earth's surface. Oceans (96.5% of water), ice caps (1.74%), groundwater (1.7%), lakes/rivers (0.013%).

**Hydrothermal**: Hot water processes. (1) Metamorphism: Water-facilitated mineral reactions. (2) Ore deposits: Metal precipitation from hot fluids (porphyry copper, vein gold). (3) Mid-ocean ridge alteration.

## I

**Ice age**: Period of extensive glaciation (ice sheets covering continents). Most recent: Pleistocene (2.7 Ma-11.7 ka). Glacial-interglacial cycles driven by Milankovitch.

**Ice core**: Cylindrical ice sample drilled from ice sheet/glacier. Records ancient atmosphere (CO₂ in bubbles), temperature (δ¹⁸O), volcanic eruptions (sulfate), dust. Longest: 800 ka (EPICA Dome C, Antarctica).

**Igneous rock**: Rock formed from cooling and solidification of magma (intrusive) or lava (extrusive). ~95% of crust volume (but sedimentary rocks cover surface).

**Impact structure**: Crater formed by meteorite/asteroid collision. Features: shocked quartz, tektites, central peak (large craters). Examples: Chicxulub (66 Ma, dinosaur extinction), Vredefort (2 Ga, largest: 300 km).

**Index fossil**: Fossil useful for dating rocks. Requirements: widespread distribution, short time range, easily recognized. Examples: trilobites (Paleozoic), ammonites (Mesozoic).

**Index mineral**: Mineral indicating specific metamorphic grade (P-T conditions). Examples: chlorite (low), garnet (medium), kyanite/sillimanite (high).

**Induced seismicity**: Earthquakes triggered by human activities. Causes: wastewater injection (Oklahoma), reservoir filling (Hoover Dam), geothermal extraction, fracking.

**Industrial Revolution**: Transition to machine-based manufacturing (~1760-1840). Powered by coal → steam engines. Initiated anthropogenic climate change (CO₂ rise).

**Insolation**: Incoming solar radiation. Varies with latitude, season, orbital parameters (Milankovitch cycles). Controls ice age cycles.

**Intrusive**: Igneous rock formed below surface from magma. Coarse-grained (slow cooling). Examples: granite, gabbro, diorite. Also called plutonic.

**Ion**: Atom or molecule with electrical charge (gained/lost electrons). Cation: positive (lost e⁻); anion: negative (gained e⁻).

**Iron catastrophe**: Early Earth differentiation event. Dense iron sank to form core, released enormous gravitational energy as heat. ~4.5 Ga.

**Island arc**: Curved chain of volcanic islands formed where oceanic plate subducts beneath oceanic plate. Examples: Aleutians, Marianas, Tonga, Japan.

**Isostasy**: Gravitational equilibrium of lithosphere floating on asthenosphere. Mountains have deep roots (low-density crust extends into mantle). Removing mass (erosion, ice melt) → uplift.

**Isostatic rebound**: Uplift after removal of overlying mass (ice sheet melts, erosion). Scandinavia rising ~10 mm/yr (ice melted 10 ka ago, still rebounding). Slow process (mantle viscous flow).

**Isotope**: Atoms of same element with different neutron numbers (same protons, different mass). Stable: unchanging. Radioactive: spontaneously decay to other isotopes. Used for dating, tracers.

## J

**Joint**: Fracture in rock with no displacement (compare fault: displacement). Forms from stress, unloading, cooling shrinkage. Provides pathways for water, weathering.

**Jurassic**: Period spanning 201-145 Ma (Mesozoic Era). Named for Jura Mountains. Dinosaurs dominate, first birds (Archaeopteryx), Atlantic opens, warm climate.

## K

**K-Ar dating**: Potassium-argon radiometric dating. ⁴⁰K decays to ⁴⁰Ar (half-life 1.25 Ga). Useful for volcanic rocks (Ar gas escapes during eruption, clock resets). Range: 100 ka to 4.5 Ga.

**Karst**: Landscape formed by dissolution of soluble rocks (limestone, dolomite, gypsum). Features: sinkholes, caves, disappearing streams, underground drainage. ~20% of land surface karst terrain.

**Kerogen**: Solid, insoluble organic matter in source rocks. Heated during burial → generates oil/gas. Types: I (algal, most oil-prone), II (mixed marine), III (terrestrial plants, gas-prone).

**Kimberlite**: Rare volcanic rock carrying diamonds from mantle depths (>150 km) to surface at extreme speed (~30 m/s ascent). Pipe-shaped intrusions. Main diamond source (South Africa, Russia, Canada, Botswana).

**Komatiite**: Ultramafic lava (>18% MgO) erupted at extremely high temperature (>1,600°C). Found only in Archean greenstone belts. Modern mantle too cool to produce komatiite.

## L

**Lahar**: Volcanic mudflow (water + volcanic debris). Triggered by: eruption melting ice/snow, heavy rain on loose ash, crater lake breach. Speed: 30-60 km/h. Consistency: wet concrete. Highly destructive (1985 Nevado del Ruiz, Colombia: 23,000 deaths).

**Landslide**: Downslope movement of rock/soil under gravity. Types: rockfall, avalanche, slump, debris flow, creep. Triggers: rain, earthquakes, undercutting, overloading.

**Large Igneous Province (LIP)**: Massive volcanic event (>500,000 km³ basalt) over ~1 Ma from mantle plume. Examples: Siberian Traps (252 Ma), Deccan Traps (66 Ma). Often associated with mass extinctions.

**Laterite**: Soil/rock rich in iron/aluminum oxides formed by intense tropical weathering. Brick-red color. Nickel laterites: major Ni ore (New Caledonia).

**Lava**: Molten rock erupted at surface. Basaltic lava: low viscosity, forms flows. Rhyolitic lava: high viscosity, forms domes/explosive eruptions.

**Lava tube**: Tunnel formed by flowing lava. Outer surface cools/solidifies while interior drains, leaving hollow tube. Can be km-long (Kazumura Cave, Hawaii: 65 km).

**Lignite**: Low-rank coal (60-70% carbon). Brown, soft, high moisture. Lowest energy content. Used for power generation where abundant (Germany, Australia, US).

**Limestone**: Sedimentary rock composed primarily of calcite (CaCO₃). Forms in warm, shallow seas. Biological origin (shells) or chemical precipitation. Reacts with acid (fizzes). Main uses: cement, building stone, flux (steelmaking).

**Liquefaction**: Process where saturated sediment loses strength during shaking, behaves as liquid. Pore water pressure increases, grains separate. Causes buildings to sink, tilt. Major earthquake hazard (1964 Niigara, 1989 Loma Prieta).

**Lithification**: Conversion of loose sediment to solid sedimentary rock. Processes: compaction (reduces porosity), cementation (minerals bind grains).

**Lithium**: Alkali metal (Li). Lightest metal (0.53 g/cm³). Uses: batteries (85%), ceramics, lubricants, pharmaceuticals. "White petroleum"—critical for EVs. Lithium Triangle (Chile-Argentina-Bolivia): 60% reserves. China: 60-70% refining capacity.

**Lithosphere**: Rigid outer layer of Earth (crust + upper mantle, 0-100 km). Broken into tectonic plates. Floats on ductile asthenosphere.

**Loess**: Wind-deposited silt. Massive, homogeneous, buff-colored. Fertile soil (Chinese Loess Plateau). Produced by glacial grinding in ice ages, transported by wind.

**Longshore drift**: Transport of sediment parallel to coastline by wave action (swash-backwash at angle). Builds spits, barriers.

**Luster**: Appearance of mineral in reflected light. Types: metallic (galena, pyrite), vitreous/glassy (quartz), pearly (talc), greasy (jade), dull/earthy (kaolinite).

## M

**Mafic**: Silica-poor igneous rock (45-52% SiO₂). Dark-colored (black, dark gray). High Fe-Mg content. Low viscosity → effusive eruptions. Examples: basalt, gabbro.

**Magma**: Molten rock beneath Earth's surface. Composition: liquid (melt) + crystals + dissolved gases. Becomes lava when erupted.

**Magma chamber**: Underground reservoir of magma. Source for volcanic eruptions. Cooling/crystallization forms intrusive rocks (batholiths).

**Magnetic field (Earth's)**: Dipole field generated by liquid outer core convection (geodynamo). Shields from solar wind, cosmic rays. Reversals: ~every 500 ka average. Essential for habitability.

**Magnetic reversal**: Switch in Earth's magnetic polarity (N-S poles flip). Evidence: alternating polarity in seafloor basalts (key evidence for seafloor spreading). Last reversal: 780 ka ago (Brunhes-Matuyama).

**Magnetite**: Iron oxide (Fe₃O₄). Major iron ore (~70% Fe). Strongly magnetic (lodestone). Black, metallic luster. Used in navigation (natural compass) historically.

**Magnitude**: Measure of earthquake size. Moment magnitude (Mw): Based on seismic moment. Each unit ~32× more energy. Open-ended scale (largest recorded: M9.5).

**Mantle**: Layer between crust and core (35-2,900 km depth). 84% of Earth's volume, 67% of mass. Composition: ultramafic (peridotite). Solid but flows over geologic time.

**Mantle plume**: Narrow upwelling of abnormally hot mantle from core-mantle boundary. Creates hotspots (Hawaii, Iceland, Yellowstone). Hundreds of km wide, rises over millions of years.

**Marble**: Non-foliated metamorphic rock from limestone/dolostone. Interlocking calcite/dolomite crystals. Reacts with acid. Uses: sculpture, building stone.

**Mass extinction**: Event where >75% of species die over geologically short period (<2 Ma). Five major extinctions in Phanerozoic (Ordovician 444 Ma, Devonian 375 Ma, Permian 252 Ma, Triassic 201 Ma, Cretaceous 66 Ma). Sixth ongoing (human-caused)?

**Mass wasting**: Downslope movement of rock/soil under gravity. Includes: rockfall, landslide, slump, debris flow, creep. Driven by gravity; water often trigger.

**Meander**: Sinuous curve in river channel. Forms in low-gradient regions. Erosion on outside bend (cutbank), deposition on inside (point bar).

**Mesosphere**: (1) Atmosphere: 50-85 km altitude. (2) Mantle: Lower mantle (660-2,900 km). Avoid confusion—specify atmospheric vs mantle.

**Mesozoic**: Era spanning 252-66 Ma (Age of Reptiles). Periods: Triassic, Jurassic, Cretaceous. Dinosaurs dominate, flowering plants evolve, ends with asteroid impact extinction.

**Metamorphic rock**: Rock transformed from pre-existing rock by heat, pressure, fluids (without melting). Types: foliated (slate, schist, gneiss), non-foliated (marble, quartzite).

**Metamorphism**: Transformation of rock by heat, pressure, chemically active fluids. Mineralogy and texture change. Types: contact (heat from intrusion), regional (burial + compression), hydrothermal.

**Meteorite**: Fragment of asteroid/comet that survives atmospheric entry and hits Earth. Types: stony (chondrites, achondrites), iron, stony-iron. Oldest materials in solar system (4.567 Ga).

**Methane (CH₄)**: Simplest hydrocarbon. Main component of natural gas (70-90%). Greenhouse gas (25-30× more potent than CO₂ over 100 years). Sources: wetlands, ruminants, fossil fuels, thawing permafrost.

**Methane hydrate (clathrate)**: Ice-like solid of methane trapped in water lattice. Stable at low T, high P (deep ocean, permafrost). Enormous reservoir (~1,000-10,000 Gt CH₄). Unstable if warmed → climate feedback risk.

**Mica**: Sheet silicate mineral group with perfect basal cleavage (splits into thin, flexible sheets). Muscovite (white, KAl₂(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂), biotite (black, K(Mg,Fe)₃(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂).

**Mid-ocean ridge**: Divergent plate boundary on ocean floor where new oceanic crust forms. 60,000 km total length (longest mountain range). Median valley, shallow earthquakes, high heat flow, hydrothermal vents. Spreading rate: 10-180 mm/yr.

**Milankovitch cycles**: Variations in Earth's orbit affecting solar radiation (insolation) and climate. Three cycles: eccentricity (~100 ka), obliquity (~41 ka), precession (~26 ka). Drive Pleistocene ice age cycles.

**Mineral**: Naturally occurring, inorganic, crystalline solid with definite chemical composition. ~5,400 known; ~20 rock-forming minerals dominate crust.

**Modified Mercalli Intensity (MMI)**: Earthquake intensity scale (I-XII) based on observed effects (shaking, damage). Qualitative. Contrast with magnitude (quantitative energy measure).

**Moho (Mohorovičić discontinuity)**: Boundary between crust and mantle. Seismic velocity increases abruptly. Depth: ~7 km (oceanic), ~35 km (continental average), ~70 km (under mountains).

**Mohs hardness scale**: Relative scale of scratch resistance (1-10). Talc (1), gypsum (2), calcite (3), fluorite (4), apatite (5), orthoclase (6), quartz (7), topaz (8), corundum (9), diamond (10).

**Moment magnitude (Mw)**: Earthquake size based on seismic moment (rigidity × rupture area × slip). Replaces Richter scale (saturates above M6.5). Logarithmic: each unit ~32× more energy.

**Mudstone**: Fine-grained clastic sedimentary rock (<0.0625 mm grain size). Clay + silt. Similar to shale but lacks fissility (doesn't split into thin layers).

## N

**Native element**: Mineral composed of single uncombined element. Examples: gold (Au), silver (Ag), copper (Cu), sulfur (S), carbon (diamond, graphite).

**Natural gas**: Gaseous hydrocarbon mixture, primarily methane (CH₄). Forms with oil or separately in gas fields. Cleanest fossil fuel (~50% less CO₂ than coal). Uses: electricity, heating, chemicals, hydrogen production.

**Natural selection**: Mechanism of evolution. Individuals with advantageous traits survive/reproduce more → traits become common over generations (Darwin, 1859).

**Neoproterozoic**: Late Proterozoic (1,000-541 Ma). Snowball Earth glaciations, eukaryote diversification, Ediacaran biota, supercontinent Rodinia breakup.

**Nonrenewable resource**: Resource consumed faster than formed. Fossil fuels, minerals form over millions of years but extracted in decades-centuries.

**Normal fault**: Fault where hanging wall moves down relative to footwall. Indicates crustal extension. Common at divergent boundaries (mid-ocean ridges, rifts).

## O

**Obliquity**: Axial tilt of Earth (currently 23.44°). Varies 22.1°-24.5° over ~41,000 years (Milankovitch cycle). Greater tilt → stronger seasons.

**Obsidian**: Volcanic glass (no crystals). Rapid cooling prevents crystallization. Felsic composition. Conchoidal fracture. Used for prehistoric tools (sharper than surgical steel).

**Ocean acidification**: Decrease in ocean pH from absorbing atmospheric CO₂. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. pH decreased 0.1 units since pre-industrial (8.2 → 8.1). Dissolves carbonate shells, threatens coral reefs, marine ecosystems.

**Oceanic crust**: Thin (7 km), young (<200 Ma), mafic (50% SiO₂), dense (3.0 g/cm³). Layers: sediment, pillow basalt, sheeted dikes, gabbro. Forms at mid-ocean ridges, destroyed at subduction zones.

**Oil window**: Depth/temperature range (2-5 km, 60-120°C) where kerogen transforms to oil. Too shallow: oil doesn't form. Too deep (>150°C): oil cracks to gas. Critical for petroleum exploration.

**Olivine**: Isolated silicate mineral (Mg,Fe)₂SiO₄. Olive-green, glassy luster. First mineral to crystallize from mafic magma (Bowen's Series). Major component of mantle peridotite.

**OPEC**: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Cartel coordinating oil production (attempt to control price). 13 members (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, Venezuela, etc.). Controls ~40% of global production, 70% of reserves. Influence declining (shale revolution, renewables).

**Ophiolite**: Section of oceanic crust/upper mantle tectonically emplaced on land. Provides access to otherwise inaccessible deep ocean rocks. Example: Oman ophiolite.

**Ore**: Rock containing valuable mineral(s) in economically extractable concentration. Ore grade: % metal content. Declining grades globally (more energy, waste per tonne metal).

**Ore deposit**: Natural concentration of ore minerals. Formation processes: magmatic segregation, hydrothermal, sedimentary, weathering.

**Original horizontality**: Principle that sediments deposit in horizontal/near-horizontal layers. Tilted layers indicate post-depositional deformation.

**Orogeny**: Mountain-building episode. Caused by: continent-continent collision (Himalayas, Alps), arc-continent collision (Andes, Cascades), accretion of terranes.

**Outcrop**: Exposure of bedrock at Earth's surface (not covered by soil, vegetation, water).

**Outer core**: Liquid iron-nickel layer (2,900-5,150 km depth). Convection generates Earth's magnetic field (geodynamo). Temperature: 3,700-4,800°C.

**Outwash**: Sediment deposited by meltwater streams from glacier. Well-sorted, stratified sand/gravel. Forms outwash plains (sandurs).

**Overburden**: Rock/sediment overlying ore deposit or coal seam. Must be removed in surface mining (open-pit, mountaintop removal).

**Oxidation**: (1) Chemistry: Reaction with oxygen or loss of electrons. (2) Weathering: Rust formation (iron minerals exposed to O₂, water). Produces reddish-brown colors in rocks, soils.

**Oxygen isotopes (δ¹⁸O)**: Ratio of ¹⁸O/¹⁶O in material relative to standard. Varies with temperature, ice volume. Used for: paleoclimate (ice cores, foraminifera), paleoelevation.

## P

**P-wave (Primary)**: Compressional seismic wave. Particle motion parallel to wave direction. Fastest seismic wave (5-14 km/s). Travels through solids, liquids, gases. First arrival at seismometer.

**Pahoehoe (lava)**: Basaltic lava with smooth, ropy surface. Low viscosity. Forms by slow, continuous flow. Compare aa (blocky, rough).

**Paleoclimate**: Ancient climate. Reconstructed from proxies: ice cores, tree rings, ocean sediments, fossils, coral, speleothems, paleosoils.

**Paleogene**: Period spanning 66-23 Ma (early Cenozoic). Mammals rapidly diversify post-dinosaurs. India collides with Asia. PETM warming event (56 Ma).

**Paleomagnetic**: Ancient magnetic field recorded in rocks. Iron minerals align with field when cooling/forming, preserve orientation. Used for: plate reconstructions, dating (magnetic reversals), paleolatitudes.

**Paleoseismology**: Study of prehistoric earthquakes via geological evidence (trenching across faults, dating offset layers). Determines recurrence intervals for hazard assessment.

**Paleozoic**: Era spanning 541-252 Ma (ancient life). Periods: Cambrian, Ordovician, Silurian, Devonian, Carboniferous, Permian. Cambrian explosion, life colonizes land, coal forests, ends with Permian mass extinction.

**Pangea**: Last supercontinent (assembly ~300 Ma, peak ~250 Ma, breakup ~200 Ma). All continents joined. Extreme climate: hot, arid interior. Preceded by Gondwana + Laurasia.

**Parent isotope**: Radioactive isotope that decays to daughter isotope. Examples: ²³⁸U (parent) → ²⁰⁶Pb (daughter), ⁴⁰K → ⁴⁰Ar, ¹⁴C → ¹⁴N.

**Partial melting**: Melting of only some minerals in rock (not complete melting). Low-melting minerals liquefy first. Changes magma composition (usually more felsic than source). Key to forming continental crust from mantle.

**Peak ground acceleration (PGA)**: Maximum acceleration during earthquake shaking (% of g). <10%g: minor damage; 10-30%g: moderate; >50%g: severe even in well-built structures.

**Peat**: Partially decayed plant material accumulating in waterlogged conditions (swamps, bogs). First stage of coal formation (not coal yet). Used as fuel where abundant (Ireland, Russia).

**Pegmatite**: Exceptionally coarse-grained igneous rock (crystals >1 cm, often >10 cm). Forms from water-rich late-stage magma. Source of rare elements (Li, Be, Ta, REEs, gemstones).

**Pelagic**: Relating to open ocean (water column). Pelagic sediments: Fine-grained, accumulate slowly on deep ocean floor (clay, calcareous/siliceous ooze).

**Permeability**: Measure of how easily fluids flow through rock/sediment. High: sandstone, gravel; low: shale, granite. Essential for aquifers, oil reservoirs.

**Permafrost**: Ground remaining frozen (≤0°C) for ≥2 consecutive years. Contains ~1,700 Gt carbon (frozen organic matter). Thawing releases CO₂, CH₄ → climate feedback.

**Permian**: Period spanning 299-252 Ma (late Paleozoic). Pangea assembly, synapsids (mammal ancestors) dominant, climate warming, ends with largest mass extinction (96% species die).

**Petroleum**: Liquid hydrocarbon mixture (oil). Forms from marine microorganisms buried in source rocks, heated in oil window (60-120°C). Composition varies: light (gasoline-like) to heavy (tar).

**PETM**: Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (56 Ma). Rapid warming event (+5-8°C in <20,000 years). Caused by massive carbon release (methane hydrates?, volcanism?). Analogue for anthropogenic warming.

**Petrostate**: Country where oil/gas dominates economy (>50% government revenue or >25% GDP). Examples: Saudi Arabia, Russia, Venezuela, Nigeria. Vulnerable to price volatility, resource curse.

**Petrology**: Study of rocks—origin, composition, structure. Subfields: igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic.

**Phanerozoic**: Eon spanning 541 Ma-present (visible life). Abundant fossils. Eras: Paleozoic, Mesozoic, Cenozoic. All complex multicellular life occurred in this eon.

**Phenocryst**: Large crystal in finer-grained matrix (porphyritic texture). Formed during slow cooling (deep), then erupted (rapid cooling of groundmass).

**Phyllite**: Low-to-medium grade metamorphic rock (between slate and schist). Lustrous sheen from fine mica. Foliated.

**Pillow lava**: Basaltic lava erupted underwater. Rapid cooling forms pillow-shaped blobs stacked on each other. Diagnostic of submarine eruption.

**Placer deposit**: Ore deposit where dense minerals (gold, diamonds, tin, platinum) concentrated by water/wind sorting. Found in river channels, beaches. California Gold Rush exploited placers.

**Plagioclase**: Solid solution series of feldspars from albite (NaAlSi₃O₈) to anorthite (CaAl₂Si₂O₈). Most abundant mineral in oceanic crust.

**Plate tectonics**: Theory that lithosphere is divided into rigid plates moving relative to each other. Explains earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building, ocean basins. Paradigm shift (1960s).

**Platinum group metals (PGMs)**: Six metals (Pt, Pd, Rh, Ru, Ir, Os) with similar properties. Uses: catalytic converters (70%), jewelry, hydrogen fuel cells, electronics. South Africa: 70% of production (Bushveld Complex).

**Pleistocene**: Epoch spanning 2.58 Ma-11.7 ka (Ice Age). Glacial-interglacial cycles (Milankovitch-driven), human evolution, megafauna extinctions.

**Plume**: See mantle plume.

**Pluton**: Intrusive igneous body. Types by size/shape: batholith (>100 km²), stock (<100 km²), dike (cuts across layers), sill (parallel to layers).

**Plutonic**: Synonym for intrusive igneous. Named for Pluto (Roman god of underworld).

**Pore pressure**: Fluid pressure in pore spaces. High pore pressure: reduces effective stress, destabilizes slopes/faults. Raised by injection (wastewater, fracking), reservoir filling.

**Porphyry copper**: Large, low-grade (0.3-1% Cu) copper deposit associated with porphyritic intrusion. Disseminated chalcopyrite in fracture networks. Forms from hydrothermal fluids above cooling magma. Accounts for 60% of global copper production. Examples: Escondida (Chile), Morenci (Arizona).

**Porosity**: Fraction of rock volume that is pore space (voids). Sandstone: 10-30%; shale: 5-15%; granite: <1%. High porosity needed for aquifers, oil reservoirs.

**Potassium-argon (K-Ar) dating**: See K-Ar dating.

**Precambrian**: All time before Cambrian (4.54 Ga-541 Ma). 88% of Earth history. Includes Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic eons. Life present but mostly microbial; sparse fossil record.

**Precession**: Wobble of Earth's axis of rotation (like spinning top). Period: ~26,000 years (Milankovitch cycle). Changes which hemisphere has summer when Earth closest to Sun.

**Pressure**: Force per unit area. SI unit: pascal (Pa). 1 atmosphere (atm) = 101,325 Pa ≈ 101 kPa ≈ 0.1 MPa. Deep Earth: gigapascals (GPa).

**Primary energy**: Energy in natural form before conversion (crude oil, coal, sunlight, wind, uranium). Contrast with secondary (electricity, gasoline).

**Proterozoic**: Eon spanning 2.5 Ga-541 Ma. Great Oxidation Event, eukaryotes, multicellular life, Snowball Earth events, supercontinents Nuna/Rodinia.

**Proxy (paleoclimate)**: Indirect indicator of past climate. Examples: δ¹⁸O (temperature, ice volume), tree rings (precipitation, temperature), pollen (vegetation → climate), coral bands (sea surface temperature).

**Pumice**: Highly vesicular (porous) volcanic rock. Felsic composition. So many gas bubbles it floats on water. Forms from explosive eruptions (rapid gas exsolution).

**P-wave shadow zone**: Region 103°-142° from earthquake where P-waves absent (refracted by core). Discovered outer core.

**Pyroclastic**: Fragmental volcanic material explosively ejected. Greek: pyro (fire), klastos (broken). Includes ash (<2 mm), lapilli (2-64 mm), blocks/bombs (>64 mm).

**Pyroclastic flow**: Ground-hugging avalanche of hot gas, ash, rock fragments (500-1,000°C, 100-700 km/h). Most lethal volcanic hazard. Incinerates everything in path. Examples: 79 CE Pompeii, 1902 Mt. Pelée (29,000 deaths).

**Pyroxene**: Single-chain silicate mineral group. Example: augite. Dark color, ~90° cleavage angles. Common in mafic igneous rocks (basalt, gabbro).

**Pyrite**: Iron sulfide (FeS₂). "Fool's gold"—golden metallic luster. Cubic crystals. Oxidation produces sulfuric acid (acid mine drainage).

## Q

**Quartz**: Silicon dioxide (SiO₂). Second most abundant mineral in continental crust (after feldspars, ~12%). Very hard (Mohs 7), no cleavage, conchoidal fracture. Resistant to weathering (survives to form sandstone). Varieties: clear (rock crystal), purple (amethyst), pink (rose), smoky, yellow (citrine).

**Quartzite**: Non-foliated metamorphic rock from quartz sandstone. Interlocking quartz grains (recrystallized). Very hard, massive. Breaks through grains, not around them (unlike sandstone).

**Quaternary**: Period spanning 2.58 Ma-present (recent). Epochs: Pleistocene (Ice Age), Holocene (last 11.7 ka). Human evolution, agriculture, civilization.

## R

**Radioactive decay**: Spontaneous transformation of unstable atomic nucleus to different nucleus, emitting particles/energy. Rate constant (unaffected by T, P, chemistry). Basis for radiometric dating.

**Radiometric dating**: Determining numerical age by measuring parent/daughter isotope ratios. Methods: ¹⁴C (up to 50 ka), K-Ar (>100 ka), U-Pb (>1 Ma), Rb-Sr (>10 Ma).

**Rare earth elements (REEs)**: 17 elements (lanthanides + Sc + Y). Critical for technology (magnets, phosphors, catalysts, batteries). "Rare" is misnomer—relatively abundant but rarely concentrated. China: 70% mining, 85-90% processing. Geopolitical concern.

**Recharge**: Replenishment of aquifer by infiltration of precipitation/surface water. Sustainable groundwater use requires extraction ≤ recharge rate.

**Recrystallization**: Formation of new crystals in solid rock (no melting). Occurs during metamorphism. Increases grain size, obliterates original texture.

**Reef**: Wave-resistant structure built by organisms. Modern: coral (warm, shallow, clear seas). Ancient: stromatolites (Precambrian), rugose corals (Paleozoic), rudist clams (Cretaceous). Paleolatitude indicator.

**Regional metamorphism**: Metamorphism affecting large areas (100s-1,000s km²) from burial + tectonic compression. Forms foliated rocks (slate → schist → gneiss) in mountain belts.

**Relative age**: Age relative to other rocks (older/younger). Determined by superposition, cross-cutting relationships, fossils. Compare absolute age (numerical).

**Relief**: Difference in elevation between highest and lowest points in area. High relief: mountains. Low relief: plains.

**Reservoir rock**: Porous, permeable rock storing oil, gas, or groundwater. Examples: sandstone, fractured limestone, reef carbonate. Must be sealed by cap rock.

**Resource curse**: Paradox where resource-rich nations often have slower growth, more corruption/conflict than resource-poor nations. Mechanisms: Dutch Disease, rent-seeking, weak institutions.

**Resource nationalism**: Government assertion of control over natural resources, often expropriating foreign companies. Examples: Bolivia lithium (2008), Venezuela oil (2000s), Russia oil (2003 Yukos).

**Reverse fault**: Fault where hanging wall moves up relative to footwall. Indicates compression. Low-angle reverse fault (<30°) = thrust fault.

**Rheology**: Study of how materials deform and flow. Rocks behave brittle (shallow, cold) or ductile (deep, hot). Mantle solid but flows viscously over Ma timescales.

**Rhyolite**: Felsic volcanic rock (>70% SiO₂). Fine-grained, light-colored (white, pink, gray). Volcanic equivalent of granite. High viscosity → explosive eruptions.

**Rift valley**: Linear depression formed by normal faulting during crustal extension. Examples: East African Rift (active), Rio Grande Rift (USA), Mid-Atlantic Ridge median valley.

**Ring of Fire**: Zone of volcanoes and earthquakes encircling Pacific Ocean. Coincides with subduction zones (Pacific Plate margins). ~75% of world's active volcanoes, 90% of earthquakes.

**Rock**: Solid aggregate of minerals (or organic matter). Three types: igneous (from magma), sedimentary (from lithified sediment), metamorphic (transformed by heat/pressure).

**Rock cycle**: Continuous transformation between igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic rock types. No inherent direction—any rock can transform to any other.

**Rock-forming minerals**: ~20 common minerals comprising >95% of crust. Include: feldspars, quartz, micas, pyroxenes, amphiboles, olivine, calcite.

**Rodinia**: Supercontinent existing 1.1-0.75 Ga (Neoproterozoic). Breakup contributed to Snowball Earth events. Preceded Pangea.

**Rubidium-strontium (Rb-Sr) dating**: Radiometric method. ⁸⁷Rb decays to ⁸⁷Sr (half-life 48.8 Ga). Used for igneous/metamorphic rocks, meteorites. Range: >10 Ma.

**Run-up**: Maximum height above normal sea level reached by tsunami wave. Depends on wave energy, seafloor bathymetry, coastline shape. 2011 Tōhoku: up to 40 m.

## S

**S-wave (Secondary)**: Shear seismic wave. Particle motion perpendicular to wave direction. Cannot travel through liquids (no shear strength). Slower than P-waves (3-7 km/s). Second arrival.

**S-wave shadow zone**: Region 103°-180° from earthquake where S-waves absent. Evidence that outer core is liquid (stops S-waves).

**Salar**: Salt flat (playa) in closed basin (arid region). Lithium-rich brines in some salars (Atacama, Uyuni) = major Li source. Formed by evaporation concentrating dissolved salts.

**Sandstone**: Clastic sedimentary rock with sand-sized grains (0.0625-2 mm). Composition: mostly quartz (resistant). Porous, permeable → important aquifer, oil reservoir.

**Saturated zone**: Subsurface region where all pore spaces filled with water. Top boundary = water table. Contains groundwater.

**Schist**: Medium-to-high grade metamorphic rock with schistosity (visible foliation from aligned mica flakes). Minerals: mica, garnet, staurolite, kyanite. From shale, phyllite, or mafic rocks.

**Sea level**: Average height of ocean surface. Current: ~7,000 km³ ice in ice sheets. Melting all ice → +70 m sea level. Last Glacial Maximum (20 ka): -120 m. Cretaceous peak: +200 m (no ice, thermal expansion).

**Seafloor spreading**: Process where new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges and spreads outward. Key evidence for plate tectonics (magnetic stripes, age progression).

**Seamount**: Underwater volcano (doesn't reach sea surface). Truncated seamount = guyot (wave-eroded, then subsided). Hawaiian-Emperor Chain: seamounts + islands.

**Sediment**: Loose particles transported by water, wind, ice, or gravity. Becomes sedimentary rock through lithification.

**Sedimentary rock**: Rock formed from lithified sediment. Types: clastic (sandstone, shale), chemical (limestone, evaporite), organic (coal). Comprises <5% of crust volume but covers ~75% of land surface. Only rocks preserving fossils.

**Seismic gap**: Segment of fault that hasn't ruptured recently. Higher probability of future earthquake (strain accumulating). Example: Cascadia subduction zone (last rupture 1700 → "overdue").

**Seismic wave**: Energy wave propagating through Earth from earthquake, explosion, or impact. Types: P-waves, S-waves, Love waves, Rayleigh waves.

**Seismogram**: Record of ground motion vs time from seismometer. Shows P-wave arrival, S-wave arrival, surface waves. Time difference → distance to earthquake.

**Seismology**: Study of earthquakes and seismic waves. Applications: earthquake hazards, Earth's interior structure (tomography), nuclear test monitoring.

**Seismometer**: Instrument measuring ground motion. Based on inertia of suspended mass. Modern instruments detect motion < 1 nanometer (atomic scale).

**Shale**: Clastic sedimentary rock with clay/silt-sized grains (<0.0625 mm). Fissile (splits into thin layers). Most abundant sedimentary rock. Forms from mud in quiet water (lakes, lagoons, deep ocean). Petroleum source rocks, cap rocks.

**Shale gas/oil**: Natural gas or oil trapped in low-permeability shale. Requires horizontal drilling + fracking to extract. Examples: Marcellus (gas), Bakken (oil), Eagle Ford (oil + gas).

**Shield**: Large area of exposed Precambrian crystalline basement (cratons). Flat, low relief. Examples: Canadian, Baltic, African shields.

**Shield volcano**: Broad, gently sloping volcano built by fluid basaltic lava flows. Shape resembles warrior's shield. Examples: Mauna Loa (Hawaii, largest volcano on Earth by volume).

**Sial**: Old term for continental crust (silica + alumina-rich). Contrast with sima (oceanic crust). Largely obsolete terminology.

**Siderophile**: Element with chemical affinity for iron. Concentrated in Earth's core during differentiation (Fe, Ni, Co, Pt, Au). Gold/platinum scarcity in crust explained by siderophile nature.

**Silicate**: Mineral containing silicon-oxygen tetrahedra (SiO₄⁴⁻). Most abundant mineral class in crust/mantle (~90%). Subclasses: isolated, chain, sheet, framework.

**Silicate weathering**: Chemical breakdown of silicate minerals consuming atmospheric CO₂. CaSiO₃ + 2CO₂ + 3H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ + H₄SiO₄. Long-term climate regulation (negative feedback).

**Sill**: Tabular intrusive igneous body parallel to existing rock layers (usually horizontal). Younger than layers it intrudes.

**Silt**: Sediment particle size 0.004-0.0625 mm (between clay and sand). Feels gritty, not sticky. Forms siltstone when lithified.

**Sinkhole**: Collapse of ground into subsurface void. Common in karst terrain (limestone dissolution creates caves). Triggers: groundwater extraction, heavy rain, construction vibration. Florida: 10,000+ sinkholes.

**Slate**: Low-grade metamorphic rock from shale/mudstone. Slaty cleavage (splits into thin, flat sheets). Used for roofing tiles, billiard tables.

**Snowball Earth**: Global glaciation events (720, 650 Ma) where ice reached equator, possibly covering entire surface. Evidence: glacial deposits at paleoequator, cap carbonates. Exit via volcanic CO₂ accumulation → super-greenhouse.

**Soil**: Weathered rock + organic matter at surface. Supports plant growth. Layers (horizons): O (organic), A (topsoil), B (subsoil), C (weathered rock), R (bedrock). Takes centuries to form, easily eroded.

**Solid solution**: Mineral series where one element substitutes for another without changing structure. Example: olivine (Mg,Fe)₂SiO₄ (Mg and Fe freely substitute).

**Source rock**: Organic-rich sedimentary rock (typically >1% total organic carbon) that generates oil/gas when heated. Typically marine shales. Cretaceous shales = major Mideast source rocks.

**Specific gravity**: Density relative to water (water = 1). Mineral property. Light: <2.5 (halite 2.2); average silicate: 2.7-3.0; heavy: >4 (magnetite 5.2, galena 7.5, gold 19.3).

**Spinel**: Mineral group with cubic structure. Includes magnetite (Fe₃O₄), chromite (FeCr₂O₄). At mantle depths (>410 km), olivine transforms to spinel structure (denser).

**Spodumene**: Lithium aluminum silicate (LiAlSi₂O₆). Primary hard-rock lithium mineral. Found in pegmatites. Greenbushes (Australia) = largest spodumene mine.

**Spreading center**: See mid-ocean ridge.

**Stalactite**: Icicle-shaped calcite deposit hanging from cave ceiling. Forms by dripping water depositing CaCO₃. Mnemonic: stalactite "holds tight" to ceiling.

**Stalagmite**: Cone-shaped calcite deposit rising from cave floor. Forms by dripping water depositing CaCO₃. Mnemonic: stalagmite "might" reach ceiling.

**Stock**: Intermediate-sized pluton (<100 km² exposed area). Smaller than batholith.

**Strain**: Deformation (change in shape/volume) resulting from stress. Elastic: recovers when stress removed. Plastic: permanent.

**Strata**: Layers (beds) in sedimentary rocks. Singular: stratum. Stratigraphy: study of strata.

**Stratovolcano**: Steep-sided composite volcano built of alternating lava flows and pyroclastic material. Andesitic composition, explosive eruptions. Examples: Mt. Fuji, Mt. Rainier, Mt. St. Helens.

**Streak**: Color of mineral in powdered form (rub on unglazed porcelain plate). More diagnostic than surface color. Hematite: reddish-brown streak (despite metallic gray appearance).

**Strike**: Compass direction of horizontal line on inclined geological surface (bedding, fault, etc.). Perpendicular to dip direction.

**Strike-slip fault**: Fault with horizontal displacement (blocks slide past each other). Minimal vertical movement. Examples: San Andreas, Alpine (NZ), Anatolian.

**Stromatolite**: Layered sedimentary structure built by microbial mats (cyanobacteria). Oldest fossils: 3.48 Ga (Pilbara, Australia). Dominated Precambrian; rare today (Shark Bay, Australia).

**Structural geology**: Study of rock deformation (folds, faults, fractures) and stresses causing it.

**Subduction**: Process where oceanic lithosphere sinks into mantle at convergent boundary. Requires dense, old oceanic plate. Generates deep earthquakes, volcanic arcs, deep-sea trenches. Recycles crust.

**Subduction zone**: Convergent boundary where subduction occurs. Types: ocean-ocean (island arc), ocean-continent (Andean-type). Most powerful earthquakes (megathrusts), explosive volcanism.

**Subsidence**: Lowering of ground surface. Causes: groundwater/oil extraction, compaction, karst dissolution, mining, thawing permafrost. Examples: San Joaquin Valley (9 m), Mexico City (10 m).

**Sulfate aerosols**: Atmospheric H₂SO₄ droplets from volcanic SO₂. Reflect sunlight → cooling. Persist 1-3 years in stratosphere. Pinatubo 1991: 20 Mt SO₂ → -0.5°C for 2 years.

**Sulfide**: Mineral composed of metal + sulfur. Examples: pyrite (FeS₂), galena (PbS), chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂). Important ore minerals. Oxidation produces sulfuric acid (environmental hazard).

**Supercontinent**: Continental landmass comprising most/all continental crust. Examples: Pangea (300-200 Ma), Rodinia (1.1-0.75 Ga), Nuna (1.8-1.5 Ga). Form and break up cyclically (~400-600 Ma cycles—Wilson Cycle).

**Superposition**: Principle that in undisturbed sedimentary sequences, each layer is younger than the one below, older than the one above. Fundamental to relative dating.

**Supereruption**: Extremely large volcanic eruption (VEI-7 or VEI-8, >100 km³ ejecta). Causes volcanic winter (decade-long cooling), global famine. Last: Toba (74 ka). Future risk: Yellowstone (~0.001%/year probability).

**Surface wave**: Seismic wave traveling along Earth's surface (not through interior). Slowest but largest amplitude → most damaging. Types: Love (horizontal shear), Rayleigh (rolling).

**Suspended load**: Fine sediment (silt, clay) carried in water column by turbulence (doesn't settle quickly). Rivers look muddy from suspended load.

**S-wave shadow zone**: Region 103°-180° from earthquake epicenter where S-waves absent (blocked by liquid outer core). Revealed core is liquid.

**Syncline**: Downward-curving fold (trough shape: ∪). Youngest rocks at core. Opposite of anticline.

## T

**Talc**: Sheet silicate (Mg₃Si₄O₁₀(OH)₂). Softest mineral (Mohs 1). Greasy feel. Uses: talcum powder, ceramics, paint, paper filler.

**Talus**: Accumulation of rock fragments at base of cliff from mechanical weathering (frost wedging, rockfall). Angular, unsorted.

**Tantalum (Ta)**: Refractory metal. Uses: capacitors (80%), superalloys, surgical implants. DRC: 40% production (conflict mineral—funded armed groups). Mined as coltan (columbite-tantalite).

**Tectonic plate**: Rigid segment of lithosphere moving across asthenosphere. ~15 major plates. Boundaries: divergent, convergent, transform.

**Tectonics**: Study of large-scale deformation of lithosphere and forces causing it. Plate tectonics: unifying theory.

**Terrigenous**: Sediment derived from weathering of land rocks (contrast with biogenic or chemical). Transported to ocean by rivers, wind.

**Texture**: Size, shape, arrangement of components (grains, crystals) in rock. Igneous: aphanitic (fine), phaneritic (coarse), porphyritic (two sizes), glassy. Sedimentary: clastic, crystalline. Metamorphic: foliated, non-foliated.

**Theia impact**: Giant impact hypothesis. Mars-sized protoplanet collided with proto-Earth ~4.51 Ga. Debris coalesced to form Moon. Explains Earth-Moon similarity (isotopes), Moon's low density (mantle material, little iron).

**Thermal expansion**: Increase in volume with temperature. Seawater: ~0.02% per °C. Major contributor to sea level rise (currently ~1 mm/yr from thermal expansion, ~2 mm/yr from ice melt).

**Thermodynamics**: Science of energy, heat, work. Laws set absolute constraints. First Law: energy conserved. Second Law: entropy increases (efficiency <100%). Carnot limit: η = 1 - T_cold/T_hot.

**Thrust fault**: Low-angle (<30°) reverse fault. Hanging wall pushed up/over footwall. Forms in compressional settings (collision zones, accretionary wedges). Can transport rock 10s-100s km.

**Till**: Unsorted, unstratified sediment deposited directly by glacier. Mixed grain sizes (clay to boulders). Lithified = tillite (evidence of ancient glaciation).

**Tillite**: Lithified glacial till. Ancient tillites at paleoequator = evidence for Snowball Earth.

**Tombolo**: Sandy isthmus connecting island to mainland. Forms by wave refraction and longshore drift. Example: Angel Island (San Francisco Bay).

**Topaz**: Aluminum fluorosilicate (Al₂SiO₄(F,OH)₂). Hard (Mohs 8). Gemstone. Found in pegmatites, greisens.

**Transform boundary**: Plate boundary with horizontal sliding (strike-slip motion). No creation/destruction of lithosphere. Shallow earthquakes, no volcanism. Examples: San Andreas, Alpine, Dead Sea.

**Transgression**: Advance of sea over land (sea level rise or land subsidence). Deposits coarsen upward (shallow → deep → shallow). Opposite: regression.

**Transition zone**: Mantle layer 410-660 km depth. Olivine transforms to denser structures (spinel at 410 km, perovskite at 660 km). Seismic velocity increases.

**Triassic**: Period spanning 252-201 Ma (early Mesozoic). Recovery from Permian extinction, dinosaurs originate, Pangea peak then breakup begins, ends with CAMP volcanism extinction.

**Trilobite**: Extinct marine arthropod (540-252 Ma). Excellent index fossil (Paleozoic). Segmented body, compound eyes. Diverse forms (~20,000 species).

**Tsunami**: Series of ocean waves from sudden water displacement (submarine earthquake, landslide, volcano, meteorite). Open ocean: <1 m height, 700 km/h speed. Approaching shore: height increases to 10s of meters. Multiple waves over hours. 2004 Indian Ocean: ~230,000 deaths (14 countries).

**Tuff**: Volcanic rock composed of consolidated ash/lapilli. Fine-to-coarse grained. Welded tuff: hot ash fuses together.

**Turbidite**: Sedimentary deposit from turbidity current (underwater landslide/density current). Graded bedding (coarse → fine upward). Deposited on continental slope, abyssal plain.

**Type locality**: Geographic location where rock unit (formation, member) is first described and named. Example: Morrison Formation named for town of Morrison, Colorado.

## U

**Ultramafic**: Igneous rock with very low silica (<45% SiO₂), very high Mg-Fe content. Dark green-black. Examples: peridotite (mantle rock), komatiite (Archean lava). Too hot for modern volcanism.

**Unconformity**: Gap in rock record where erosion removed strata before deposition resumed. Represents missing time. Types: angular (tilted layers), disconformity (parallel layers), nonconformity (sediments on crystalline rock).

**Uniformitarianism**: "Present is key to past" (James Hutton, Charles Lyell). Physical/chemical laws operating today operated throughout Earth history. Processes we observe explain ancient rock record. Enabled geology to become science.

**Uplift**: Raising of rock/landmass. Causes: tectonic compression (orogeny), isostatic rebound (erosion removes weight, ice melts), mantle upwelling (hotspot).

**Uranium-lead (U-Pb) dating**: Most precise radiometric method. ²³⁸U → ²⁰⁶Pb (4.47 Ga half-life), ²³⁵U → ²⁰⁷Pb (704 Ma). Zircon ideal mineral (incorporates U, excludes Pb). Range: >1 Ma. Used for Earth's age (4.54 Ga).

**Uranium-235 (²³⁵U)**: Fissile isotope (0.7% of natural U). Enrichment to 3-5% = reactor fuel, >90% = weapons-grade. Half-life: 704 Ma. Decays to ²⁰⁷Pb.

**Uranium-238 (²³⁸U)**: Most abundant uranium isotope (99.3%). Half-life: 4.47 Ga. Decays to ²⁰⁶Pb. Main heat source in Earth's interior (radioactive decay).

## V

**Valley glacier**: Glacier confined to mountain valley (follows drainage). Carves U-shaped valley. Examples: Athabasca (Canada), Aletsch (Switzerland).

**VEI**: Volcanic Explosivity Index (0-8 scale). Measures eruption size by ejecta volume, plume height. Logarithmic: each unit ~10× more ejecta. VEI-8 (supereruption): >1,000 km³. Last: Toba (74 ka).

**Vent**: Opening where volcanic material erupts. Central vent (most common), fissure vent (linear crack).

**Viscosity**: Resistance to flow. Basaltic lava: low viscosity (flows easily, 10² Pa·s). Rhyolitic lava: high viscosity (barely flows, 10⁶ Pa·s). Temperature, silica content control viscosity.

**Volatile**: Substance easily vaporized. In magma: H₂O, CO₂, SO₂, HCl, HF. Dissolved at depth (high P), exsolve approaching surface (low P) → drive eruptions.

**Volcanic arc**: Chain of volcanoes above subduction zone. Island arc (oceanic overriding plate): Aleutians, Japan. Continental arc (continental overriding plate): Andes, Cascades.

**Volcanic bomb**: Pyroclastic fragment >64 mm ejected while molten, solidifies in flight. Aerodynamic shapes (spindle, breadcrust).

**Volcanic winter**: Cooling from large eruption injecting SO₂ into stratosphere. Sulfate aerosols reflect sunlight. Duration: years (VEI-6) to decade+ (supereruption). Historic: 1816 "Year Without Summer" (Tambora 1815).

**Volcanism**: Eruption of magma at Earth's surface. Settings: mid-ocean ridges (60% of output), subduction zones (explosive), hotspots (Hawaii, Iceland), rifts.

**Volcano**: Vent where magma erupts, plus accumulation of erupted material (mountain). Types: shield (basaltic, gentle), stratovolcano (andesitic, steep), cinder cone (scoria), caldera (collapsed).

**VMS**: Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide deposit. Cu-Zn-Pb-Ag ore formed by hydrothermal precipitation on ancient seafloor. Example: Kidd Creek (Canada).

## W

**Wadati-Benioff zone**: Inclined plane of earthquakes marking subducting slab. Depth: 0-700 km. Discovered independently by Wadati (1930s) and Benioff (1950s). Direct evidence of subduction.

**Water table**: Top of saturated zone. Depth varies (meters to 100s of meters) with topography, climate, geology. Wells must penetrate below water table.

**Weathering**: Breakdown of rock at Earth's surface. Mechanical: Physical disintegration (frost wedging, root growth, thermal expansion). Chemical: Dissolution, oxidation, hydrolysis (silicate weathering). Biological: Organisms aid both.

**Welded tuff**: Pyroclastic deposit where hot ash particles fuse together (>800°C). Glassy texture, can resemble lava. Forms from pyroclastic flows.

**Wilson Cycle**: Opening and closing of ocean basins over hundreds of millions of years. Stages: rifting → seafloor spreading → mature ocean → subduction → closure → collision. Named for J. Tuzo Wilson. Supercontinent cycle.

## X

**Xenolith**: Foreign rock fragment enclosed in igneous rock. "Stranger rock" (Greek: xenos = stranger, lithos = rock). Provides samples of deep crust/mantle (brought up by magma).

## Y

**Yellowstone**: Hotspot beneath Yellowstone National Park (Wyoming). Track of volcanism: Snake River Plain (17 Ma-present). Last supereruption: 640 ka (VEI-8, 1,000 km³). Caldera: 55 × 72 km. Geysers, hot springs. Future eruption: low probability but catastrophic impact.

## Z

**Zeolite**: Group of hydrated aluminosilicate minerals with open framework structure. Forms by low-temperature alteration of volcanic glass. Uses: water softeners, catalysts, molecular sieves.

**Zircon**: Zirconium silicate (ZrSiO₄). Extremely durable, resistant to weathering. Incorporates U (not Pb) → ideal for U-Pb dating. Oldest mineral: 4.404 Ga (Jack Hills, Australia). Records Earth's earliest history.

**Zone (metamorphic)**: Region characterized by index mineral indicating specific P-T conditions. Example: garnet zone (intermediate metamorphic grade).

## Summary

This glossary provides comprehensive definitions for ~500 key terms in geology and Earth science. Terms span all major subfields: mineralogy, petrology, structural geology, geomorphology, plate tectonics, seismology, volcanology, sedimentology, stratigraphy, geochemistry, paleontology, economic geology, geophysics, hydrogeology, and Earth history. Understanding these terms enables comprehension of geological processes, Earth's 4.54-billion-year history, natural hazards, resource distribution, and human-Earth interactions. Geology bridges fundamental science (physics, chemistry, biology) with practical applications (resource extraction, hazard mitigation, environmental management, climate science). This foundation is essential for informed decision-making about energy, materials, land use, and planetary stewardship.
