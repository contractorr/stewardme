# Resource Extraction & Geopolitics

## The Geological Lottery

Natural resource distribution is fundamentally determined by geology—plate tectonics, volcanism, sedimentation, weathering. This creates geographic concentration that shapes global power dynamics, trade relationships, and conflicts.

**Key principle**: Countries cannot choose their geology. Resource endowment is geological fate, not policy choice.

### Resource Curse Paradox

**Observation**: Resource-rich nations often have slower economic growth, more corruption, more conflict than resource-poor nations.

**Examples**:
- **Norway vs Nigeria**: Both major oil producers
  - Norway: GDP/capita $89k, democracy, low corruption
  - Nigeria: GDP/capita $2k, instability, high corruption
- **Botswana vs DRC**: Both mineral-rich
  - Botswana (diamonds): GDP/capita $7k, stable democracy
  - DRC (cobalt, copper, coltan): GDP/capita $600, decades of conflict

**Mechanisms**:
1. **Dutch Disease**: Resource exports → currency appreciation → other sectors uncompetitive
2. **Rent-seeking**: Profits from resources ("rents") attract corruption, not innovation
3. **Weak institutions**: Resource wealth enables autocrats to avoid taxation/accountability
4. **Conflict**: Valuable, concentrated resources fund armed groups (blood diamonds, conflict minerals)
5. **Volatility**: Commodity price swings destabilize economies

**Counterexamples** (resource wealth + good governance):
- Norway: Oil fund, democratic institutions
- Botswana: Diamond revenues invested in infrastructure, education
- Chile: Copper revenues + strong institutions

## Oil and Natural Gas

### Geological Control of Distribution

**Why Middle East dominates oil**:

**1. Source rocks**: Jurassic-Cretaceous warm seas deposited organic-rich shales
- High marine productivity (plankton blooms)
- Oxygen-poor seafloors preserved organics
- **Optimal**: Type II kerogen (marine mixed) → high oil yield

**2. Reservoir rocks**: Porous carbonates (reefs, platform carbonates) + sandstones
- Cretaceous Arab Formation (limestone): Primary Middle East reservoir
- Porosity 15-30%, permeability good

**3. Seal rocks**: Thick Cenozoic evaporites + shales
- Miocene Gachsaran Formation (anhydrite/salt): Perfect seal (impermeable, ductile, self-sealing)

**4. Trap geometry**: Simple anticlines, fault blocks
- Arabian Platform: Gentle folds, large structures
- Minimal deformation (stable craton since Paleozoic)

**5. Timing**: Optimal burial history
- Source rocks entered oil window 20-100 Ma
- Migration to traps before overburden loss

**Result**: **Giant oil fields** (>500 million barrels):
- Saudi Arabia: Ghawar (60-80 billion barrels, largest conventional field)
- Kuwait: Burgan (66 billion barrels)
- Iran: Ahvaz (37 billion barrels)
- Iraq: multiple giant fields

**World oil reserves** (2023):
| Country | Billion Barrels | % Global | Geological Setting |
|---------|----------------|----------|--------------------|
| **Venezuela** | 303 | 18% | Orinoco Belt heavy oil (controversial reserves) |
| **Saudi Arabia** | 298 | 17% | Arabian Platform, Cretaceous-Jurassic |
| **Iran** | 157 | 9% | Zagros fold belt, Cretaceous-Jurassic |
| **Iraq** | 145 | 8% | Arabian Platform/Mesopotamian Basin |
| **Kuwait** | 101 | 6% | Arabian Platform |
| **UAE** | 97 | 6% | Arabian Platform |
| **Russia** | 80 | 5% | West Siberian Basin, Volga-Urals |

| **Nigeria** | 37 | 2% | Niger Delta, Tertiary |

**Concentration**: Top 5 countries = 64% of global reserves

**Natural gas reserves** (trillion cubic meters):
- Russia (48, 24%), Iran (32, 16%), Qatar (25, 12%), Turkmenistan (14, 7%), US (13, 7%)

### The Shale Revolution (US)

**Technology breakthrough** (2000s-2010s):
- **Horizontal drilling**: Drill vertically, then turn 90° horizontally through shale layer
  - Exposes 1-3 km of reservoir (vs 10-50 m for vertical well)
- **Hydraulic fracturing** ("fracking"): High-pressure fluid fractures rock, props open cracks
  - Water + sand + chemicals (0.5% volume)
  - Creates network of microfractures → releases trapped oil/gas

**Key formations**:
- **Bakken** (North Dakota/Montana): Devonian-Mississippian shale, 7-11 billion barrels
- **Eagle Ford** (Texas): Cretaceous shale, 10 billion barrels
- **Permian Basin** (Texas/New Mexico): Stacked formations (conventional + unconventional)
- **Marcellus/Utica** (Appalachia): Devonian shale, massive gas (>100 trillion cubic feet)

**Impact**:
- **US production surge**: 5 million barrels/day (2008) → 13 million (2019) → 13 million (2023)
- **Net exporter**: First time since 1949 (2019)
- **Geopolitical**: Reduced US dependence on Middle East oil
- **OPEC weakened**: Shale responds quickly to price signals (6-12 months to bring wells online vs 5-10 years for conventional mega-projects)
- **Gas prices collapsed**: US natural gas dropped from $8-13/MMBtu (2008) to $2-4/MMBtu (2010s)

**Controversies**:
- **Water use**: 2-8 million gallons per well (strains arid regions)
- **Induced seismicity**: Wastewater injection → earthquakes (Oklahoma: <2 M3+ per year before 2009 → 900+ M3+ in 2015)
- **Methane emissions**: Leaks during production (potent greenhouse gas)
- **EROEI**: Lower than conventional (10-20:1 vs 30-100:1)

### Petrostates and Power

**Definition**: Economies dominated by hydrocarbon exports (>50% government revenue or >25% GDP).

**Dependency creates vulnerabilities**:

**Russia**:
- Oil + gas = 40% government revenue, 15% GDP, 60% exports
- **Weapon**: Gazprom controls European gas supply
  - Nord Stream pipelines (Germany)
  - Ukraine transit routes (political leverage)
- **2022 Ukraine war**: Europe scrambles to diversify (US LNG, Norwegian gas, renewables)
- **Vulnerability**: Oil price crashes devastate budget (2014-2016, 2020)

**Saudi Arabia**:
- Oil = 70% government revenue, 40% GDP, 90% exports
- **Power**: Swing producer (spare capacity ~2 million barrels/day)
  - Can flood market → crash prices (discipline producers)
  - Can cut production → support prices
- **OPEC kingpin**: De facto leader of cartel (coordination with Russia via OPEC+)
- **Diversification**: Vision 2030 (reduce oil dependence via Neom, tourism, finance)—challenging

**Venezuela**:
- World's largest reserves but collapsing production
- Peak: 3.5 million barrels/day (1997) → 0.7 million (2020)
- **Causes**: Underinvestment, mismanagement, corruption, sanctions, PDVSA (state oil company) prioritized politics over operations
- **Humanitarian crisis**: GDP collapsed 75% (2013-2020), hyperinflation, emigration

**Iran**:
- Sanctions limit exports, development
- Oil = 60% government revenue
- **Geopolitics**: Nuclear program linked to oil revenue security

**Norway** (counter-example—avoiding curse):
- Oil = 20% GDP, 50% exports, but **Government Pension Fund Global** (oil fund)
  - $1.4 trillion (2023), largest sovereign wealth fund
  - Invests abroad (avoids Dutch Disease)
  - Ensures intergenerational wealth transfer

## Critical Minerals

### Rare Earth Elements (REEs)

**17 elements**: Lanthanides (La to Lu) + scandium + yttrium

**Uses** (indispensable for modern technology):
| Element | Application | Why Critical |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| **Neodymium (Nd)** | Permanent magnets | Electric motors (EVs, wind turbines), hard drives |
| **Dysprosium (Dy)** | High-temp magnets | Enables magnets to work at 200°C (EV motors) |
| **Terbium (Tb)** | Phosphors | LED/LCD screens, fluorescent lights |
| **Europium (Eu)** | Phosphors | Red color in displays, €50/€100 anti-counterfeiting |
| **Lanthanum (La)** | Batteries, catalysts | NiMH batteries (hybrids), fluid cracking catalysts |
| **Cerium (Ce)** | Catalysts, polishing | Catalytic converters, glass polishing |
| **Yttrium (Y)** | Phosphors, lasers | LED lights, yttrium aluminum garnet (YAG) lasers |
| **Praseodymium (Pr)** | Magnets, alloys | Aircraft engines (high-strength alloys) |

**Misleading name**: Not rare! Cerium more abundant than copper; thulium rarer than platinum.

**True challenge**: **Concentration** + **Separation**

**Why REEs occur together**:
- Similar ionic radius, chemistry (lanthanide contraction)
- Always found as mixture, never individual elements
- **Separation**: Complex, expensive, toxic process (ion exchange, solvent extraction)
  - 20-30 stages
  - Generates radioactive waste (thorium, uranium often present)

**Geological occurrence**:

**1. Carbonatites** (igneous carbonate rocks):
- Rare (only ~1% of igneous rocks)
- Form from mantle plumes, rifts
- REE minerals: Bastnäsite (CeFCO₃), monazite ((Ce,La,Th)PO₄)
- **Bayan Obo, China**: World's largest deposit (35% of global reserves)
  - Mixed carbonatite-iron ore body
  - Discovered 1927, REE production since 1980s

**2. Ion-adsorption clays**:
- Tropical weathering breaks down granites
- REEs adsorb onto clay minerals
- Low-grade (0.05-0.2%) but easy to process (acid leach)
- **Southern China**: Dominates heavy REE production (Dy, Tb, Y)
  - Environmental disaster: Acid leaching pollutes water, no topsoil replacement

**3. Alkaline igneous rocks**:
- Nepheline syenites, pegmatites
- **Mountain Pass, California**: Once 100% of global supply (1960s-1980s)
  - Reopened 2018 (MP Materials) after China dominance

**Geopolitical dominance (China)**:

**Mining**: 60-70% of global production (2023)
- Bayan Obo (Inner Mongolia)
- Southern China ion-clays

**Processing/refining**: 85-90% of global capacity
- Separation technology
- Vertical integration (mine → refine → magnets)

**Historical path to dominance**:
1. **1970s-80s**: US (Mountain Pass) dominates
2. **1990s**: China undercuts prices, accepts environmental costs
3. **2000s**: Mountain Pass closes (2002), China near-monopoly (>95%)
4. **2010**: Export restrictions → price spike (Nd oxide: $20/kg → $270/kg)
   - Electronics, defense industries panicked
5. **2010s**: Diversification efforts (Mountain Pass reopens, Australia mines)
6. **2020s**: China still dominant, but competitors growing

**Strategic implications**:
- **Military**: Guided missiles (Nd magnets for control surfaces), jet engines (high-temp alloys), night vision (phosphors)
- **Clean energy**: Wind turbines (1 MW turbine = 200 kg Nd), EVs (1 EV = 1-2 kg REEs)
- **Supply shock risk**: China could embargo (hasn't, but threat exists)

**Alternatives**:
- Recycling (hard drives, batteries)—currently <1% recycled
- Substitution (ferrite magnets, but weaker)
- New mines (Vietnam, Greenland, Tanzania under development)

### Lithium

**"White petroleum"** of 21st century.

**Uses**:
- **Batteries** (85% of demand): Li-ion (phones, laptops, EVs), LiFePO₄ (grid storage)
- Ceramics/glass (5%): Heat-resistant, low-expansion
- Lubricants (3%): Grease (lithium stearate)
- Pharmaceuticals (2%): Bipolar disorder treatment
- Metallurgy (2%): Aluminum-lithium alloys (aerospace)

**Why lithium for batteries?**
- Lightest metal (density 0.53 g/cm³)
- Highest electrochemical potential (-3.04 V)
- **Result**: Highest energy density of any metal (3,860 mAh/g theoretical)

**Geological occurrence**:

**1. Brine deposits** (60% of production):
- **Salars** (salt flats): Arid regions, closed basins
- Evaporation concentrates lithium (+ K, Mg, Na, B)
- **Process**: Pump brine → evaporation ponds (12-18 months) → lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃)
- **Advantages**: Low cost ($4-6k/tonne), low energy
- **Disadvantages**: Slow, water-intensive (500k liters water per tonne Li), arid ecosystems impacted

**Top deposits**:
- **Salar de Atacama, Chile**: 8.6 million tonnes Li reserves (SQM, Albemarle)
  - Driest place on Earth → ideal for solar evaporation
  - Conflict: Flamingo habitat, indigenous water rights
- **Salar de Uyuni, Bolivia**: 21 million tonnes (largest reserve)
  - Nationalized (YLB state company)
  - Underdeveloped (political instability, inexperience, high Mg/Li ratio = harder to process)
- **Salar del Hombre Muerto, Argentina**: 3 million tonnes

**2. Hard rock** (spodumene, LiAlSi₂O₆) (40% of production):
- Pegmatites (very coarse granite, last magma stage)
- Li₂O grades: 1-4%
- **Process**: Mine → crush → roast at 1,000°C → acid leach → lithium hydroxide/carbonate
- **Advantages**: Fast (no evaporation), higher purity, less water
- **Disadvantages**: Energy-intensive, higher cost ($7-10k/tonne)

**Top mines**:
- **Greenbushes, Australia**: 1.4 million tonnes Li reserves, largest hard-rock mine (Talison Lithium = Albemarle + Tianqi)
- **Bikita, Zimbabwe**: 11 million tonnes reserves
- **Pilbara region, Australia**: Multiple projects (Pilgangoora, Wodgina)

**Geopolitical concentration**:

**Reserves** (2023):
- Chile (38%), Australia (26%), Argentina (9%), China (7%), US (4%)
- **Lithium Triangle** (Chile-Argentina-Bolivia): 60% of global reserves

**Production** (2023):
- Australia (52%): Hard-rock dominance
- Chile (25%): Brine dominance
- China (14%): Domestic mines + processing imports
- Argentina (6%): Brine growing

**Processing**:
- China: 60-70% of lithium refining (hydroxide, carbonate)
- China: 80% of battery cell production
- **Strategic vulnerability**: West mines → ships to China → processes → imports batteries

**Price volatility**:
- $17k/tonne (2018) → $75k (2022, EV boom) → $15k (2023, oversupply)
- Boom-bust cycle challenges investment

**Future demand**:
- IEA projection: 40× increase by 2040 (EV adoption)
- **Question**: Enough supply?
  - **USGS**: 98 million tonnes reserves, 2.9 million tonnes/year production (2023)
  - At 2023 rate: 34 years; at 2040 projected rate: <10 years
  - **But**: Resources ~120 million tonnes (additional identified but not economically viable yet), exploration continuing

**Environmental concerns**:
- Salar brine: Water depletion (arid regions), ecosystem disruption
- Hard-rock: Energy use, tailings, land disturbance
- Processing: Chemical waste

**Alternatives**:
- Sodium-ion batteries (Na abundant, cheap, but lower energy density)
- Solid-state batteries (less lithium per kWh)
- Recycling (currently <1% of Li recycled—end-of-life EVs will change this)

### Cobalt

**Uses**:
- **Batteries** (50% of demand): Li-ion cathodes (NMC, NCA chemistries) → EVs, phones
- Superalloys (20%): Jet engines, gas turbines (heat-resistant)
- Catalysts (10%): Petrochemical, pharmaceutical
- Magnets (5%): AlNiCo magnets, hard drives

**Why cobalt in batteries?**
- Stabilizes cathode structure (prevents oxygen loss)
- Enables high energy density + long cycle life
- **Problem**: Expensive, toxic, supply concentrated

**Geological occurrence**:
- **Sedimentary copper-cobalt deposits** (Central African Copperbelt):
  - Stratiform (layered) deposits in Neoproterozoic sedimentary rocks
  - Copper sulfides + cobalt arsenides/sulfides
  - 50-60% of global reserves
- **Nickel laterites**: Tropical weathering concentrates Co (10-20% of production)
- **Magmatic sulfides**: Ni-Cu-Co from mafic/ultramafic intrusions

**Geopolitical concentration (extreme)**:

**Production** (2023):
- **Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC)**: 70% (~120,000 tonnes)
  - Katanga Province (Tenke Fungurume, Mutanda, Sicomines, etc.)
- **Russia**: 5% (Norilsk)
- **Australia**: 4% (nickel laterites)
- **Philippines**: 4% (nickel laterites)
- **Cuba, Madagascar, Canada**: 2-3% each

**Reserves**:
- DRC: 50% (~5 million tonnes)
- Australia: 20%
- Russia, Cuba, Philippines: 5-10% each

**Ethical concerns**:

**Artisanal mining (ASM)**:
- 20-30% of DRC cobalt from artisanal mines
- Estimated 200,000 miners (including children)
- **Conditions**: Hand-dug tunnels, no safety equipment, toxic dust, cave-ins
- **Child labor**: Reports of 40,000+ children (7-17 years old) in mines
- **Wage**: $1-2/day

**Corporate control**:
- Chinese companies control ~80% of DRC industrial cobalt production (CMOC, Zhejiang Huayou, etc.)
- Vertical integration: Mine → refine in China → battery cells

**Blood cobalt**:
- Armed groups extort miners, control routes
- Funds militias, fuels conflict (similar to "blood diamonds")

**Certification efforts**:
- **Responsible Cobalt Initiative**: Apple, Tesla, others pledge ethical sourcing
- **OECD Due Diligence Guidance**: Supply chain transparency
- **Challenges**: Mixing of artisanal + industrial cobalt, difficult to trace

**Cobalt-free alternatives**:
- **LFP (LiFePO₄)**: No cobalt, safer, cheaper, but lower energy density (~20% less range)
  - BYD, Tesla (standard range) now using LFP
- **LMNO (LiMn₁.₅Ni₀.₅O₄)**: Research stage
- **Sodium-ion**: No cobalt, but immature technology

**Future demand vs supply**:
- IEA: 6-8× increase by 2040
- Supply: Enough reserves but capacity constraints, geopolitical risk

### Copper

**"Metal of electrification"**

**Uses**:
- **Electrical** (60%): Wiring, motors, transformers
- **Construction** (20%): Plumbing, roofing
- **Transport** (10%): Wiring, radiators
- **Equipment** (10%): Electronics, machinery

**Why indispensable?**
- Best electrical conductor (except silver)
- Excellent thermal conductor
- Ductile (wiring), corrosion-resistant
- **No substitute** for most electrical applications

**Clean energy copper intensity**:
- **EV**: 83 kg copper (vs 23 kg for ICE vehicle)
- **Onshore wind**: 4,000 kg/MW
- **Offshore wind**: 8,000 kg/MW
- **Solar PV**: 5,000 kg/MW
- **Electrification requires massive copper** (IEA: 2× demand by 2040)

**Geological occurrence**:

**1. Porphyry copper** (60% of production):
- Hydrothermal system above cooling magma intrusion
- Disseminated chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂) in fractured rock
- Large tonnage (billions of tonnes ore), low grade (0.3-1% Cu)
- **Examples**:
  - Escondida, Chile: 5% of global Cu production
  - Chuquicamata, Chile: 100+ years operation
  - Morenci, Arizona: Largest US producer

**2. Sediment-hosted** (20%):
- Copper sulfides in sedimentary rocks
- **Central African Copperbelt** (DRC, Zambia): 10% of global reserves
- Higher grade (2-5% Cu) but smaller deposits

**3. VMS (Volcanogenic Massive Sulfide)** (10%):
- Seafloor hydrothermal deposits
- Cu-Zn-Pb sulfides

**Geopolitical distribution**:

**Reserves** (2023):
- Chile (23%, 190 Mt), Peru (10%, 84 Mt), Australia (10%, 93 Mt), Russia (7%, 62 Mt), Indonesia (5%, 44 Mt), US (5%, 48 Mt), Mexico (5%, 53 Mt)

**Production** (2023):
- Chile (28%, 5.3 Mt), Peru (12%, 2.8 Mt), China (9%, 2.0 Mt), DRC (9%, 2.0 Mt), US (5%, 1.2 Mt), Australia, Russia, Zambia, Canada, Mexico (3-5% each)

**Strategic control**:
- Chile: State-owned Codelco (10% of global production) + private (BHP, Anglo American, Antofagasta)
- China: 40% of global smelting/refining capacity
  - Imports ore → refines → exports products
  - Strategic stockpiling

**Challenges**:

**1. Declining ore grades**:
- Average grade: 3-5% (1900s) → 1% (2000s) → 0.5-0.7% (2020s)
- **Implication**: More rock moved, more energy, more waste per tonne copper
- Escondida: 1.68% (1990) → 0.69% (2023)

**2. Energy intensity**:
- Copper smelting: ~2,000 kWh/tonne (vs aluminum 15,000 kWh/tonne, steel 500 kWh/tonne)
- Lower grades → higher energy per tonne copper

**3. Water scarcity**:
- Chile (Atacama Desert): 50% of mines in water-stressed regions
- Seawater desalination (energy-intensive)

**4. Permitting timescales**:
- New mine: 10-20 years (discovery → permitting → construction)
- Supply inelastic in short-term

**Recycling**:
- 30-35% of supply from recycled copper
- Infinitely recyclable (no quality loss)
- But: Demand growth outpaces recycling supply

## Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)

**Concept**: Government-held emergency oil stockpile.

**US SPR** (largest):
- **Capacity**: 714 million barrels (salt caverns, Gulf Coast)
- **Peak inventory**: 727 million barrels (2009)
- **Current** (2024): ~350 million barrels (after 2022 releases to counter Russian oil supply shock)
- **Purpose**: Supply disruptions (war, embargo, natural disasters)
- **IEA requirement**: Member nations hold 90 days of imports

**Other major reserves**:
- China: 500+ million barrels (strategic + commercial)
- Japan: 490 million barrels (90 days imports)
- India: 40 million barrels (building to 100+ million)

**History**:
- **1975**: Established after 1973 Arab oil embargo
- **Releases**:
  - 1991: Gulf War (17 million barrels)
  - 2005: Hurricane Katrina (11 million barrels)
  - 2011: Libya civil war (30 million barrels, coordinated with IEA)
  - 2022: Russia-Ukraine war (180 million barrels) — largest ever release

**Effectiveness**:
- Moderates price spikes (market psychology as much as physical supply)
- **Refill challenge**: Need low prices to refill without market distortion

## Geopolitical Flashpoints

### South China Sea

**Resources**:
- **Oil**: 11 billion barrels reserves (estimates vary 2-100 billion)
- **Natural gas**: 190 trillion cubic feet
- **Seabed minerals**: REEs, manganese nodules (speculative)

**Territorial disputes**:
- China (nine-dash line claims 80-90% of sea)
- Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, Taiwan (overlapping claims)
- **Spratly Islands**: Reefs, rocks, atolls — militarized by China

**Strategic importance**:
- **Trade**: $3.4 trillion/year passes through (30% of global shipping)
- **Energy security**: China imports 80% of oil via Malacca Strait → vulnerable chokepoint

**Flashpoint risk**: Resource competition + great power rivalry (US-China)

### Arctic

**Why resources accessible?**:
- Sea ice retreat (climate change): Northwest Passage, Northern Sea Route opening
- **USGS estimate** (2008): 13% undiscovered oil (90 billion barrels), 30% undiscovered gas (1,670 trillion cubic feet)
- Mostly offshore (deepwater, harsh)

**Territorial disputes**:
- Russia (claims Lomonosov Ridge extends continental shelf)
- Canada, US (Alaska), Norway, Denmark (Greenland)
- **UNCLOS** (UN Convention on Law of the Sea): Defines continental shelf claims

**Current production**:
- Russia: Arctic oil/gas ~20% of production (Yamal, Sakhalin)
- Norway: Barents Sea fields
- Alaska: Prudhoe Bay (declining)

**Challenges**:
- Extreme conditions (ice, cold, darkness)
- Environmental risk (spills in ice-covered waters near impossible to clean)
- Indigenous communities

**Geopolitical competition**:
- Russia militarizing Arctic (bases, icebreakers, Northern Sea Route control)
- China self-declares "near-Arctic state" (invests in Russian projects)
- NATO concerns

### Rare Earth Processing Monopoly

**Problem**: Even if countries develop mines, China controls processing.

**Example—Lynas (Australia)**:
- Mines Mt. Weld (Western Australia)—one of world's richest REE deposits
- **Cannot refine in Australia** (environmental approvals difficult)
- Ships ore to Malaysia for processing
- Faces local opposition (radioactive waste concerns)

**US vulnerability**:
- Mountain Pass mine (California) produces ore
- **But**: Limited US refining capacity
- Ships to China for processing, re-imports

**Why China dominates processing**:
1. **Environmental tolerance**: Accepts pollution (acid, radioactive waste)
2. **Economies of scale**: Decades of investment, vertical integration
3. **Expertise**: Separation chemistry knowledge, experienced workforce
4. **Cheap labor, energy**

**Diversification efforts**:
- US: Mountain Pass expanding processing (MP Materials)
- Australia: Building refineries (slow, expensive)
- Europe: REE processing R&D (fragmented, small-scale)
- **Challenge**: 10-20 year gap to build competitive processing capacity

## Resource Nationalism

**Definition**: Governments asserting control over natural resources, often expropriating foreign companies.

**Examples**:

**Bolivia (lithium)**:
- 2008: Morales nationalized lithium (YLB state company)
- Expelled foreign companies
- **Result**: Slow development, underdeveloped resources (21 million tonnes, world's largest)
- Changed in 2023: Opened to foreign investment again

**Venezuela (oil)**:
- 2000s: Chávez increased state take, forced foreign companies into minority partnerships
- Expelled ExxonMobil, ConocoPhillips
- **Result**: Underinvestment, production collapsed (3.5M bbl/day → 0.7M)

**Kazakhstan (oil)**:
- 2007: Threatened to revoke Kashagan field license (ENI, ExxonMobil, Shell, Total)
- Forced renegotiation, increased state share

**DRC (cobalt, copper)**:
- 2018: Mining code revision—royalties 2% → 10%, super-profits tax
- Foreign miners contested (Glencore, others)

**Russia (oil, gas)**:
- 2003: Yukos dismantled, Khodorkovsky jailed
- Assets transferred to Rosneft (state-owned)
- Foreign companies (Shell, ExxonMobil) forced into minority positions

**Drivers**:
- **Commodity supercycles**: High prices → governments want larger share
- **Populism**: "National patrimony" rhetoric
- **Fiscal pressure**: Resource revenues fund budgets

**Consequences**:
- **Short-term**: Increased government revenue
- **Long-term**: Reduced investment, production declines, technology stagnation

## Key Terms

- **Petrostate**: Country where oil/gas dominates economy (>50% gov revenue or >25% GDP)
- **Dutch Disease**: Resource exports appreciate currency → other sectors uncompetitive
- **OPEC**: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (cartel coordinating production)
- **Shale oil/gas**: Hydrocarbons in low-permeability shale, extracted via fracking
- **Hydraulic fracturing**: High-pressure fluid fractures rock, releases trapped oil/gas
- **Rare Earth Elements (REEs)**: 17 elements critical for technology, 70% mined in China
- **Lithium Triangle**: Chile-Argentina-Bolivia; 60% of global lithium reserves
- **Blood cobalt**: Cobalt mined under exploitative conditions, funds conflict (esp. DRC)
- **Porphyry copper**: Large, low-grade copper deposits from hydrothermal systems
- **Brine deposits**: Lithium-rich brine in salt flats (salars), evaporated to extract Li
- **Spodumene**: Lithium aluminum silicate (LiAlSi₂O₆), primary hard-rock lithium mineral
- **Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)**: Government oil stockpile for supply disruptions
- **Resource curse**: Resource-rich nations often have slower growth, more corruption/conflict
- **Resource nationalism**: Government expropriation/control of natural resources

## Summary

Natural resource distribution is determined by geology, creating geographic concentration with profound geopolitical consequences. Middle East holds 60%+ of conventional oil due to Jurassic-Cretaceous organic-rich source rocks, porous carbonate reservoirs, thick evaporite seals, and simple trap geometries (Saudi Ghawar: 60-80 billion barrels). US shale revolution (horizontal drilling + fracking) unlocked tight oil/gas, making US net exporter by 2019, weakening OPEC dominance but raising environmental concerns (water use, induced seismicity, methane emissions).

Critical minerals for clean energy concentrate in few countries, creating strategic vulnerabilities. Rare earths: 70% mined in China (Bayan Obo carbonatite), 85-90% processed in China (complex separation technology, accepts toxic waste). Lithium: 60% reserves in "Lithium Triangle" (Chile-Argentina-Bolivia salars); Australia dominates hard-rock production (Greenbushes spodumene), but China controls 60-70% refining. Cobalt: 70% production in DRC (Central African Copperbelt), 20-30% from artisanal mines with child labor, Chinese companies control 80% of industrial mining. Copper: Chile 28% of production (porphyry deposits); ore grades declining (3-5% historically → 0.5-0.7% now) → more energy, waste per tonne.

Resource curse paradox: Resource-rich nations often suffer from Dutch Disease (currency appreciation harms other sectors), rent-seeking corruption, weak institutions, and conflict (blood diamonds, coltan). Counter-examples (Norway, Botswana) show strong governance overcomes curse. Resource nationalism (Venezuela oil expropriation, Bolivia lithium nationalization) provides short-term revenue but causes long-term underinvestment and production collapse.

Strategic reserves (US SPR: 350 million barrels after 2022 releases) buffer supply disruptions. Geopolitical flashpoints: South China Sea (oil/gas disputes, $3.4 trillion/year trade), Arctic (USGS: 13% undiscovered oil, 30% gas; Russia militarizing), China's REE processing monopoly (10-20 year gap for competitors). Clean energy transition amplifies mineral geopolitics—IEA projects 40× lithium demand, 6-8× cobalt demand by 2040—but reserves, processing capacity, and ethical sourcing constraints create bottlenecks. Geology shapes which nations control resources essential for decarbonization, military technology, and economic growth—geological fate determines geopolitical power.
