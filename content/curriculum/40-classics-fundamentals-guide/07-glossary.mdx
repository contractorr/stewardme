# Glossary

## Core terms

**Classics**  
The study of the ancient Mediterranean world, especially Greece and Rome, through literature, history, language, philosophy, religion, and material culture.

**Classical**  
Used broadly for the Greek and Roman world, especially its influential texts, ideas, and artistic forms.

**Epic**  
A long, elevated narrative poem focused on heroic action and collective memory.

**Myth**  
A traditional story that expresses shared beliefs, identities, origins, or moral tensions.

**Tragedy**  
A dramatic form centred on conflict, suffering, decision, and consequences.

**Comedy**  
A dramatic form that often uses satire, reversal, and social observation.

**Polis**  
The Greek city-state, combining political community, territory, and civic identity.

**Hellenistic**  
The period after Alexander in which Greek language and culture spread widely across a larger multicultural world.

**Republic**  
A political order in which public affairs are treated as belonging to the community rather than a single monarch, though in practice republics can still be unequal and oligarchic.

**Empire**  
A large political order ruling multiple peoples or territories from a dominant centre.

**Latin**  
The language of ancient Rome and the ancestor of the Romance languages.

**Rhetoric**  
The art of persuasion in speech and writing.

**Civic virtue**  
The ideal that citizens should place public duty and the common good above narrow self-interest.

**Canon**  
A group of texts treated as especially authoritative, influential, or essential.

**Reception**  
The process by which later societies interpret, adapt, praise, criticise, or repurpose older texts and ideas.
