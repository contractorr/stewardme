# Glossary of Key Terms

## Overview

This glossary provides quick reference definitions for key terms used throughout the history of science study guide. Terms are organized alphabetically with cross-references where relevant. Page numbers refer to the file where the term is most extensively discussed.

## A

**Alchemy**
Medieval/early modern practice combining chemistry, mysticism, and philosophy. Sought transmutation of base metals to gold and the philosopher's stone. Developed many laboratory techniques (distillation, crystallization) used in later chemistry. Practiced by many early scientists including Newton. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Anomaly**
In Kuhn's theory, an observation or experimental result that doesn't fit the predictions of the current paradigm. Anomalies may be ignored initially but their accumulation can trigger scientific crisis. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

**Antimatter**
Matter composed of antiparticles with opposite properties to normal matter (e.g., positron vs. electron). Predicted by Dirac (1928), first detected in 1932. When matter meets antimatter, both annihilate producing energy. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Astrolabe**
Medieval astronomical instrument used to measure the altitude of celestial bodies. Improved versions developed in Islamic world. Essential for navigation and astronomy before telescopes. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Atomism**
Ancient Greek philosophical position (Democritus, Leucippus) that matter consists of indivisible atoms moving in void. Rejected by Aristotle. Revived in 17th century (Gassendi) and became foundation of modern chemistry (Dalton). → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md, 03-scientific-revolution.md, 05-19th-century.md

**Axiomatic Method**
Mathematical approach starting with basic postulates/axioms and deriving conclusions through logical deduction. Developed by Euclid in *Elements*. Model for scientific reasoning. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

## B

**Binomial Nomenclature**
Linnaeus's two-name system for classifying species: Genus species (e.g., *Homo sapiens*). Became international standard. Still used today. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Blackbody Radiation**
Electromagnetic radiation emitted by heated objects. Classical physics predicted "ultraviolet catastrophe" (infinite energy). Planck solved problem (1900) by introducing energy quanta, launching quantum revolution. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

## C

**Conservation of Energy (First Law of Thermodynamics)**
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. Established 1840s by Joule, Mayer, Helmholtz. Unified different phenomena (heat, work, electricity). → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Conservation of Mass**
In chemical reactions, total mass of reactants equals total mass of products. Established by Lavoisier through careful weighing. Foundation for balanced chemical equations. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Copernican Revolution**
Shift from geocentric (Earth-centered) to heliocentric (Sun-centered) model of cosmos. Initiated by Copernicus (1543), confirmed by Kepler's laws and Galileo's observations, explained by Newton's physics. Took 150+ years. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Corpuscular Theory**
View that matter consists of small particles (corpuscles). Held by Boyle, Newton, and many mechanical philosophers. Opposed to Aristotelian continuous matter. Foundation for modern atomic theory. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

## D

**Deep Time**
Concept that Earth is extremely old (millions or billions of years), not thousands as biblical chronology suggested. Developed by geologists like Hutton. Essential for evolutionary theory. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Deduction**
Reasoning from general principles to specific conclusions. Associated with Descartes and rationalism. Contrasts with induction. Actual science uses both. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Determinism**
View that if initial conditions are known, future can be predicted with certainty. Laplace's vision of classical mechanics. Challenged by quantum mechanics. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md, 06-modern-physics.md

## E

**Electromagnetic Induction**
Moving magnet generates electric current in conductor. Discovered by Faraday (1831). Basis for electric generators and transformers. Crucial for electrical power industry. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Empiricism**
Philosophical position that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience. Associated with Bacon and British tradition (Locke, Hume). Emphasizes observation and experiment over pure reason. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Enlightenment**
18th-century intellectual movement emphasizing reason, progress, and natural law. Science became part of broader culture. Public demonstrations, encyclopedias, salons. Influenced American and French Revolutions. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Entropy**
Measure of disorder or unavailability of energy for work. Second Law of Thermodynamics: entropy always increases in isolated systems. Introduced by Clausius. Statistical interpretation by Boltzmann (S = k log W). → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Epicycle**
In Ptolemaic astronomy, circle whose center moves on another circle (deferent). Used to explain retrograde motion of planets in geocentric model. Copernican model initially claimed to eliminate epicycles but still used some. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md, 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Evolution**
Change in species over time. Darwin and Wallace proposed natural selection as mechanism. Faced resistance but gradually accepted. Revolutionized biology. Mechanism clarified by genetics in 20th century. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Externalism**
Approach to history of science emphasizing social, economic, political, and cultural context. Explains why certain questions asked and who funded research. Complements internalism. → See: 01-introduction.md

## F

**Field**
Region of space with force properties. Concept developed by Faraday for electromagnetism (lines of force). Contrasted with action-at-distance. Maxwell formalized mathematically. Essential for modern physics. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Fission**
Splitting of heavy atomic nucleus (e.g., uranium) into lighter nuclei, releasing enormous energy (E = mc²). Discovered 1938 (Hahn, Strassmann, Meitner, Frisch). Basis for nuclear reactors and atomic bombs. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Four Elements**
Aristotelian theory: all terrestrial matter composed of earth, water, air, fire. Each has natural place. Dominated until chemical revolution. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Four Humors**
Galenic medical theory: four bodily fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile) determine health and temperament. Imbalance causes disease. Dominated medicine until 19th century. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Fusion**
Combining light atomic nuclei (e.g., hydrogen) into heavier ones, releasing energy. Powers stars. Achieved in hydrogen bombs. Controlled fusion for energy generation remains challenging. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

## G

**General Relativity**
Einstein's theory (1915) describing gravity as curvature of spacetime caused by mass-energy. Explained Mercury's orbit, predicted light bending and gravitational waves. Foundation for modern cosmology. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Geocentric Model**
Earth-centered model of cosmos. Aristotelian cosmology adopted by Ptolemy. Dominated until Copernican revolution. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md, 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Germ Theory**
Theory that microorganisms cause infectious diseases. Developed by Pasteur and Koch in 19th century. Revolutionized medicine, enabling antiseptic surgery, vaccination, and public health measures. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Gestalt Switch**
Kuhn's metaphor for scientific revolution: sudden perceptual reorganization where same phenomena seen differently under new paradigm. Like visual illusions that flip between interpretations. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

## H

**Heliocentric**
Sun-centered model of cosmos. Proposed by Copernicus (1543), refined by Kepler (ellipses), supported by Galileo (observations), explained by Newton (gravitation). → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Humanism**
Renaissance intellectual movement emphasizing classical learning, human potential, and critical textual analysis. Contributed to scientific revolution by recovering Greek texts and promoting independent inquiry. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

## I

**Incommensurability**
Kuhn's controversial claim that successive paradigms cannot be directly compared because they employ different concepts and standards. Terms change meaning across revolutions. Raises questions about scientific progress and rationality. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

**Induction**
Reasoning from particular observations to general principles. Associated with Bacon and empiricism. Challenges: problem of induction (Hume), how many observations sufficient? → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Inertia**
Newton's First Law: object continues in uniform motion unless acted upon by force. Opposed to Aristotelian physics where force needed to maintain motion. Foundation of classical mechanics. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Internalism**
Approach to history of science emphasizing intellectual development of ideas—theories, experiments, logical progression. Focuses on scientific content. Complements externalism. → See: 01-introduction.md

## K

**Kinetic Theory**
Theory that gas properties result from motion of molecules. Developed by Maxwell and Boltzmann (1860s-1870s). Statistical approach to large numbers of particles. Temperature as average kinetic energy. → See: 05-19th-century.md

## L

**Leyden Jar**
Early capacitor (1745) that stores electrical charge. Enabled electrical experiments. Named after Leiden, Netherlands. Public demonstrations made electricity popular. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

## M

**Mechanical Philosophy**
17th-century worldview: nature operates like machine. Matter is inert particles in motion. Quantitative properties only (size, shape, motion). No purposes or qualities. Replaced Aristotelian physics. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

## N

**Natural History**
Descriptive study of plants, animals, and minerals. Dominant in 18th century with Linnaeus, Buffon, exploratory expeditions. Classification and collection emphasized. Later overshadowed by experimental laboratory sciences. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Natural Philosophy**
Pre-modern term for study of nature, roughly equivalent to science. Used through 18th century. "Scientist" coined 1833 (Whewell). → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Natural Selection**
Darwin and Wallace's mechanism for evolution. Variations that increase survival and reproduction become more common over generations. Acts like artificial selection by breeders. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Normal Science**
Kuhn's term for research within established paradigm. Puzzle-solving activity that extends and refines paradigm without questioning fundamentals. Most science, most of the time. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

## O

**Oxygen Theory**
Lavoisier's theory that combustion involves combination with oxygen from air, not release of phlogiston. Explained weight gain in burning. Revolutionized chemistry with new nomenclature and quantitative methods. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

## P

**Paradigm**
Kuhn's influential concept: shared framework of theories, methods, standards, and exemplary problems within which scientists work. Provides worldview and research agenda. Paradigm shifts are scientific revolutions. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

**Periodic Table**
Mendeleev's arrangement (1869) of chemical elements by atomic weight, showing periodic patterns in properties. Left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties. Explained later by atomic structure. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Phlogiston**
Hypothetical substance supposedly released during combustion. Explained many phenomena but couldn't account for weight gain. Overthrown by Lavoisier's oxygen theory (1780s). Classic example of discarded scientific theory. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Photoelectric Effect**
Light hitting metal surface ejects electrons. Classical wave theory couldn't explain frequency dependence. Einstein (1905) explained with photons (light quanta). Won him Nobel Prize. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Presentism**
Judging past by present standards. Generally avoided in history of science because it misses how revolutionary ideas were in original context. Opposite: historicism (understanding past on own terms). → See: 01-introduction.md

**Primary Source**
Original documents from period studied: scientific papers, lab notebooks, correspondence, instruments. Contrasts with secondary sources (later analyses). Essential for historical research. → See: 01-introduction.md

## Q

**Quantum**
Discrete, indivisible amount (of energy, angular momentum, etc.). Planck introduced energy quanta (1900). Fundamental to quantum mechanics. Challenged classical physics' assumption of continuity. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

## R

**Rationalism**
Philosophical position emphasizing reason as primary source of knowledge. Associated with Descartes and Continental tradition. Mathematical certainty as model. Contrasts with empiricism. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Research Programme**
Lakatos's concept: scientific theory with hard core (protected assumptions) and protective belt (auxiliary hypotheses adjusted to protect core). Progressive programmes solve problems; degenerating ones accumulate ad hoc modifications. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

## S

**Scholasticism**
Medieval method of learning emphasizing logical analysis, disputation, and reconciling authorities (Aristotle, Bible, Church Fathers). Taught in universities. Formal structure influenced later scientific debate. → See: 02-ancient-medieval.md

**Scientific Revolution**
Transformation of European natural philosophy (roughly 1543-1687). Heliocentric astronomy, experimental method, mechanical philosophy, mathematical laws. Key figures: Copernicus, Galileo, Newton. Whether single revolution or multiple transformations debated. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

**Social Constructivism**
View that scientific knowledge is socially constructed, shaped by interests, conventions, and power. Strong versions claim social factors determine belief, not nature. Controversial; Kuhn rejected strong relativism. → See: 07-paradigm-shifts.md

**Spacetime**
Four-dimensional continuum combining three space dimensions and one time dimension. Introduced by Minkowski formalizing Einstein's special relativity. In general relativity, gravity is spacetime curvature. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Special Relativity**
Einstein's theory (1905) of space and time for constant velocities. Two postulates: laws of physics same in all inertial frames, light speed constant for all observers. Consequences: time dilation, length contraction, E = mc². → See: 06-modern-physics.md

## T

**Thermodynamics**
Science of heat, work, and energy. First Law: energy conservation. Second Law: entropy increases. Developed mid-19th century (Carnot, Joule, Clausius, Kelvin). Foundation for engineering. → See: 05-19th-century.md

## U

**Uncertainty Principle**
Heisenberg's principle (1927): cannot simultaneously know position and momentum precisely (Δx Δp ≥ ℏ/2). Fundamental limit of nature, not measurement technology. Similar relations for energy-time. Challenged determinism. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Uniformitarianism**
Hutton's geological principle: present processes (erosion, sedimentation) explain past. "Present is key to past." Implied vast age for Earth ("deep time"). Contrasted with catastrophism. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

**Universal Gravitation**
Newton's law: every mass attracts every other mass. Force proportional to product of masses, inversely proportional to square of distance (F = Gm₁m₂/r²). Unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics. → See: 03-scientific-revolution.md

## V

**Vitalism**
Belief that living organisms contain special "vital force" distinguishing them from non-living matter. Challenged by Wöhler's synthesis of urea from inorganic compounds (1828). Gradually abandoned as biochemistry explained life processes mechanistically. → See: 05-19th-century.md

**Voltaic Pile**
First battery, invented by Volta (1800). Stack of alternating zinc and copper discs with brine-soaked cloth. Produced continuous electric current (not just static discharge). Enabled electrochemistry experiments. → See: 04-enlightenment-science.md

## W

**Wave-Particle Duality**
Quantum mechanical principle: objects exhibit both wave and particle properties depending on experimental context. Light shows interference (wave) and photoelectric effect (particle). Electrons show diffraction (wave) and discrete impacts (particle). → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Wavefunction (Ψ)**
Mathematical description of quantum state in Schrödinger's wave mechanics. |Ψ|² gives probability density for finding particle at location. Measurement "collapses" wavefunction to definite value. Central to quantum mechanics. → See: 06-modern-physics.md

**Whig History**
Narrating history as inevitable progress toward present state. Portrays past as leading necessarily to current knowledge. Generally avoided in history of science because it distorts past and misses contingency. → See: 01-introduction.md

## Summary

This glossary covers key concepts, theories, methods, and terms from ancient Greek science through modern physics and philosophy of science. Terms are drawn from all periods: ancient and medieval foundations, scientific revolution transformations, Enlightenment expansion, 19th-century professionalization, 20th-century relativity and quantum mechanics, and meta-level analysis of scientific change.

Understanding these terms enables deeper engagement with history of science texts and facilitates connections across periods and disciplines. Many terms have evolved in meaning (e.g., "atom" from indivisible philosophical concept to divisible physical entity), illustrating how scientific language changes with paradigms.

The cross-references organize terms thematically, helping identify related concepts and trace development of ideas across files. This glossary serves as quick reference during study and review tool before examinations.
