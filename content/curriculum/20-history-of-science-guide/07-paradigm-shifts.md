# Paradigm Shifts and Scientific Change

## Overview

How does science change? Is it gradual accumulation or revolutionary upheaval? Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) transformed understanding of scientific progress by arguing that science alternates between "normal science" within paradigms and "revolutionary science" that replaces paradigms. Though controversial and refined over time, Kuhn's framework remains influential for analyzing major transitions like Copernican astronomy, Darwinian evolution, and quantum mechanics.

This section examines:
- Kuhn's paradigm theory
- Criticisms and refinements
- Case studies of scientific revolutions
- Alternative models of scientific change
- Implications for understanding science

## Thomas Kuhn's Theory

### Background: *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962)

**Thomas Kuhn (1922-1996)**:
- Physicist turned historian of science
- Studied historical cases deeply
- Challenged positivist philosophy of science
- Book became one of most cited academic works ever

### Key Concepts

**Paradigm**:
- Shared examples, theories, methods, standards
- Framework within which scientists work
- Provides:
  - Exemplary problems and solutions
  - Acceptable methods and equipment
  - Standards for what counts as explanation
  - Worldview or "disciplinary matrix"

**Normal Science**:
- Research within established paradigm
- "Puzzle-solving" activity
- Extending and refining paradigm
- Not testing paradigm itself
- Most science, most of the time

**Anomalies**:
- Observations that don't fit paradigm
- Initially ignored, dismissed, or modified
- Accumulation increases tension
- Not immediately revolutionary

**Crisis**:
- Anomalies multiply and resist resolution
- Confidence in paradigm erodes
- Extraordinary research begins
- Alternatives considered
- Discipline in turmoil

**Revolution**:
- New paradigm replaces old
- Not purely rational process
- "Gestalt switch" in perception
- Young scientists more open to change
- Old paradigm's practitioners may never convert

**New Normal Science**:
- New paradigm guides research
- New puzzles, methods, standards
- Cycle repeats

### Stages of Scientific Development

```
Pre-paradigm → Normal Science → Crisis → Revolution → New Normal Science → Crisis → ...
```

| Stage | Characteristics | Example |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| Pre-paradigm | Multiple competing schools, no consensus | Electricity studies before Franklin |
| Normal Science | Single paradigm dominates, puzzle-solving | Newtonian physics 18th-19th century |
| Crisis | Anomalies accumulate, confidence erodes | Physics 1890s-1900s (blackbody, etc.) |
| Revolution | Paradigm overthrow | Quantum mechanics emerges |
| New Normal Science | Consensus restored with new paradigm | Quantum physics 1930s onward |

### Incommensurability

**Controversial Claim**:
- Successive paradigms incommensurable (can't be compared directly)
- Different worlds: scientists before/after revolution "live in different worlds"
- Terms change meaning across paradigms
- No neutral observation language
- No purely rational choice between paradigms

**Implications**:
- Scientific progress not straightforward
- Can't say later theories simply "truer"
- Relativism concern (is science arbitrary?)
- Kuhn later moderated claims

### Scientific Progress

**Not Linear Accumulation**:
- Textbooks create false history of steady progress
- Revolutions erase previous frameworks
- Discontinuous change

**But Still Progress**:
- Each paradigm solves more puzzles
- Better predictions and control
- Not converging on "Truth" but improving problem-solving
- Evolutionary model: adaptation, not progress toward goal

## Criticisms and Refinements

### Major Criticisms

**Paradigm Vagueness**:
- Kuhn used "paradigm" in multiple senses (counted 21+ meanings)
- Later distinguished "disciplinary matrix" and "exemplar"
- Term became buzzword, overused

**Incommensurability Exaggerated**:
- Scientists do communicate across revolutions
- Translation possible, if imperfect
- Continuities exist (mathematics, instruments, some methods)

**Rationality Concern**:
- If paradigm choice not purely rational, is science irrational?
- Sociologists of science pushed relativism further
- Kuhn defended: multiple rational considerations exist

**Normal Science Overstated**:
- Not all science is puzzle-solving
- Exploratory science exists
- Multiple paradigms sometimes coexist

**Revolutions Not Uniform**:
- Some changes gradual
- Some fields lack clear paradigms
- Chemistry, biology less revolutionary than physics

### Refinements by Kuhn and Others

**Kuhn's Later Work**:
- Emphasized local lexicons (specialized vocabularies)
- Partial communication possible
- Incommensurability doesn't mean incomparability

**Lakatos's Research Programmes**:
- Imre Lakatos: scientific research programmes have:
  - Hard core: protected assumptions
  - Protective belt: auxiliary hypotheses adjusted
  - Progressive vs. degenerating programmes
- More continuous than Kuhn's revolutions

**Laudan's Research Traditions**:
- Larry Laudan: emphasis on problem-solving
- Traditions evolve gradually
- Multiple traditions can coexist

## Case Studies of Scientific Revolutions

### Case 1: Copernican Revolution

**Old Paradigm: Ptolemaic Geocentrism**:
- Earth at center
- Complex epicycles
- Accurate predictions
- Aligned with Aristotelian physics
- Supported by common sense and Scripture

**Anomalies**:
- Increasing epicycle complexity
- Calendar drift issues
- Conceptual inelegance (but not empirical crisis)

**New Paradigm: Copernican Heliocentrism**:
- Sun at center
- Simpler geometry (initially claimed)
- No more accurate initially
- Required new physics (eventually Newton)

**Revolution Characteristics**:
- Extended over 150+ years (not sudden)
- Kepler's ellipses crucial refinement
- Galileo's observations provided evidence
- Newton's physics completed paradigm
- Resistance from religious and scientific authorities

**Incommensurability**:
- "Earth moves" meaningless in geocentric paradigm
- "Heavy objects fall to Earth" vs. "to center of universe"
- Different criteria: mathematical elegance vs. physical realism

### Case 2: Chemical Revolution

**Old Paradigm: Phlogiston Theory**:
- Combustible substances contain phlogiston
- Burning releases phlogiston
- Explained many phenomena
- Active research program

**Anomalies**:
- Metals gain weight when burned (how if losing phlogiston?)
- Ad hoc adjustments (negative weight phlogiston?)
- Gas discoveries complicated picture

**New Paradigm: Lavoisier's Oxygen Theory**:
- Combustion adds oxygen
- Weight gain explained
- Conservation of mass
- New nomenclature

**Revolution Characteristics**:
- Rapid conversion (1780s-1790s)
- New experimental standards (quantitative weighing)
- Renamed substances (completely new vocabulary)
- Older chemists often didn't convert (Priestley remained phlogistonist)

**Incommensurability**:
- "Dephlogisticated air" (Priestley) vs. "oxygen" (Lavoisier)
- Same substance, different theoretical meaning
- Different research questions asked

### Case 3: Darwinian Revolution

**Old Paradigm: Fixed Species**:
- Species created separately
- Designed for purposes
- Fixity over time
- Natural theology framework

**Anomalies**:
- Fossils of extinct species
- Geographical distribution patterns
- Vestigial organs
- Artificial selection's power

**New Paradigm: Evolution by Natural Selection**:
- Species change over time
- Common descent
- Natural selection mechanism
- No purpose or design necessary

**Revolution Characteristics**:
- Gradual acceptance (decades)
- Natural selection doubted even by evolutionists (until genetics)
- Different reception in different fields
- Religious/philosophical resistance strong

**Incommensurability**:
- "Function" changes meaning (design purpose → selected effect)
- "Species" from fixed to arbitrary category
- Questions about origins shift dramatically

### Case 4: Quantum Revolution

**Old Paradigm: Classical Mechanics**:
- Deterministic
- Continuous change
- Wave or particle (not both)
- No measurement limits

**Anomalies**:
- Blackbody radiation
- Photoelectric effect
- Atomic spectra
- Stability of atoms

**New Paradigm: Quantum Mechanics**:
- Probabilistic
- Quantized
- Wave-particle duality
- Uncertainty principle

**Revolution Characteristics**:
- Rapid development (1900-1930)
- Older physicists resisted (Einstein, Schrödinger uncomfortable)
- Younger generation led
- Conceptually radical but mathematically continuous

**Incommensurability**:
- "Position" and "momentum" no longer simultaneously definite
- "Causality" and "determinism" redefined
- Observation changes system

## Alternative Models of Scientific Change

### Evolutionary Models

**Toulmin and Hull**:
- Science evolves like biological evolution
- Variation (new ideas)
- Selection (testing, acceptance)
- Retention (textbooks, training)
- Gradual change predominates

**Advantages**:
- Less dramatic than Kuhn
- Explains continuity
- No need for revolutions

**Limitations**:
- Underplays radical conceptual shifts
- Selection criteria unclear
- Purpose and design in science (unlike evolution)

### Social Constructivism

**Strong Programme (Edinburgh School)**:
- Science socially constructed
- Beliefs explained by social factors, not nature
- Symmetry principle: true and false beliefs explained same way

**Relativism**:
- No privileged access to reality
- Scientific knowledge contingent
- Controversy over whether undermines science's authority

**Kuhn's Response**:
- Rejected strong relativism
- Science constrained by nature
- Social factors important but not everything

### Feminist Critiques

**Sandra Harding, Evelyn Fox Keller, others**:
- Gender shapes what gets studied and how
- Masculine values in science (objectivity, domination of nature)
- Alternative approaches possible
- Standpoint epistemology: marginalized perspectives reveal hidden assumptions

**Applications**:
- Primatology: male vs. female researchers saw different behaviors
- Medicine: male bodies as default
- Which research funded

### Postmodern Critiques

**Challenge Grand Narratives**:
- Doubt progress stories
- Skeptical of universal method
- Power/knowledge connection
- Science as discourse

**Science Wars (1990s)**:
- Conflict between scientists and postmodernists
- Sokal hoax (1996): physicist published nonsense in cultural studies journal
- Debate over nature of scientific knowledge

## Implications and Applications

### For Scientists

**Awareness of Assumptions**:
- Recognize paradigmatic commitments
- Anomalies may be significant
- Revolutionary ideas possible (if rare)

**Normal Science Value**:
- Most work is refining, not overthrowing
- Puzzle-solving is productive
- Deep knowledge within framework necessary

**Resistance Can Be Rational**:
- New theories immature initially
- Established paradigm has proven value
- Not all anomalies lead to revolutions

### For Science Education

**Textbook Problem**:
- Present sanitized history
- Hide controversies and false starts
- Create impression of inevitable progress
- Kuhn: misleads about nature of science

**Alternative**:
- Teach historical cases with genuine uncertainty
- Show how questions change
- Convey science as human activity
- Multiple valid approaches

### For Science Policy

**Funding Implications**:
- Revolutionary ideas hard to evaluate by normal standards
- Risk assessment for paradigm-challenging research
- Balance normal and revolutionary science

**Expert Disagreement**:
- Genuine scientific controversies exist
- Not always resolvable by "just looking at data"
- Paradigms shape interpretation

### For Understanding Current Science

**Climate Change**:
- Paradigm of anthropogenic warming
- "Skeptics" often outside paradigm
- Normal science: refining models
- Policy debates distinct from scientific consensus

**Evolution and Creation**:
- Evolution is paradigm in biology
- Creationism/Intelligent Design outside scientific paradigm
- Not genuine scientific controversy

**Paradigm Shifts Claimed**:
- String theory?
- Consciousness studies?
- Origin of life research?
- Debates about whether genuine revolutions or normal science

## Comparison: Models of Scientific Change

| Model | Key Idea | Change Pattern | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-------|----------|----------------|-----------|------------|
| Kuhn's Paradigms | Normal science + revolutions | Punctuated equilibrium | Explains radical shifts | Vague concepts |
| Evolutionary | Variation and selection | Gradual | Continuity explained | Underplays revolutions |
| Social Constructivism | Social factors determine beliefs | Contingent | Reveals social influences | Can deny nature's role |
| Cumulative Progress | Steady accumulation | Linear | Simple, intuitive | Ignores conceptual shifts |
| Research Programmes | Hard core + protective belt | Progressive/degenerating | More flexible than Kuhn | Complex to apply |

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Plate Tectonics

**Old Paradigm**: Continents fixed, mountains from cooling/contraction

**Wegener's Continental Drift (1912)**:
- Rejected initially
- No mechanism
- Ridiculed by establishment

**New Evidence (1950s-60s)**:
- Seafloor spreading
- Magnetic stripes
- Deep-sea mapping

**Rapid Acceptance**:
- Plate tectonics paradigm by 1970s
- Explains earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building
- Classic revolution

### Example 2: Ulcers and Bacteria

**Old Paradigm**: Ulcers from stress, spicy food, acid

**Marshall and Warren (1980s)**:
- Bacteria (H. pylori) cause ulcers
- Initially dismissed
- Marshall drank bacteria culture to prove it

**Paradigm Shift**:
- Antibiotics cure ulcers
- Nobel Prize 2005
- Small-scale revolution in gastroenterology

### Example 3: Epigenetics

**Current Status**: Potential paradigm shift?

**Old View**: Genes fixed, environment irrelevant to inheritance

**Epigenetics**: Environmental factors affect gene expression, some inherited

**Revolution or Refinement?**:
- Debate ongoing
- Extends molecular biology or replaces it?
- Example of science in flux

## Key Terms

- **Paradigm**: Shared framework of theories, methods, standards, and exemplars
- **Normal Science**: Puzzle-solving research within paradigm
- **Anomaly**: Observation inconsistent with paradigm
- **Crisis**: Period when anomalies undermine confidence in paradigm
- **Scientific Revolution**: Replacement of one paradigm with another
- **Incommensurability**: Inability to directly compare successive paradigms
- **Gestalt Switch**: Sudden perceptual reorganization (Kuhn's metaphor for revolution)
- **Research Programme**: Lakatos's alternative: theory with hard core and protective belt
- **Social Constructivism**: View that scientific knowledge is socially constructed

## Summary

Thomas Kuhn's *Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) transformed understanding of how science changes. Rather than steady accumulation, Kuhn described alternation between normal science (puzzle-solving within paradigms) and revolutionary science (paradigm replacement). Paradigms provide shared frameworks but also constrain thinking. Anomalies accumulate, trigger crises, and occasionally lead to revolutions where fundamental assumptions change.

Kuhn's controversial concept of incommensurability—that successive paradigms can't be directly compared—raised questions about rationality and progress. Critics found paradigm vague and incommensurability exaggerated. Refinements by Lakatos, Laudan, and Kuhn himself addressed concerns while preserving insight that major conceptual shifts occur.

Case studies illustrate paradigm shifts: Copernican revolution overthrew geocentrism, Lavoisier's oxygen theory replaced phlogiston, Darwin's evolution challenged fixed species, and quantum mechanics superseded classical determinism. Each showed resistance from established scientists, younger generation's openness, and fundamental reconceptualization.

Alternative models—evolutionary, social constructivist, feminist—complement or challenge Kuhn, revealing multiple factors in scientific change. No single model captures all aspects. Understanding scientific change helps interpret current controversies, education, and policy decisions.

Kuhn's legacy: science is historical, human activity embedded in communities, not algorithmic method converging on Truth. Yet it progresses by solving more problems with greater precision. This complex view, neither pure rationalism nor pure relativism, better captures science's actual nature than earlier philosophical accounts.
