# 19th Century Science

## Overview

The nineteenth century witnessed extraordinary scientific expansion across all fields. Professionalization accelerated with dedicated careers, specialized training, and disciplinary boundaries. Industrial revolution both drove and was driven by scientific advances. Major theoretical frameworks emerged: evolution, conservation of energy, atomic theory, electromagnetism, and thermodynamics.

Key transformations:
- Biology: Evolution by natural selection
- Physics: Energy conservation, thermodynamics, field theory
- Chemistry: Atomic theory, periodic table, organic chemistry
- Medicine: Germ theory, antiseptics, anesthesia
- Institutions: Research universities, government science, professional societies

Science became essential to industry, medicine, and state power. By century's end, laboratory research and mathematical theory dominated over natural history and observation.

## Evolution and Biology

### Pre-Darwinian Evolution Ideas

**Jean-Baptiste Lamarck (1744-1829)**:
- Proposed species change over time (transformism)
- Mechanism: inheritance of acquired characteristics
  - Giraffe stretches neck to reach leaves
  - Slightly longer neck passed to offspring
  - Gradual lengthening over generations
- Use and disuse principle
- Progressive ladder of life
- Rejected by most contemporaries
- Wrong mechanism but right that species change

**Georges Cuvier (1769-1832)**:
- Opposed evolution
- Founded paleontology
- Catastrophism: Earth shaped by sudden catastrophes
- Extinction proven through fossil record
- Comparative anatomy: structure reveals function
- Fossils show progression but explained by successive creations

### Charles Darwin (1809-1882)

**Background**:
- HMS Beagle voyage (1831-1836): naturalist on surveying expedition
- Galápagos Islands finches and tortoises showed variation
- Read Malthus on population (struggle for existence)
- Developed theory 1830s-1840s, delayed publication

**On the Origin of Species (1859)**:

**Core Argument**:
1. **Variation**: Individuals within species vary
2. **Heritability**: Offspring resemble parents
3. **Overproduction**: More offspring than can survive
4. **Struggle for Existence**: Competition for limited resources
5. **Differential Survival**: Some variants survive and reproduce better
6. **Natural Selection**: Advantageous traits accumulate over generations

**Key Concepts**:
- **Natural Selection**: Nature "selects" beneficial variations (like breeders select in artificial selection)
- **Common Descent**: All life shares common ancestors
- **Gradualism**: Change occurs slowly over vast time
- **Adaptation**: Organisms suited to environments through selection

**Evidence**:
- Artificial selection (dog breeds, pigeons)
- Biogeography (island species resemble mainland)
- Comparative anatomy (homologous structures)
- Embryology (similar development patterns)
- Fossil record (progressive forms)
- Vestigial organs (remnants of ancestral features)

**Reception**:
- Immediate controversy and sensation
- Scientific community gradually accepted (especially younger scientists)
- Religious objections: humans descended from animals
- Social objections: undermines moral order
- Scientific objections: no mechanism for heredity (resolved later)

**The Descent of Man (1871)**:
- Applied evolution to humans
- Sexual selection: competition for mates
- More controversial than Origin

### Alfred Russel Wallace (1823-1913)

- Independently developed natural selection theory
- Sent manuscript to Darwin (1858)
- Joint announcement prompted Darwin to publish
- Focused on biogeography
- Less credit than Darwin (published less, deferred to Darwin)

### Post-Darwin Developments

**"Eclipse of Darwinism" (late 19th century)**:
- Natural selection doubted by many
- Alternative mechanisms proposed (Lamarckism, orthogenesis, saltationism)
- Heredity mechanism unknown
- Resolved in early 20th century with genetics

**Social Darwinism**:
- Applied "survival of fittest" to human society (term from Herbert Spencer, not Darwin)
- Justified imperialism, capitalism, eugenics
- Misused evolution for social/political purposes
- Darwin distanced himself from extreme applications

### Genetics Origins (Late 19th Century)

**Gregor Mendel (1822-1884)**:
- Augustinian monk in Moravia
- Pea plant experiments (1856-1863)
- Discovered laws of inheritance:
  - Segregation: traits don't blend, they separate
  - Independent assortment: different traits inherited independently
  - Dominant and recessive factors
- Published 1866, ignored until 1900
- Provided heredity mechanism Darwin lacked

## Physics: Energy and Fields

### Thermodynamics

**Conservation of Energy (First Law)**:

Multiple independent discoveries (1840s):
- James Joule (1818-1889): mechanical work converts to heat quantitatively
- Julius von Mayer (1814-1878): physiological and physical processes
- Hermann von Helmholtz (1821-1894): general principle across all phenomena

**Statement**: Energy neither created nor destroyed, only transformed

**Impact**:
- Unified different phenomena (heat, work, electricity, light)
- Ended perpetual motion machine dreams
- Foundation for industrial engineering

**Second Law of Thermodynamics**:

**Carnot's Engine (1824)**:
- Sadi Carnot analyzed ideal heat engine
- Efficiency depends on temperature difference
- Maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine

**Entropy (1850s)**:
- Rudolf Clausius: heat flows from hot to cold spontaneously
- Entropy: measure of disorder/unavailability of energy
- Entropy always increases in isolated systems
- "Heat death" of universe predicted

**Kelvin's Formulation**:
- William Thomson (Lord Kelvin): impossible to extract all heat as work
- Absolute zero temperature scale

**Impact**:
- Limits on efficiency (engines, refrigerators)
- Direction of time (entropy increases)
- Statistical interpretation later (Boltzmann)

### Electromagnetism

**Hans Christian Ørsted (1777-1851)**:
- Discovered (1820): electric current deflects compass needle
- Electricity and magnetism connected

**André-Marie Ampère (1775-1836)**:
- Currents attract or repel based on direction
- Mathematical laws of electrodynamics

**Michael Faraday (1791-1867)**:
- Electrochemistry: coined terms electrode, ion, electrolysis
- Electromagnetic induction (1831): moving magnet generates current
- Basis for generators and transformers
- Field concept: space filled with lines of force
- No formal mathematics, visual thinking

**James Clerk Maxwell (1831-1879)**:
- Mathematical theory unifying electricity and magnetism
- Four equations (Maxwell's equations)
- Predicted electromagnetic waves travel at light speed
- Concluded light is electromagnetic wave
- Unified optics with electromagnetism

**Heinrich Hertz (1857-1894)**:
- Experimentally confirmed electromagnetic waves (1888)
- Radio waves discovered
- Validated Maxwell's theory

| Development | Year | Significance |
|-------------|------|--------------|
| Ørsted's discovery | 1820 | Electricity and magnetism connected |
| Faraday's induction | 1831 | Basis for electric generators |
| Maxwell's equations | 1865 | Mathematical unification, predicted EM waves |
| Hertz's radio waves | 1888 | Experimental confirmation |

### Atomic Theory in Physics

**Kinetic Theory of Gases**:
- James Clerk Maxwell, Ludwig Boltzmann (1860s-1870s)
- Gas properties explained by molecular motion
- Temperature as average kinetic energy
- Statistical approach to large numbers of molecules

**Ludwig Boltzmann (1844-1906)**:
- Statistical mechanics
- Entropy as probability (microscopic states)
- S = k log W (on his tombstone)
- Faced opposition, tragically committed suicide

**Atoms Debated**:
- Chemists accepted atoms
- Some physicists skeptical (Ernst Mach, Wilhelm Ostwald)
- "Energetics" movement tried avoiding atoms
- Resolved early 20th century (Einstein's Brownian motion paper, 1905)

## Chemistry Advances

### Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)

**John Dalton (1766-1844)**:

**Postulates**:
1. Elements made of indivisible atoms
2. All atoms of element identical in mass
3. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios
4. Chemical reactions rearrange atoms

**Evidence**:
- Law of definite proportions
- Law of multiple proportions

**Impact**:
- Quantitative chemistry
- Atomic weights determined
- Explained chemical combinations

### Periodic Table

**Dmitri Mendeleev (1834-1907)**:
- Arranged elements by atomic weight (1869)
- Noticed periodic patterns in properties
- Left gaps for undiscovered elements
- Predicted properties of missing elements

**Predictions Confirmed**:
- Gallium (predicted 1871, discovered 1875)
- Scandium (predicted 1871, discovered 1879)
- Germanium (predicted 1871, discovered 1886)

**Significance**:
- Organized chemical knowledge
- Predictive power demonstrated scientific value
- Revealed underlying order (later explained by atomic structure)

**Lothar Meyer** independently developed similar table

### Organic Chemistry

**Friedrich Wöhler (1800-1882)**:
- Synthesized urea from inorganic compounds (1828)
- Challenged vitalism (belief that organic compounds require "vital force")
- Opened possibility of synthetic organic chemistry

**Structural Theory**:
- August Kekulé (1829-1896): benzene ring structure (supposedly from dream of snake biting tail)
- Archibald Couper and others: molecular structure determines properties
- Chemical formulas show bonding patterns

**Dyes and Industry**:
- William Perkin: mauve dye (1856), first synthetic aniline dye
- German chemical industry dominated (BASF, Bayer)
- Connection between academic chemistry and industry

## Medicine and Germ Theory

### Anesthesia

**Ether and Chloroform (1840s)**:
- William Morton: public demonstration of ether (1846)
- James Simpson: chloroform
- Made surgery painless
- Ethical debates (should women feel childbirth pain?)

**Impact**:
- Longer, more complex surgeries possible
- Reduced surgical shock
- Not yet sterile—infections still common

### Germ Theory

**Louis Pasteur (1822-1895)**:

**Fermentation Studies**:
- Showed microorganisms cause fermentation
- Different microbes produce different results
- Not spontaneous generation

**Pasteurization (1864)**:
- Heating kills microbes without ruining taste
- Applied to wine, beer, later milk

**Disease Research**:
- Showed microbes cause diseases (anthrax, rabies, chicken cholera)
- Developed vaccines (weakened pathogens)
- Rabies vaccine (1885): dramatic public test

**Robert Koch (1843-1910)**:

**Koch's Postulates** (criteria for disease causation):
1. Microbe found in diseased, not healthy
2. Microbe isolated and cultured
3. Cultured microbe causes disease when introduced
4. Same microbe re-isolated from experimental disease

**Discoveries**:
- Tuberculosis bacterium (1882)
- Cholera bacterium (1883)
- Solid culture media (agar plates)
- Bacterial staining techniques

**Impact of Germ Theory**:
- Transformed medicine from empirical to scientific
- Public health measures (sanitation, clean water)
- Antiseptic surgery (Joseph Lister)
- Disease prevention through hygiene
- Immunization programs

### Antiseptic Surgery

**Joseph Lister (1827-1912)**:
- Applied germ theory to surgery
- Carbolic acid spray in operating room (1867)
- Sterilized instruments
- Dramatically reduced post-operative infections
- Later replaced by aseptic technique (preventing contamination)

## Professionalization and Institutions

### Research Universities

**German Model**:
- Research and teaching combined
- Laboratory training for students
- Seminars and dissertations
- Justus von Liebig's chemistry lab (1826): prototype
- Spread to other countries

**PhD Degree**:
- Became standard research credential
- Laboratory apprenticeship
- Original research required

**Specialized Training**:
- Distinct disciplines (physics, chemistry, biology)
- Professional identity
- Career path established

### Scientific Societies and Journals

**Specialized Societies**:
- Chemical Society (1841)
- Physical Society (1874)
- American Chemical Society (1876)
- Many others for subdisciplines

**Journals**:
- Nature (1869): interdisciplinary
- Science (1880): American general science
- Specialized journals for each field
- Rapid communication
- Peer review developing

### Government and Industry Support

**Government Science**:
- Geological surveys
- National observatories
- Standards bureaus (Bureau of Standards, 1901)
- Public health institutions

**Industrial Research**:
- German chemical and electrical companies led
- Research labs in corporations (General Electric, AT&T)
- Patents and commercial applications
- Science for profit

## Women in 19th Century Science

**Barriers**:
- Universities excluded women (until late century)
- Professional societies excluded women
- Cultural assumptions about women's capabilities

**Participation**:
- Mary Anning (1799-1847): fossil collector, paleontology
- Ada Lovelace (1815-1852): mathematics, early computing ideas
- Maria Mitchell (1818-1889): astronomer, first female professor at Vassar
- Sophie Germain (1776-1831): mathematics, elasticity theory

**Late Century Progress**:
- Women's colleges founded (Bryn Mawr, Wellesley, etc.)
- Some European universities admitted women (1870s-1890s)
- Marie Curie (1867-1934): PhD 1903, two Nobel Prizes
- Still significant obstacles remaining

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Darwin's Finches

Galápagos finches showed variation in beak shape:
- Different islands had different food sources
- Beak shapes adapted to available food
- All descended from common ancestor
- Natural selection in action

**Modern studies** (Grants' research, 1970s-present):
- Documented natural selection occurring
- Beak size changes during droughts
- Evolution observable in real time

### Example 2: Pasteur's Rabies Vaccine

Joseph Meister, 9-year-old boy, bitten by rabid dog (1885):
- Otherwise fatal
- Pasteur administered experimental vaccine (weakened virus)
- Boy survived
- Public vindication of germ theory and vaccination
- Pasteur became hero, founded Pasteur Institute

### Example 3: Mendeleev's Predictions

Mendeleev predicted "eka-silicon" (1871):
- Atomic weight ~72
- Density ~5.5 g/cm³
- Grayish metal
- Forms dioxide XO₂

Germanium discovered (1886):
- Atomic weight 72.6
- Density 5.47 g/cm³
- Gray metal
- Forms GeO₂

Nearly exact match demonstrated periodic table's power.

## Comparison: Early vs. Late 19th Century Science

| Aspect | Early 1800s | Late 1800s |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Career Path | Gentleman scientists, physicians | Professional scientists, PhD required |
| Institutions | Universities, academies | Research universities, corporate labs |
| Methods | Natural history, observation | Laboratory experiment, mathematical theory |
| Disciplines | Natural philosophy broadly | Specialized fields (physics, chemistry, biology) |
| Applications | Limited | Industrial applications common |
| Women | Mostly excluded | Beginning to participate (still barriers) |
| Funding | Private wealth, some state | Government, industry, universities |

## Key Terms

- **Natural Selection**: Mechanism of evolution; advantageous traits increase in frequency
- **Thermodynamics**: Study of heat, work, and energy transformations
- **First Law**: Energy conservation
- **Second Law**: Entropy increases; heat flows hot to cold
- **Entropy**: Measure of disorder or energy unavailability
- **Electromagnetic Induction**: Moving magnet generates electric current (Faraday)
- **Field**: Region of space with force properties (Faraday, Maxwell)
- **Germ Theory**: Microorganisms cause infectious diseases (Pasteur, Koch)
- **Periodic Table**: Arrangement of elements by properties (Mendeleev)
- **Vitalism**: Belief that organic matter requires vital force (refuted)

## Summary

Nineteenth-century science transformed from gentleman pursuit to professional career. Institutional structures emerged: research universities with laboratory training, specialized societies, dedicated journals, government and industrial funding. Science became essential to economic and military power.

Evolution revolutionized biology when Darwin and Wallace proposed natural selection as mechanism for species change. Despite controversy and incomplete understanding of heredity, evolution gradually gained scientific acceptance. Mendel's genetics (rediscovered 1900) eventually provided missing mechanism.

Physics achieved grand unifications: thermodynamics unified heat and work with energy conservation and entropy increase. Maxwell unified electricity, magnetism, and light in electromagnetic theory, confirmed by Hertz. Statistical mechanics connected microscopic atoms to macroscopic properties.

Chemistry matured with Dalton's atomic theory, Mendeleev's periodic table, and organic chemistry synthesis. Germ theory revolutionized medicine through Pasteur's and Koch's work, enabling antiseptic surgery, vaccination, and public health measures.

By century's end, science was disciplinary, professional, laboratory-based, mathematically sophisticated, and industrially relevant. These characteristics defined modern science entering the twentieth century, setting stage for relativity, quantum mechanics, and molecular biology.
