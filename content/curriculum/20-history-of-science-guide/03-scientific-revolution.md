# The Scientific Revolution

## Overview

The Scientific Revolution (roughly 1543-1687) transformed how Europeans understood nature and investigated it. This period saw the overthrow of Aristotelian physics, adoption of heliocentrism, development of experimental method, mathematization of nature, and new institutional forms for science.

Key transitions:
- Geocentric → Heliocentric cosmos
- Qualitative → Quantitative description
- Aristotelian → Mechanical philosophy
- Authority-based → Evidence-based reasoning
- Individual scholars → Scientific societies

The revolution wasn't sudden or complete—medieval continuities existed, and some "revolutionary" figures (like Newton) still practiced alchemy. Nevertheless, by 1700 European natural philosophy had fundamentally changed.

## Copernican Revolution in Astronomy

### Nicolaus Copernicus (1473-1543)

**Background**:
- Polish canon (church administrator)
- Studied at Italian universities
- Aware of Ptolemaic system's complexity

**De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium (1543)**:
- Published year of his death
- Sun at center, Earth orbits annually, rotates daily
- Explained apparent retrograde motion of planets as perspective effect
- Still used circles and some epicycles (not yet ellipses)

**Advantages**:
- Simplified planetary motion explanation
- Natural explanation for retrograde motion
- Unified system with quantitative relationships

**Problems**:
- No more accurate than Ptolemy for predictions
- Violated common-sense physics (why don't we feel Earth moving?)
- Contradicted Scripture passages (sun "standing still" for Joshua)
- Stellar parallax not observed (stars must be extremely distant)

**Reception**:
- Initially limited—technical astronomy book
- Some astronomers adopted as calculating device
- Catholic Church initially tolerant

### Tycho Brahe (1546-1601)

**Contributions**:
- Most accurate pre-telescopic observations
- Island observatory (Uraniborg) with royal patronage
- 20+ years of planetary position data
- Discovered supernova (1572): challenged celestial immutability

**Tychonic System**:
- Compromise: Earth at center, Sun orbits Earth, planets orbit Sun
- Retained Earth's centrality and stability
- Accommodated some Copernican geometry

**Legacy**:
- Data enabled Kepler's laws
- Demonstrated need for precise observation

### Johannes Kepler (1571-1630)

**Background**:
- Tycho's assistant, inherited his data
- Deeply religious, sought divine harmony in cosmos
- Skilled mathematician

**Three Laws of Planetary Motion**:

1. **Law of Ellipses (1609)**: Planets orbit in ellipses with Sun at one focus
2. **Law of Equal Areas (1609)**: Line from Sun to planet sweeps equal areas in equal times (planets move faster when closer to Sun)
3. **Harmonic Law (1619)**: Square of orbital period proportional to cube of average distance from Sun (P² ∝ a³)

**Significance**:
- Abandoned perfect circles (major conceptual break)
- Made heliocentrism more accurate than geocentrism
- Introduced mathematical laws describing planetary motion
- Combined physics and astronomy

**Other work**:
- Optics (how lenses work)
- Rudolphine Tables (accurate planetary predictions)
- Mysterium Cosmographicum (geometric model with Platonic solids—wrong but creative)

### Galileo Galilei (1564-1642)

**Telescopic Observations (1609-1610)**:

Galileo didn't invent the telescope but improved it and turned it skyward.

**Discoveries reported in Sidereus Nuncius (1610)**:
- Mountains on Moon (not perfect sphere)
- Four moons orbiting Jupiter (not everything orbits Earth)
- Venus shows phases like Moon (evidence for heliocentric model)
- Countless stars invisible to naked eye
- Sunspots (Sun not perfect either)

**Impact**:
- Challenged Aristotelian cosmology
- Provided observational evidence for Copernican system
- Made Galileo famous across Europe

**Physics Contributions**:
- Law of falling bodies: all objects accelerate equally regardless of weight
- Parabolic trajectory of projectiles
- Pendulum motion studies
- Principle of relativity: motion is relative to observer

**Method**:
- Mathematical description of motion
- Thought experiments to isolate variables
- Experimental verification (though sometimes exaggerated)

**Conflict with Church**:
- Advocated Copernicanism publicly
- *Dialogue Concerning Two Chief World Systems* (1632) mocked geocentrism
- Inquisition trial (1633): forced to recant, house arrest
- Book banned (until 1835)

**Why the conflict?**
- Protestant Reformation made Catholic Church defensive about doctrine
- Galileo's tone and political enemies
- Scripture interpretation disputes
- Lack of conclusive proof (stellar parallax not yet detected)

### Timeline of Astronomical Revolution

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1543 | Copernicus publishes *De Revolutionibus* | Heliocentric theory proposed |
| 1572 | Tycho observes supernova | Celestial realm not immutable |
| 1609 | Kepler's first two laws | Elliptical orbits, mathematical laws |
| 1610 | Galileo's telescopic discoveries | Observational evidence for heliocentrism |
| 1619 | Kepler's third law | Harmonic relationship in solar system |
| 1633 | Galileo's trial | Science-religion conflict dramatized |
| 1687 | Newton's *Principia* | Universal gravitation explains Kepler's laws |

## Development of Scientific Method

### Francis Bacon (1561-1626)

**Novum Organum (1620)**:
- "New Instrument" for acquiring knowledge
- Critiqued Aristotelian logic as insufficient
- Advocated inductive method: particular observations → general laws

**Baconian Method**:
1. Collect observations systematically
2. Make tables of presence, absence, degree
3. Inductively derive general principles
4. Test predictions

**Idols (biases) to avoid**:
- Idols of the Tribe: human nature (seeing patterns that aren't there)
- Idols of the Cave: individual background and prejudices
- Idols of the Marketplace: language and communication problems
- Idols of the Theater: philosophical dogmas and traditions

**Impact**:
- Emphasized experiment and observation
- Collective enterprise: many observers needed
- Practical utility: knowledge for improving human condition
- Influenced Royal Society (founded 1660)

**Limitations**:
- Undervalued mathematics and deduction
- Overly optimistic about induction
- Didn't acknowledge role of hypothesis and creativity

### René Descartes (1596-1650)

**Discourse on Method (1637)**:
- Mathematical certainty as model for all knowledge
- Doubt everything except self-awareness ("I think, therefore I am")
- Rebuild knowledge on certain foundations

**Cartesian Method**:
1. Accept only clear and distinct ideas
2. Divide problems into parts
3. Order thoughts from simple to complex
4. Review comprehensively

**Mechanical Philosophy**:
- Universe as vast machine
- Matter is extension in space
- Motion explained by contact (no action at distance)
- Animals as automatons; humans have rational souls

**Contributions**:
- Analytic geometry (Cartesian coordinates)
- Law of refraction (co-discovered)
- Vortex theory of planetary motion (wrong but influential)

**Legacy**:
- Rationalism: reason as path to knowledge
- Mind-body dualism
- Mechanistic worldview

### Contrasting Approaches

| Aspect | Baconian (Empiricist) | Cartesian (Rationalist) |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Starting Point | Observations | Clear ideas, mathematical truths |
| Method | Induction | Deduction |
| Emphasis | Experiment | Mathematics |
| Goal | Control of nature | Certain knowledge |
| Influence | English science, Royal Society | Continental philosophy |

**Synthesis**: Actual science used both—hypotheses (rational) tested by experiment (empirical).

## Mechanical Philosophy and New Physics

### Shift from Aristotelian to Mechanical Worldview

**Aristotelian Physics** (old):
- Natural place: objects seek inherent locations
- Four elements with qualities (hot, cold, wet, dry)
- Teleology: purpose explains motion (acorn "wants" to become oak)
- Qualitative description

**Mechanical Philosophy** (new):
- Matter is inert particles in motion
- Quantitative properties only: size, shape, motion
- No purposes—just causes (pushes and pulls)
- Mathematical laws govern motion

### Key Figures in Mechanical Philosophy

**Pierre Gassendi (1592-1655)**:
- Revived atomism
- Matter composed of indivisible atoms
- Motion by collision

**Robert Boyle (1627-1691)**:
- Experimental approach to chemistry
- Boyle's Law: pressure × volume = constant (for gas at fixed temperature)
- *The Sceptical Chymist* (1661): critiqued alchemical theories
- Matter as "corpuscles" (small particles)

**Christiaan Huygens (1629-1695)**:
- Wave theory of light
- Laws of pendulum motion
- Collision laws
- Mechanical clocks (pendulum, balance spring)

## Isaac Newton (1642-1727): Synthesis

### Background
- Born year Galileo died
- Cambridge professor (Lucasian Chair)
- Reclusive, difficult personality
- Worked on alchemy, theology, chronology as well as mathematics and physics

### Principia Mathematica (1687)

Full title: *Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy*

**Three Laws of Motion**:
1. **Inertia**: Body continues in uniform motion unless acted on by force
2. **F = ma**: Force equals mass times acceleration
3. **Action-Reaction**: Every action has equal and opposite reaction

**Universal Gravitation**:
- Every mass attracts every other mass
- Force proportional to product of masses, inversely proportional to square of distance
- F = G(m₁m₂)/r²
- Applies to apples, moons, planets, stars

**Achievements**:
- Explained Kepler's laws (ellipses, areas, harmonic law)
- Predicted comet returns (Halley's comet)
- Explained tides (gravitational pull of Moon and Sun)
- Unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics (same laws everywhere)

**Method**:
- Mathematical deduction from principles
- Experimental verification
- "Hypotheses non fingo" (I don't make hypotheses)—focused on mathematical description

**Controversies**:
- Calculus priority dispute with Leibniz
- Action at a distance (gravity works across empty space—seemed mysterious)

### Opticks (1704)

- Light composed of particles (corpuscles)
- White light is mixture of colors
- Prism experiments showing dispersion and recombination
- Queries: speculative questions about light, matter, forces

### Legacy

- Newtonian mechanics dominated until Einstein (1905-1915)
- Model of mathematical physics
- Became cultural icon: embodied Scientific Revolution
- Synthesized mechanical philosophy with mathematical law

## Institutional Changes

### Scientific Societies

**Accademia dei Lincei (Rome, 1603)**:
- First scientific society
- Galileo was member
- Short-lived

**Royal Society (London, 1660)**:
- Royal charter from Charles II
- Baconian ideals: observation, experiment, utility
- Motto: "Nullius in verba" (Take nobody's word for it)
- *Philosophical Transactions* (1665-): first scientific journal
- Public demonstrations of experiments

**Académie Royale des Sciences (Paris, 1666)**:
- State-funded
- Salaried positions for scientists
- Organized research programs

### New Communication Forms

- **Scientific journals**: Rapid publication and priority claims
- **Correspondence networks**: Letters sharing results before publication
- **Demonstrations**: Public experiments to establish facts
- **Vernacular publications**: Not just Latin—broader audience

### Shift in Values

- Experiment and observation valued over ancient authority
- Replication: others should be able to repeat experiments
- Openness: share methods and results (though secrets still kept sometimes)
- Utility: science should improve life (Baconian ideal)

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Galileo's Leaning Tower Experiment

**Legend**: Galileo dropped unequal weights from Leaning Tower of Pisa, proving Aristotle wrong.

**Reality**:
- Probably didn't do this exact experiment
- Used thought experiments to show logical contradiction in Aristotle
- Rolling balls down inclined planes to slow motion and measure it
- Shows myth-making around scientific heroes

**Significance**:
- Challenged authority through experiment (real or imagined)
- Mathematical analysis of motion
- Founded modern kinematics

### Example 2: Newton's Prism Experiments

Newton showed white light contains all colors:
1. Prism splits white light into spectrum
2. Second prism recombines spectrum into white light
3. Single color from first prism cannot be split further

**Impact**:
- Proved light is not fundamentally white
- Color is intrinsic property of light, not added by prism
- Experimental method: isolate variables, control conditions

### Example 3: Halley's Comet Prediction

Edmund Halley used Newton's laws to calculate that comets of 1531, 1607, 1682 were same object.

**Prediction**: Return in 1758

**Result**: Appeared December 1758 (Halley had died 1742)

**Significance**:
- Triumphant confirmation of Newtonian mechanics
- Predictive power impressed public and scientists
- Comets explained as natural, not omens

## Key Terms

- **Heliocentric**: Sun-centered model of cosmos (Copernican)
- **Geocentric**: Earth-centered model (Ptolemaic/Aristotelian)
- **Ellipse**: Oval orbit shape (Kepler's first law)
- **Mechanical Philosophy**: Nature as machine, matter as particles in motion
- **Induction**: Reasoning from particular observations to general principles (Bacon)
- **Deduction**: Reasoning from general principles to particular conclusions (Descartes)
- **Corpuscular Theory**: Matter composed of small particles
- **Universal Gravitation**: Single force explaining terrestrial and celestial motion (Newton)
- **Inertia**: Tendency of objects to maintain motion unless acted upon (Newton's first law)
- **Empiricism**: Knowledge from sensory experience

## Summary

The Scientific Revolution transformed European natural philosophy between Copernicus's *De Revolutionibus* (1543) and Newton's *Principia* (1687). Astronomy shifted from geocentric to heliocentric, with Copernicus proposing the model, Tycho providing data, Kepler discovering elliptical orbits and mathematical laws, and Galileo offering telescopic evidence.

New scientific methods emerged: Bacon's inductive empiricism emphasized observation and experiment, while Descartes' deductive rationalism emphasized mathematics and clear reasoning. Actual practice combined both approaches.

The mechanical philosophy replaced Aristotelian physics, viewing nature as matter in motion governed by mathematical laws, not purposes. Newton's synthesis unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics through universal gravitation and three laws of motion, providing mathematical framework that dominated until twentieth century.

Institutional changes accompanied intellectual shifts: scientific societies (Royal Society, Académie des Sciences) created forums for sharing research, journals enabled rapid publication, and experimentation became valued over ancient authority. Science began transformation from individual pursuit to collective, organized enterprise.

The revolution wasn't complete or uniform—Newton practiced alchemy, Galileo's trial showed religious resistance, and many Aristotelian ideas persisted. Nevertheless, by 1700 fundamental assumptions about nature and how to study it had profoundly changed, setting stage for modern science.
