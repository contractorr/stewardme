# Enlightenment Science (1700-1800)

## Overview

The eighteenth century saw Newtonian mechanics spread across Europe, experimental methods applied to new fields, and science become part of broader culture. This "Age of Enlightenment" emphasized reason, progress, and improvement. Science moved from rarified intellectual pursuit to cultural force with public demonstrations, popular books, and practical applications.

Key developments:
- Classification systems organizing natural world
- Experimental electricity and magnetism
- Chemistry emerging from alchemy
- Systematic exploration and mapping
- Scientific institutions proliferating
- Public engagement with science

The Enlightenment belief in reason and natural law extended beyond physics to society, politics, and human nature, influencing revolutions in America and France.

## Newtonian Synthesis Spreads

### Mathematical Physics Advances

**Leonhard Euler (1707-1783)**:
- Most prolific mathematician ever (80+ volumes)
- Developed calculus notation still used (e, π, f(x), Σ, i)
- Fluid mechanics and differential equations
- Optics and astronomy
- Combined mathematical rigor with practical applications

**Pierre-Simon Laplace (1749-1827)**:
- *Celestial Mechanics*: complete Newtonian solar system
- Nebular hypothesis: solar system formed from rotating cloud
- Probability theory applications
- Determinism: "Laplace's demon" knowing all positions and velocities could predict future

**Lagrange and Hamilton**:
- Reformulated Newtonian mechanics more abstractly
- Variational principles
- Foundation for later theoretical physics

### Newton's Reception

**England**: National hero, buried in Westminster Abbey

**France**: Initially resisted (Cartesian vortices preferred), then championed by Voltaire

**Germany**: Leibniz's calculus used instead of Newton's fluxions

By mid-18th century, Newtonian approach dominated European science.

## Electricity and Magnetism

### Early Experiments

**Static Electricity**:
- Known since ancient Greeks (amber attracts when rubbed)
- 17th century: Otto von Guericke built first generator

**18th Century Experiments**:
- Leyden jar (1745): early capacitor, stored electrical charge
- Public demonstrations: sparks, shocks, attractions
- Electrical machines popular in salons and lecture halls

### Benjamin Franklin (1706-1790)

**Electrical Theory**:
- Single-fluid theory: electricity as fluid that could be positive or negative
- Simpler than competing two-fluid theories

**Kite Experiment (1752)**:
- Flew kite in thunderstorm (dangerous!)
- Showed lightning is electrical
- United atmospheric and laboratory electricity

**Practical Applications**:
- Lightning rod: protects buildings from strikes
- First practical application of electrical science
- Franklin declined to patent—wanted public benefit

**Legacy**:
- Showed American could contribute to European science
- Combined theory with practical utility (Baconian ideal)
- Electrical terminology: positive, negative, battery, conductor

### Luigi Galvani (1737-1798) and Alessandro Volta (1745-1827)

**Galvani's Animal Electricity**:
- Frog legs twitched when touched with metal in electrical field
- Proposed "animal electricity" in nerves and muscles

**Volta's Challenge**:
- Attributed effect to dissimilar metals, not animal electricity
- Built "voltaic pile" (1800): first battery
- Stacked zinc and copper discs with brine-soaked cloth
- Continuous electrical current (not just static discharge)

**Impact**:
- Enabled electrical experiments impossible before
- Founded electrochemistry
- Debate showed value of scientific controversy

### Charles-Augustin de Coulomb (1736-1806)

- Torsion balance: measured electrical and magnetic forces
- Coulomb's Law: force between charges inversely proportional to square of distance
- Parallel to Newton's gravitation

## Chemistry Revolution

### From Alchemy to Chemistry

**Alchemy's legacy**:
- Laboratory techniques (distillation, crystallization)
- Chemical substances (acids, alkalis, salts)
- Empirical knowledge

**Limitations**:
- No clear theory
- Secretive traditions
- Transmutation focus

**18th century shift**: Systematic experiments, quantitative methods, theoretical frameworks

### Pneumatic Chemistry: Discovering Gases

Before 1700s, "air" considered single element.

**Stephen Hales (1677-1761)**:
- Collected gases over water
- Measured volumes

**Joseph Black (1728-1799)**:
- "Fixed air" (carbon dioxide)
- Quantitative experiments
- Concept of specific heats

**Henry Cavendish (1731-1810)**:
- "Inflammable air" (hydrogen)
- Extremely careful measurements
- Determined water composition (H₂ + O → H₂O)

**Joseph Priestley (1733-1804)**:
- Discovered "dephlogisticated air" (oxygen)
- Many other gases (ammonia, nitrous oxide, etc.)
- Supported phlogiston theory

**Carl Wilhelm Scheele (1742-1786)**:
- Independently discovered oxygen (called "fire air")
- Many other discoveries
- Published late, so less credit

### Antoine Lavoisier (1743-1794): Chemical Revolution

**Background**:
- French aristocrat, tax collector, reformer
- Wealthy enough to afford best equipment

**Oxygen Theory of Combustion**:
- Burning adds oxygen, doesn't release phlogiston
- Weight gain in combustion from oxygen added
- Respiration also uses oxygen

**Conservation of Mass**:
- Careful weighing in closed systems
- Matter neither created nor destroyed in reactions
- Foundation for chemical equations

**New Nomenclature (1787)**:
- Systematic chemical naming
- Elements, compounds clearly distinguished
- Collaborative project with colleagues

**Elementary Treatise of Chemistry (1789)**:
- First modern chemistry textbook
- List of elements (included some actually compounds)
- Defined element operationally: substance that can't be decomposed

**Tragic End**:
- Executed during French Revolution (1794)
- Tax collection made him target
- "Republic has no need of scientists" (possibly apocryphal)

**Impact**:
- Overthrew phlogiston theory
- Established modern chemistry foundations
- Quantitative approach became standard
- Chemical revolution as important as Newtonian in physics

| Phlogiston Theory (Old) | Oxygen Theory (New) |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Combustible substances contain phlogiston | Combustion requires oxygen from air |
| Burning releases phlogiston | Burning combines substance with oxygen |
| Metals are calx + phlogiston | Metals lose oxygen when heated with carbon |
| Explained color changes | Explained weight gain in combustion |
| Couldn't explain weight gain | Explained all observations quantitatively |

## Classification and Natural History

### Carl Linnaeus (1707-1778)

**Systema Naturae (1735, expanded over lifetime)**:
- Hierarchical classification system
- Kingdom → Class → Order → Genus → Species
- Binomial nomenclature: Genus species (e.g., Homo sapiens)
- Applied to plants, animals, minerals

**Sexual System for Plants**:
- Classified by number of stamens and pistils
- Artificial but practical
- Some critics found it improper ("loathesome harlotry")

**Impact**:
- International standard for naming
- Organized exploding knowledge from exploration
- Made collection and comparison systematic
- Assumed fixed species created by God

### Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon (1707-1788)

**Natural History (44 volumes, 1749-1804)**:
- Comprehensive survey of animal kingdom
- Rich descriptions, not just classification
- Speculated on species change (dangerous idea)
- Popular, widely read (unlike Linnaeus's technical work)

**Ideas**:
- Earth much older than biblical 6000 years
- Species might transform over time
- Environmental influence on animals
- More dynamic view than Linnaeus

### Exploration and Empire

**Expeditions**:
- James Cook's Pacific voyages (1768-1779): Joseph Banks collected specimens
- Alexander von Humboldt's South American expedition (1799-1804)
- Many others sponsored by European powers

**Purposes**:
- Imperial: map territories, identify resources
- Scientific: collect, classify, document biodiversity
- Economic: find useful plants, animals, minerals

**Colonial Science**:
- Botanical gardens: Royal Gardens at Kew, Jardin du Roi in Paris
- Sent collectors worldwide
- Indigenous knowledge appropriated (often uncredited)
- Science intertwined with colonialism

## Geology and Earth History

### James Hutton (1726-1797)

**Theory of the Earth (1795)**:
- Earth shaped by slow, continuous processes
- "Present is key to the past" (uniformitarianism)
- Erosion wears down mountains, sediment forms new rock
- Cycle has "no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end"

**Deep Time**:
- Earth extremely old (millions of years at least)
- Challenged biblical chronology
- Time scale vast enough for gradual processes

**Plutonism vs. Neptunism**:
- Hutton: many rocks formed from heat (granite from molten magma)
- Abraham Werner: most rocks precipitated from primordial ocean
- Debate resolved in Hutton's favor over time

## Instruments and Precision

### Thermometers

- Multiple scales developed (Fahrenheit, Celsius, Réaumur)
- Standardization slow
- Enabled quantitative heat studies

### Barometers

- Measure atmospheric pressure
- Weather prediction attempts
- Altitude determination

### Chronometers

- John Harrison's marine chronometer (1735-1761)
- Solved longitude problem
- Prize from British government
- Enabled accurate navigation

### Microscopes

- Improved lens-making
- Revealed microscopic world
- Infusoria (microorganisms) discovered

### Precision Culture

- Accurate instruments became markers of serious science
- Instrument makers crucial craftsmen
- Quantification and measurement increasingly valued

## Science and Public Culture

### Popular Science

**Lecture Circuits**:
- Public demonstrations of experiments
- Subscription series
- Science as entertainment and education
- Both men and women attended

**Books and Encyclopedias**:
- Diderot's Encyclopédie (1751-1772): summarized Enlightenment knowledge
- Popular astronomy books
- Natural histories with illustrations
- Science made accessible to educated public

**Cabinets of Curiosities**:
- Private collections of natural specimens, instruments
- Displayed wealth and learning
- Social events around demonstrations

### Women and Science

**Barriers**:
- Universities excluded women
- Scientific societies mostly male
- Publishing under own name difficult

**Participation**:
- Émilie du Châtelet (1706-1749): translated Newton into French, added commentary
- Caroline Herschel (1750-1848): astronomer, discovered comets
- Maria Sibylla Merian (1647-1717): naturalist, illustrated insects
- Jane Marcet (1769-1858): science popularizer
- Many assisted husbands/brothers/fathers

**Salons**:
- French salons mixed aristocrats, intellectuals, scientists
- Women hosted and participated in discussions
- Alternative to formal institutions

## Scientific Organizations

### Academies Proliferate

- Berlin Academy (1700)
- St. Petersburg Academy (1724)
- Royal Swedish Academy (1739)
- American Philosophical Society (1743)

**Functions**:
- Meetings for presentations
- Journals for publications
- Prizes for solving problems
- Validation of discoveries

**Patronage Models**:
- Royal/state funding (France, Prussia, Russia)
- Membership fees (England)
- Mix of both

### Professionalization Begins

- Science not yet full profession (most scientists had other income)
- Some salaried positions in academies
- University chairs limited
- Physician and military positions for scientifically trained
- Next century would see full professionalization

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Celsius Temperature Scale

Anders Celsius (1701-1744) proposed scale in 1742:
- Originally: 0° = boiling point, 100° = freezing point
- Reversed after his death to current form
- Metric system adoption made Celsius international standard
- Shows standardization importance for science

### Example 2: Franklin's Lightning Rod Controversy

**Science**: Lightning rod worked as Franklin predicted

**Controversy**:
- French preferred blunt rods, English pointed (national pride in Franklin)
- Religious objections: thwarting God's will?
- Eventually practical success overcame objections

**Significance**: Science entering public policy debates

### Example 3: Humboldt's Isotherms

Alexander von Humboldt mapped lines of equal temperature (isotherms):
- Showed temperature depends on more than latitude
- Ocean currents, altitude, continentality matter
- Combined observation, measurement, visualization
- Founded biogeography

## Key Terms

- **Phlogiston**: Hypothetical substance released in combustion (overthrown by Lavoisier)
- **Conservation of Mass**: Matter neither created nor destroyed in reactions (Lavoisier)
- **Binomial Nomenclature**: Two-name system for species (Linnaeus)
- **Uniformitarianism**: Present processes explain past geology (Hutton)
- **Deep Time**: Earth's vast age, millions of years (geology)
- **Leyden Jar**: Early capacitor storing electrical charge
- **Voltaic Pile**: First battery, producing continuous current (Volta)
- **Enlightenment**: 18th-century emphasis on reason, progress, natural law
- **Natural History**: Descriptive study of plants, animals, minerals

## Summary

Eighteenth-century science extended Newtonian methods to new domains while becoming part of broader culture. Mathematics advanced with Euler and Laplace, completing Newtonian mechanics' reach to entire solar system. Electricity transformed from curiosity to science with Franklin's lightning experiments and Volta's battery.

Chemistry revolutionized when Lavoisier overthrew phlogiston theory, established oxygen theory of combustion, and introduced systematic nomenclature and quantitative methods. This chemical revolution paralleled the earlier physical revolution of Newton.

Natural history boomed as European exploration brought flood of new species. Linnaeus created lasting classification system with binomial nomenclature. Buffon provided rich descriptions and hints at species change. Geology began establishing Earth's deep time with Hutton's uniformitarianism.

Science became public culture: lectures, demonstrations, books, and encyclopedias spread knowledge beyond specialists. Women participated despite institutional barriers. Scientific societies proliferated across Europe and colonies. Instruments improved, enabling precision measurements.

The Enlightenment made science part of broader project to understand nature through reason and improve human condition. This cultural integration, combined with institutional growth and methodological sophistication, set stage for explosive expansion of science in nineteenth century.
