# Modern Physics: Relativity and Quantum Mechanics

## Overview

Early 20th-century physics underwent two revolutionary transformations that overthrew Newtonian mechanics' absolute certainty. Einstein's relativity theories (1905, 1915) showed space and time are relative, flexible, and interconnected. Quantum mechanics (1900-1930) revealed atomic-scale phenomena as probabilistic, with particles exhibiting wave properties. These theories, though counterintuitive, proved extraordinarily successful and enabled nuclear energy, semiconductors, lasers, and modern electronics.

Together, relativity and quantum mechanics define "modern physics," distinguishing it from "classical physics" (Newton through 19th century).

## Prelude: Crisis in Classical Physics (1890s)

### Problems Accumulating

**Blackbody Radiation**:
- Hot objects emit electromagnetic radiation
- Classical theory predicted "ultraviolet catastrophe" (infinite energy at high frequencies)
- Observations showed finite spectrum peaking at specific wavelength

**Photoelectric Effect**:
- Light hitting metal ejects electrons
- Classical wave theory couldn't explain why low-intensity high-frequency light works but intense low-frequency doesn't

**Atomic Spectra**:
- Elements emit light at specific discrete frequencies
- No classical explanation for discrete lines

**Michelson-Morley Experiment (1887)**:
- Tried to detect Earth's motion through "luminiferous ether" (supposed medium for light waves)
- Null result: no ether detected
- Contradicted expectation of absolute space

## Quantum Theory Origins

### Max Planck (1858-1947): Quantum Hypothesis

**Blackbody Radiation Problem (1900)**:
- Classical physics failed to predict observed spectrum
- Planck found formula that fit data perfectly
- Required radical assumption: energy emitted in discrete "quanta"
- E = hν (energy equals Planck's constant times frequency)

**Reluctant Revolution**:
- Planck initially considered quanta mathematical trick
- Hoped to derive from classical physics
- Eventually accepted quanta as real
- Quantum revolution began almost accidentally

### Albert Einstein (1879-1955): Photons

**Photoelectric Effect Paper (1905)**:
- Light consists of particle-like quanta (later called photons)
- Each photon has energy E = hν
- Explained why only high-frequency light ejects electrons (need minimum energy)
- Won Nobel Prize for this (1921), not relativity

**Significance**:
- Light has particle properties (Newton vindicated in some sense)
- Wave-particle duality hinted

### Niels Bohr (1885-1962): Atomic Model

**Bohr Model (1913)**:
- Electrons orbit nucleus in specific allowed orbits
- Each orbit has quantized energy
- Electrons jump between orbits, emitting/absorbing photons
- Explained hydrogen spectrum beautifully

**Limitations**:
- Only worked perfectly for hydrogen
- Ad hoc quantization rules
- Why these specific orbits?
- Needed more fundamental theory

## Special Relativity (1905)

### Einstein's 1905 Paper: "On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies"

**Two Postulates**:
1. **Principle of Relativity**: Laws of physics same in all inertial reference frames
2. **Constancy of Light Speed**: Light speed c same for all observers regardless of motion

**Consequences**:

**Time Dilation**:
- Moving clocks run slower
- t' = t / √(1 - v²/c²)
- At everyday speeds, negligible
- At near-light speeds, dramatic

**Length Contraction**:
- Moving objects contracted along direction of motion
- L' = L √(1 - v²/c²)

**Relativity of Simultaneity**:
- Events simultaneous in one frame not simultaneous in another
- No absolute "now" across universe

**Mass-Energy Equivalence**:
- E = mc²
- Mass and energy interconvertible
- Tiny mass = enormous energy (c² is huge)

**Implications**:
- No absolute space or time
- Space and time intertwined as "spacetime"
- Nothing travels faster than light
- Classical mechanics approximation valid at low speeds

### Reception

- Initially few readers understood
- Gradually accepted by physicists
- Experimental confirmation (particle accelerators, GPS satellites)
- Philosophically shocking: common sense misleading

## General Relativity (1915)

### Gravity Reimagined

**Einstein's Insight**:
- Free fall indistinguishable from weightlessness
- Gravity and acceleration equivalent (equivalence principle)
- Gravity not a force but curvature of spacetime
- Mass tells spacetime how to curve; spacetime tells mass how to move

**Einstein Field Equations**:
- Ten coupled nonlinear partial differential equations
- Relate spacetime geometry to mass-energy distribution
- Difficult to solve except special cases

### Predictions and Tests

**Mercury's Orbit (1915)**:
- Perihelion precession unexplained by Newton
- General relativity explained it perfectly
- Einstein's first test

**Light Bending (1919)**:
- Starlight grazes Sun during eclipse
- Predicted deflection 1.75 arcseconds
- Arthur Eddington's expedition confirmed
- Made Einstein international celebrity

**Gravitational Redshift**:
- Light climbing out of gravity well loses energy (frequency decreases)
- Confirmed in laboratory and astronomical observations

**Gravitational Waves**:
- Predicted 1916
- Detected 2015 (LIGO)
- Ripples in spacetime from colliding black holes

### Cosmological Implications

**Expanding Universe**:
- General relativity implies dynamic universe (expanding or contracting)
- Einstein initially resisted, added cosmological constant
- Hubble's observations (1929) showed expansion
- Big Bang cosmology emerged

**Black Holes**:
- Schwarzschild solution (1916): regions from which nothing escapes
- Initially considered mathematical curiosity
- Now known to exist (supermassive ones at galaxy centers)

## Quantum Mechanics Development (1920s)

### Wave-Particle Duality

**Louis de Broglie (1924)**:
- If light (wave) has particle properties, maybe particles have wave properties
- Matter waves: λ = h/p (wavelength = Planck's constant / momentum)
- Electrons should show diffraction

**Electron Diffraction (1927)**:
- Davisson and Germer experimentally confirmed electron waves
- Particles definitely have wave properties

### Heisenberg's Matrix Mechanics (1925)

**Werner Heisenberg (1901-1976)**:
- Focused on observable quantities (not electron orbits)
- Matrices represent observables
- Matrix multiplication non-commutative (AB ≠ BA)
- Successfully calculated hydrogen spectrum

**Uncertainty Principle (1927)**:
- Cannot simultaneously know position and momentum precisely
- Δx Δp ≥ ℏ/2 (ℏ = h/2π)
- Fundamental limit, not measurement problem
- Similar relations for energy-time

**Implications**:
- Determinism impossible at quantum scale
- Limits of knowledge built into nature
- Einstein deeply uncomfortable ("God does not play dice")

### Schrödinger's Wave Mechanics (1926)

**Erwin Schrödinger (1887-1961)**:
- Wave equation for matter waves
- Ψ (psi): wavefunction describes quantum state
- Hψ = Eψ (Schrödinger equation)
- Continuous, visualizable (more intuitive than matrices)

**Success**:
- Reproduced Bohr model results
- Extended to complex atoms
- Easier to use than matrix mechanics
- Soon shown equivalent to Heisenberg's approach

### Copenhagen Interpretation (1927)

**Bohr and Heisenberg**:
- Wavefunction gives probabilities, not certainties
- |Ψ|² = probability density
- Measurement "collapses" wavefunction
- Complementarity: wave and particle aspects complementary (mutually exclusive descriptions both needed)

**Bohr-Einstein Debates**:
- Einstein challenged with thought experiments
- Bohr successfully countered each challenge
- Fundamental disagreement about nature of reality
- "Is the moon there when nobody looks?"

**Other Interpretations**:
- Many-worlds (Hugh Everett, 1957): all possibilities realized in parallel universes
- Pilot-wave (de Broglie, Bohm): deterministic hidden variables
- Debate continues, though Copenhagen remains standard

### Quantum Mechanics Applications

**Pauli Exclusion Principle (1925)**:
- Wolfgang Pauli: no two electrons in same quantum state
- Explains periodic table structure
- Electrons fill shells

**Dirac Equation (1928)**:
- Paul Dirac: combined quantum mechanics with special relativity
- Predicted antimatter (positron discovered 1932)
- Quantum electrodynamics foundation

**Chemical Bonding**:
- Molecular orbital theory
- Covalent bonds explained by electron sharing
- Quantum chemistry emerged

## Atomic and Nuclear Physics

### Structure of Atom

**Ernest Rutherford (1871-1937)**:
- Gold foil experiment (1909): alpha particles mostly pass through, some deflect sharply
- Concluded: atom mostly empty space with tiny, dense, positive nucleus
- Electrons orbit nucleus

**Nucleus Discovery**:
- Proton identified (1919)
- Neutron discovered (James Chadwick, 1932)
- Nucleus: protons and neutrons bound by strong nuclear force

### Radioactivity

**Becquerel and Curies**:
- Henri Becquerel (1896): uranium emits penetrating radiation spontaneously
- Marie and Pierre Curie: isolated radium and polonium
- Three types: alpha (helium nuclei), beta (electrons), gamma (high-energy photons)

**Half-Life**:
- Radioactive decay probabilistic
- Half-life: time for half of sample to decay
- Enables radiometric dating (age of rocks, artifacts)

### Nuclear Fission

**Discovery (1938)**:
- Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann: bombarding uranium with neutrons splits nucleus
- Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch: explained as fission
- Releases enormous energy (E = mc²)

**Chain Reaction**:
- Fission releases more neutrons
- Can trigger additional fissions
- Critical mass needed for self-sustaining reaction

**Manhattan Project (1942-1945)**:
- U.S. wartime program to build atomic bomb
- First controlled chain reaction (Enrico Fermi, 1942)
- Trinity test (July 1945): first nuclear explosion
- Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945)
- Ushered in atomic age

**Nuclear Energy**:
- Power plants (1950s onward)
- Controlled fission produces heat → steam → electricity
- Waste disposal problem
- Safety concerns (Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, Fukushima)

### Nuclear Fusion

- Combining light nuclei (hydrogen → helium)
- Powers Sun and stars
- Even more energy than fission
- Hydrogen bomb (1952)
- Controlled fusion for power: ongoing challenge

## Particle Physics

### Cosmic Rays and New Particles

**Positron (1932)**: Carl Anderson, predicted by Dirac

**Muon (1936)**: Heavy electron

**Pion (1947)**: Yukawa's predicted nuclear force carrier

### Particle Accelerators

- Cyclotron (Ernest Lawrence, 1930s)
- Synchrotron (1940s onward)
- Larger accelerators → higher energies → new particles
- CERN, Fermilab, etc.

### Standard Model (developed 1960s-1970s)

**Quarks and Leptons**:
- Fundamental particles (not protons/neutrons which are composite)
- Six quarks: up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom
- Six leptons: electron, muon, tau, and three neutrinos

**Forces**:
- Electromagnetic (photon)
- Weak nuclear (W and Z bosons)
- Strong nuclear (gluons)
- Gravity (not yet integrated)

**Higgs Boson**:
- Predicted 1964
- Gives particles mass
- Discovered 2012 at CERN

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: GPS and Relativity

GPS satellites require both relativity corrections:

**Special Relativity**: Satellite clocks run slower (moving fast)
- Effect: -7 microseconds/day

**General Relativity**: Satellite clocks run faster (weaker gravity)
- Effect: +45 microseconds/day

**Net**: +38 microseconds/day

Without corrections, GPS would accumulate 10 km error per day!

### Example 2: Scanning Tunneling Microscope

Uses quantum tunneling:
- Electrons can "tunnel" through classically forbidden barriers
- Sharp tip scans nanometers above surface
- Tunneling current varies with distance
- Maps atoms on surface
- Won Nobel Prize 1986

### Example 3: Nuclear Medicine

Radioactive tracers:
- Inject short-lived isotope attached to biologically active molecule
- PET scan detects gamma rays from positron annihilation
- Maps metabolic activity
- Cancer diagnosis, brain imaging

## Comparison: Classical vs. Modern Physics

| Aspect | Classical (Newton-19th C) | Modern (20th C) |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|
| Space & Time | Absolute, independent | Relative, interconnected (spacetime) |
| Determinism | Complete | Probabilistic (quantum) |
| Causality | Local, contact | Action at distance (entanglement) |
| Measurement | No fundamental limit | Uncertainty principle |
| Light | Wave OR particle | BOTH wave and particle |
| Gravity | Force | Spacetime curvature |
| Matter | Continuous | Quantized at small scales |
| Validity | Low speeds, large scales | Universal (includes classical as approximation) |

## Key Terms

- **Spacetime**: Four-dimensional continuum combining space and time
- **Special Relativity**: Theory of space and time for constant velocities
- **General Relativity**: Theory of gravity as spacetime curvature
- **Quantum**: Discrete, indivisible amount (of energy, angular momentum, etc.)
- **Wavefunction (Ψ)**: Mathematical description of quantum state
- **Uncertainty Principle**: Fundamental limit on simultaneously knowing complementary variables
- **Wave-Particle Duality**: Quantum objects exhibit both wave and particle properties
- **Entanglement**: Quantum correlation between particles regardless of distance
- **Fission**: Splitting heavy nucleus
- **Fusion**: Combining light nuclei
- **Antimatter**: Particles with opposite charge of normal matter

## Summary

Modern physics revolutionized understanding of nature at extreme scales: very fast (relativity), very small (quantum mechanics), and very energetic (nuclear physics). These theories overthrew Newtonian certainty but proved extraordinarily accurate and enabled transformative technologies.

Einstein's special relativity (1905) unified space and time, showed mass-energy equivalence (E=mc²), and made light speed absolute. General relativity (1915) reimagined gravity as spacetime curvature, explained Mercury's orbit, predicted gravitational waves (detected 2015), and implied expanding universe leading to Big Bang cosmology.

Quantum mechanics emerged from crises in classical physics. Planck's quanta (1900), Einstein's photons (1905), and Bohr's atomic model (1913) led to full quantum mechanics in 1920s. Heisenberg's matrices and uncertainty principle, Schrödinger's wave equation, and Copenhagen interpretation established probabilistic worldview. Applications explained atomic structure, chemical bonding, and enabled quantum technologies.

Atomic and nuclear physics revealed atom's structure (Rutherford's nucleus), radioactivity, and nuclear energy from fission and fusion. Manhattan Project built atomic bomb, ushering in nuclear age with profound implications for energy and weapons.

Particle physics developed Standard Model describing fundamental particles (quarks, leptons) and forces (electromagnetic, weak, strong). Experiments at increasingly powerful accelerators discovered predicted particles, culminating in Higgs boson (2012).

Modern physics, though counterintuitive, forms foundation for contemporary technology (semiconductors, lasers, nuclear power, medical imaging, GPS) and continues pushing boundaries of knowledge toward quantum computers, gravitational wave astronomy, and unified theories.
