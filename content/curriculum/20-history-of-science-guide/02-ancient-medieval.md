# Ancient and Medieval Science

## Overview

Science before the Scientific Revolution (roughly 600 BCE - 1500 CE) laid foundations for modern inquiry. Greek natural philosophy established systematic investigation and mathematical description of nature. Islamic scholars preserved, translated, and extended this knowledge during Europe's early Middle Ages. Medieval European universities created institutional structures for learning and debate.

This period is not a "dark age" of ignorance but a time of significant achievement: astronomical models predicting planetary motion, optical theories explaining vision, mathematical innovations (algebra, algorithms), and technological advances (paper, mechanical clocks, gunpowder).

## Greek Science (600 BCE - 400 CE)

### Pre-Socratic Natural Philosophers

Early Greek thinkers sought natural explanations rather than purely mythological ones.

**Thales of Miletus (c. 624-546 BCE)**
- Predicted solar eclipse (585 BCE)
- Proposed water as fundamental substance
- Sought natural causes for phenomena

**Pythagoras and Followers (c. 570-495 BCE)**
- Mathematics as key to understanding cosmos
- Numerical relationships in music, astronomy
- "All is number" philosophy

**Atomists: Leucippus and Democritus (5th century BCE)**
- Matter composed of indivisible atoms
- Motion and arrangement explain change
- Remarkably prescient mechanistic view

### Classical Period Achievements

**Hippocrates (c. 460-370 BCE) and Medical School**
- Naturalistic disease theory (not divine punishment)
- Clinical observation and case histories
- Hippocratic Oath ethical framework
- Four humors theory (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile)

**Plato (428-348 BCE)**
- Mathematical forms as true reality
- Physical world as imperfect reflection
- Academy as model for later universities
- Emphasis on geometry and astronomy

**Aristotle (384-322 BCE)**
Most influential for medieval science:
- Systematic classification of animals
- Four causes: material, formal, efficient, final
- Geocentric cosmos with Earth at center
- Four elements: earth, water, air, fire
- Natural place theory (objects seek their natural location)
- Distinction between terrestrial (changeable) and celestial (eternal) realms

### Hellenistic Science (323-31 BCE)

**Alexandria as Scientific Center**
- Great Library and Museum (research institute)
- Royal patronage of scholars
- International scholarly community

**Euclid (c. 300 BCE)**
- *Elements*: systematic geometry textbook used for 2000+ years
- Axiomatic method: start with definitions and postulates, derive theorems
- Model for mathematical reasoning

**Archimedes (c. 287-212 BCE)**
- Principle of buoyancy (Archimedes' principle)
- Lever and pulley mechanics
- Area and volume calculations
- Combined mathematics with practical engineering

**Eratosthenes (c. 276-194 BCE)**
- Calculated Earth's circumference using shadow angles
- Remarkably accurate result (within 2-15% of true value)

**Aristarchus of Samos (c. 310-230 BCE)**
- Proposed heliocentric model (Sun at center)
- Rejected by contemporaries—too radical
- Rediscovered concept in Copernican revolution

**Ptolemy (c. 100-170 CE)**
- *Almagest*: comprehensive astronomical treatise
- Geocentric model with epicycles (circles on circles)
- Accurately predicted planetary positions
- Geography and optics works also influential

| Greek Scientist | Field | Key Contribution | Lasting Impact |
|----------------|-------|------------------|----------------|
| Aristotle | Natural Philosophy | Systematic classification, four causes | Dominated medieval thought |
| Euclid | Mathematics | *Elements*, axiomatic geometry | Standard textbook until 20th century |
| Archimedes | Mathematics, Physics | Buoyancy, levers, areas/volumes | Inspired modern mechanics |
| Ptolemy | Astronomy | Geocentric predictive model | Used until Copernicus |
| Galen | Medicine | Anatomy, physiology, theory | Medical authority until Renaissance |

**Galen (129-216 CE)**
- Physician to gladiators and emperors
- Dissection (animals, not humans in Rome)
- Physiological experiments
- Medical authority for 1400 years

## Islamic Golden Age (8th-14th centuries)

### Translation Movement

**House of Wisdom (Baghdad, founded 830s)**
- Translated Greek, Persian, Indian texts to Arabic
- Preserved works lost in Europe
- Added commentaries and extensions

**Key translators**: Hunayn ibn Ishaq, Thabit ibn Qurra

### Major Achievements

**Al-Khwarizmi (c. 780-850)**
- *Algebra* (word derives from his book title *al-jabr*)
- Introduced Hindu-Arabic numerals to Islamic world
- Algorithms (word derived from his Latinized name)
- Trigonometry tables

**Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen, 965-1040)**
- *Book of Optics*: groundbreaking work on vision
- Light travels in straight lines, enters eye (not emitted from eye)
- Camera obscura experiments
- Experimental method combining observation and mathematics
- Influenced European optics for centuries

**Al-Razi (Rhazes, 854-925)**
- Distinguished smallpox from measles
- Clinical observation and case studies
- Experimental medicine
- Critique of authority (including Galen when wrong)

**Ibn Sina (Avicenna, 980-1037)**
- *Canon of Medicine*: encyclopedic medical text
- Standard medical textbook in Europe until 1600s
- Philosophy blending Aristotle with Islamic thought

**Al-Biruni (973-1048)**
- Calculated Earth's radius with new method
- Studied Indian science and culture
- Encyclopedic natural historian
- Critical, empirical approach

**Ibn Rushd (Averroes, 1126-1198)**
- Commentaries on Aristotle
- Influenced European scholasticism
- Argued for compatibility of reason and faith

### Innovations

- **Mathematics**: Algebra, algorithms, trigonometry advances
- **Astronomy**: Improved planetary models, critiqued Ptolemy
- **Chemistry**: Alchemy but also distillation, classification of substances
- **Medicine**: Hospitals, pharmaceutical preparations, surgical techniques
- **Instruments**: Astrolabe improvements, mechanical clocks

### Why the Golden Age?

- Political stability under Abbasid Caliphate (initially)
- Trade routes facilitated knowledge exchange
- Islamic emphasis on learning and literacy
- Patronage by rulers and wealthy elites
- Translation movement preserved and extended Greek learning

## Medieval European Science (1000-1500)

### Rise of Universities

**First universities (12th-13th centuries)**:
- Bologna (1088)
- Paris (c. 1150)
- Oxford (1167)
- Cambridge (1209)

**Structure**:
- Faculties: Arts, Medicine, Law, Theology
- Arts faculty taught natural philosophy (basically science)
- Curriculum based on Aristotle and Islamic commentators
- Degrees: Bachelor, Master, Doctor

**Scholastic Method**:
- Systematic logical analysis
- Disputations (formal debates)
- Reconciling authorities (Aristotle, Bible, Church Fathers)
- Quaestio format: state question, present arguments, resolve

### Key Figures

**Robert Grosseteste (c. 1175-1253)**
- Chancellor of Oxford
- Optics and light theory
- Emphasized mathematics in natural philosophy
- Experimental verification of theories

**Roger Bacon (c. 1219-1292)**
- Advocated experimental methods
- Optics research
- "Experimental science" program
- Criticized reliance on authority alone

**Albertus Magnus (c. 1200-1280)**
- Naturalist who studied animals, plants, minerals
- Introduced Aristotle to Christian Europe
- Tried to reconcile faith and reason

**Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)**
- Systematized Christian theology using Aristotelian framework
- Natural theology: nature reveals God's design
- Reason and revelation compatible

**William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347)**
- Ockham's Razor: prefer simpler explanations
- Nominalism: challenge to Aristotelian realism
- Separation of reason and faith

**Jean Buridan (c. 1300-1358)**
- Impetus theory: motion continues until impeded
- Step toward inertia concept
- Challenged Aristotelian motion theory

**Nicole Oresme (c. 1320-1382)**
- Graphical representation of changing quantities
- Early coordinate geometry
- Earth's rotation hypothesis (ultimately rejected it)

### Medieval Technology

Often more advanced than theory:

- **Agriculture**: Heavy plow, three-field system, horseshoe and collar
- **Mechanical**: Water wheels, windmills, mechanical clocks
- **Military**: Gunpowder (from China), cannons, armor
- **Architecture**: Gothic cathedrals requiring engineering knowledge
- **Navigation**: Compass, astrolabe, portolan charts
- **Manufacturing**: Eyeglasses, paper mills, printing press (late medieval)

### Alchemy

Not just primitive chemistry:

- Sought transmutation of metals (especially lead to gold)
- Philosopher's stone legend
- Developed laboratory techniques (distillation, sublimation)
- Chemical knowledge accumulated
- Influenced Newton, Boyle, others into 17th century

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Ptolemaic Astronomy in Practice

Ptolemy's model with epicycles accurately predicted planetary positions for navigation and calendar-making. Its complexity wasn't a "bug"—it was feature showing mathematical sophistication. Medieval astronomers used it to:
- Calculate Easter date (tied to spring equinox)
- Navigate by stars on sea voyages
- Cast astrological horoscopes (major motivation for astronomy)

Even after Copernicus, Ptolemaic tables remained more accurate until Kepler refined heliocentric model.

### Example 2: Hospital System

Islamic world developed first hospitals (bimaristans) as institutions for healing:
- Baghdad hospital (9th century) had staff physicians, pharmacists, specialized wards
- Patients treated regardless of religion or ability to pay
- Medical schools attached to hospitals
- Clinical teaching and observation

This model spread to medieval Europe, establishing institutional medicine.

### Example 3: Scholastic Debates

Medieval disputations trained logical thinking:
- Student must argue both sides of question
- Formal structure: objections, counterarguments, resolution
- Public intellectual culture
- Method influenced scientific debate later (e.g., Galileo's *Dialogue*)

## Comparison: Greek, Islamic, Medieval Approaches

| Aspect | Greek | Islamic | Medieval European |
|--------|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Institutional Base | Philosophical schools, Alexandria | Courts, House of Wisdom, hospitals | Universities |
| Primary Language | Greek | Arabic | Latin |
| Key Texts | Aristotle, Euclid, Ptolemy | Greek texts + Islamic additions | Aristotle, Islamic commentators |
| Method | Logic, observation, mathematics | Empirical observation, experiment | Scholastic logic, some experiment |
| Religious Context | Polytheism, philosophy autonomous | Islamic framework, science valued | Christian theology integration |
| Major Innovations | Geometry, astronomy, medicine | Algebra, optics, hospital medicine | Universities, mechanical clocks |

## Key Terms

- **Natural Philosophy**: Pre-modern term for study of nature (roughly equivalent to science)
- **Geocentric Model**: Earth-centered cosmos (Aristotelian-Ptolemaic)
- **Epicycle**: Circle whose center moves on another circle (Ptolemaic astronomy)
- **Four Elements**: Earth, water, air, fire (Aristotelian physics)
- **Four Humors**: Blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile (Galenic medicine)
- **Scholasticism**: Medieval method of learning emphasizing logic and disputation
- **Alchemy**: Medieval chemistry seeking transmutation and philosopher's stone
- **Astrolabe**: Instrument for measuring altitude of celestial bodies
- **Axiomatic Method**: Start with basic postulates, derive conclusions logically (Euclidean)

## Summary

Ancient and medieval science established lasting foundations. Greek natural philosophers created systematic investigation using logic, observation, and mathematics. Aristotle's comprehensive system dominated for two millennia. Hellenistic scientists like Euclid, Archimedes, and Ptolemy made mathematical and empirical advances that shaped subsequent science.

Islamic scholars preserved Greek learning during European instability, translated texts, and extended knowledge in mathematics (algebra), optics (Ibn al-Haytham), medicine (hospitals, pharmacology), and astronomy. The Islamic Golden Age (8th-14th centuries) was crucial for transmitting ancient science to medieval Europe.

Medieval European universities institutionalized learning, using scholastic methods of logical disputation. While heavily influenced by Aristotle and concerned with reconciling faith and reason, medieval scholars made advances in optics, motion theory (impetus), and mathematics. Technology often outpaced theory, with mechanical clocks, gunpowder, and architectural achievements.

This era was not a dark age but a period of preservation, synthesis, and innovation across cultures. The foundations laid—logical reasoning, mathematical description, empirical observation, institutional structures—enabled the Scientific Revolution to follow.
