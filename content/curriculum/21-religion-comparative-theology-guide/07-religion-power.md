# Religion & Power: Institutions, Politics, Conflict

## Overview

Religion ≠ private belief; shapes power structures, legitimates rule, drives conflict, builds institutions. Religious authority competes with/reinforces political authority. Churches/mosques/temples = social infrastructure. Religious law (Sharia, Canon, Halakha) governs billions. Wars fought over doctrine (Thirty Years' War), identity (Crusades, partition of India), political control (ISIS). Understanding religion-power nexus essential for history, geopolitics, law.

---

## Political Theology

### Divine Right of Kings

**Concept:** Monarch rules by God's will; accountable to God alone, not subjects

**Examples:**
- **Europe (Medieval-Early Modern):** Kings anointed by Church; rebellion = sin
- **China:** Mandate of Heaven (different: lose mandate if unjust → rebellion justified)
- **Japan:** Emperor descended from Amaterasu (until 1946)

**Decline:** Enlightenment (social contract theory), French Revolution (1789), democratization

---

### Theocracy

**Definition:** Rule by religious authorities or religious law as state law

**Examples:**
| State | Religion | Structure | Leadership |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vatican City** | Catholic | Absolute theocracy | Pope (elected by cardinals) |
| **Iran** | Shia Islam | Islamic Republic | Supreme Leader (Ayatollah); elected president subordinate |
| **Taliban Afghanistan (1996-2001, 2021-present)** | Sunni Islam (Deobandi) | Emirate | Emir; clerics hold power |
| **Historical:** Papal States (756-1870), Caliphates (Rashidun, Umayyad, Abbasid, Ottoman), Tibetan theocracy (until 1951) |

**Characteristics:** Religious law = state law; clerics = judges, legislators; dissent = heresy; often authoritarian

---

### State Religion

**Definition:** Official religion; state supports; other religions tolerated or restricted

**Examples:**
- **Christianity:** England (Anglican), Denmark (Lutheran), Greece (Orthodox), Costa Rica (Catholic)
- **Islam:** Saudi Arabia, Pakistan, Malaysia, Maldives (23 countries total)
- **Buddhism:** Thailand, Bhutan, Sri Lanka
- **Hinduism:** Nepal (until 2006)

**Implications:** Public funding (churches, clergy salaries); blasphemy laws; religious education mandatory; minority religions restricted

---

### Separation of Church and State

**US Model (First Amendment, 1791):** "Congress shall make no law respecting establishment of religion, or prohibiting free exercise thereof"
- No official religion; religious freedom; but "ceremonial deism" (In God We Trust, Pledge)
- Debates: School prayer, Ten Commandments in courthouses, faith-based initiatives

**French Model (Laïcité):** Strict secularism; religion private; ban on religious symbols in public schools (hijab, yarmulke, cross)
- Goal: Social cohesion; prevent religious division
- Criticism: Targets Muslims disproportionately

**Turkey Model (Kemalism, 1923-2000s):** Secular state; military enforced; Erdoğan reversed (2000s onward); Islamization

## Religious Institutions & Authority

### Catholic Church

**Structure:** Hierarchical; Pope (Bishop of Rome) → Cardinals → Archbishops → Bishops → Priests

**Infallibility:** Pope cannot err when teaching ex cathedra on faith/morals (rarely invoked; twice since 1870)

**Wealth:** Vast real estate, art, investments; Vatican Bank; estimated $10-15 billion assets

**Influence:** 1.3 billion members; hospitals, schools, universities; diplomatic relations (Holy See); political lobbying (abortion, contraception, LGBTQ+)

**Scandals:** Sexual abuse crisis; cover-ups; billions in settlements; trust collapse

---

### Papal States (756-1870)

**Territory:** Central Italy; Rome capital

**Function:** Temporal (worldly) power of Pope; financed via taxes, tithes

**Loss:** Italian unification (1870); reduced to Vatican City (1929 Lateran Treaty)

**Significance:** Church wielded political power; kings competed with Pope for authority; Investiture Controversy (who appoints bishops?)

---

### Islamic Caliphate

**Definition:** Political-religious leader of Muslim community; successor to Muhammad

**Historical:**
- **Rashidun (632-661):** Four "rightly guided" caliphs
- **Umayyad (661-750):** Damascus; Arab-centric
- **Abbasid (750-1258):** Baghdad; multi-ethnic; Golden Age
- **Ottoman (1517-1924):** Istanbul; last caliphate; abolished by Atatürk

**Modern:** ISIS declared caliphate (2014-2019); rejected by mainstream scholars; territorial defeat 2019

**Debate:** Can caliphate be restored? Islamists say yes; secularists, modernists say no/unnecessary

---

### Buddhist Sangha

**Monasticism:** Central; monks/nuns = ideal; laypeople support → merit

**Authority:** Decentralized (no pope-equivalent); senior monks respected; texts authoritative

**Political Role:**
- **Thailand:** Monks bless government; official Buddhism; military ties
- **Myanmar:** 969 Movement (nationalist monks); anti-Rohingya
- **Tibet:** Dalai Lama = political + spiritual leader (until 2011, political role ceded)
- **Sri Lanka:** Monks in parliament; Buddhist nationalism

---

### Hindu Temple Economy

**Temples:** Massive landowners (historically); donations, festivals generate revenue

**Priests (Brahmins):** Hereditary; perform rituals; collect fees

**Modern:** Indian government manages some major temples (Tirupati); corruption scandals

## Religion & Law

### Sharia (Islamic Law)

**Sources:** Quran, Hadith, Ijma (consensus), Qiyas (analogy)

**Scope:** Worship, transactions, marriage, inheritance, crime, governance

**Implementation:**
- **Full:** Saudi Arabia (Wahhabi), Iran (Shia), Taliban Afghanistan; hudud punishments (amputation, stoning)
- **Partial:** Pakistan, Egypt, Malaysia; family law (marriage, divorce) religious; criminal law secular
- **Secular:** Turkey, Tunisia, Central Asia; Sharia courts abolished

**Controversies:**
- **Hudud:** Amputation for theft, stoning for adultery; human rights violations
- **Apostasy:** Death penalty (13 countries); rarely enforced but chilling effect
- **Blasphemy:** Pakistan = death penalty; used against minorities (Asia Bibi case)
- **Women:** Testimony = half men's (debated); inheritance = half; divorce easier for men
- **Reform:** Modernists reinterpret; contextualize; some countries reform (Morocco family law)

---

### Canon Law (Catholic)

**Codex Iuris Canonici (1983):** Code of Canon Law; 1,752 canons

**Scope:** Internal Church governance; clergy discipline; sacraments; marriage annulment; Catholic schools/hospitals

**Courts:** Ecclesiastical courts; marriage tribunals; no civil authority but Church recognizes

**Influence on Western Law:** Contract law, marriage law, legal reasoning, universities (Bologna, Paris)

---

### Halakha (Jewish Law)

**Sources:** Torah, Talmud, Responsa (rabbinic rulings)

**Scope:** Ritual (prayer, Sabbath, kashrut), civil (contracts, torts), family (marriage, divorce)

**Authority:** Decentralized; rabbis interpret; Orthodox stricter, Reform looser

**Modern:** Israel = Jewish state; religious courts (rabbinical) handle marriage/divorce for Jews; secular law for civil matters; tensions over Sabbath, conversions, military draft (ultra-Orthodox exempt)

---

### Hindu Law

**Sources:** Dharmashastra texts (Manusmriti, etc.), custom

**Scope:** Family law (marriage, inheritance, adoption)

**India:** Personal law system; Hindus governed by Hindu law, Muslims by Sharia, Christians by Christian law (marriage/divorce); debated: Uniform Civil Code (one law for all) vs religious pluralism

**Controversies:** Caste discrimination sanctioned by Manusmriti; women's rights (inheritance, divorce)

## Religion & War

### Holy War Concepts

**Crusades (Christianity, 1095-1291):** Recapture Jerusalem from Muslims; Pope Urban II; indulgences (forgiveness of sins); brutal; failed

**Jihad (Islam):**
- **Greater Jihad:** Internal struggle against sin
- **Lesser Jihad:** Physical defense of Islam; conditions: defense, just cause, no targeting civilians/non-combatants, proportional
- **Hijacked:** Al-Qaeda, ISIS = offensive jihad; kill civilians; excommunicate Muslims (takfir)

**Just War Theory (Christian):**
- **Jus ad bellum:** Just cause, legitimate authority, right intention, last resort, probability of success, proportional
- **Jus in bello:** Discrimination (no targeting civilians), proportionality
- **Origins:** Augustine, Aquinas; influenced international law

**Dharma Yuddha (Hindu):** Righteous war; *Bhagavad Gita* = Arjuna's duty to fight (kshatriya caste); rules (no attacking unarmed, fleeing)

**Buddhist Paradox:** Non-violence ideal vs warrior monks (Japan), militant nationalism (Myanmar, Sri Lanka)

---

### Religious Conflicts

#### Crusades (1095-1291)

- **First Crusade (1095-1099):** Recaptured Jerusalem; massacred Muslims, Jews
- **Later Crusades:** Mostly failed; Fourth Crusade (1204) sacked Constantinople (Christian city)
- **Legacy:** Christian-Muslim animosity; "Crusader" = insult in Muslim world

#### Thirty Years' War (1618-1648)

- Catholic vs Protestant (Holy Roman Empire)
- 8 million dead; Germany devastated
- Peace of Westphalia (1648): State sovereignty; cuius regio, eius religio (ruler's religion = state's)

#### Partition of India (1947)

- British India divided: Hindu-majority India, Muslim-majority Pakistan (+ East Pakistan, now Bangladesh)
- 10-20 million displaced; 1-2 million dead (communal violence)
- Kashmir: Disputed; Muslim-majority but Hindu ruler chose India; wars (1947, 1965, 1999), insurgency

#### Israeli-Palestinian Conflict (1948-present)

- Jewish-Muslim (+ Christian Palestinians); land, nationhood, Jerusalem (Temple Mount/Haram al-Sharif)
- Wars (1948, 1967, 1973), intifadas, settlements, Gaza blockade
- Religious dimensions: Biblical land claims, Al-Aqsa Mosque, evangelical Christian Zionism

#### Yugoslav Wars (1991-2001)

- Catholic Croats vs Orthodox Serbs vs Muslim Bosniaks
- Genocide (Srebrenica 1995: 8,000 Bosniak men/boys killed by Serbs)
- Religion = ethnic marker; nationalism primary but religious identity mobilized

#### Rohingya Crisis (2016-2017)

- Myanmar military + Buddhist nationalists vs Rohingya Muslims
- 700,000 fled to Bangladesh; villages burned; rape, killings
- Monks incited violence (969 Movement); "ethnic cleansing" (UN)

### Religious Terrorism

**Motivations:** Eschatology (end times), martyrdom (heavenly reward), cosmic war framing (good vs evil), grievances (occupation, humiliation)

**Examples:**
- **Al-Qaeda (1988):** Osama bin Laden; 9/11 (2001, ~3,000 dead); global jihad; anti-US/West
- **ISIS (2014-2019):** Caliphate declared; genocide (Yazidis), beheadings, terror attacks (Paris 2015, Brussels 2016); territorially defeated 2019
- **Aum Shinrikyo (Japan, 1995):** Doomsday cult; sarin gas attack on Tokyo subway (13 dead, 5,000 injured)
- **Christian Identity:** White supremacist theology; Eric Rudolph (Olympic Park bombing 1996), Timothy McVeigh (influenced)
- **Jewish Extremism:** Baruch Goldstein (Cave of Patriarchs massacre 1994, 29 dead); Price Tag attacks (anti-Palestinian/Christian violence)

**Mainstream Rejection:** Vast majority of religious adherents reject terrorism; scholars issue fatwas, condemnations

## Religious Nationalism

### Hindutva (India)

**Ideology:** India = Hindu nation; Muslims, Christians = foreigners

**RSS (Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh, 1925):** Paramilitary org; Hindu nationalist; BJP political wing

**BJP (Bharatiya Janata Party):** Power 1998-2004, 2014-present; Prime Minister Narendra Modi

**Events:**
- **Babri Masjid Demolition (1992):** Mosque destroyed by Hindu mob; claim built on Ram's birthplace; riots (2,000 dead)
- **Gujarat Riots (2002):** Train fire blamed on Muslims → riots (1,000-2,000 dead, mostly Muslim); Modi = Gujarat Chief Minister; accused of complicity
- **Citizenship Amendment Act (2019):** Fast-track citizenship for non-Muslim refugees; seen as anti-Muslim

---

### Buddhist Nationalism (Myanmar, Sri Lanka)

**969 Movement (Myanmar):** Monks; anti-Rohingya; "Buddhist nation under threat"; Ashin Wirathu leader

**Bodu Bala Sena (Sri Lanka):** "Buddhist Power Force"; anti-Muslim; 2014 riots (Muslims attacked)

**Contradiction:** Buddhism = non-violence; nationalism = violence; justified as defending Buddhism

---

### Christian Nationalism (US)

**Beliefs:** US = Christian nation; Constitution based on Bible; secular threats (abortion, LGBTQ+, immigration)

**Manifestations:** Opposition to separation of church-state; Christian symbols in public; "values voters"

**January 6, 2021:** Capitol riot; Christian symbols (Jesus Saves, Jericho March); Qing Anon (apocalyptic)

**Criticism:** Theocracy threat; white Christian privilege; exclusion of minorities

---

### Zionism (Israel)

**Secular Zionism:** Theodor Herzl; Jewish homeland for safety; not inherently religious

**Religious Zionism:** Biblical land promises; settlements in West Bank; messianic (hasten Messiah)

**Haredi (Ultra-Orthodox):** Some anti-Zionist (state should await Messiah, not be created by humans); others support

**Tensions:** Secular vs religious; settlements vs peace; military draft (ultra-Orthodox exempt)

## Religion & Social Movements

### Abolitionism (Slavery)

**Christian Abolitionists:** Quakers (1688 Germantown Quaker Petition), William Wilberforce (UK), Frederick Douglass (US); Bible used against slavery ("no Jew nor Greek, slave nor free")

**Pro-Slavery Christians:** Southern US; Bible used to justify (Ham's curse, slavery in Old Testament)

**Result:** Religion mobilized both sides; abolitionists won

---

### Civil Rights Movement (US, 1950s-60s)

**Religious Leadership:** Martin Luther King Jr. (Baptist minister); SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference)

**Theology:** Beloved community; non-violence (Gandhi-inspired); prophetic tradition (Amos, Isaiah)

**Opposition:** White supremacist churches; Biblical "curse of Ham" used to justify segregation

---

### Liberation Theology (Latin America, 1960s-80s)

**Core:** "Preferential option for the poor"; Jesus as liberator; structural sin (poverty, oppression)

**Praxis:** Base ecclesial communities; literacy, consciousness-raising; political activism

**Figures:** Gustavo Gutiérrez, Archbishop Romero (El Salvador, assassinated 1980)

**Vatican:** John Paul II suppressed; Benedict XVI (was Joseph Ratzinger) = opponent; Francis = sympathetic

---

### Islamic Modernism vs Fundamentalism

**Modernism:** Muhammad Abduh, Jamal al-Din al-Afghani; reconcile Islam with reason, science, democracy

**Fundamentalism:** Wahhabism, Salafism; return to "pure" early Islam; reject innovation

**Conflict:** Reformists vs traditionalists; secularism vs Islamism; modernity vs authenticity

---

## Key Terms

**Theocracy:** Rule by religious authorities or religious law

**State Religion:** Official religion; state-supported

**Caesaropapism:** Monarch controls church (Byzantine Empire, Russia)

**Investiture Controversy:** Who appoints bishops? Pope vs Emperor (11th-12th century)

**Just War Theory:** Conditions for justifying war; Christian (Augustine, Aquinas)

**Jihad:** Struggle; greater (internal) vs lesser (physical defense); hijacked by extremists

**Religious Nationalism:** Fusion of religious + national identity; exclusionary

**Liberation Theology:** Christianity as liberation of oppressed; Latin America

**Sharia:** Islamic law; based on Quran, Hadith, consensus, analogy

**Caliphate:** Islamic political-religious leadership; successor to Muhammad

## Summary

Religion-power nexus: Divine right (kings rule by God's will), theocracy (Iran, Vatican), state religion (23 Muslim countries, Denmark, England), vs separation (US First Amendment, French laïcité). Religious institutions wield power: Catholic Church (1.3B members, hospitals/schools, lobbying), Islamic Caliphate (historical; ISIS attempted revival), Buddhist Sangha (Thai military ties, Myanmar nationalism). Religious law governs: Sharia (hudud punishments, apostasy/blasphemy laws), Canon law (Church governance, influenced Western law), Halakha (Israeli religious courts), Hindu law (Indian personal law). Holy war: Crusades (1095-1291), jihad (defense vs ISIS offensive), Just War Theory (Augustine/Aquinas), Dharma Yuddha (*Bhagavad Gita*). Religious conflicts: Thirty Years' War (8M dead), Partition of India (1-2M dead), Israeli-Palestinian, Yugoslav Wars (Srebrenica genocide), Rohingya crisis. Religious terrorism: Al-Qaeda (9/11), ISIS (caliphate, genocide), rejected by mainstream. Religious nationalism: Hindutva (India, BJP), Buddhist (Myanmar 969, Sri Lanka BBS), Christian (US Capitol riot), Zionism (settlements). Social movements: Abolitionism, Civil Rights (MLK), Liberation Theology (preferential option for poor). Religion shapes law, war, politics, conflict; understanding essential for history, geopolitics.
