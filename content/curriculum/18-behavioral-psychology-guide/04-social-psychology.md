# Social Psychology

## Overview

Social psychology examines how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by others' actual, imagined, or implied presence. Humans are fundamentally social beings who constantly navigate group dynamics, conform to norms, respond to authority, and alter behavior based on social context. Understanding these patterns reveals why people act differently in groups than alone, how social pressure overrides individual judgment, and what factors enable resistance to harmful influences. Applications span organizational behavior, marketing, public health campaigns, and social change movements.

## Conformity

### Definition
Changing beliefs or behaviors to match others in group, either due to real or imagined pressure. Distinct from compliance (behavioral change without belief change) and obedience (following direct orders).

### Types of Conformity

| Type | Definition | Motivation | Duration |
|------|------------|------------|----------|
| **Compliance** | Public conformity without private acceptance | Avoid rejection, gain approval | Temporary, context-dependent |
| **Identification** | Conform to liked/admired group | Maintain desired identity | Continues while relationship valued |
| **Internalization** | Genuine belief change | Information assumed correct | Permanent, context-independent |

### Asch Conformity Experiments (1951)

**Procedure:**
- 7-9 participants judge which line matches reference line (obvious answer)
- All but one are confederates giving wrong answer
- Real participant answers second-to-last

**Results:**
- 75% conformed at least once
- 32% of responses conformed to incorrect majority
- Control condition errors: <1%

**Variations:**
- Unanimous majority: 32% conformity
- One dissenter (even if also wrong): 5% conformity
- Private written responses: Conformity drops dramatically
- Larger majority (3+ confederates): No additional conformity increase

**Interpretation:**
- **Normative influence** - Desire to be accepted, avoid standing out
- **Informational influence** - Assume majority has better information
- Dissent legitimizes alternative views, breaking unanimity effect

### Factors Increasing Conformity

**Group Characteristics:**
- Unanimous majority (no dissent)
- Cohesive group (strong member bonds)
- High status group members
- Larger group size (up to 3-4, then plateaus)
- Face-to-face interaction

**Individual Characteristics:**
- Low self-confidence in judgment
- High need for social approval
- Collectivist cultural background
- Female (in some contexts, especially face-to-face)

**Situational Factors:**
- Public responses (vs. private)
- Ambiguous task (vs. objective)
- Difficult decision
- Importance of group acceptance

### Cultural Variations

**Individualist Cultures (US, Western Europe):**
- Lower baseline conformity
- Value independent thought, uniqueness
- Conformity seen negatively ("sheep")

**Collectivist Cultures (East Asia, Latin America):**
- Higher baseline conformity
- Value group harmony, consensus
- Conformity seen positively (social responsibility)

Meta-analysis: Conformity rates 25% higher in collectivist cultures

### Real-World Implications

**Fashion and Trends:** Bandwagon effects drive adoption curves

**Financial Markets:** Herding behavior creates bubbles and crashes

**Online Reviews:** Early ratings disproportionately influence later reviewers

**Political Movements:** Visible dissent legitimizes alternative views, breaks conformity

**Organizational Culture:** Unanimous leadership suppresses innovation and dissent

## Obedience to Authority

### Milgram Obedience Experiments (1961-1963)

**Setup:**
- Participant assigned "teacher" role (actually all participants are teachers)
- Confederate "learner" strapped to electric shock chair
- Teacher administers increasing shocks for wrong answers (15V to 450V)
- Learner shows escalating distress (scripted; no real shocks)
- Experimenter uses prods: "Please continue," "The experiment requires that you continue"

**Predictions:**
- Psychiatrists predicted <1% would administer maximum shock
- Expected disobedience around 150V (learner requests to stop)

**Results:**
- 65% administered maximum 450V shock
- 100% went to at least 300V (learner becomes unresponsive)
- Participants showed extreme stress (sweating, trembling, nervous laughter)

**Key Insight:** Ordinary people commit harmful acts under authority pressure, not due to sadistic personality

### Factors Increasing Obedience

| Factor | Baseline (65%) | Modified Condition | Obedience Rate |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Proximity to Authority** | Same room | Phone instructions | 21% |
| **Proximity to Victim** | Separate room | Same room | 40% |
| **Proximity to Victim** | Separate room | Touch victim | 30% |
| **Authority Legitimacy** | Yale professor | Rundown office | 48% |
| **Peer Rebellion** | Alone | Two confederates refuse | 10% |
| **Personal Responsibility** | Direct administration | Indirect role | 92% |

### Mechanisms of Obedience

**Gradual Escalation (Foot-in-Door):**
- Start with small shocks, incrementally increase
- Each shock only slightly more than last
- Difficult to identify clear stopping point

**Diffusion of Responsibility:**
- Authority figure claims responsibility
- "I'm responsible for what happens"
- Participant feels less personally accountable

**Authority Symbols:**
- Lab coat and title signal expertise
- Prestigious institutional setting (Yale)
- Confident, formal demeanor

**Social Proof Deficit:**
- No models of disobedience visible
- When peers disobey, participants follow (90% disobedience)

**Commitment and Consistency:**
- Agreed to participate
- Already administered initial shocks
- Difficult to backtrack without admitting error

### Historical Examples

**Holocaust:** "Just following orders" defense by Nazis
- Ordinary soldiers, not psychopaths
- Incremental escalation of persecution
- Authority legitimacy through state apparatus

**My Lai Massacre (Vietnam):** US soldiers killed unarmed civilians
- Orders from superior officers
- Group participation normalized behavior
- Remote command authority

**Stanford Prison Experiment:** Guards became abusive in role
- Authority of experimenter and role
- Institutional setting legitimized actions
- Gradual escalation of aggressive behavior

### Resisting Destructive Obedience

**Individual Strategies:**
- Question authority legitimacy
- Increase psychological distance from authority
- Increase psychological proximity to victims
- Seek peer support for resistance
- Define personal responsibility boundaries in advance

**Institutional Safeguards:**
- Distribute authority (checks and balances)
- Encourage questioning and dissent
- Increase transparency and accountability
- Create clear ethical guidelines
- Provide whistleblower protections

## Group Dynamics

### Social Facilitation

**Definition:** Performance changes in presence of others

**Pattern:**
- Simple/well-learned tasks: Performance improves
- Complex/novel tasks: Performance worsens

**Mechanism:** Arousal increases dominant response tendency
- Easy tasks: Dominant response is correct → improvement
- Hard tasks: Dominant response is error → impairment

**Example:** Experienced basketball player shoots better with crowd (facilitation); novice shoots worse (impairment)

### Social Loafing

**Definition:** Reduced individual effort in group settings when contributions aren't identifiable

**Rope Pulling Experiment:**
- Alone: 100% effort
- Groups of 2: 93% individual effort
- Groups of 8: 49% individual effort

**Conditions:**
- Individual contributions not identifiable
- Task low personal relevance
- Group members seen as capable
- Individualist cultural context

**Mitigation:**
- Make individual contributions identifiable
- Increase task meaningfulness
- Create smaller teams
- Strengthen group cohesion
- Set clear individual standards

### Groupthink

**Definition:** Deteriorated decision quality due to pressure for unanimity overriding realistic appraisal

**Symptoms:**
1. Illusion of invulnerability (overconfidence)
2. Collective rationalization (dismiss warnings)
3. Belief in group morality (ignore ethical concerns)
4. Stereotyped views of out-groups
5. Direct pressure on dissenters
6. Self-censorship
7. Illusion of unanimity (silence = consent)
8. Self-appointed mindguards (shield from contradictory information)

**Famous Cases:**
- **Bay of Pigs Invasion** - Kennedy advisors dismissed concerns, leading to disaster
- **Challenger Disaster** - NASA ignored engineer warnings about O-rings
- **Financial Crisis 2008** - Wall Street consensus ignored housing bubble risks

**Prevention:**
- Leader encourages critical evaluation
- Assign devil's advocate role
- Divide into subgroups
- Invite outside experts
- Hold second-chance meeting before finalizing

### Group Polarization

**Definition:** Group discussion shifts average position toward more extreme version of initial tendency

**Risky Shift:** Groups make riskier decisions than individuals (original finding)

**Broader Pattern:** Shifts toward whichever pole initially favored
- Conservative group → more conservative
- Liberal group → more liberal
- Risk-averse group → more cautious

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Social Comparison** - Discover others share views, shift further to distinguish self
2. **Persuasive Arguments** - Hear new reasons supporting initial tendency
3. **Repeated Expression** - Stating view increases commitment

**Implications:**
- Online echo chambers amplify polarization
- Juries reach more extreme verdicts than individual jurors
- Social movements become more radical over time

### Deindividuation

**Definition:** Loss of self-awareness and individual accountability in group contexts, leading to disinhibited behavior

**Conditions:**
- Anonymity (masks, uniforms, darkness)
- Large group size
- Arousal/excitement
- Reduced self-awareness
- Diffused responsibility

**Examples:**
- Riots and mob violence
- Online trolling and harassment
- Military atrocities in uniform
- Halloween vandalism with masks

**Classic Study:** Women administered longer shocks when wearing hoods (deindividuated) vs. name tags and identifiable

**Prevention:**
- Increase self-awareness (mirrors, cameras, name tags)
- Reduce anonymity
- Establish individual accountability
- Create strong group norms against destructive behavior

## Social Influence

### Minority Influence

**Definition:** How small consistent minorities create social change despite majority opposition

**Moscovici Color Perception Study:**
- Two confederates consistently call blue slides "green"
- 32% of majority participants shift toward "green" at least once
- 8% of responses shifted
- Control condition: <1% error rate

**Conditions for Minority Influence:**
1. **Consistency** - Unwavering position over time
2. **Confidence** - Demonstrate certainty in position
3. **Defection from Majority** - Ex-majority members join minority
4. **Relevance** - Issue matters to group
5. **Flexibility** - Appear open-minded, not rigid

**Process:**
- Majority creates compliance through normative pressure (fit in)
- Minority creates conversion through informational influence (reconsider)
- Minority influence slower but leads to genuine belief change

**Historical Examples:**
- Suffrage movements
- Civil rights movements
- Environmental movements
- Scientific paradigm shifts

### Social Norms

**Definition:** Unwritten rules about acceptable behavior in social groups

**Descriptive Norms:** What people actually do
- "Most guests reuse towels"
- Leverages conformity and social proof

**Injunctive Norms:** What people approve/disapprove
- "Please protect environment by reusing towels"
- Leverages desire for social approval

**Effectiveness:** Descriptive norms typically more influential than injunctive

**Norm Violation Consequences:**
- Social sanctions (criticism, exclusion)
- Damaged reputation
- Loss of status
- Formal punishment (in codified norms)

**Changing Social Norms:**
1. Make new behavior visible (models)
2. Highlight early adopters
3. Connect to existing values
4. Create social pressure toward new norm
5. Reduce visibility of old norm

## Prosocial Behavior and Helping

### Bystander Effect

**Definition:** Individuals less likely to help when others present

**Kitty Genovese Case (1964):**
- Woman murdered outside apartment building
- 38 witnesses allegedly watched without intervening
- (Later investigations complicated narrative, but sparked research)

**Lab Studies:**
- Smoke filling room: Alone, 75% report; groups of 3, 38% report
- Seizure over intercom: Alone, 85% help; 5 people, 31% help

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Diffusion of Responsibility** - Others will help; not my job
2. **Pluralistic Ignorance** - Others calm = situation not emergency
3. **Evaluation Apprehension** - Fear of looking foolish if misinterpreting situation

**Intervention Increases:**
- Direct requests ("You in the red shirt, call 911")
- Smaller groups or alone
- Clear emergency (ambiguity reduced)
- Competence (trained individuals)
- Personal responsibility cues

### Altruism Motivations

**Evolutionary Explanations:**
- Kin selection (help genetic relatives)
- Reciprocal altruism (expect future return)
- Costly signaling (demonstrate fitness/resources)

**Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis:**
- Empathy produces genuine altruistic motivation
- Help even without personal benefit
- Reduces other's distress, not just one's own

**Negative State Relief:**
- Help to reduce own distress at witnessing suffering
- Egoistic motivation disguised as altruism

**Key Terms**

- **Conformity** - Changing beliefs/behaviors to match group
- **Obedience** - Following direct orders from authority figure
- **Social Facilitation** - Performance changes in presence of others
- **Groupthink** - Deteriorated decision quality from pressure for unanimity
- **Group Polarization** - Discussion shifts position toward extreme
- **Deindividuation** - Loss of self-awareness and accountability in groups
- **Bystander Effect** - Reduced helping when others present
- **Diffusion of Responsibility** - Individual feels less accountable when others present

## Summary

Social psychology reveals powerful influences of real, imagined, or implied presence of others. Asch's conformity studies demonstrate normative and informational social influence, with conformity reduced by dissent. Milgram's obedience experiments show ordinary people harm others under authority pressure through gradual escalation, diffusion of responsibility, and authority legitimacy. Group dynamics include social facilitation/loafing, groupthink deteriorating decision quality, polarization amplifying initial tendencies, and deindividuation reducing accountability. Minority influence creates social change through consistency and confidence. Bystander effect reduces helping through diffused responsibility and pluralistic ignorance. Understanding these patterns enables resistance to destructive influences, improved group decision-making, and effective social change strategies.
