# Glossary of Key Terms

## A

**Anchoring Bias**
Cognitive bias where initial information disproportionately influences subsequent judgments. In negotiations, the first offer anchors the final settlement; in estimates, initial numbers bias final values even when arbitrary.

**Approach Goals**
Goals framed in terms of desired end-states to achieve (e.g., "exercise 30 minutes daily") rather than undesired states to avoid. More effective than avoidance goals for sustained motivation and well-being.

**Asymmetric Dominance Effect (Decoy Effect)**
Phenomenon where introducing inferior option (decoy) increases preference for similar but superior option. Common in pricing where medium option makes expensive option appear better value.

**Authority Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: people comply with credible authorities and expertise signals including titles, uniforms, and status symbols. Effectiveness increases with trustworthy authority admitting weaknesses or arguing against self-interest.

**Availability Bias (Availability Heuristic)**
Mental shortcut judging frequency or probability by ease of recalling examples. Recent, vivid, emotional, or personally experienced events judged as more common than they are, distorting risk perception.

## B

**BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)**
Strongest alternative course of action if negotiation fails. Determines negotiating power—accept only agreements better than BATNA. Improving BATNA increases leverage.

**Behavioral Economics**
Field integrating psychology with economics to explain systematic deviations from rational choice. Studies how cognitive biases, social factors, and limited willpower influence economic decisions.

**Behaviorism**
Psychological approach focusing on observable behavior rather than internal mental states. Founded by Watson, developed by Skinner. Emphasizes learned behaviors through conditioning and reinforcement.

**Bounded Rationality**
Herbert Simon's concept that decision-making is constrained by cognitive limits, time pressure, and information costs. Humans "satisfice" (accept good enough) rather than optimize.

**Bystander Effect**
Phenomenon where individuals less likely to help when others present. Caused by diffusion of responsibility ("someone else will help"), pluralistic ignorance (others' calm suggests no emergency), and evaluation apprehension (fear of looking foolish).

## C

**Classical Conditioning**
Learning process where neutral stimulus becomes associated with automatic response through repeated pairing. Pavlov's dogs: bell (neutral) paired with food (automatic salivation) until bell alone triggers salivation.

**Cognitive Bias**
Systematic pattern of deviation from rational judgment. Unlike random errors, biases consistently push decisions in predictable directions due to mental shortcuts, social influences, and information processing limits.

**Cognitive Dissonance**
Uncomfortable tension from holding contradictory beliefs or acting inconsistently with beliefs. Motivates attitude or behavior change to reduce dissonance and restore consistency.

**Cognitive Load**
Mental effort required for task completion. High cognitive load impairs performance, decision quality, and self-control. Design should minimize unnecessary load through simplification and chunking.

**Commitment and Consistency Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: people desire to act consistently with prior commitments, especially public and effortful ones. Underlies foot-in-door technique and low-ball technique.

**COM-B Model**
Behavior change framework by Michie: Behavior requires Capability (physical and psychological), Opportunity (physical and social), and Motivation (reflective and automatic). Diagnose barriers and match interventions.

**Confirmation Bias**
Tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information confirming pre-existing beliefs while dismissing contradictory evidence. Manifests in biased search, interpretation, memory, and evaluation.

**Conformity**
Changing beliefs or behaviors to match group norms. Types include compliance (public only), identification (valued relationships), and internalization (genuine belief change). Asch experiments demonstrated power of majority influence.

## D

**Decoy Effect**
See Asymmetric Dominance Effect.

**Default Options**
Pre-selected choices that take effect if no active selection made. Powerful nudge leveraging status quo bias and inertia. Examples: organ donation opt-out, retirement savings auto-enrollment.

**Deindividuation**
Loss of self-awareness and individual accountability in group contexts, leading to disinhibited behavior. Occurs with anonymity, large groups, and reduced self-awareness. Examples: mob behavior, online trolling.

**Diffusion of Responsibility**
Phenomenon where individuals feel less personal responsibility when others present. Contributes to bystander effect (assume others will help) and enables harmful obedience (authority claims responsibility).

**Dual Process Theory**
Framework distinguishing two cognitive systems: System 1 (fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical, logical). Interaction between systems determines decision quality.

**Dunning-Kruger Effect**
Cognitive bias where unskilled individuals overestimate competence while experts underestimate relative ability. Requires expertise to recognize expertise; novices lack metacognitive awareness of limitations.

## E

**EAST Framework**
Behavioral insights approach: Make interventions Easy (reduce friction), Attractive (appealing design), Social (leverage norms), Timely (prompt at decision moment).

**Ego Depletion**
Theory that self-control relies on limited cognitive resources that deplete with use. After sustained mental effort, self-control and analytical thinking decline temporarily.

**Endowment Effect**
Phenomenon where ownership increases perceived value due to loss aversion. People demand more to give up owned objects than they'd pay to acquire them. Underlies free trial effectiveness and money-back guarantees.

**Expected Utility Theory**
Classical economic model: rational decision-makers calculate expected value (probability × outcome) and choose option maximizing utility. Behavioral research reveals systematic violations.

**Extrinsic Motivation**
Engaging in behavior to obtain external rewards or avoid punishments rather than inherent satisfaction. Types range from external regulation (purely for reward/punishment) to integrated regulation (aligns with self-identity).

## F

**Foot-in-Door Technique**
Persuasion method: small initial request (accepted) followed by larger related request. Initial compliance creates self-perception consistent with larger request, increasing acceptance.

**Framing Effect**
Presenting logically equivalent information differently influences decisions. Types include gain vs. loss framing, temporal framing, attribute framing, and goal framing. Loss frames motivate detection behaviors; gain frames motivate prevention.

**Fundamental Attribution Error**
Overweighting dispositional factors and underweighting situational factors when explaining others' behavior (reverse pattern for self-explanations). "They're rude" vs. "I was stressed."

## G

**Gamification**
Applying game elements (points, badges, leaderboards, challenges) to non-game contexts to increase engagement and motivation. Most effective when intrinsic interest already exists.

**Group Polarization**
Phenomenon where group discussion shifts average position toward more extreme version of initial tendency. Caused by social comparison (distinguish self) and persuasive arguments (new supporting reasons).

**Groupthink**
Deteriorated decision quality from pressure for unanimity overriding realistic appraisal. Symptoms include illusion of invulnerability, stereotyped views of outgroups, self-censorship, and direct pressure on dissenters. Famous cases: Bay of Pigs, Challenger disaster.

**Growth Mindset**
Belief that abilities develop through effort rather than being fixed traits. Contrasts with fixed mindset. Associated with embracing challenges, viewing failure as learning opportunity, and persisting through obstacles.

## H

**Habit**
Automatic behavior triggered by contextual cues, requiring minimal conscious awareness or motivation. Habit loop: Cue → Routine → Reward. Forms over average 66 days through basal ganglia automatization.

**Habit Stacking**
Behavior change technique: link new habit to existing habit. Format: "After [existing habit], I will [new habit]." Leverages established neural pathways and natural sequencing.

**Health Belief Model**
Framework predicting health behavior based on perceived susceptibility, severity, benefits, barriers, cues to action, and self-efficacy. Behavior occurs when threat high, benefits outweigh barriers, and sufficient self-efficacy.

**Heuristic**
Mental shortcut enabling fast decisions with minimal cognitive effort. Usually effective but creates systematic biases. Examples: availability, representativeness, recognition, anchoring.

**Hindsight Bias**
After outcome known, believing it was predictable ("I knew it all along"). Consequence: overconfidence in future predictions, inadequate learning from surprises.

## I

**Implementation Intention**
If-then plan specifying when and how to act. Format: "When [situation], I will [behavior]." Pre-decides response to cue, reducing in-moment willpower demand. Doubles success rates vs. vague goals.

**Intrinsic Motivation**
Engaging in behavior for inherent satisfaction rather than external consequences. Activity is its own reward. Supported by autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Self-Determination Theory).

## L

**Liking Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: people comply with requests from those they like. Factors increasing liking include physical attractiveness, similarity, compliments, familiarity, cooperation, and positive association.

**Loss Aversion**
Losses loom larger than equivalent gains (approximately 2:1 ratio). Core component of prospect theory. Drives endowment effect, framing effects, and risk attitude reversal (risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses).

**Low-Ball Technique**
Persuasion method: attractive offer induces commitment, then hidden costs revealed or offer worsened. Commitment maintained despite worse deal due to consistency pressure and sunk cost.

## M

**Mental Accounting**
Treating money differently based on source, use, or mental category. Windfall money spent more freely than earned income; gift cards treated as "play money." Explains seemingly irrational financial decisions.

**Mere Exposure Effect**
Repeated exposure increases liking (absent negative context). Explains advertising frequency effectiveness. Face recognition studies show more exposures increase attractiveness ratings.

**Minority Influence**
How small consistent minorities create social change despite majority opposition. Requires consistency, confidence, defection from majority, relevance, and flexible approach. Creates genuine belief change vs. majority's surface compliance.

## N

**Nudge**
Intervention altering choice architecture to predictably influence behavior without restricting options or significantly changing economic incentives. Examples: default options, simplification, salience cues, social norms.

**Normative Social Influence**
Conforming to fit in and gain social approval (even without believing majority correct). Contrasts with informational social influence (conforming because assuming majority has better information).

## O

**Obedience**
Following direct orders from authority figure. Milgram experiments demonstrated 65% of ordinary people administer maximum shock under authority pressure. Factors increasing obedience: authority proximity, victim distance, institutional legitimacy, gradual escalation.

**Operant Conditioning**
Learning process where behavior frequency changes based on consequences. Reinforcement (increases behavior) vs. punishment (decreases behavior). Positive (add stimulus) vs. negative (remove stimulus). Developed by B.F. Skinner.

**Overjustification Effect**
External rewards undermine intrinsic motivation for previously enjoyed activities. Mechanism: attribution shifts from "I enjoy this" to "I do this for reward." Most problematic when rewards expected, tangible, and controlling.

**Overconfidence Bias**
Excessive confidence in judgment accuracy. Manifestations: overprecision (confidence intervals too narrow), overestimation (abilities above actual), overplacement (rate self above average). Entrepreneurs systematically underestimate failure probability.

## P

**Pluralistic Ignorance**
Situation where individuals privately reject norm but incorrectly assume others accept it, leading everyone to go along. Contributes to bystander effect (others' calm suggests no emergency despite private concern).

**Precommitment Device (Commitment Device)**
Voluntary restriction limiting future choices to increase goal achievement likelihood. Types: financial stakes, social stakes, technological blocks, physical removal of temptations.

**Progressive Disclosure**
UX design pattern revealing information gradually rather than all at once. Reduces cognitive load by showing only immediately relevant content. Advanced features behind menus.

**Prospect Theory**
Kahneman and Tversky's alternative to expected utility theory. Key insights: reference dependence (outcomes evaluated as gains/losses from reference point), loss aversion (losses weigh more than equivalent gains), diminishing sensitivity (marginal value decreases).

**Psychological Reactance**
Motivational state when freedom restricted, increasing desire for forbidden option. Underlies scarcity effect ("can't have it" makes it more desirable).

## R

**Rational Choice Theory**
Economic model assuming complete information, stable preferences, utility maximization, and perfect computation. Behavioral research reveals systematic violations.

**Reciprocity Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: people feel obligated to return favors, gifts, or concessions. Even uninvited favors create obligation. Underlies door-in-face technique (reciprocal concessions).

**Recognition Heuristic**
If one option recognized and another isn't, infer recognized option has higher value. Effective when recognition correlates with quality. Fails when recognition indicates negative factors.

**Reinforcement**
Process of increasing behavior frequency through consequences. Positive reinforcement (add desirable stimulus), negative reinforcement (remove aversive stimulus). Schedules include continuous, fixed ratio/interval, variable ratio/interval.

**Representativeness Heuristic**
Judging probability by similarity to mental prototype. Base judgments on typicality rather than statistical base rates. Creates base rate neglect and conjunction fallacy.

## S

**Satisficing**
Accepting first adequate option rather than searching for optimal solution. Strategy under bounded rationality when costs of continued search exceed marginal benefits. Contrasts with maximizing.

**Scarcity Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: opportunities appear more valuable when availability limited. Types include limited quantity, limited time, access restriction, and competition. Newly scarce more powerful than always scarce.

**Self-Determination Theory**
Motivational framework identifying three universal psychological needs: autonomy (choice/volition), competence (mastery/effectiveness), relatedness (connection to others). Satisfying needs supports intrinsic motivation.

**Social Facilitation**
Performance changes in presence of others. Simple/well-learned tasks improve; complex/novel tasks worsen. Mechanism: arousal increases dominant response tendency.

**Social Loafing**
Reduced individual effort in group settings when contributions aren't identifiable. Example: rope pulling effort drops from 100% alone to 49% in groups of 8. Mitigated by identifiable contributions.

**Social Norm**
Unwritten rule about acceptable behavior. Descriptive norms (what people actually do) typically more influential than injunctive norms (what people approve/disapprove).

**Social Proof Principle**
Cialdini's influence principle: people look to others' behavior to determine correct action, especially under uncertainty. More effective when others similar, multiple people involved, and situation ambiguous.

**Stages of Change (Transtheoretical Model)**
Behavior change model: Precontemplation (unaware), Contemplation (ambivalent), Preparation (intending soon), Action (actively changing), Maintenance (sustaining 6+ months), Termination (no temptation). Interventions must match stage.

**Status Quo Bias**
Preference for current state over change. Caused by loss aversion, anticipated regret, and cognitive effort of evaluation. Underlies power of default options.

**Sunk Cost Fallacy**
Continuing investment because of past expenditure (which cannot be recovered) rather than evaluating future costs/benefits. Rational approach ignores sunk costs. Example: attending bad movie because ticket purchased.

**System 1 / System 2**
Dual process theory distinction. System 1: fast, automatic, intuitive, effortless, emotional, parallel processing. System 2: slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful, logical, serial processing. Interaction determines decision quality.

## T

**Temptation Bundling**
Pairing tempting activity with beneficial behavior to increase motivation. Format: "I only allow myself [temptation] while doing [beneficial behavior]." Example: favorite show only while exercising.

## U

**Utility Maximization**
Rational choice assumption that decision-makers choose options maximizing expected utility (satisfaction). Behavioral economics documents systematic violations through framing effects, reference dependence, loss aversion.

## Summary

This glossary provides quick reference for behavioral psychology terminology. Terms span decision-making (heuristics, biases, bounded rationality), social influence (conformity, obedience, group dynamics), persuasion (Cialdini's principles, framing, nudges), motivation (intrinsic/extrinsic, self-determination theory), and behavior change (habits, commitment devices, stages of change). Understanding this vocabulary enables precise communication about psychological principles and their applications across UX design, public policy, negotiations, marketing, and personal development. For deeper exploration, consult foundational books, academic sources, and classic experiments listed in references section.
