# Decision-Making

## Overview

Decision-making is the cognitive process of selecting among alternatives. Classical economic theory assumed humans make rational decisions by maximizing expected utility. Behavioral research reveals systematic deviations from rationality. Humans use mental shortcuts (heuristics), are constrained by limited cognitive resources (bounded rationality), and employ dual processing systems that sometimes conflict. Understanding these mechanisms explains everyday choices and enables better decision architecture.

## Rational Choice Theory

### Core Assumptions
1. **Complete Information** - Decision-makers have access to all relevant data
2. **Stable Preferences** - Goals and values remain consistent over time
3. **Utility Maximization** - Choices optimize outcomes based on preferences
4. **Perfect Computation** - Ability to calculate optimal choices instantly

### Expected Utility Theory
Decision value = Σ(Probability × Outcome Value)

**Example:** Investment decision
- Option A: 100% chance of $100 = $100 expected value
- Option B: 50% chance of $200 = $100 expected value
- Rational actor is indifferent between options with equal expected value

### Limitations in Real Behavior
- **Framing Effects** - Choices change based on how options are presented
- **Preference Reversals** - Rankings change with elicitation method
- **Time Inconsistency** - Present preferences conflict with past/future preferences
- **Context Dependence** - Irrelevant alternatives influence choices

## Bounded Rationality

### Herbert Simon's Framework
Humans are "satisficers" not optimizers - they seek acceptable solutions rather than optimal ones due to:

| Constraint | Description | Impact |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| **Cognitive Limits** | Working memory holds ~7 items | Cannot process all information simultaneously |
| **Time Pressure** | Decisions needed before complete analysis | Rely on quick heuristics over thorough evaluation |
| **Information Costs** | Gathering data requires effort and resources | Search stops at "good enough" rather than exhaustive |
| **Computational Limits** | Complex calculations exceed mental capacity | Use approximations and rules of thumb |

### Satisficing Strategy
1. Set aspiration level (minimum acceptable outcome)
2. Search sequentially through options
3. Select first option meeting aspiration level
4. Stop search (no exhaustive comparison)

**Example:** Job hunting - Accept first offer meeting salary, location, and role requirements rather than interviewing at every possible company.

## Heuristics

Mental shortcuts enabling fast decisions with minimal cognitive effort. Usually effective but create systematic biases.

### Availability Heuristic
**Definition:** Judge probability by ease of recalling examples

**Mechanism:** Recent, vivid, or emotional events are more mentally accessible

**Examples:**
- After news coverage of plane crashes, people overestimate flight risk and choose driving (statistically more dangerous)
- Investors overweight recent market performance when predicting future returns

**Bias Created:** Overestimate frequency of memorable events, underestimate mundane risks

### Representativeness Heuristic
**Definition:** Judge probability by similarity to mental prototype

**Mechanism:** Base judgments on typicality rather than statistical base rates

**Example - Linda Problem:**
> Linda is 31, single, outspoken, and philosophy major. As student, concerned with discrimination and social justice.
>
> What's more probable?
> A) Linda is a bank teller
> B) Linda is a bank teller and active in feminist movement

Most choose B despite violating probability theory (conjunction cannot be more likely than constituent). Linda's description "represents" feminists more than generic bank tellers.

**Bias Created:** Base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy

### Anchoring and Adjustment
**Definition:** Estimates biased toward initial reference point

**Mechanism:** Start with anchor value, adjust insufficiently toward true value

**Experiment:** Spin wheel showing random number (10 or 65), then estimate % of African nations in UN
- Wheel showed 10: Average estimate = 25%
- Wheel showed 65: Average estimate = 45%
- Random anchor influenced estimates despite obvious irrelevance

**Applications:**
- Negotiation: First offer anchors final price
- Retail: Original price anchors perceived discount value
- Real estate: Asking price anchors buyer offers

### Recognition Heuristic
**Definition:** If one option is recognized and another isn't, infer the recognized option has higher value

**When Effective:** Recognition correlates with quality (city size, brand reliability)

**Example:** Stock picking - Recognized companies often outperform obscure ones in certain markets

**Limitation:** Fails when recognition correlates with negative factors (media coverage of corporate scandals)

## Dual Process Theory

### System 1: Intuitive Thinking
- **Fast** - Operates automatically in milliseconds
- **Effortless** - No conscious deliberation
- **Parallel** - Multiple processes simultaneously
- **Emotional** - Incorporates feelings and affect
- **Associative** - Triggered by contextual cues

**Examples:** Recognizing faces, detecting hostility in voice, driving on empty road, understanding simple sentences

### System 2: Analytical Thinking
- **Slow** - Requires deliberate processing
- **Effortful** - Depletes cognitive resources
- **Serial** - One step at a time
- **Logical** - Rule-based reasoning
- **Controlled** - Consciously directed attention

**Examples:** Complex calculations, comparing product features, filling tax forms, parking in narrow space

### Interaction Patterns

| Scenario | System 1 | System 2 | Outcome |
|----------|----------|----------|---------|
| **Routine** | Generates intuitive response | Accepts without scrutiny | Fast, accurate decision |
| **Conflict Detection** | Generates incorrect response | Detects error and overrides | Correct but effortful decision |
| **Cognitive Busy** | Generates biased response | Too depleted to correct | Biased decision |
| **Illusion** | Generates incorrect response | Fails to detect error | Confident but wrong decision |

### Ego Depletion
System 2 relies on limited cognitive resources. After sustained mental effort, self-control and analytical thinking decline.

**Experiment:** Judges grant parole to 65% of first cases heard, drops to ~10% before breaks, returns to 65% after meals. Decision quality degrades with mental fatigue.

**Practical Implications:**
- Make important decisions when mentally fresh
- Reduce cognitive load to improve decision quality
- Automate routine decisions to preserve mental resources

## Decision-Making Biases

### Status Quo Bias
**Definition:** Preference for current state over change

**Causes:** Loss aversion, anticipated regret, cognitive effort of evaluation

**Example:** 70% of German organ donors (opt-out default) vs. 12% of Americans (opt-in). Decision identical, outcomes dramatically different due to defaults.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
**Definition:** Continued investment because of past expenditure (which cannot be recovered)

**Rational Approach:** Consider only future costs and benefits

**Example:** Attending concert despite feeling ill because ticket already purchased. Past cost is sunk; decision should compare future enjoyment vs. rest benefit.

### Confirmation Bias in Decision-Making
**Definition:** Seeking information supporting pre-existing preference

**Mechanism:** Biased search process before decision made

**Example:** Favoring job candidate, interviewer asks questions likely to elicit positive answers and interprets ambiguous responses favorably.

## Improving Decision Quality

### Pre-Commitment Strategies
Lock in future behavior before temptation arises
- Automatic savings transfers
- Website blockers during work hours
- Pre-packaged meal portions

### Checklists
Reduce errors by systematizing decision process
- Surgical safety checklists reduce complications by 35%
- Pilot pre-flight checklists prevent accidents
- Investment evaluation criteria sheets reduce emotional trading

### Decision Journals
Track predictions and outcomes to identify personal biases
- Record confidence levels with predictions
- Review accuracy to calibrate future confidence
- Identify patterns in errors

### Outside View
Base predictions on reference class statistics rather than case-specific details
- "How long do similar projects typically take?" vs. "How long will this specific project take?"
- Reduces planning fallacy and overconfidence

### Devil's Advocate
Assign someone to argue against preferred option
- Surfaces unconsidered risks
- Reduces groupthink
- Improves decision robustness

**Key Terms**

- **Bounded Rationality** - Decision-making constrained by cognitive limits, time, and information costs
- **Heuristic** - Mental shortcut enabling fast decisions with minimal cognitive effort
- **Satisficing** - Accepting first adequate option rather than searching for optimal solution
- **System 1** - Fast, automatic, intuitive cognitive processing
- **System 2** - Slow, deliberate, analytical cognitive processing
- **Anchoring** - Bias toward initial reference point in estimates
- **Availability Heuristic** - Judging probability by ease of recalling examples

---

## Summary

Human decision-making systematically deviates from rational choice models. Bounded rationality constrains information processing, leading to satisficing strategies. Heuristics like availability, representativeness, and anchoring enable fast decisions but create predictable biases. Dual process theory distinguishes intuitive System 1 from analytical System 2, with interaction patterns determining decision quality. Understanding these mechanisms enables better choice architecture through defaults, pre-commitment, checklists, and bias-correcting procedures. Effective decision-making balances speed and accuracy by knowing when to trust intuition and when to engage deliberate analysis.
