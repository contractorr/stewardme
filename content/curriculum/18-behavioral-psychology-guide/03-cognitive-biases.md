# Cognitive Biases

## Overview

Cognitive biases are systematic patterns of deviation from rational judgment. Unlike random errors, biases consistently push decisions in predictable directions. They emerge from mental shortcuts (heuristics), social influences, emotional states, and information processing limits. While biases evolved as adaptive mechanisms enabling fast decisions in ancestral environments, they create suboptimal outcomes in modern contexts. Understanding specific biases improves decision quality, reduces manipulation vulnerability, and enhances persuasion effectiveness.

## Anchoring Bias

### Definition
Over-reliance on first piece of information (anchor) when making decisions. Subsequent judgments assimilate toward anchor even when it's arbitrary or irrelevant.

### Mechanisms
1. **Insufficient Adjustment** - Start with anchor, adjust incrementally but stop before reaching true value
2. **Selective Accessibility** - Anchor makes consistent information more mentally accessible
3. **Numeric Priming** - Any number activates magnitude-compatible associations

### Classic Experiments

**UN Nations Study (Tversky & Kahneman):**
- Spin wheel showing 10 or 65 (obviously random)
- Estimate % of African nations in UN
- Low anchor group: Average 25%
- High anchor group: Average 45%
- Random number influenced estimates by 20 percentage points

**Real Estate Valuation:**
- Professional appraisers shown same house
- Listing prices varied: $119,900 vs $149,900
- Appraisals differed by $20,000+ based solely on arbitrary listing price
- Even experts with training susceptible to anchoring

### Real-World Applications

| Context | Anchor | Effect |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **Negotiation** | First offer | Final settlement gravitates toward initial number |
| **Retail Pricing** | "Was $199" | $99 seems like better value (vs. just "$99") |
| **Salary Discussion** | Current compensation | New offers anchor to existing pay rather than market value |
| **Litigation** | Damage request | Jury awards correlate with plaintiff's requested amount |
| **Real Estate** | Asking price | Buyer offers cluster around listing price |

### Mitigation Strategies
- Generate independent estimate before seeing anchor
- Consider opposite extreme to widen range
- Focus on absolute value rather than relative comparison
- Use multiple reference points
- Be first in negotiations to set advantageous anchor

## Confirmation Bias

### Definition
Tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information confirming pre-existing beliefs while dismissing contradictory evidence.

### Four Manifestations

**1. Biased Search**
- Actively seek confirming evidence
- Avoid disconfirming sources
- Example: Researching political issues only from preferred media outlets

**2. Biased Interpretation**
- Interpret ambiguous evidence as supporting belief
- Apply higher scrutiny to contradictory information
- Example: Sports fans perceive referee calls as biased against their team

**3. Biased Memory**
- Better recall of confirming information
- Forget or minimize contradictory data
- Example: Remember successful predictions, forget failures

**4. Biased Evaluation**
- Accept confirming evidence uncritically
- Demand higher standard for contradictory evidence
- Example: Accept health claims supporting diet choice, scrutinize criticisms

### Classic Studies

**Wason Selection Task:**
- Four cards: E, K, 4, 7
- Rule: "If vowel on one side, even number on other"
- Which cards to flip to test rule?
- Correct: E (confirm vowel→even) and 7 (check not odd→vowel)
- Most choose: E and 4 (seeking confirmation, not falsification)

**Political Belief Study:**
- Show mixed evidence on death penalty effectiveness
- Pro-death penalty participants: Evidence confirms it works
- Anti-death penalty participants: Same evidence confirms it doesn't work
- Both groups more convinced of original position after seeing identical mixed evidence

### Domains of Impact

**Medical Diagnosis**
- Doctors anchor on initial hypothesis, seek confirming symptoms, downplay contradictory tests
- Diagnostic errors more common when first impression strong

**Investment Decisions**
- Seek news confirming stock pick quality
- Dismiss warnings as temporary setbacks
- Hold losing positions too long

**Scientific Research**
- Experimenters unconsciously bias procedures toward expected results
- Publication bias favors confirming studies over null results
- Paradigm shifts resisted despite contradictory evidence

**Legal Proceedings**
- Investigators focus on evidence implicating prime suspect
- Exculpatory evidence discounted or rationalized
- Wrongful convictions result

### Debiasing Techniques
- **Consider the opposite** - Actively generate counterarguments
- **Pre-commitment** - Specify disconfirming evidence in advance
- **Devil's advocate** - Assign role of arguing against position
- **Blind analysis** - Analyze data before knowing which hypothesis it supports
- **Falsification mindset** - Seek evidence that could disprove belief

## Availability Bias

### Definition
Judge frequency or probability by ease of bringing examples to mind. If instances are easily remembered, they're perceived as common.

### What Makes Information Available?

| Factor | Mechanism | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Recency** | Recent events more accessible | Shark attack news increases beach fear for weeks |
| **Vividness** | Dramatic events more memorable | Plane crashes more available than car accidents despite rarity |
| **Personal Experience** | Direct experience more salient | Knowing crime victim increases perceived crime rate |
| **Media Coverage** | Repeated exposure increases accessibility | Terrorism fear exceeds statistical risk due to coverage |
| **Emotional Impact** | Strong emotions enhance memory encoding | Tragic stories remembered better than statistics |

### Experimental Evidence

**Word Frequency Study:**
- Does "r" appear more often as first letter or third letter in English words?
- Most answer "first" (road, river, rain vs. car, street, through)
- Actually 3x more common in third position
- Words starting with "r" more easily retrieved

**Cause of Death Estimates:**
- Participants overestimate: Homicide, tornado, flood (dramatic, covered)
- Participants underestimate: Diabetes, stroke, asthma (common, undramatic)
- Media coverage distorts perceived risk landscape

### Practical Consequences

**Risk Perception**
- Overestimate dramatic, rare risks (terrorism, shark attacks)
- Underestimate mundane, common risks (heart disease, car accidents)
- Policy attention misallocated to available risks

**Insurance Purchases**
- Flood insurance spikes after floods, then declines
- Decision based on availability rather than stable probability
- Insurance companies profit from availability fluctuations

**Career Choices**
- Visible professions (lawyer, doctor) overestimated as common
- Behind-scenes jobs (logistics coordinator, database admin) undervalued
- Career guidance skewed toward available options

**Product Development**
- Vocal minority complaints drive features
- Silent majority preferences neglected
- Squeaky wheel availability bias

### Correcting for Availability
- Consult base rate statistics before judging frequency
- Consider silent majority unreported in dramatic cases
- Use structured data collection rather than anecdotal memory
- Distinguish emotional impact from actual probability
- Track decisions and outcomes to identify availability-driven errors

## Loss Aversion

### Definition
Losses loom larger than equivalent gains. Losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good. Asymmetry ratio approximately 2:1.

### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky)

**Value Function Characteristics:**
1. **Reference Dependence** - Outcomes evaluated as gains/losses from reference point, not absolute states
2. **Loss Aversion** - Curve steeper for losses than gains
3. **Diminishing Sensitivity** - Difference between $0-$100 feels larger than $900-$1000

**Graph Representation:**
```
   Value
     |
Gain |        /
     |       /
     |      /
     |     /
     |____/____ Outcome
     |   /
     |  /
Loss | /
     |/
```

### Classic Demonstrations

**Mug Experiment:**
- Give participants mugs
- Owners selling price: Average $7
- Non-owners buying price: Average $3
- Same mug, 2x difference based on endowment
- **Endowment Effect** - Ownership creates loss aversion

**Gamble Choices:**
- Option A: Certain $50
- Option B: 50% chance $100, 50% chance $0
- Most choose A (both have $50 expected value)
- But for losses:
  - Option C: Certain loss $50
  - Option D: 50% chance lose $100, 50% chance lose $0
  - Most choose D (risk-seeking to avoid certain loss)

### Risk Attitude Reversal

| Domain | Behavior | Reason |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Gains** | Risk-averse | Prefer certain $50 over 50% chance $100 |
| **Losses** | Risk-seeking | Prefer 50% chance lose $100 over certain loss $50 |

**Implications:**
- Escalation of commitment (risk-seeking to recoup losses)
- Hesitancy to sell losing stocks (avoiding realized loss)
- Preference for sure gains vs. gambling on larger gains

### Framing Effects

**Disease Outbreak Scenario:**

*Gain Frame:*
- Program A: Save 200 people (certain)
- Program B: 1/3 chance save 600, 2/3 chance save 0
- 72% choose A

*Loss Frame (identical outcomes):*
- Program C: 400 people die (certain)
- Program D: 1/3 chance 0 die, 2/3 chance 600 die
- 78% choose D

Framing as gains induces risk aversion; framing as losses induces risk-seeking.

### Applications

**Marketing**
- "Don't miss out" more effective than "Get this deal"
- Free trials leverage endowment effect (cancel = loss)
- Money-back guarantees reduce loss fear

**Policy**
- Default opt-in organ donation increases participation
- Framing taxes as losses vs. reducing take-home pay
- Penalty frames vs. bonus frames for energy conservation

**Behavioral Finance**
- Holding losing stocks too long (avoid realizing loss)
- Selling winners too early (secure gains)
- "House money effect" - Risk-seeking with prior gains

**Negotiations**
- Concessions framed as losses feel costly
- Status quo option privileged due to loss aversion
- Deadlines create fear of lost opportunity

## Other Major Biases

### Recency Bias
**Definition:** Recent events disproportionately influence judgments

**Example:** Investors extrapolate recent market performance, buying high after bull markets and selling low after crashes

### Hindsight Bias
**Definition:** After outcome known, believe it was predictable ("I knew it all along")

**Example:** Post-crisis claims of obvious warning signs that were ambiguous beforehand

**Consequence:** Overconfidence in future predictions, inadequate learning from surprises

### Overconfidence Bias
**Definition:** Excessive confidence in judgment accuracy

**Manifestations:**
- Overprecision: Confidence intervals too narrow (90% confidence captures true value only 50% of time)
- Overestimation: Believe abilities above actual performance
- Overplacement: Rate self above average (90% of drivers believe they're above average)

**Example:** Entrepreneurs estimate 10% failure probability; actual rate exceeds 50%

### Dunning-Kruger Effect
**Definition:** Unskilled individuals overestimate competence; experts underestimate relative ability

**Mechanism:** Requires expertise to recognize expertise; novices lack metacognitive awareness

**Graph Pattern:**
- Beginners: High confidence, low competence
- Intermediate: Confidence drops as awareness grows
- Experts: Confidence appropriate but underestimate gap to average

### Fundamental Attribution Error
**Definition:** Overweight dispositional factors, underweight situational factors when explaining others' behavior (reverse for self)

**Example:**
- Someone cuts you off in traffic: "They're a reckless jerk" (disposition)
- You cut someone off: "I didn't see them because of blind spot" (situation)

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
**Definition:** Continue investment because of past expenditure rather than future value

**Rational Approach:** Evaluate marginal costs/benefits, ignore sunk costs

**Example:** Continue attending boring movie because ticket purchased; reading bad book because started it

---

**Key Terms**

- **Anchoring** - Over-reliance on initial information when making judgments
- **Confirmation Bias** - Seeking and interpreting information to confirm existing beliefs
- **Availability Bias** - Judging frequency by ease of recalling examples
- **Loss Aversion** - Losses feel approximately 2x more impactful than equivalent gains
- **Endowment Effect** - Ownership increases perceived value due to loss aversion
- **Framing Effect** - Decisions change based on how options are presented
- **Hindsight Bias** - Believing past events were predictable after knowing outcome

---

## Summary

Cognitive biases create systematic deviations from rational judgment. Anchoring causes over-reliance on initial information in negotiations, pricing, and estimates. Confirmation bias drives preferential treatment of belief-consistent evidence in search, interpretation, and memory. Availability bias causes overestimation of easily recalled events, distorting risk perception. Loss aversion creates 2:1 asymmetry between losses and gains, driving endowment effects, framing effects, and risk attitude reversals. Additional biases include recency, hindsight, overconfidence, Dunning-Kruger effect, and fundamental attribution error. Debiasing strategies involve considering alternatives, using external reference points, consulting base rates, pre-commitment, and structured decision procedures. Understanding biases improves personal decisions, reduces manipulation vulnerability, and enables effective persuasion design.
