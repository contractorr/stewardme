# Introduction to Energy Systems & Materials Science

## What is Energy?

Energy is the capacity to do work. It exists in multiple forms and can transform between them, but the total quantity is conserved. Every economic activity, technological advancement, and human comfort depends on converting energy from one form to another.

### Forms of Energy

**Kinetic Energy**: Energy of motion
```
KE = ½mv²
```
Where m = mass, v = velocity. A moving car, flowing water, blowing wind all possess kinetic energy.

**Potential Energy**: Stored energy due to position
```
PE = mgh (gravitational)
```
Where m = mass, g = gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s²), h = height. Water behind a dam, a lifted weight, compressed spring.

**Thermal Energy**: Random molecular motion proportional to temperature. Heat is thermal energy in transfer between objects at different temperatures.

**Chemical Energy**: Stored in molecular bonds. Fossil fuels, batteries, and food all contain chemical energy released during reactions.

**Nuclear Energy**: Stored in atomic nuclei. Released during fission (splitting heavy nuclei) or fusion (combining light nuclei).

**Electromagnetic Energy**: Radiation across the spectrum: radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, UV, X-rays, gamma rays.

**Electrical Energy**: Flow of charged particles (electrons) through conductors. The most versatile and transportable energy form.

## Why Energy is Civilization's Master Resource

Energy consumption correlates almost perfectly with economic development. The relationship isn't merely correlative — it's causal. Physical work requires energy input. Moving goods, manufacturing products, heating homes, powering computers, growing food: all impossible without energy conversion.

### Historical Energy-GDP Coupling

| Era | Per Capita Energy (kcal/day) | GDP per Capita (1990 $) | Primary Activity |
|-----|------------------------------|------------------------|------------------|
| Hunter-gatherer | ~2,000 | ~100-300 | Foraging |
| Agricultural | ~12,000 | ~400-800 | Farming |
| Early industrial (1800) | ~70,000 | ~1,000-2,000 | Manufacturing |
| Industrial (1900) | ~110,000 | ~3,000-5,000 | Mass production |
| Modern (2000) | ~230,000 | ~20,000-40,000 | Services + digital |
| Advanced (2020) | ~250,000 | ~25,000-60,000 | Information economy |

The pattern is clear: every 10x increase in energy consumption enables roughly 10-20x increase in GDP per capita. Vaclav Smil's research demonstrates this relationship holds across countries and time periods.

### Energy Enables Core Sectors

**Agriculture** (~4% of energy, foundational to all else):
- **Haber-Bosch process**: Natural gas → ammonia (NH₃) → fertilizer. Feeds ~50% of humanity
- **Mechanization**: Tractors, harvesters replaced human/animal muscle
- **Irrigation**: Pumps move water; 40% of food from irrigated land
- **Transportation**: Moving food from farm to table

Without Haber-Bosch ammonia synthesis (1913), Earth's carrying capacity would be ~4 billion people maximum. We support 8+ billion because natural gas converts to fertilizer.

**Industry** (~29% of final energy):
- **Steel**: 1,500°C blast furnace reduces iron ore. ~1.9 Gt/year production
- **Cement**: 1,450°C kiln converts limestone to clinker. ~4.4 Gt/year production
- **Chemicals**: Petrochemicals create plastics, pharmaceuticals, materials
- **Manufacturing**: Forming, machining, assembly all require energy

**Transport** (~28% of final energy):
- **Aviation**: Jet fuel has 46 MJ/kg; batteries have 0.9 MJ/kg — 50x gap
- **Shipping**: 90% of goods by weight travel by ship
- **Road transport**: 1.4 billion vehicles globally
- **Rail**: Most energy-efficient land transport per tonne-km

**Buildings** (~32% of final energy):
- **Heating**: Natural gas, electricity, oil
- **Cooling**: Air conditioning in 90% of US homes, growing rapidly in developing world
- **Lighting**: Now mostly LED, 5x more efficient than incandescent
- **Appliances**: Refrigeration, cooking, electronics

**Information** (~1-2% of electricity, growing):
- **Data centers**: ~200 TWh/year globally, roughly equal to Argentina's total electricity
- **Networks**: Internet backbone, cell towers, routers
- **Devices**: Billions of computers, phones, IoT sensors
- **AI training**: Large language models require megawatt-hours to train

## Historical Energy Transitions

Energy transitions are slow because they require replacing not just fuel sources but entire infrastructures: extraction, processing, distribution, end-use equipment, and supply chains.

### Timeline of Major Transitions

**Biomass Era (prehistory to ~1850)**:
- **Sources**: Wood, charcoal, animal power, human muscle
- **Uses**: Heating, cooking, limited mechanical work
- **Constraint**: Land-limited — forests couldn't provide enough fuel for industrialization
- **Energy per capita**: ~12,000 kcal/day

**Coal Era (1800-1950 dominance)**:
- **Breakthrough**: Steam engine (Newcomen 1712, Watt 1776)
- **Advantages**: Much higher energy density than wood; abundant deposits
- **Applications**: Railroads, factories, electricity generation, steel production
- **Peak share**: ~50% of global primary energy (1900)
- **Legacy**: Still ~27% of primary energy, 36% of electricity in 2023

**Oil Era (1900-present)**:
- **Breakthrough**: Internal combustion engine (Otto 1876, Diesel 1893)
- **Advantages**: Liquid form easy to transport; high energy density; refines to multiple products
- **Applications**: Automobiles, aviation, shipping, plastics, chemicals
- **Peak share**: ~47% of global primary energy (1970s)
- **Current**: ~31% of primary energy; 90%+ of transportation energy

**Natural Gas Era (1950-present, growing)**:
- **Breakthrough**: Pipeline networks, LNG technology
- **Advantages**: Cleanest fossil fuel (~50% less CO₂ than coal); versatile (heating, power, chemicals)
- **Applications**: Electricity, heating, industrial processes, hydrogen production
- **Current**: ~24% of primary energy, growing 2-3% annually

**Nuclear Era (1950s-present, plateaued)**:
- **Breakthrough**: Controlled fission reactors (Chicago Pile-1, 1942)
- **Advantages**: Enormous energy density; no CO₂ during operation; baseload power
- **Constraints**: High capital costs, waste disposal, public fear, proliferation
- **Peak share**: ~5% of primary energy (1990s)
- **Current**: ~4% of primary energy, ~10% of electricity

**Renewable Era (2000-present, accelerating)**:
- **Breakthrough**: Solar PV cost collapse, wind turbine scaling, battery improvements
- **Advantages**: Zero fuel cost; distributed generation; declining costs
- **Challenges**: Intermittency, grid integration, storage, material requirements
- **Current**: ~7% of primary energy (hydro + wind + solar + other), growing 10-20% annually

### Key Patterns in Energy Transitions

**1. Duration: 50-100 years**

The transition from one dominant fuel to another takes multiple human lifetimes:
- Coal → Oil: ~70 years (1890-1960) for oil to overtake coal
- Oil → ?: Started ~2000, likely won't complete until 2070-2100

**2. Stacking, Not Replacement**

New energy sources add to the mix rather than replacing old ones. Global coal consumption in absolute terms is higher than ever (~8.3 billion tonnes/year) even though its share declined from 50% to 27%.

**3. Infrastructure Lock-In**

Capital stock has 20-50 year lifetimes:
- Coal plant: 40 years
- Oil refinery: 30 years
- Natural gas pipeline: 50+ years
- Building HVAC system: 20 years
- Vehicle: 15 years

You can't strand trillions in assets overnight. This creates powerful incumbency advantages.

**4. Technology Learning Curves**

Each doubling of cumulative production drives cost reductions:
- Solar PV: ~24% cost reduction per doubling (Swanson's Law)
- Wind turbines: ~15-20% per doubling
- Batteries: ~18% per doubling (Wright's Law)

Early adopters pay premium prices that fund learning. Later adopters benefit from mature, cheap technology.

## Energy Units and Conversions

Understanding units is essential for comparing energy sources and systems.

### Base Units

**Joule (J)**: SI unit of energy
```
1 J = 1 kg⋅m²/s² = 1 N⋅m = 1 W⋅s
```
The energy required to exert 1 newton of force over 1 meter. Very small unit for energy systems.

**Watt (W)**: SI unit of power (energy per time)
```
1 W = 1 J/s
```
A 100W lightbulb consumes 100 joules per second.

**Kilowatt-hour (kWh)**: Standard electricity unit
```
1 kWh = 3.6 MJ = 3,600,000 J
```
A 1,000-watt device running for 1 hour, or a 100W lightbulb for 10 hours.

**Home electricity bill**: 900 kWh/month
= 900 × 3.6 MJ = 3,240 MJ/month
= 3.24 GJ/month
= 38.9 GJ/year

**Car fuel**: 15 gallons gasoline/week
= 15 gal × 3.785 L/gal × 34.2 MJ/L = 1,942 MJ/week
= 101 GJ/year

**Natural gas heating**: 70 therms/month (winter)
= 70 × 105.5 MJ = 7,385 MJ/month
= 7.4 GJ/month

**Total household energy**: 38.9 + 101 + 7.4×6 = 184 GJ/year
This is typical for a US household.

### Power vs Energy: The Most Common Mistake

**Energy** is total quantity (joules, kWh). **Power** is rate of use (watts = joules/second).

**Analogy**: Energy is like distance; power is like speed.
- A car traveling at 60 mph (speed/power) for 2 hours travels 120 miles (distance/energy)
- A 60W bulb (power) running for 2 hours uses 120 Wh (energy)

**Common confusion**:
- "This solar farm produces 100 MW" — No, it has 100 MW capacity (power)
- "This solar farm produces 100 MW × 8,760 hours = 876,000 MWh/year" — Only if it ran 24/7 at full power, which solar doesn't
- "This solar farm produces 100 MW × 8,760 hours × 25% capacity factor = 219,000 MWh/year" — Correct!

## Laws of Thermodynamics as Constraints

Thermodynamics sets absolute limits on what's possible. No technology can violate these laws.

### Zeroth Law: Temperature is Measurable

**Statement**: If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system B, and B is in equilibrium with C, then A is in equilibrium with C.

**Implication**: Temperature is a transitive property. This justifies thermometers — we can measure temperature by bringing a thermometer into equilibrium with an object.

**Why it matters**: Establishes temperature as a fundamental property of matter, enabling quantitative thermodynamics.

### First Law: Energy Conservation

**Statement**: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted between forms.

**Mathematical form**:
```
ΔU = Q - W
```
- ΔU = change in internal energy of system
- Q = heat added to system
- W = work done by system

**Alternative form**:
```
E_in = E_out + ΔE_stored
```
For any energy system, input equals output plus change in storage.

**Implications**:
1. **Perpetual motion machines of the first kind are impossible**: Can't create energy from nothing
2. **Energy accounting**: Every joule consumed must come from somewhere
3. **Efficiency definition**: Useful output / total input ≤ 1 (and usually much less)
4. **Conservation in conversions**: Converting coal to electricity — where does the energy go?
   - 35-45% → Electricity
   - 55-65% → Waste heat

**Example**: Coal plant with 40% efficiency burning 1 kg coal (30 MJ/kg):
- Input: 30 MJ (chemical energy in coal)
- Output: 12 MJ (electricity) + 18 MJ (waste heat to environment)
- ΔU = 0 (steady state operation)
- First law satisfied: 30 = 12 + 18

### Second Law: Entropy Always Increases

**Statement (Clausius)**: Heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot.

**Statement (Kelvin-Planck)**: No heat engine can convert heat entirely to work with no other effect.

**Statement (Entropy)**: The entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

**Mathematical form**:
```
ΔS ≥ Q/T
```
Where S = entropy, Q = heat added, T = absolute temperature. Equality holds only for reversible (ideal) processes.

**Carnot efficiency** (maximum possible for heat engine):
```
η_Carnot = 1 - T_cold/T_hot
```
Temperatures in Kelvin. This is an absolute upper limit — no real engine can exceed it.

**Detailed Carnot Examples**:

| Hot Temp (°C/K) | Cold Temp (°C/K) | ΔT (K) | Carnot η | Real Engine η | Technology |
|-----------------|------------------|--------|----------|---------------|------------|
| 100 / 373 | 25 / 298 | 75 | 20.1% | 5-10% | Low-temp steam |
| 300 / 573 | 25 / 298 | 275 | 48.0% | 25-35% | Steam turbine |
| 550 / 823 | 25 / 298 | 525 | 63.8% | 33-45% | Coal plant |
| 1,000 / 1,273 | 25 / 298 | 975 | 76.6% | 35-42% | Gas turbine |
| 1,500 / 1,773 | 25 / 298 | 1,475 | 83.2% | 55-62% | Combined cycle |
| 2,000 / 2,273 | 25 / 298 | 1,975 | 86.9% | ~65% (theoretical) | Advanced combined |

**Key insights from Second Law**:
1. **Perfect efficiency is impossible**: Even ideal heat engine wastes some energy as heat
2. **Higher temperature → higher efficiency**: This is why jet engines, gas turbines use very high combustion temperatures
3. **Waste heat is fundamental**: Not engineering failure — thermodynamic necessity
4. **Irreversibility**: Real processes always generate entropy (friction, mixing, combustion)
5. **Arrow of time**: Entropy increase gives time a direction

**Why you can't cool your house by leaving the refrigerator door open**:
- Refrigerator moves heat from cold (inside) to hot (room), using electricity
- Heat rejected to room = heat removed from fridge + electrical energy consumed
- Net effect: Room gets warmer
- Second law: Heat doesn't spontaneously go from cold to hot

### Third Law: Absolute Zero is Unreachable

**Statement**: The entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero (0 K = -273.15°C).

**Implication**: You can't reach absolute zero in finite steps. Each cooling step gets harder.

**Practical consequence**: Lowest achieved temperature: ~100 picokelvin (10⁻¹⁰ K) in laboratories. Requires extraordinary effort and energy. Cryogenics faces diminishing returns.

**Why it matters for energy systems**: Superconductors (zero electrical resistance) require very low temperatures. High-temperature superconductors work at 77 K (liquid nitrogen temperature), but room-temperature superconductors remain elusive.

## EROEI (Energy Return on Energy Invested)

EROEI measures net energy available to society after accounting for energy required to obtain the energy.

### Definition and Formula

```
EROEI = Energy Delivered to Society / Energy Required for Extraction, Processing, Delivery
```

**Example**: Saudi Arabian conventional oil (1950s):
- Extract 100 barrels of oil
- Energy in those barrels: 100 × 6.12 GJ = 612 GJ
- Energy required to extract, refine, transport: ~6 GJ (mostly the oil's own energy)
- EROEI = 612 / 6 ≈ 100:1

**Modern tight oil (US shale, 2020s)**:
- Extract 100 barrels of oil
- Energy in those barrels: 612 GJ
- Energy for horizontal drilling, fracking, trucking, refining: ~40 GJ
- EROEI = 612 / 40 ≈ 15:1

### The Energy Cliff

As EROEI declines, the net energy available to society drops rapidly.

**Math**:
```
Net Energy Ratio = (EROEI - 1) / EROEI
```

| EROEI | Net Energy | Energy Reinvested |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| 100:1 | 99% | 1% |
| 50:1 | 98% | 2% |
| 20:1 | 95% | 5% |
| 10:1 | 90% | 10% |
| 5:1 | 80% | 20% |
| 3:1 | 67% | 33% |
| 2:1 | 50% | 50% |
| 1:1 | 0% | 100% |

**The cliff**: Between EROEI of 5:1 and 1:1, net energy collapses from 80% to 0%. Most complex societies need EROEI > 5:1 to maintain infrastructure, healthcare, education beyond subsistence.

**Historical pattern**: Civilizations rise on high-EROEI energy (wood, then coal, then oil). As EROEI declines, complexity becomes harder to sustain.

## Primary vs Secondary Energy

### Definitions

**Primary energy**: Energy as it exists in nature before human conversion
- Crude oil in the ground
- Coal in a mine
- Sunlight hitting a solar panel
- Wind kinetic energy
- Uranium ore
- Natural gas in a reservoir

**Secondary energy**: Energy after conversion to usable form
- Electricity
- Gasoline (refined from crude oil)
- Diesel
- Hydrogen (produced from natural gas or electrolysis)
- Heat delivered to a building
- Steam for industrial processes

**Final energy**: Energy delivered to end-use device
- Electricity at a wall outlet
- Gasoline in a car's fuel tank
- Natural gas at a home furnace

**Useful energy**: Energy actually performing desired service
- Motion of a vehicle
- Heat in a room
- Light from a bulb
- Information processing

### Conversion Losses

Global energy system wastes ~60% of primary energy before it becomes useful energy.

**Sankey diagram concept**:
```
Primary Energy (580 EJ/year)
    ↓
    → Conversion losses (240 EJ) → Waste heat
    ↓
Final Energy (340 EJ/year)
    ↓
    → End-use losses (180 EJ) → Waste heat
    ↓
Useful Energy (160 EJ/year) ← Only 28% of primary!
```

**Where the losses occur**:
1. **Power generation**: 60-65% lost (coal/gas → electricity)
2. **Oil refining**: 10-15% lost
3. **Transportation**: 75-80% lost (gasoline → motion)
4. **Industrial processes**: 20-50% lost (varies by process)
5. **Building heating**: 10-40% lost (depends on furnace efficiency)

### Implications for Different Applications

**Aviation**:
- Requires high gravimetric density (weight matters enormously)
- Jet fuel: 43 MJ/kg
- Li-ion battery: 0.9 MJ/kg
- To store equal energy, battery weighs **48× more**
- Aircraft weight exponentially increases fuel needs
- Conclusion: Batteries impractical for long-haul aviation

**Shipping**:
- Requires high volumetric density (space constrained)
- Heavy fuel oil: 42 MJ/kg, ~40 MJ/L
- Batteries: 0.7 MJ/kg, 2.5 MJ/L
- Conclusion: Batteries impractical for transoceanic shipping
- Alternatives: Ammonia (18.6 MJ/kg), methanol (19.9 MJ/kg), hydrogen

**Road transport**:
- Moderate energy requirements
- Regenerative braking recovers energy
- Battery weight acceptable for cars (Tesla Model 3: 480 kg battery)
- Conclusion: EVs viable for cars and light trucks, marginal for heavy trucks

**Grid storage**:
- Weight doesn't matter
- Volumetric density matters moderately
- Cost per kWh dominates
- Conclusion: Li-ion, flow batteries, pumped hydro all viable

## Key Terms

- **Primary Energy**: Energy in its natural form before conversion (coal, crude oil, sunlight)
- **Secondary Energy**: Energy after conversion to usable form (electricity, gasoline, diesel)
- **Final Energy**: Energy delivered to end-use device
- **Useful Energy**: Energy actually performing desired service (~28% of primary globally)
- **EROEI (Energy Return on Energy Invested)**: Net energy available to society = Energy Out / Energy In
- **Energy Density**: Energy per unit mass (gravimetric, MJ/kg) or volume (volumetric, MJ/L)
- **Capacity Factor**: Actual output / maximum possible output over time (nuclear ~92%, solar ~25%)
- **Thermodynamics**: Science of energy, work, heat, and entropy — sets absolute constraints
- **Entropy**: Measure of energy unavailable for useful work; always increases in isolated systems
- **Carnot Efficiency**: Maximum theoretical heat engine efficiency = 1 - T_cold/T_hot
- **Exajoule (EJ)**: 10¹⁸ joules — unit for global energy accounting (world uses ~580 EJ/year)
- **Tonne of Oil Equivalent (toe)**: Standard energy unit = 41.87 GJ
- **Baseload Power**: Continuous minimum demand; met by always-on generation (nuclear, coal, hydro)
- **Intermittency**: Variable renewable output depending on weather/time (wind, solar)
- **Electrification**: Replacing fossil fuel end-use with electricity (EVs, heat pumps)

## Summary

Energy is civilization's master resource. Every dollar of GDP, every technological capability, every human comfort depends on converting energy from one form to another. Global energy consumption (~580 EJ/year) correlates almost perfectly with economic development and human welfare.

Historical energy transitions — biomass → coal → oil → gas → renewables — each take 50-100 years because they require replacing vast infrastructures, not just fuel sources. New sources stack on top of old ones rather than replacing them; coal consumption in absolute terms remains at all-time highs despite declining share.

The laws of thermodynamics impose hard constraints no technology can violate. The first law (energy conservation) means no free lunch. The second law (entropy) limits heat engine efficiency and creates waste heat — civilization rejects ~60% of primary energy as waste. The Carnot limit shows maximum efficiency depends on temperature difference.

EROEI (Energy Return on Energy Invested) measures net energy available to society after accounting for extraction costs. High EROEI (>20:1) enables civilizational complexity. Low EROEI (<5:1) creates the "energy cliff" where most energy goes to getting more energy. Conventional oil has declined from 100:1 to 15-20:1; this matters enormously.

Energy density varies by orders of magnitude across sources. Nuclear fuel contains ~90 million times the energy per kg of lithium-ion batteries. This ~50,000× gap between gasoline and batteries is why electrifying aviation and shipping is extraordinarily difficult.

Despite rapid renewable growth (solar +20%/year, wind +15%/year), fossil fuels still provide 82% of global primary energy. Electricity is only 18% of primary energy — decarbonizing the grid is necessary but insufficient. Transport (28% of energy, 90% oil), industry (29% of energy, high-heat processes), and buildings (32% of energy, heating/cooling) all require solutions beyond renewable electricity.

Understanding physical constraints — thermodynamics, energy density, EROEI, material requirements, transition timescales — is essential for evaluating energy policy, technology investment, and decarbonization strategies. Good energy policy requires understanding both the physics and the economics. This guide provides the physical foundations.
