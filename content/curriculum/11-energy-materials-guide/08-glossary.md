# Glossary

## A-Z Energy & Materials Terms

**AC (Alternating Current)**: Electric current that periodically reverses direction, typically 50 or 60 times per second (50/60 Hz). Standard for power grids because transformers can easily change voltage levels. Invented by Nikola Tesla, championed by George Westinghouse, won the "war of currents" against Thomas Edison's DC system in the 1890s.

**AGR (Advanced Gas-Cooled Reactor)**: British nuclear reactor design using carbon dioxide gas as coolant and graphite as moderator. ~4 GW capacity remaining in UK, all scheduled for decommissioning by 2030s. Represents legacy technology superseded by light-water reactors globally.

**Anthracite**: Highest grade of coal, 86-97% carbon content. Hard, high energy density (~33-35 MJ/kg), low volatiles. Formed under extreme metamorphic conditions. Rare compared to bituminous coal. Used for heating, metallurgy, carbon products. Global reserves ~75 billion tonnes.

**ASML**: Dutch company (Advanced Semiconductor Materials Lithography) with monopoly on EUV lithography machines. Each machine costs $350-400M, weighs 180 tonnes, requires 40 shipping containers, uses tin plasma to generate 13.5nm light. Only manufacturer of equipment needed for 3nm and smaller chip fabrication. Geopolitical chokepoint in semiconductor supply chain.

**Band Gap (E_g)**: Energy difference between valence band (highest occupied) and conduction band (lowest unoccupied) in a solid. Determines electrical properties: metals have no gap (overlap), semiconductors have small gap (0.5-3.5 eV), insulators have large gap (>4 eV). Silicon: 1.12 eV. GaN: 3.4 eV. Diamond: 5.5 eV.

**Baseload**: Minimum continuous power demand on electrical grid, typically met by always-on generation (nuclear, coal, run-of-river hydro, geothermal). Contrasts with intermediate (load-following) and peaking (demand spikes only) generation. Baseload plants run at high capacity factor (80-95%) to amortize high capital costs.

**Battery (Lithium-ion)**: Rechargeable electrochemical energy storage using lithium ions moving between anode (typically graphite) and cathode (metal oxides: NMC, NCA, LFP). Energy density: 130-260 Wh/kg (cell level). Cycle life: 500-10,000 cycles depending on chemistry. Dominates EVs and grid storage. Invented 1980s (John Goodenough, Akira Yoshino), commercialized by Sony 1991.

**Betz Limit**: Maximum fraction of kinetic energy extractable from wind by any turbine: 59.3% (16/27). Derived by German physicist Albert Betz (1919). If turbine extracted 100%, air would stop (no flow → no power). If 0%, no turbine. Optimal compromise: 59.3%. Real turbines achieve 45-50% (75-85% of Betz limit) due to aerodynamic losses, friction, tip vortices.

**Bituminous Coal**: Most common coal grade (76-86% carbon). Formed under high pressure over 100-300 million years. Energy content 25-33 MJ/kg. Used for electricity generation and coking coal (steel production). Global reserves ~430 billion tonnes. Major producers: China, India, US, Australia.

**BWR (Boiling Water Reactor)**: Nuclear reactor where water boils directly in core, producing radioactive steam that drives turbine. Simpler than PWR (no steam generator) but radioactive steam contaminates turbine (maintenance challenges). ~15% of global nuclear fleet. Fukushima Daiichi units were BWRs.

**Capacity Factor**: Actual energy output divided by maximum possible output over a time period (typically annual). Nuclear ~90-93%, coal ~40-70%, onshore wind ~25-45%, solar PV ~15-30%. Critical for comparing intermittent vs dispatchable generation. 1 GW nuclear at 92% CF produces 8,060 GWh/year; 1 GW solar at 25% CF produces 2,190 GWh/year.

**Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)**: Capturing CO₂ from point sources (power plants, industrial facilities) or air (direct air capture), then storing it permanently in geological formations. Post-combustion capture: 90%+ capture, ~$50-100/tonne CO₂. Current deployment: ~40 Mt CO₂/year globally. Net zero scenarios require 7,600 Mt CO₂/year by 2050 (190x scale-up).

**Carbon Fiber**: Strong, lightweight material made from polyacrylonitrile (PAN) or pitch precursor, pyrolyzed to pure carbon fibers (5-10 μm diameter). Tensile strength ~3,000-7,000 MPa. Density ~1.8 g/cm³ (vs 7.85 g/cm³ steel). Specific strength 5-10x steel. Used in aerospace, wind blades (60-100m), high-performance vehicles. Cost: $15-150/kg.

**Carnot Efficiency**: Maximum theoretical efficiency of heat engine operating between hot source (T_h) and cold sink (T_c): η_max = 1 - T_c/T_h (temperatures in Kelvin). Derived by Sadi Carnot (1824). Represents thermodynamic limit; real engines achieve 60-80% of Carnot due to irreversibilities. Coal plant (550°C hot, 25°C cold): Carnot η = 63.8%, real η = 33-45%.

**CCGT (Combined Cycle Gas Turbine)**: Natural gas power plant combining gas turbine (Brayton cycle) and steam turbine (Rankine cycle). Gas turbine exhaust (~600°C) heats water for steam turbine. Efficiency: 55-62% (vs 35-40% simple cycle). Stacks two Carnot cycles (high-temp and mid-temp). Dominant technology for new gas plants. Typical size: 400-800 MW.

**CHP (Combined Heat and Power, Cogeneration)**: Simultaneous generation of electricity and useful heat from single fuel source. Overall efficiency 75-95% vs 35-45% for electricity-only. Captures waste heat for district heating, industrial process heat, or building HVAC. Economics improve where heat demand is consistent and electricity prices are high. Common in Denmark, Germany, industrial sites.

**CHIPS Act**: US legislation (2022) providing $52 billion for domestic semiconductor manufacturing and R&D. Incentivizes reshoring chip production (TSMC Arizona fab: $40B, Intel Ohio expansion). Response to Taiwan dependence and China's semiconductor ambitions. Accompanied by export controls restricting China's access to advanced chips and EUV equipment.

**Coal**: Sedimentary rock formed from ancient plant material subjected to heat and pressure over millions of years. Types by carbon content: lignite (60-70%), sub-bituminous (70-76%), bituminous (76-86%), anthracite (86-97%). Global production ~8.3 billion tonnes/year. Combustion produces ~900-1,000 g CO₂/kWh electricity. Still ~27% of global primary energy despite climate concerns.

**Cobalt (Co)**: Transition metal (atomic number 27) critical for high-energy-density lithium-ion batteries (NMC, NCA cathodes). ~70% mined in DRC (Democratic Republic of Congo), often under problematic conditions (child labor, artisanal mining, political instability). China refines ~73% globally. Supply chain concentration and ethics driving de-cobaltization (NMC 811, LFP batteries). Current production ~180,000 tonnes/year.

**COP (Coefficient of Performance)**: Ratio of heat delivered to work input for heat pumps. COP = Q_heat / W_electricity. Typically 2.5-5.0 for air-source heat pumps, 4.0-5.5 for ground-source. COP >1 because heat pump moves heat rather than creating it. Theoretical maximum (Carnot COP) = T_hot/(T_hot - T_cold). Real systems achieve ~20-30% of Carnot COP due to irreversibilities.

**Critical Minerals**: Materials with concentrated supply chains (few producers/processors) essential for energy transition and technology. Includes lithium, cobalt, nickel, graphite, rare earths, copper, gallium, germanium. China dominates refining: lithium (65%), cobalt (73%), rare earths (90%), graphite (98%). Strategic concern analogous to oil dependence. Building alternative supply chains requires 5-15 years.

**Crude Oil (Petroleum)**: Unrefined liquid hydrocarbon mixture formed from ancient marine organisms under heat and pressure (60-160°C, 2-4 km depth) over 10-100 million years. Composition varies by source. Classified by API gravity (density) and sulfur content. Refined into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals. Global production ~100 million barrels/day. Energy density ~46 MJ/kg.

**Czochralski Process**: Method for growing single-crystal silicon ingots. Dip seed crystal into molten silicon (~1,414°C), slowly pull upward while rotating. Crystal grows as liquid solidifies onto seed. Produces 300mm diameter, 2m long boules with 11-12N purity (99.99999999% pure). Essential for semiconductor wafers. Developed by Jan Czochralski (1916), adapted for silicon in 1950s.

**DC (Direct Current)**: Electric current flowing in one direction (constant polarity). Used in batteries, solar cells, electronic devices. Requires power electronics (inverters, converters) to interface with AC grid. High-voltage DC (HVDC) used for long-distance transmission (>600 km) or undersea cables where AC has high capacitive losses. HVDC advantages: lower losses, asynchronous grid interconnection. Thomas Edison championed DC in 1880s, lost to AC.

**Direct Air Capture (DAC)**: Extracting CO₂ directly from ambient air (~420 ppm concentration). Technologies: solid sorbents (Climeworks), liquid solvents (Carbon Engineering). Energy requirement: 1.5-2.5 MWh per tonne CO₂. Current cost: $250-600/tonne. Deployment: <0.01 Mt CO₂/year (2023). Target: $100-200/tonne, scaling to 60 Mt/year (2030) and 1,000 Mt/year (2050) in net zero scenarios.

**Doping (Semiconductor)**: Adding impurity atoms (typically 1 ppm to 0.1%) to control electrical properties. N-type: Group V element (P, As) donates electrons → negative charge carriers. P-type: Group III element (B, Ga) creates holes → positive charge carriers. Enables transistors, diodes, solar cells. Ion implantation accelerates dopant ions (10-500 keV) into silicon.

**DRC (Democratic Republic of Congo)**: Central African country producing ~70% of global cobalt (critical for batteries). Major mines in Katanga province. Issues: political instability, artisanal mining (child labor, dangerous conditions), Chinese corporate control (~80% of mining capacity). Human rights concerns drive battery industry toward cobalt-free chemistries (LFP).

**Duck Curve**: Net load curve (demand minus solar/wind) resembling duck shape. "Belly" at midday (solar peak reduces net load), steep "neck" ramping requirement at sunset (solar drops, demand stays high), "head" at evening peak. First identified by California ISO. Challenges: rapid ramping (gas plants), curtailment (excess midday solar), grid flexibility. Addressed by batteries, demand response, geographic diversity.

**Dutch Disease (Resource Curse)**: Economic phenomenon where resource wealth (oil, minerals) damages non-resource sectors and overall economy. Mechanism: resource exports → currency appreciation → other exports become uncompetitive → manufacturing declines → economy depends on volatile commodities. Named after Netherlands post-1960s gas discovery. Examples: Nigeria, Venezuela. Exceptions (strong institutions): Norway, Botswana.

**EAF (Electric Arc Furnace)**: Steelmaking technology melting scrap steel using electric arc (thousands of amperes, ~1,500-1,800°C). Electricity consumption ~15 MWh per tonne steel. If from renewables, emissions ~0.4 tonnes CO₂/tonne vs ~1.85 tonnes for blast furnace. Limitation: requires scrap availability (~30% of current steel production). Can't scale to 100% without decades of scrap accumulation.

**Energy Density**: Energy per unit mass (gravimetric, MJ/kg) or volume (volumetric, MJ/L). Critical for mobile applications. Gasoline: 46 MJ/kg, 34 MJ/L. Lithium-ion battery: 0.9-1.0 MJ/kg, 2-2.5 MJ/L. This 40-50x gap explains why electrifying aviation is extremely difficult. Hydrogen: 142 MJ/kg but only 5.6 MJ/L (at 700 bar), 10.1 MJ/L (liquid).

**Energy Return on Energy Invested (EROEI)**: Ratio of energy delivered divided by energy required for extraction, processing, delivery. Conventional oil (1930s): ~100:1. Conventional oil (2020s): ~15-20:1. Oil sands: ~3-5:1. Solar PV: ~10-20:1. Wind: ~20-30:1. Nuclear: ~50-75:1. Societies require EROEI >5:1 to maintain complexity. Below 5:1, too much energy goes to getting energy.

**Enrichment (Uranium)**: Increasing U-235 concentration from natural 0.72% to reactor-grade (3-5% LEU) or weapons-grade (>90% HEU). Modern method: gas centrifuge spinning UF₆ at 50,000-70,000 RPM. Separative work measured in SWU (Separative Work Units). Producing 1 kg of 4.5% enriched U requires ~8.9 kg natural U and ~6.6 SWU. Global capacity ~66 million SWU/year. Major proliferation concern.

**EROEI**: See Energy Return on Energy Invested.

**EUV (Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography)**: Semiconductor manufacturing technology using 13.5nm wavelength light generated by tin plasma (~200,000°C). Enables 3nm and smaller feature sizes. Only supplier: ASML (Netherlands). Machine cost: $350-400M each. Input power: 170 kW to generate 250W usable EUV (~0.15% efficiency). Requires vacuum (EUV absorbed by air, glass). Geopolitical chokepoint — China cannot buy EUV machines (export controls).

**Exergy**: Maximum useful work obtainable as system reaches equilibrium with environment. Unlike energy (conserved), exergy is destroyed in irreversible processes. For thermal energy: Exergy = Energy × (1 - T_ambient/T_source). High-temperature heat has high exergy; low-temperature waste heat has low exergy. Explains why 60°C waste heat is thermodynamically less valuable than electricity despite same energy content.

**Fission (Nuclear)**: Splitting heavy atomic nuclei (typically U-235, Pu-239) into lighter fragments, releasing energy (~200 MeV per fission) and 2-3 neutrons. Chain reaction occurs when neutrons trigger additional fissions. Critical (k=1): sustains itself. Supercritical (k>1): increasing power. Subcritical (k<1): decreasing. Discovered by Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann (1938), explained by Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch.

**Fracking (Hydraulic Fracturing)**: Injecting high-pressure fluid (water + sand + chemicals) into shale rock to fracture formation and release trapped oil/gas. Pressure: 8,000-12,000 psi. Water use: 5-10 million gallons per well. Enabled US shale revolution: tight oil production 0.5 million b/d (2008) → 9 million b/d (2019). Controversial due to water use, contamination concerns, induced seismicity.

**Fusion (Nuclear)**: Combining light atomic nuclei to form heavier nucleus, releasing energy. Deuterium-tritium reaction: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV. Requires temperature >100 million °C to overcome electrostatic repulsion. Confinement approaches: magnetic (tokamak like ITER), inertial (laser compression like NIF). NIF achieved ignition December 2022 (fusion energy > laser energy). Practical power plants: 2040s+ if ever.

**GaN (Gallium Nitride)**: Wide-bandgap semiconductor (3.4 eV) enabling high-frequency, high-efficiency power electronics. Applications: phone chargers (65W in palm size), EV onboard chargers, 5G base stations, radar, LEDs. Advantages vs silicon: 2x electron mobility, can switch at MHz frequencies (vs kHz for Si), smaller passives. Tesla, BYD using GaN in vehicle chargers.

**Geothermal Energy**: Heat from Earth's interior, typically extracted 1-10 km depth. Conventional hydrothermal: 150-350°C water/steam in volcanic regions (Iceland, Philippines, US Pacific). Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS): Fracture hot dry rock, circulate water. Capacity factor: 85-95% (baseload, like nuclear). Global capacity ~16 GW, generating ~95 TWh/year. US potential >100 GW if deep drilling costs decline.

**Graphite**: Form of carbon with layered hexagonal structure. Strong in-plane covalent bonds, weak van der Waals between layers (soft, lubricating). Electrically conductive. Used in battery anodes (~90% of lithium-ion anode material), electrodes, high-temperature applications. China produces 65% of natural graphite, 100% of synthetic graphite. Supply chain chokepoint for battery production.

**Green Hydrogen**: Hydrogen produced via electrolysis of water using renewable electricity. Contrast to gray hydrogen (natural gas reforming, emits ~10 kg CO₂/kg H₂) and blue hydrogen (gray + CCS). Current production <0.1 Mt/year. Cost: $3-8/kg (vs $1-2/kg gray). IEA net zero scenario requires ~430 Mt/year by 2050. Applications: steel (H-DRI), chemicals, seasonal energy storage, potentially shipping/aviation.

**Grid (Electrical)**: Interconnected system for generating, transmitting, and distributing electricity. Structure: generation → step-up transformer → high-voltage transmission (115-765 kV) → step-down → distribution (4-35 kV) → service drop (120-240V or 230-400V). Balances supply and demand in real-time. Frequency (50 or 60 Hz) must be maintained within ±0.05 Hz. Total losses: 5-10% (developed countries).

**Half-Life (t_½)**: Time for half of radioactive atoms to decay. Ranges from microseconds to billions of years. I-131: 8 days (thyroid, acute hazard). Cs-137: 30 years (ground contamination). Pu-239: 24,100 years (long-term waste concern). After ~10 half-lives, radioactivity drops to ~0.1% of initial (negligible). Determines waste management requirements and radiotoxicity timescales.

**H-DRI (Hydrogen Direct Reduced Iron)**: Steel production method using hydrogen to reduce iron ore instead of carbon. Reaction: Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂ → 2Fe + 3H₂O (water, not CO₂). Requires ~50-55 kg H₂ per tonne steel. Pilot plants: HYBRIT (Sweden), Thyssen-Krupp (Germany). Challenge: green H₂ at $3-8/kg adds $200-400/tonne to steel cost vs $600-1,000/tonne current price. Potentially eliminates ~1.5 tonnes CO₂ per tonne steel.

**Heat Pump**: Device moving heat from cold to hot using work input (compressor). Refrigerant evaporates in cold side (absorbing heat), condenses in hot side (rejecting heat). COP (heat delivered / work input) typically 2.5-5.0 for space heating. Essentially a refrigerator run backward. Can replace gas furnaces with 3-5x better efficiency (on energy basis). Critical technology for building decarbonization.

**HVDC (High-Voltage Direct Current)**: Long-distance power transmission using DC instead of AC. Advantages: lower losses for distances >600 km, no reactive power, can connect asynchronous grids (different frequencies). Disadvantages: expensive converter stations ($200-500M each), not easily tapped along route. Major projects: Xiangjiaba-Shanghai (China, 2,071 km, ±800 kV, 6.4 GW), Pacific Intertie (US, 1,362 km, ±500 kV).

**Hydroelectric Power**: Electricity from gravitational potential energy of water. Power = ηρgQH where η = turbine efficiency (~90-95%), ρ = water density, g = gravity, Q = flow rate, H = head (elevation difference). Global capacity ~1,400 GW, generating ~4,200 TWh/year (~15% of global electricity). Types: run-of-river (35-50% CF), reservoir (40-70% CF, dispatchable), pumped storage (energy storage).

**Intermittency**: Variable output of renewable generation depending on weather and time. Solar: zero at night, ~6-10 hours productive generation per day, clouds reduce output 50-90%. Wind: varies unpredictably minute-to-minute, hour-to-hour, season-to-season. Requires flexible backup (gas plants, storage, demand response) or overbuild + curtailment. Central challenge for high-renewable grids (>60-70% penetration).

**Ion Implantation**: Doping method accelerating dopant ions (B⁺, P⁺, As⁺) to 10-500 keV and shooting into silicon wafer. Penetration depth: 10-1,000 nm depending on energy and ion mass. Creates precise doping profiles critical for MOSFETs. Damages crystal lattice → requires annealing (900-1,100°C) to repair damage and activate dopants. Standard process in semiconductor fabrication.

**ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor)**: Massive tokamak fusion experiment under construction in France. 23,000 tonnes, 25,000 m³ vacuum vessel. Target: Q = 10 (fusion power / input power). First plasma ~2035, full fusion power ~2040s. Cost: ~$25-30 billion (international collaboration). Goal: demonstrate sustained fusion, not generate electricity (no turbine). Next step: DEMO reactor (electricity generation) in 2050s+.

**LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy)**: Total lifetime cost per MWh including capital, fuel, operations, maintenance, financing, decommissioning. Enables comparison across technologies with different cost structures. Solar PV: $24-96/MWh. Onshore wind: $24-75/MWh. Nuclear (new): $60-150/MWh. Gas CCGT: $40-80/MWh. Limitation: ignores system integration costs (storage, backup, transmission) for intermittent sources.

**LED (Light-Emitting Diode)**: Semiconductor device emitting light via electroluminescence. Forward-biased p-n junction: electrons and holes recombine in depletion zone, releasing photons. Photon energy (wavelength) determined by bandgap: GaN (blue/white), GaAs (red), GaP (green). Efficiency: 40-50% (electrical → visible light) vs 2-5% incandescent. Global adoption since 2010s reduced lighting electricity ~50% vs counterfactual.

**LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate, LiFePO₄)**: Lithium-ion battery cathode chemistry. Energy density: 130-160 Wh/kg (lower than NMC). Advantages: no cobalt, long cycle life (3,000-10,000 cycles), safer (thermal runaway ~270°C vs ~210°C NMC), cheaper (~$90-130/kWh). Winning chemistry for grid storage and increasingly for EVs (40% of global EV batteries in 2024, up from ~10% in 2020). China dominates production (~95% global capacity).

**Lignite (Brown Coal)**: Lowest grade coal, 60-70% carbon content. Soft, high moisture (30-60%), low energy density (15-20 MJ/kg). Formed at shallow depth over <50 million years. Used for power generation despite low quality due to cost (cheap to mine). Global reserves ~450 billion tonnes. Major producers: Germany, Russia, US, Australia. High emissions per kWh (~1,100 g CO₂) due to low energy content.

**Lithium (Li)**: Lightest metal (atomic number 3), critical for lithium-ion batteries. Reserves: ~25 million tonnes (decades at projected demand). Extraction: hard rock spodumene (Australia, ~50% production, energy-intensive) or brine evaporation (Chile, Argentina, ~45%, water-intensive, 12-18 months). China refines ~65% globally. Current production ~130,000 tonnes/year. Projected need (2030): ~500,000-1,000,000 tonnes/year (5-8x increase).

**LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas)**: Methane cooled to -162°C (boiling point), reducing volume 600x for shipping. Infrastructure: liquefaction terminal ($10-20B), LNG carriers ($200-250M each), regasification terminal ($1-5B). Breaks pipeline constraint, creating global gas markets. Major trade flows: Qatar/Australia/US → Asia/Europe. Global trade ~400 million tonnes/year (2023). Enabled Europe to replace Russian pipeline gas rapidly in 2022.

**Methane (CH₄)**: Primary component of natural gas (70-95%). Simplest hydrocarbon (one carbon, four hydrogen). Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + 890 kJ/mol. Energy density: 53.6 MJ/kg. Greenhouse gas: 80x CO₂ over 20 years, 27x over 100 years. Leakage from production/distribution systems (~1.5-2.5% in US) significant climate concern. At 3%+ leakage, natural gas rivals coal for climate impact over 20-year timeframe.

**Moore's Law**: Observation by Gordon Moore (Intel co-founder, 1965): transistors per chip double approximately every 2 years. Held for 50+ years: Intel 4004 (1971): 2,300 transistors → Apple M3 (2023): 25+ billion transistors. Enabled exponential computing performance improvement. Slowing due to physical limits: quantum tunneling, atomic scale, heat dissipation, wire resistance. Continues through new tricks: 3D stacking, EUV, GAA transistors, specialized accelerators.

**MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor)**: Building block of all digital electronics. Structure: source, drain (doped regions), gate (conductive), gate oxide (insulating SiO₂, 1-5nm). Gate voltage controls channel conductivity. Gate ON: channel conducts (logical 1). Gate OFF: channel blocks (logical 0). Advantages: very high input impedance (gate insulated), extremely scalable (billions per chip), CMOS pairs have near-zero static power consumption.

**Nameplate Capacity**: Maximum rated output of power plant or generator under ideal conditions (MW or GW). Differs from actual output by capacity factor. Nuclear plant: 1 GW nameplate, 92% CF → 8,060 GWh/year. Solar PV: 1 GW nameplate, 25% CF → 2,190 GWh/year. Comparing nameplate capacities without capacity factors is misleading — solar requires ~3.7x nameplate capacity to match nuclear's annual output.

**Net Zero**: Balancing CO₂ emissions with removals (carbon capture, natural sinks). Differs from zero emissions (producing no CO₂). Net zero allows residual emissions (hard-to-abate sectors like aviation, cement) offset by removals. Target: limit warming to 1.5-2°C. IEA Net Zero 2050 scenario requires massive electrification, renewable scale-up, CCS, hydrogen, behavioral change. Feasibility debated — requires deployment rates exceeding historical precedent.

**NMC (Nickel-Manganese-Cobalt)**: Lithium-ion battery cathode chemistry. Formula: LiNi_xMn_yCo_zO₂. Variants: NMC 111 (33/33/33%), NMC 622 (60/20/20%), NMC 811 (80/10/10%). Higher nickel → higher energy density (200-260 Wh/kg) but reduced thermal stability and higher cost. Trend: reducing cobalt to address supply chain and ethics concerns. Dominant chemistry for EVs alongside LFP.

**Nord Stream**: Natural gas pipelines from Russia to Germany via Baltic Sea, bypassing Ukraine and Poland. Nord Stream 1 (2011): 55 bcm/year capacity. Nord Stream 2 (completed 2021, never operated): 55 bcm/year. NS1 sabotaged September 2022 (explosions). Represented Europe's energy dependence on Russia. Collapse of relationship demonstrated limits of energy interdependence as stabilizing force when geopolitical conflict erupts.

**Nuclear Fuel Cycle**: Sequence from uranium mining to waste disposal. Front end: mining → milling (yellowcake) → conversion (UF₆) → enrichment (3-5% U-235) → fuel fabrication (UO₂ pellets). Reactor: 3-6 year in-core residence. Back end: interim storage (spent fuel pools, dry casks) → reprocessing (optional, separates U/Pu) or direct disposal → geological repository. US uses once-through (no reprocessing); France reprocesses to extract U/Pu for MOX fuel.

**OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries)**: Cartel founded 1960 to coordinate oil production and influence prices. Members: Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, Iran, Venezuela, Libya, Algeria, Nigeria, others. Controls ~35-40% of global oil production. OPEC+ (since 2016) includes Russia, Kazakhstan → ~55% of production. Mechanism: production quotas. Historical crises: 1973 embargo, 1979 Iranian revolution, 2014 price war, 2020 COVID crash.

**Peaking Plant**: Power plant operating only during demand spikes (hours per day). Low capital cost, high fuel cost per MWh. Technologies: simple-cycle gas turbines, diesel generators, batteries. Capacity factor: 5-15%. Economics: low utilization amortizes low capital over few hours. Complements baseload (high capital, low fuel cost, runs continuously). Grid needs full portfolio: baseload, intermediate, peaking.

**Peak Oil**: Point of maximum oil production rate. M. King Hubbert (1956) predicted US peak ~1970 (correct for conventional oil). US total production peaked 1970, declined until 2008 shale revolution produced second peak (13 million b/d by 2019). Global peak debated: geological peak (when will reserves peak?) vs demand peak (when will consumption decline due to EVs, efficiency, policy?). Demand peak possibly 2025-2035.

**Petrodollar**: System of pricing oil in US dollars and recycling revenue into dollar-denominated assets (US Treasuries, real estate, equities). Established 1974 (Saudi Arabia post-1973 oil shock). Creates global dollar demand beyond US trade, allowing US to borrow cheaply (exorbitant privilege). Benefits: US financing, Saudi security guarantee. Challenges: de-dollarization efforts (China-Saudi yuan sales), dollar weaponization (Russia sanctions). Dollar still dominant but share eroding.

**Photovoltaic (PV) Effect**: Conversion of light to electricity in semiconductors. Photon with energy > bandgap creates electron-hole pair. Built-in electric field at p-n junction separates electron and hole before recombination. Discovered by Edmond Becquerel (1839), explained by Einstein (1905, Nobel Prize 1921). Avoids Carnot efficiency limits (not a heat engine) but faces Shockley-Queisser limit (~33.7% for single-junction silicon).

**P-N Junction**: Interface between P-type (hole-rich) and N-type (electron-rich) semiconductor. Diffusion → electrons and holes recombine near junction → depletion zone (no free carriers) → built-in electric field. Forward bias: current flows exponentially. Reverse bias: minimal current (leakage). Foundation of diodes, LEDs, solar cells, transistors. Created by doping adjacent regions differently.

**Primary Energy**: Energy as it exists in nature before conversion. Examples: crude oil, coal, natural gas, uranium, sunlight, wind, falling water. Contrast to secondary energy (after conversion: electricity, gasoline, hydrogen) and final energy (delivered to consumers). Global primary energy consumption ~580 EJ/year. ~82% from fossil fuels (2023), ~18% low-carbon.

**Pumped Hydro Storage**: Energy storage using two water reservoirs at different elevations. Excess electricity pumps water uphill (charging). When needed, water flows downhill through turbines (discharging). Round-trip efficiency: 75-85%. Global capacity ~160 GW (~95% of grid-scale storage by energy capacity). Duration: 4-12 hours typical, up to 24+ hours if reservoirs large. Examples: Bath County (US, 3 GW / 24 GWh), Dinorwig (UK, 1.7 GW / 9 GWh).

**PWR (Pressurized Water Reactor)**: Most common nuclear reactor type (~67% of global fleet). Water at ~155 bar pressure (~2,250 psi) heated to ~315°C in reactor core but remains liquid. Heat transferred to secondary loop via steam generator. Secondary loop boils at lower pressure (~70 bar, ~280°C). Two barriers between radioactive fuel and turbine. Thermal efficiency ~33-37% (limited by modest steam temperature). Examples: Westinghouse AP1000, EPR, Russian VVER.

**Rare Earth Elements (REEs)**: 17 elements (lanthanides + scandium + yttrium) critical for permanent magnets, electronics, phosphors, catalysts. Misnomer — not rare (cerium more abundant than copper) but dispersed and difficult to separate. Supply chain: China mines 60%, refines 90%, manufactures 92% of magnets. US 15% mining, 5% refining. Applications: Nd/Pr (wind turbine magnets, EV motors), Dy/Tb (high-temp magnet additives), Eu/Tb (LED phosphors). Processing generates toxic and radioactive waste.

**Resource Curse**: See Dutch Disease.

**Round-Trip Efficiency**: Energy output / energy input for storage technologies. Lithium-ion battery: 85-90%. Pumped hydro: 75-85%. Compressed air: 50-70%. Hydrogen (electrolysis + fuel cell): 30-40%. Hydrogen (electrolysis + combustion turbine): 30-35%. Lower efficiency acceptable for seasonal storage (few cycles per year) but not for daily cycling (365 cycles/year). Efficiency fundamentally limited by thermodynamics (Second Law).

**Saline Aquifer**: Deep porous rock formation (>800m depth) saturated with brine (salt water), suitable for CO₂ storage. CO₂ at depth becomes supercritical fluid (liquid-like density, gas-like viscosity). Trapped by impermeable cap rock (shale, salt). Over decades, dissolves in brine and mineralizes (reacts to form carbonates). Global capacity: 1,000-10,000 Gt CO₂ (centuries of emissions). Examples: Sleipner (Norway, injecting since 1996), Illinois Basin.

**Secondary Energy**: Energy after conversion from primary form. Examples: electricity (from coal/gas/nuclear/renewables), gasoline (from crude oil), hydrogen (from natural gas or electrolysis). Conversion always involves losses (First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics). Global primary energy ~580 EJ; final energy delivered to consumers ~400 EJ; useful energy (after all conversion losses) ~240 EJ.

**Semiconductor**: Material with electrical conductivity between metals (very high) and insulators (very low). Bandgap 0.5-3.5 eV. Conductivity tunable by doping, electric fields, temperature. Silicon (1.12 eV bandgap) dominates electronics. Wide-bandgap semiconductors (SiC 3.26 eV, GaN 3.4 eV) enable power electronics. Compound semiconductors (GaAs, InP) used for optoelectronics, RF.

**Shockley-Queisser Limit**: Theoretical maximum efficiency for single-junction solar cell: ~33.7% for optimal bandgap (~1.34 eV, close to GaAs). Silicon (1.12 eV): ~32% theoretical max. Best lab silicon cell: 26.7%. Commercial modules: 20-22%. Loss mechanisms: sub-bandgap photons transmitted (23%), above-bandgap excess energy thermalized (33%), radiative recombination (7%), other (4%). Derived by William Shockley and Hans Queisser (1961).

**SiC (Silicon Carbide)**: Wide-bandgap semiconductor (3.26 eV vs 1.12 eV for Si). Bandgap enables higher voltage, higher temperature operation (200-300°C without cooling), lower losses. Applications: EV inverters (DC battery → AC motor), fast chargers, grid power electronics. SiC MOSFET: 98-99% efficient vs 95-97% for Si IGBT. System efficiency gain 2-3% → 5-10% more range or smaller battery. Tesla Model 3 (2018) first mass-market EV with full SiC inverter.

**Spent Fuel (Nuclear)**: Used nuclear fuel containing fission products (~3.5%), transuranic actinides (~1.5%), unused uranium (~95%). Highly radioactive; decay heat requires cooling (spent fuel pool 5-10 years, then dry cask). Radiotoxicity: first 100 years dominated by fission products (Cs-137, Sr-90); 100-10,000 years dominated by actinides (Pu, Am, Cm); after 300 years drops below natural uranium ore. Disposal: deep geological repository (Finland's Onkalo under construction, US Yucca Mountain licensed but politically blocked).

**Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)**: Emergency oil storage to buffer supply disruptions. US SPR: established 1975 after 1973 embargo. Capacity: 714 million barrels in underground salt domes (Gulf Coast). Historical peak: 727 million barrels (2010). Current: ~370 million barrels (after 180 million barrel release 2022). IEA requires member countries maintain 90 days of import reserves. Japan: ~590 million barrels, China: ~550 million barrels (growing), India: ~40 million barrels (growing).

**Swanson's Law**: Solar module prices fall ~20-24% per doubling of cumulative production. Named after Richard Swanson (SunPower founder). Historical data: 1976: $76/W → 2023: $0.18-0.22/W (99.7% reduction over 47 years). Analogous to Moore's Law for semiconductors. Drivers: manufacturing scale (450x production increase), efficiency gains (15% → 21% average module), thinner wafers, supply chain integration, China's dominance (~80% global capacity).

**TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company)**: World's largest semiconductor foundry. ~54% of global foundry revenue, ~90% of advanced chips (<7nm node). Fabless customers: Apple, Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm, etc. all depend on TSMC. Only manufacturer at 3nm node (Samsung also produces 3nm but lower yield). Annual capex $40B. Geopolitical risk: Taiwan-China tensions make TSMC most critical single point of failure in global economy. CHIPS Act incentivizes alternative (Arizona fab under construction).

**Thermodynamics**: Science of energy, work, heat, and entropy. Four laws govern all energy systems: Zeroth (temperature well-defined), First (energy conserved), Second (entropy increases, efficiency limited by Carnot), Third (absolute zero unreachable). These are not engineering challenges but absolute constraints. No technology can violate thermodynamic laws. Understanding them essential for evaluating energy systems and debunking impossible claims.

**Tokamak**: Magnetic confinement fusion device with toroidal (donut) shape. Magnetic field (5-10 Tesla) confines plasma (~100-200 million °C). Largest: ITER (France, 23,000 tonnes, ~$25B, first plasma ~2035). Challenges: plasma instabilities, neutron damage to materials, tritium breeding, engineering complexity. Alternative approaches: stellarator (twisted torus), inertial confinement (laser compression). Commercial fusion power: 2040s+ if successful, possibly never.

**Turbine (Steam)**: Device converting thermal energy of steam to mechanical work via expansion through rotating blades. Efficiency ~85-90% (thermal to mechanical). Used in coal, nuclear, geothermal, concentrated solar power plants. High-pressure steam (100-250 bar, 540-600°C) expands through multiple turbine stages to condenser (~0.05 bar, 30-40°C). Exhaust steam condenses; water pumped back to boiler. Typically coupled with generator (mechanical → electrical, ~98% efficient).

**Turbine (Wind)**: Device extracting kinetic energy from moving air. Power = ½ρAv³ (density × swept area × velocity cubed). Betz limit: maximum 59.3% extraction. Real turbines: 45-50%. Size evolution: 1980s (50 kW, 15m rotor) → 2020s onshore (6 MW, 170m) → 2020s offshore (15 MW, 240m). Larger turbines capture more energy (swept area scales as r²) and access stronger, more consistent winds (higher hub height). Capacity factor: onshore 25-45%, offshore 42-55%.

**Uranium (U)**: Heaviest naturally occurring element (atomic number 92). Three natural isotopes: U-234 (0.0054%), U-235 (0.72%, fissile), U-238 (99.27%, fertile → Pu-239 via neutron capture). Global reserves: ~1.7 trillion kg (decades at current consumption). Mining: open pit, underground, in-situ leaching (Kazakhstan 45% of production). Enrichment to 3-5% U-235 required for most reactors (CANDU uses natural U). Energy density: ~80,000 MJ/kg if fully fissioned (~2 million times coal).

**Waste Heat**: Thermal energy rejected to environment during energy conversion. ~60% of global primary energy (~380 EJ/year out of ~580 EJ) becomes waste heat before performing useful work. Sources: power plant condensers (30-150°C), industrial processes (100-1,000°C), vehicle exhaust (200-700°C), buildings HVAC (30-50°C). Combined heat and power (CHP) captures some waste heat for useful purposes (district heating, industrial process heat), achieving 75-95% total efficiency vs 35-45% electricity-only.

**Yield (Semiconductor)**: Percentage of chips passing testing. Varies by process maturity and complexity: mature process (28nm, simple): 90-95%, leading edge (3nm, complex GPU): 50-70%, first wafers of new design: 10-30% (improves over time). Single particle (<0.1 μm dust) can kill chip. Cleanrooms maintain Class 1 cleanliness (<1 particle per cubic foot vs ~1 million in normal air). Yield directly impacts economics — 50% yield doubles effective cost per working chip.

## Key Formulas

| Formula | Name/Application | Variables |
|---------|------------------|-----------|
| **η = 1 - T_cold/T_hot** | Carnot Efficiency (maximum for heat engines) | T in Kelvin |
| **P = ½ρAv³** | Wind Power Available | ρ = air density, A = swept area, v = wind speed |
| **η_Betz = 16/27 ≈ 59.3%** | Betz Limit (max wind energy extraction) | Dimensionless |
| **EROEI = E_out / E_in** | Energy Return on Energy Invested | Dimensionless ratio |
| **COP = Q_heat / W_work** | Coefficient of Performance (heat pumps) | Can exceed 1.0 |
| **E = mc²** | Mass-Energy Equivalence | m in kg, c = 3×10⁸ m/s, E in joules |
| **ΔU = Q - W** | First Law of Thermodynamics | ΔU = internal energy change, Q = heat added, W = work done |
| **ΔS ≥ Q/T** | Second Law of Thermodynamics (entropy) | Equality for reversible, inequality for irreversible |
| **Capacity Factor = E_actual / (P_nameplate × t)** | Actual vs potential output | E in kWh, P in kW, t in hours |
| **Power Density = Power / Area** | Power per unit area (W/m²) | Varies by source |
| **P_loss = I²R** | Resistive losses in transmission | I = current, R = resistance |
| **f = (P × RPM) / 120** | Generator frequency | P = pole pairs, RPM = revolutions/min, f in Hz |
| **Q = mCpΔT** | Heat energy | m = mass, Cp = specific heat, ΔT = temperature change |
