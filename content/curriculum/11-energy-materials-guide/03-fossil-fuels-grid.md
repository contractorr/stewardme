# Fossil Fuels & The Electrical Grid

## Overview

Fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas — provide approximately 82% of global primary energy consumption in 2024, despite decades of renewable energy development and climate policy. Understanding their geology, chemistry, extraction methods, refining processes, combustion characteristics, infrastructure requirements, and the electrical grid that distributes much of their output is essential for evaluating energy transitions, climate policy, and geopolitical dynamics.

These fuels are called "fossil" because they formed from ancient organic matter (plants and marine organisms) buried and transformed over millions of years under heat and pressure. Their energy density and chemical properties made industrialization possible — and their combustion produces the CO₂ emissions driving climate change.

## Coal

### Formation and Types

Coal forms from plant material (trees, ferns, mosses) that accumulated in oxygen-poor swamps and bogs. Over millions of years, sediment burial, heat, and pressure drove out water, oxygen, and hydrogen, concentrating carbon through a process called **coalification**. The longer and deeper the burial, the higher the carbon content and energy density.

| Type | Carbon Content | Moisture | Energy (MJ/kg) | Formation Depth/Time | Primary Use | Global Reserves |
|------|----------------|----------|----------------|----------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Peat** | <60% | 75-90% | 12-15 | Surface, recent (~1,000s years) | Soil amendment, fuel in some regions | Not counted as coal |
| **Lignite** (brown coal) | 60-70% | 30-60% | 15-20 | Shallow, <50M years | Power generation (low quality) | ~450 Gt |
| **Sub-bituminous** | 70-76% | 15-30% | 20-25 | Medium depth, 50-100M years | Power generation | ~390 Gt |
| **Bituminous** | 76-86% | 2-15% | 25-33 | Deep burial, 100-300M years | Power, steel (coking coal) | ~430 Gt |
| **Anthracite** | 86-97% | <5% | 33-35 | Deepest, metamorphic pressure | Heating, metallurgy, carbon products | ~75 Gt |

**Total global coal reserves**: ~1.1 trillion tonnes (recoverable with current technology and prices). At current consumption rates (~8 billion tonnes/year), this represents ~140 years of supply. However, climate constraints make extracting all reserves incompatible with limiting warming to 1.5°C or even 2°C.

### Combustion Chemistry

Complete combustion of carbon:
```
C + O₂ → CO₂ + 393.5 kJ/mol
```

For a typical bituminous coal (assume 80% carbon, 5% hydrogen, 10% ash, 5% sulfur/other):

**Carbon combustion** (primary energy source):
```
C + O₂ → CO₂
1 kg carbon → 2.67 kg CO₂ + 32.8 MJ
```

**Hydrogen combustion** (secondary):
```
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
1 kg hydrogen → 9 kg water + 142 MJ
```

**Sulfur combustion** (pollutant, not desired):
```
S + O₂ → SO₂ (sulfur dioxide, causes acid rain)
```

**Nitrogen oxides formation** (high-temperature side reaction):
```
N₂ + O₂ → 2NO (at combustion temperatures >1,500°C)
2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂ (in atmosphere)
```

### Emissions Profile

Burning one tonne of bituminous coal (assume 27 MJ/kg, 75% carbon) produces approximately:

- **CO₂**: ~2.5 tonnes (1,000g CO₂ per kWh electricity at 35% plant efficiency)
- **SO₂**: 10-50 kg (depending on sulfur content; scrubbers can remove 95%+)
- **NOₓ**: 5-10 kg (smog precursor, acid rain)
- **Particulates (PM2.5, PM10)**: 1-5 kg (ash, unburned carbon — major health hazard)
- **Mercury**: 0.1-0.3 grams (neurotoxin, bioaccumulates in fish)
- **Arsenic, lead, cadmium**: trace amounts (heavy metals)
- **Fly ash**: 80-120 kg (captured in electrostatic precipitators; used in concrete)
- **Bottom ash**: 20-40 kg (grate residue)

**Health impacts**: The WHO estimates ~800,000 premature deaths per year globally from outdoor air pollution attributable to coal combustion, primarily from PM2.5 and NO₂. China's coal pollution alone caused an estimated 360,000 premature deaths in 2013 before aggressive pollution control measures reduced this to ~200,000 by 2020.

### Coal Mining Methods

**Surface mining** (60% of global production):
- **Strip mining**: Remove overburden (soil/rock), extract horizontal coal seams, restore land
- **Mountaintop removal**: Blast off mountain peaks to access coal seams (Appalachia, US)
- **Open-pit mining**: Large excavations for thick, deep deposits

**Underground mining** (40% of global production):
- **Room-and-pillar**: Excavate "rooms" while leaving coal pillars to support roof
- **Longwall mining**: Mechanized extraction of entire coal seam in 200-300m wide panels
- **Continuous mining**: Machine with rotating drum cuts coal from seam face

**Safety**: Coal mining remains dangerous. China: ~300-600 deaths per year (down from 6,000+ in early 2000s). US: ~15-20 deaths per year. Global: ~12,000 deaths per year, primarily in China, India, and developing countries.

**Environmental impacts**: Acid mine drainage (sulfuric acid leaching from exposed pyrite), groundwater contamination, methane emissions (coal seams release CH₄), land disruption, black lung disease (coal workers' pneumoconiosis) affecting ~60,000 US miners.

## Oil (Petroleum)

### Formation and Extraction

Oil forms from marine microorganisms (plankton, algae, bacteria) that settled in oxygen-poor sedimentary basins (ancient seas, lake beds). Burial under sediment layers subjected the organic material to heat and pressure in the **"oil window"** — 60-160°C at 2-4 km depth. Below 160°C, oil "cracks" into natural gas (thermogenic gas).

The process takes 10-100 million years. Oil migrates upward through porous rock until trapped by impermeable cap rock, forming a reservoir.

### Conventional vs Unconventional Oil

**Conventional oil**: Flows naturally or with pumping from porous rock reservoirs.
- **Primary recovery** (~10-25% of oil in place): Natural pressure drives oil to wells
- **Secondary recovery** (~20-40% total): Water or gas injection to maintain pressure
- **Enhanced oil recovery (EOR)** (~30-60% total): CO₂ injection, steam injection, chemicals

**Unconventional oil** (requires special extraction techniques):

| Type | Formation | Extraction | EROEI | Major Regions | Production Cost |
|------|-----------|-----------|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Tight oil / Shale oil** | Oil trapped in low-permeability shale | Hydraulic fracturing + horizontal drilling | 5-10:1 | US (Permian, Bakken, Eagle Ford) | $40-70/bbl |
| **Oil sands (bitumen)** | Bitumen mixed with sand/clay | Surface mining or steam injection (SAGD) | 3-5:1 | Canada (Athabasca), Venezuela | $50-80/bbl |
| **Extra-heavy oil** | Very viscous crude | Steam injection, diluents | 4-7:1 | Venezuela (Orinoco Belt) | $45-75/bbl |
| **Deepwater** | Offshore reservoirs >300m depth | Floating platforms, subsea systems | 10-20:1 | Brazil, Gulf of Mexico, West Africa | $50-90/bbl |
| **Ultra-deepwater** | >1,500m depth | Advanced drilling technology | 8-15:1 | Brazil pre-salt, Gulf of Mexico | $60-100/bbl |

**The US shale revolution** (2008-2020): Horizontal drilling + hydraulic fracturing unlocked ~8 million barrels per day of tight oil production, making the US the world's largest oil producer (~13 million bbl/day in 2023). This reversed decades of declining US production and reshaped global oil markets.

**Hydraulic fracturing ("fracking")**: Inject water, sand (proppant), and chemicals at high pressure (8,000-12,000 psi) to fracture shale rock, creating pathways for oil/gas to flow. A single horizontal well might use 5-10 million gallons of water and 1,000-4,000 tonnes of sand. Wells typically decline 60-80% in first year, requiring continuous drilling to maintain production.

### Crude Oil Composition and Refining

Crude oil is a complex mixture of hundreds of hydrocarbon compounds, classified by:

**API gravity** (density):
- Light crude: >31° API (low density, high value — more gasoline/diesel)
- Medium crude: 22-31° API
- Heavy crude: 10-22° API (high density, requires more refining)

**Sulfur content**:
- Sweet crude: <0.5% sulfur (easier to refine, higher value)
- Sour crude: >0.5% sulfur (requires desulfurization)

**Example crude oils**:
- WTI (West Texas Intermediate): 39.6° API, 0.24% sulfur (light sweet — benchmark)
- Brent: 38° API, 0.37% sulfur (light sweet — benchmark)
- Dubai/Oman: 31° API, 2% sulfur (medium sour)
- Venezuelan: 15-25° API, 2-3% sulfur (heavy sour — low value)

### Refining Process

**Fractional distillation**: Heat crude to ~350-400°C and separate by boiling point in a distillation column:

| Fraction | Boiling Range (°C) | Carbon Chain | Primary Products | Share of Barrel | Value (relative) |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Light gases** | <30 | C1-C4 | LPG, petrochemical feedstock | 2-4% | Medium |
| **Naphtha** | 30-180 | C5-C12 | Gasoline, jet fuel, petrochemicals | 25-30% | High |
| **Middle distillates** | 180-350 | C10-C20 | Kerosene, jet fuel, diesel | 30-40% | Highest |
| **Heavy gas oil** | 350-500 | C20-C35 | Industrial fuel, cracking feedstock | 15-20% | Medium |
| **Residuum** | >500 | >C35 | Asphalt, heavy fuel oil, lubricants | 15-25% | Low |

**Additional refining processes**:
- **Catalytic cracking**: Break heavy molecules into lighter ones (increase gasoline yield)
- **Hydrocracking**: Add hydrogen while cracking (produces cleaner diesel)
- **Catalytic reforming**: Rearrange molecules to increase octane rating (premium gasoline)
- **Desulfurization**: Remove sulfur (meet environmental regulations)
- **Alkylation**: Combine small molecules to create high-octane gasoline components

**Refinery complexity**: Simple refineries (just distillation) produce fixed ratios of products. Complex refineries (with cracking, reforming, etc.) can adjust product slate to market demand, producing more gasoline or diesel as needed. Complex refineries cost $5-15 billion and take 5-7 years to build.

### Peak Oil Theory and Reality

**M. King Hubbert** (1956) predicted US oil production would peak around 1970 based on the logistic curve of resource extraction. US production did peak at 9.6 million b/d in 1970, then declined to 5 million b/d by 2008 — vindicating Hubbert's analysis.

But the **shale revolution** (2008-2020) produced a second peak, reaching 13 million b/d by 2019. This demonstrated that "peak oil" depends on technology and price, not just geology.

**Current consensus** (IEA, major analysts):
- **Geological peak supply**: Probably 2030-2050, depending on investment and technology
- **Peak demand**: Possibly 2025-2035, driven by electric vehicles, efficiency, and policy

The question has shifted from "when will we run out of oil?" to "when will we stop wanting it?"

**Proven reserves** (economically recoverable at current prices/technology): ~1.7 trillion barrels
- At 100 million b/d consumption: 47 years of supply
- But climate constraints make extracting all reserves incompatible with Paris Agreement targets

**Ultimately recoverable resources** (including unconventional, requiring higher prices): ~3-4 trillion barrels — centuries of supply at current consumption, but burning it would cause catastrophic climate change.

### Oil Reserve Classifications

Petroleum engineers classify reserves by probability of recovery:

- **1P (Proved)**: ≥90% probability of recovery under current economic and operating conditions
- **2P (Proved + Probable)**: ≥50% probability
- **3P (Proved + Probable + Possible)**: ≥10% probability

Companies report 1P reserves to regulators. National oil companies often report 2P or even 3P, making international comparisons difficult. OPEC countries' reserves are particularly opaque — several OPEC members increased reported reserves dramatically in the 1980s without major discoveries, likely for quota allocations.

## Natural Gas

### Composition and Properties

Natural gas is primarily **methane (CH₄)** with smaller amounts of heavier hydrocarbons and impurities:

**Typical composition**:
- Methane (CH₄): 70-95%
- Ethane (C₂H₆): 0-20%
- Propane (C₃H₈): 0-10%
- Butane (C₄H₁₀): 0-5%
- Nitrogen (N₂): 0-10%
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂): 0-10%
- Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S): 0-5% (removed during processing — "sour gas")
- Water vapor: varies (removed during processing)

**Processing**: Raw natural gas is processed to remove impurities and separate heavier hydrocarbons:
1. Remove H₂S and CO₂ (acid gas removal — sweetening)
2. Dehydration (remove water to prevent pipeline corrosion and hydrate formation)
3. Separate NGLs (natural gas liquids: ethane, propane, butane — sold separately)
4. Compress for pipeline transport or liquefy for shipping

### Combustion and Emissions

Complete methane combustion:
```
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + 890 kJ/mol
```

1 kg methane → 2.75 kg CO₂ + 2.25 kg H₂O + 55.5 MJ

**For electricity generation**:
- Modern combined cycle gas turbine (CCGT): ~55-60% efficiency
- Emissions: ~400-450g CO₂ per kWh
- Compare to coal: ~900-1,000g CO₂ per kWh

**But methane leakage matters**: CH₄ is a potent greenhouse gas — ~80x the warming potential of CO₂ over 20 years, ~27x over 100 years. Leakage from production, processing, and distribution can offset much of the climate advantage over coal.

**Estimated leakage rates**:
- US natural gas system: 1.5-2.5% of production (EPA estimates)
- Some studies suggest 3-5% in certain basins
- At ~3% leakage, natural gas is no better than coal for climate over 20-year timeframe

### Extraction Technologies

**Conventional gas**: Flows from porous reservoirs (sandstone, carbonate). Often associated with oil deposits.

**Unconventional gas**:
- **Shale gas**: Trapped in low-permeability shale. Extracted via hydraulic fracturing + horizontal drilling (same technique as tight oil). US shale gas production grew from ~2 trillion cubic feet (Tcf) in 2000 to ~30 Tcf in 2020 — revolutionizing US energy markets.
- **Tight gas**: Trapped in low-porosity sandstone or carbonate. Requires fracturing.
- **Coalbed methane (CBM)**: Methane adsorbed onto coal seams. Extracted by depressurizing coal.

**Major shale plays** (US):
- Marcellus/Utica (Appalachia): Largest natural gas field in US
- Permian (Texas/New Mexico): Primarily oil, but significant associated gas
- Haynesville (Louisiana/Texas): Deep, high-pressure gas

### LNG: Liquefied Natural Gas

Natural gas at atmospheric pressure occupies ~600 times the volume of crude oil with equivalent energy. This makes long-distance shipping uneconomical unless liquefied.

**Liquefaction**: Cool gas to -162°C (boiling point of methane), reducing volume by 600x.

**LNG value chain**:

1. **Liquefaction terminal**: $10-20 billion capital cost, 3-5 years construction, 5-10 million tonnes/year capacity
   - Gas purification (remove CO₂, water, mercury, heavy hydrocarbons)
   - Cooling in refrigeration cycles (propane, ethylene, methane cascade)
   - Storage in cryogenic tanks

2. **LNG carrier (tanker)**: ~$200-250 million each, specialized double-hulled ships with insulated tanks, capacity ~150,000-260,000 cubic meters

3. **Regasification terminal**: $1-5 billion, vaporize LNG back to gas
   - Offshore floating storage regasification units (FSRUs): faster, cheaper alternative
   - Pipeline connection to local gas network

**Global LNG trade** (2023): ~400 million tonnes (~540 billion cubic meters)

**Major exporters**:
- Qatar: ~80 million tonnes/year (largest, expanding to 126 Mt/y by 2027)
- Australia: ~80 Mt/y (multiple projects)
- United States: ~85 Mt/y (shale gas boom enabled rapid expansion from ~10 Mt/y in 2016)
- Russia: ~30 Mt/y (Yamal, Sakhalin; limited expansion due to sanctions)
- Malaysia, Indonesia, Nigeria, Algeria: ~10-20 Mt/y each

**Major importers**:
- China: ~80 Mt/y (growing ~10%/year)
- Japan: ~75 Mt/y (post-Fukushima expansion, now declining)
- South Korea: ~45 Mt/y
- Europe: ~120 Mt/y (surged after Russia-Ukraine war; was ~80 Mt/y in 2021)
- India: ~35 Mt/y (growing)

**LNG vs pipeline gas**: LNG is more expensive ($8-15/MMBtu delivered) than pipeline gas ($3-8/MMBtu) but provides flexibility and access to global markets. After Russia cut pipeline gas to Europe (2022), European LNG imports surged from 80 to 120 Mt/y in one year — the fastest energy infrastructure shift in modern history.

## The Electrical Grid

### Grid Structure and Components

The electrical grid is the largest and most complex machine ever built — a real-time balancing act connecting hundreds of gigawatts of generation to billions of consumers across thousands of kilometers.

**Structure**:
```
Generation → Step-up transformer → Transmission (high voltage) → Step-down transformer →
Distribution (medium/low voltage) → Consumer meter → End use
```

| Stage | Voltage (typical) | Purpose | Infrastructure | Losses |
|-------|------------------|---------|----------------|--------|
| **Generation** | 11-25 kV | Produced at power plant | Generators, switchgear | ~1-2% (generator losses) |
| **Step-up transformer** | 11-25 kV → 115-765 kV | Reduce current for long-distance transmission | Power transformers | ~0.5-1% per transformation |
| **Transmission** | 115-765 kV | Long-distance bulk power transfer (100s of km) | High-voltage lines, towers, substations | ~2-4% (line resistance: I²R) |
| **Sub-transmission** | 26-69 kV | Regional distribution to cities/industrial areas | Substations, medium-voltage lines | ~1-2% |
| **Distribution** | 4-35 kV | Local delivery within communities | Distribution lines, poles, local transformers | ~3-5% |
| **Service drop** | 120/240V (US), 230/400V (EU) | Final delivery to homes and businesses | Service lines, meters | ~0.5-1% |

**Total grid losses**: 5-10% in developed countries (US: ~6%, Germany: ~4%), up to 15-25% in developing countries with poor infrastructure.

### Why High Voltage Reduces Losses

Power loss in transmission lines:
```
P_loss = I²R
```
Where I = current, R = resistance of wire.

Power transmitted:
```
P = VI
```
Where V = voltage, I = current.

For the same power transfer, higher voltage → lower current → lower I²R losses.

**Example**: Transmit 1,000 MW over 100 km:

At 100 kV:
- Current: I = P/V = 1,000 MW / 100 kV = 10,000 A
- Power loss (assume R = 0.05 Ω): P_loss = I²R = (10,000)² × 0.05 = 5 MW (0.5%)

At 10 kV (10x lower voltage):
- Current: I = 1,000 MW / 10 kV = 100,000 A
- Power loss: P_loss = (100,000)² × 0.05 = 500 MW (50% loss!)

This is why long-distance transmission uses 345-765 kV. The highest-voltage transmission lines (765 kV in US, 1,000 kV in China/India) can transmit thousands of MW over 500+ km with ~2-3% losses.

### AC vs DC

**Alternating Current (AC)** won the "war of currents" (1880s-1890s, Edison vs Tesla/Westinghouse) because transformers can easily step AC voltage up or down. DC required rotating converters (motor-generator sets), which were expensive and inefficient.

**Why AC**:
- Simple voltage transformation (transformers)
- Easy to generate (synchronous generators)
- No polarity issues (reverses 50/60 times per second)

**Why not AC**:
- Reactive power (inductive/capacitive loads → current out of phase with voltage → wasted capacity)
- Skin effect (high-frequency current concentrates on conductor surface → increased resistance)
- Synchronization required (all generators must run at exact same frequency)

**High-Voltage Direct Current (HVDC)** uses solid-state electronics (thyristors, IGBTs) to convert AC to DC and back:

**HVDC advantages**:
- No reactive power losses
- Lower losses for very long distances (>600 km)
- Underwater cables (AC has capacitive losses in long cables; DC doesn't)
- Connecting asynchronous grids (50 Hz ←→ 60 Hz, or unsynchronized systems)
- Better controllability (instant power flow adjustments)

**HVDC disadvantages**:
- Converter stations expensive ($200-500M each)
- Not easily tapped along route (point-to-point, not networked)
- DC circuit breakers more complex than AC

**Major HVDC projects**:
- Xiangjiaba-Shanghai (China): 2,071 km, ±800 kV, 6.4 GW capacity
- Rio Madeira (Brazil): 2,375 km, ±600 kV, 6.3 GW (hydropower from Amazon to São Paulo)
- North Sea Link (Norway-UK): 720 km subsea, 1.4 GW
- Pacific DC Intertie (US): Oregon → California, 1,362 km, ±500 kV, 3.1 GW

### Grid Frequency and Balancing

**Frequency standards**:
- **60 Hz**: Americas (except Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay), parts of Asia (Japan-East, Philippines, South Korea, Taiwan), Saudi Arabia
- **50 Hz**: Europe, Africa, most of Asia, Australia, South America (except noted)

Frequency is determined by the rotational speed of synchronous generators:
```
f = (P × RPM) / 120
```
Where f = frequency (Hz), P = number of poles, RPM = revolutions per minute.

A 2-pole generator running at 3,600 RPM produces 60 Hz. A 2-pole generator at 3,000 RPM produces 50 Hz.

**Frequency regulation is critical**: Grid frequency must be maintained within ±0.2 Hz (typically ±0.05 Hz for quality grids).

- **Frequency drops**: Demand exceeds supply → generators slow down → frequency drops
- **Frequency rises**: Supply exceeds demand → generators speed up → frequency rises

**Balancing mechanisms** (timescales):

1. **Inertia** (instant, 0-1 seconds): Spinning mass of generators resists frequency changes. Heavy turbines (steam, hydro) provide high inertia. Solar/wind inverters provide zero inertia (no rotating mass).

2. **Primary frequency response** (1-10 seconds): Automatic governor control adjusts turbine fuel/water flow to match frequency. All generators participate proportionally.

3. **Secondary frequency response** (10 seconds - 15 minutes): Automatic generation control (AGC) adjusts set points to restore frequency to nominal and manage congestion.

4. **Tertiary reserves** (15+ minutes): Operators bring online additional generation or curtail demand.

**Grid inertia challenge**: As renewables (inverter-based, no inertia) replace fossil/nuclear generators (synchronous, high inertia), grids have less resistance to frequency disturbances. Solutions: synthetic inertia (batteries programmed to respond to frequency changes), grid-forming inverters, minimum synchronous generation requirements.

### Load Types and Demand Curves

**Load classifications**:

| Type | Characteristics | Technologies | Economics | Share of Capacity |
|------|----------------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Baseload** | Runs continuously, low cost per kWh, slow to adjust | Nuclear, coal, run-of-river hydro, geothermal | Capital-intensive, low fuel cost | 40-60% |
| **Intermediate** (load-following) | Follows daily demand curve, moderate cost | Natural gas CCGT, reservoir hydro | Medium capital, medium fuel cost | 25-35% |
| **Peaking** | Operates during demand spikes (hours/day), high cost per kWh | Gas turbines (simple cycle), hydro, diesel, batteries | Low capital, high fuel cost (or storage cost) | 10-20% |
| **Intermittent** | Produces when available (weather-dependent), low marginal cost | Solar PV, wind | Low operating cost but requires balancing | Growing share |

**Demand curves**:
- **Daily variation**: Lowest overnight (~2-5am), peak late afternoon/early evening (~5-8pm)
- **Seasonal variation**: Summer peak (air conditioning in hot climates), winter peak (heating in cold climates), or both
- **Weekly variation**: Lower weekends, higher weekdays

**Example — California summer day**:
- Minimum demand: ~20 GW (4am)
- Midday demand: ~30 GW (solar displaces ~10 GW)
- Evening peak: ~40 GW (solar drops to zero, demand stays high)
- **Ramping requirement**: ~20 GW in 3 hours (5pm-8pm) — the "duck curve" challenge

### Capacity Factor vs Nameplate Capacity

**Nameplate capacity**: Maximum rated output under ideal conditions (MW or GW).

**Capacity factor**: Actual output / maximum possible output over a time period (typically annual).
```
Capacity Factor = (Actual energy generated) / (Nameplate capacity × Time period)
```

| Source | Typical Capacity Factor | Interpretation |
|--------|------------------------|----------------|
| Nuclear | 90-93% | Runs almost continuously; refueling outages every 18-24 months |
| Geothermal | 85-95% | Baseload operation; minimal downtime |
| Coal | 40-70% | Economic dispatch; cycling for load-following |
| Natural gas (CCGT) | 40-60% | Load-following; economics depend on gas prices |
| Natural gas (peaking) | 5-15% | Only runs during price spikes |
| Onshore wind | 25-45% | Wind varies; great sites 40%+, average sites 25-30% |
| Offshore wind | 40-55% | Stronger, more consistent winds |
| Solar PV (utility-scale) | 15-30% | Sun shines ~6-10 hours/day; clouds reduce output |
| Solar PV (rooftop) | 12-25% | Lower than utility-scale (less optimal orientation/tilt) |
| Hydroelectric | 30-60% | Run-of-river ~30-50%, reservoir ~50-70% (depends on water availability) |

**Why this matters**: Comparing nameplate capacity without capacity factor is misleading.

**Example**:
- 1 GW nuclear plant at 92% CF generates: 1 GW × 0.92 × 8,760 hours/year = **8,060 GWh/year**
- 1 GW solar farm at 25% CF generates: 1 GW × 0.25 × 8,760 hours/year = **2,190 GWh/year**

To replace the nuclear plant's annual output requires **3.7 GW of solar capacity** (nameplate), plus storage to shift generation from day to night.

### Grid Reliability and N-1 Security

**Reliability metrics**:
- **SAIDI** (System Average Interruption Duration Index): Average outage minutes per customer per year. US average: ~120 minutes/year. Japan: ~10 minutes/year. Developing countries: 500-5,000+ minutes/year.
- **SAIFI** (System Average Interruption Frequency Index): Average number of outages per customer per year.

**N-1 redundancy**: The grid must survive the loss of any single component without cascading failures. This requires:
- Redundant transmission paths
- Generator headroom (spinning reserves)
- Automatic load shedding (disconnect non-critical loads to prevent total blackout)

**Cascading failures** (blackout propagation):

When a heavily loaded line trips offline, its power flow redistributes to parallel paths. If those lines are also heavily loaded, they may overload and trip, redistributing to remaining lines, which then overload, etc. The 2003 Northeast Blackout followed this pattern:

**2003 Northeast Blackout** (August 14, 2003):
1. **Initial trigger**: Tree contact + sagging lines in Ohio (FirstEnergy)
2. **Alarm failure**: Operator unaware of situation
3. **Cascade**: Line trips → power redistributes → more lines trip
4. **Grid collapse**: 55 million people without power in US/Canada
5. **Duration**: 2 hours to 2 days (depending on location)
6. **Cost**: $6-10 billion in lost economic activity

**Lessons**: Real-time monitoring (phasor measurement units), vegetation management, operator training, N-1 testing, automatic protective relays.

### Smart Grid and Grid Modernization

**Smart grid technologies**:

1. **Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI)**: Digital meters with two-way communication. Enable time-of-use pricing, real-time monitoring, remote disconnect/reconnect.

2. **Distribution Automation**: Sensors, switches, and controls on distribution lines. Automatically reroute power around faults, reducing outage duration.

3. **Demand Response**: Incentivize or require customers to reduce consumption during peak periods or grid stress. Examples:
   - **Price signals**: Time-of-use rates, real-time pricing
   - **Direct load control**: Utility remotely cycles AC, water heaters, EV charging
   - **Industrial curtailment**: Large industrial customers paid to shut down during emergencies

4. **Grid-Scale Storage**: Batteries, pumped hydro, compressed air. Shift generation in time (charge when cheap/abundant, discharge when expensive/scarce).

5. **Distributed Energy Resources (DERs)**: Rooftop solar, home batteries, EVs. Enable local generation and storage but create grid management challenges (voltage fluctuations, reverse power flow).

6. **Phasor Measurement Units (PMUs)**: GPS-synchronized sensors measuring voltage and current at 30-60 samples per second. Provide real-time visibility into grid dynamics.

## Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

### Capture Technologies

**1. Post-combustion capture** (most common for retrofits):
- Flue gas (10-15% CO₂) passes through chemical solvent (typically amines: MEA, DEA)
- CO₂ reacts with solvent, forming carbamate
- Heat solvent to 120-140°C to release pure CO₂
- Compress CO₂ to ~100 bar for transport/storage
- **Energy penalty**: ~25-35% (reduces net plant efficiency from 38% to 28-30%)
- **Cost**: $50-80/tonne CO₂ captured

**2. Pre-combustion capture** (for new plants):
- Convert fuel to H₂ + CO₂ via gasification or reforming
- Capture CO₂ before combustion
- Burn pure H₂ in turbine or fuel cell
- **Energy penalty**: ~15-25%
- **Cost**: $40-70/tonne CO₂
- **Advantage**: Concentrated CO₂ stream (easier to capture)

**3. Oxy-fuel combustion**:
- Burn fuel in pure O₂ (from air separation unit) instead of air
- Produces concentrated CO₂ stream (~80-90% CO₂)
- **Energy penalty**: ~20-30% (oxygen production energy-intensive)
- **Cost**: $50-75/tonne CO₂

**4. Direct Air Capture (DAC)**:
- Extract CO₂ directly from ambient air (~420 ppm)
- **Challenges**: Very dilute CO₂ requires processing huge volumes of air
- **Technologies**: Solid sorbents (Climeworks), liquid solvents (Carbon Engineering)
- **Energy requirement**: ~1.5-2.5 MWh per tonne CO₂ (thermal + electrical)
- **Cost**: $250-600/tonne CO₂ (current); target $100-200/tonne

### Storage Methods

**1. Geological storage in saline aquifers** (largest capacity):
- Inject CO₂ into deep (>800m) porous rock formations saturated with brine
- CO₂ at >800m depth and >31°C is supercritical (liquid-like density, gas-like viscosity)
- Trapped by impermeable cap rock (shale, salt)
- Over decades to centuries, CO₂ dissolves in brine and mineralizes (reacts with rock to form carbonates)
- **Capacity**: ~1,000-10,000 Gt CO₂ globally (centuries of current emissions)
- **Example**: Sleipner (Norway), injecting ~1 Mt CO₂/year since 1996

**2. Depleted oil and gas reservoirs**:
- Oil/gas fields already proven to trap hydrocarbons for millions of years
- Infrastructure (wells, monitoring) may already exist
- **Capacity**: ~900-1,200 Gt CO₂ globally
- **Example**: Weyburn-Midale (Canada), ~20 Mt CO₂ injected since 2000

**3. Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)**:
- Inject CO₂ to extract additional oil (reduces viscosity, maintains pressure)
- Economically attractive if oil price > $50-70/bbl
- But ~30-50% of injected CO₂ returns to surface with oil (must recapture)
- Net effect: reduce emissions per barrel but still produce/burn oil
- **Not a climate solution** unless oil is permanently displaced (which it isn't)

**4. Mineralization**:
- React CO₂ with minerals (olivine, serpentine, basalt) to form stable carbonates
- **Example**: CarbFix (Iceland) injects CO₂ into basalt; ~95% mineralizes in <2 years
- **Challenge**: Requires specific geology (basalt, ultramafic rocks)
- **Capacity**: Large but site-specific

### CCS Economics and Deployment

**Current deployment** (2023): ~45 operating facilities capturing ~40 Mt CO₂/year globally
- Compare to global CO₂ emissions: ~37 Gt/year
- CCS captures ~0.1% of global emissions

**Costs**:
- CAPEX: $80-150/tonne/year of capacity (coal), $50-100/tonne/year (gas)
- OPEX: $30-50/tonne CO₂
- Total LCOC (levelized cost of capture): $50-100/tonne CO₂

**Economic challenges**:
1. **High cost**: $50-100/tonne CO₂ adds significantly to electricity costs
2. **Energy penalty**: Reduces plant efficiency by 25-35%, increasing fuel consumption
3. **Policy uncertainty**: CCS economics depend on carbon prices or subsidies
4. **Storage liability**: Who's responsible if CO₂ leaks in 50 years?

**US policy support**: 45Q tax credit provides $85/tonne for permanent storage, $60/tonne for EOR. This makes some CCS projects economically viable.

**Future potential**: IEA Net Zero scenario requires CCS to scale from 40 Mt/year (2023) to 1,670 Mt/year (2030) and 7,600 Mt/year (2050). This 190x scale-up over 27 years would be unprecedented for any energy technology.

## Key Terms

- **Fracking (Hydraulic Fracturing)**: High-pressure fluid injection to fracture rock and release oil/gas
- **LNG**: Liquefied Natural Gas, cooled to -162°C for shipping, reducing volume 600x
- **Capacity Factor**: Actual output / maximum possible output over time (annual average)
- **Nameplate Capacity**: Maximum rated output of a power plant under ideal conditions (MW)
- **Baseload**: Minimum continuous power demand; met by always-on plants
- **Grid Frequency**: 50 or 60 Hz; must be precisely maintained for grid stability
- **HVDC**: High-Voltage Direct Current for long-distance or underwater transmission
- **N-1 Redundancy**: Grid survives any single component failure without cascading blackout
- **CCS**: Carbon Capture and Storage — capturing CO₂ and storing it geologically
- **Peak Oil**: Debated point of maximum oil production rate (geological vs demand peak)
- **CCGT**: Combined Cycle Gas Turbine — gas turbine + steam turbine for ~60% efficiency
- **Smart Grid**: Modern grid with digital monitoring, automation, and two-way communication
- **Curtailment**: Wasting excess renewable generation when it exceeds demand
- **Duck Curve**: Net load shape with midday solar dip and steep evening ramp
- **EROEI**: Energy Return on Energy Invested — net energy ratio

## Summary

Fossil fuels dominate global energy: coal (27%), oil (31%), natural gas (24%). Each has distinct geology, chemistry, infrastructure, and emissions. Coal is the most carbon-intensive (~1,000g CO₂/kWh) but cheapest in many markets. Oil dominates transport with no easy substitute for aviation and shipping. Natural gas is cleanest fossil fuel (~450g CO₂/kWh) but methane leakage significantly erodes climate advantage.

Coal forms from terrestrial plants, compressed over millions of years, with energy density increasing from lignite (15-20 MJ/kg) to anthracite (33-35 MJ/kg). Global production ~8.3 billion tonnes/year, primarily for electricity (65%) and steel (15%). Declining in developed economies due to cheap gas and renewables, but growing in developing Asia.

Oil forms from marine organisms in sedimentary basins, requiring 10-100 million years in the "oil window" (60-160°C). Conventional production is declining, but unconventional sources (tight oil, oil sands, deepwater) extend supply. The US shale revolution (2008-2020) added ~8 million b/d, making the US the world's largest producer. Global consumption ~100 million b/d, with transport consuming 60%.

Natural gas is primarily methane, extracted from conventional reservoirs or shale via fracking. Cleanest-burning fossil fuel but vulnerable to methane leakage. LNG enables global trade, with infrastructure investments of $10-20 billion per liquefaction terminal. Major trade flows from Qatar, Australia, US to Asia and Europe.

The electrical grid is civilization's largest machine, balancing supply and demand in real-time. High-voltage transmission (115-765 kV) minimizes losses (I²R); transformers step voltage up/down. AC dominates for ease of voltage transformation, but HVDC is optimal for long distances (>600 km) and undersea cables. Grid frequency (50 or 60 Hz) must be precisely maintained — drops indicate demand exceeding supply.

Capacity factor distinguishes actual output from nameplate capacity. Nuclear ~92%, coal ~50%, wind ~35%, solar ~25%. Comparing sources requires accounting for capacity factor and dispatchability. Baseload plants run continuously, peaking plants operate during demand spikes.

Carbon Capture and Storage remains economically challenged despite decades of development. Current deployment: ~40 Mt CO₂/year (<0.1% of global emissions). Costs: $50-100/tonne for point-source capture, $250-600/tonne for direct air capture. Energy penalty reduces plant efficiency by 25-35%. Scaling to gigatonne scale faces enormous cost and infrastructure challenges.

Understanding fossil fuel infrastructure and grid operations is essential context for evaluating energy transitions. The physical scale, economic relationships, and technological inertia create path dependencies that cannot be ignored in climate and energy policy.
