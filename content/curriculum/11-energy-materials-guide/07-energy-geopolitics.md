# Energy Geopolitics & Transition

## Overview

Energy is inseparable from geopolitics. Wars are fought over oil. Economic sanctions target energy exports. Supply chains for critical minerals create new dependencies. The energy transition doesn't eliminate geopolitical competition — it shifts the battleground from oil fields to lithium mines, from OPEC to rare earth refining facilities, from pipelines to semiconductor fabs.

Understanding energy geopolitics requires recognizing that energy isn't just an economic commodity — it's a strategic resource that determines military capability, economic prosperity, and political leverage. Control over energy production, transportation chokepoints, refining capacity, and critical material processing gives nations power over others. The geography of these resources, combined with the infrastructure required to move and process them, creates dependencies that shape international relations.

## Oil Geopolitics

### OPEC and Market Power

The Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC), founded in 1960, is a cartel that coordinates production quotas among member nations to influence global oil prices. Original members: Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Venezuela. Later additions: UAE, Qatar (left 2019), Libya, Algeria, Nigeria, Ecuador, Gabon, Angola, Equatorial Guinea, Congo.

**OPEC+ (2016-present)**: Expanded alliance including Russia, Kazakhstan, Mexico, and others. Controls ~55% of global oil production (~55 million barrels/day out of ~100 million b/d total).

**Mechanism**: Member countries negotiate production quotas at ministerial meetings. Cutting production reduces supply → higher prices. Increasing production → lower prices. But cartels face a collective action problem: each member has incentive to cheat (produce more than quota to capture more revenue at high prices).

**Market share vs price tradeoff**:
- **High prices**: Maximize short-term revenue but incentivize alternatives (electric vehicles, efficiency, renewables) and make high-cost producers (US shale, Canadian oil sands) profitable
- **Low prices**: Maintain market share by bankrupting high-cost competitors but reduce producer revenue

This dilemma has shaped OPEC strategy for decades.

### Historical Oil Crises and Lessons

| Crisis | Year | Trigger | Price Impact | Economic Impact | Geopolitical Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Arab Oil Embargo** | 1973-1974 | Yom Kippur War; Arab states cut production and embargoed Western countries supporting Israel | $3 → $12/bbl (4x increase) | Global recession, 10%+ unemployment, inflation → stagflation | Demonstrated oil as political weapon; created strategic petroleum reserves |
| **Iranian Revolution** | 1979 | Iranian oil production collapsed from 6 to 1.5 million b/d during revolution | $14 → $35/bbl | Second oil shock; recession; Fed raised rates to 20% to fight inflation | Shifted Middle East power balance; Iraq-Iran War (1980-1988) disrupted ~5 million b/d |
| **Gulf War** | 1990-1991 | Iraq invaded Kuwait; coalition forces bombed Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil facilities | $17 → $40/bbl (briefly) | Brief spike; US recession 1990-1991 | Reinforced US military presence in Gulf; Kuwait oil production resumed 1991 |
| **Shale Revolution** | 2014-2016 | US tight oil production surged; Saudi Arabia refused to cut production (price war against shale) | $110 → $26/bbl | Bankruptcies in US shale sector; Russian and Venezuelan fiscal crises | US became net oil exporter (2019); OPEC market power declined |
| **COVID-19 Crash** | 2020 | Demand collapsed 20-30% as lockdowns halted transport | Briefly negative (futures contracts); ~$20/bbl spot | Worst oil demand shock in history; bankruptcies; debt crises in producer nations | OPEC+ coordinated record 10 million b/d cut; demonstrated cartel still functional |
| **Russia-Ukraine War** | 2022 | Western sanctions targeted Russian oil exports (~7.5 million b/d); supply disruption fears | $70 → $130/bbl (peak) | Inflation spike; energy crisis in Europe; rerouted trade flows | Russia redirected oil to Asia (India, China) at discount; Europe diversified away from Russian oil |

**Key lessons**:
1. **Oil price shocks trigger recessions**: High prices act as a tax on consumers, reducing demand for other goods and services
2. **Supply disruptions are geopolitically potent**: Ability to cut supply gives producer nations leverage
3. **Substitution takes time**: Alternatives (efficiency, EVs, renewables) respond to high prices but require years to deploy at scale
4. **Strategic reserves matter**: US Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) was created after 1973 embargo; held 700 million barrels at peak, released 180 million barrels in 2022 to moderate prices
5. **Cartels are unstable**: Members cheat on quotas; high prices incentivize new supply (shale); low prices cause fiscal crises in producer nations dependent on oil revenue

### The Petrodollar System

Since 1974 (following the 1973 oil shock), Saudi Arabia has priced oil in US dollars and recycled petrodollars into US Treasury bonds and financial markets. Other OPEC members followed. This creates the **petrodollar system**:

**Mechanism**:
1. Oil is priced and traded globally in US dollars
2. Countries needing oil must hold dollar reserves
3. Oil exporters earn dollars, invest in dollar assets (US Treasuries, real estate, equities)
4. Creates continuous demand for dollars beyond US trade flows

**Benefits to US**:
- **Exorbitant privilege**: Global demand for dollars allows US to borrow cheaply (foreign governments finance US deficits by buying Treasuries)
- **Seigniorage**: US earns profit from issuing currency others must hold
- **Financial leverage**: Control of dollar payment systems (SWIFT) gives US ability to impose sanctions

**Benefits to Saudi Arabia / Gulf States**:
- **Security guarantee**: US military protection (5th Fleet based in Bahrain, ~70,000 US troops in Middle East)
- **Stable currency for oil**: Dollar more stable than alternatives
- **Financial system access**: Investment returns from petrodollar recycling

**Challenges and potential changes**:
- **China-Saudi oil sales in yuan**: China (world's largest oil importer, ~11 million b/d) has negotiated yuan-denominated oil purchases with Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iran (though dollar still dominant as of 2024)
- **Sanctions circumvention**: Russia, Iran, Venezuela seek non-dollar oil trade to evade US sanctions
- **Dollar weaponization backlash**: Freezing Russian central bank reserves (~$300 billion) in 2022 alarmed other nations, accelerating de-dollarization efforts

**Reality check**: Dollar's dominance persists because no alternative combines deep, liquid markets + rule of law + stable macroeconomic policy. Euro lacks unified fiscal policy. Yuan lacks convertibility and rule of law. But long-term trend is slow erosion of dollar share (from ~70% of global reserves in 2000 to ~58% in 2024).

### The Resource Curse (Dutch Disease)

Oil-rich countries often suffer economically, politically, and socially despite enormous natural wealth. Mechanisms:

**1. Dutch Disease** (named after Netherlands' natural gas discovery in 1960s):
- Resource exports earn foreign currency → currency appreciates
- Appreciation makes non-resource exports uncompetitive → manufacturing declines
- Economy becomes dependent on volatile commodity prices
- Example: Nigeria's oil boom killed domestic agriculture and manufacturing; now imports food despite arable land

**2. Rent-Seeking and Corruption**:
- Oil revenue flows to government (unlike taxes, which require economic activity)
- Politicians compete for control of resource rents rather than creating productive economy
- Corruption flourishes (Nigeria lost an estimated $400 billion to corruption 1960-1999)
- Weak institutions: No need to tax citizens → no accountability

**3. Authoritarian Persistence**:
- Resource revenue allows governments to buy off opposition without democratic concessions
- Internal security forces funded by oil revenue suppress dissent
- "No representation without taxation" works in reverse: no taxation → no representation
- Example: Gulf monarchies maintain power through oil-funded welfare + repression

**4. Conflict and Civil War**:
- Oil revenue worth fighting over → civil wars (Nigeria, Angola, Sudan, Libya, Iraq)
- Neighboring countries intervene to capture resources (Congo wars, Chad-Libya)
- Secession attempts in oil-rich regions (Biafra in Nigeria, South Sudan)

**5. Boom-Bust Volatility**:
- Government spending ratchets up during booms, hard to cut during busts
- Fiscal crises when prices collapse (Venezuela, Nigeria, Russia, Nigeria)
- Dutch Disease makes diversification difficult even when attempted

### Countries That Escaped the Resource Curse

**Norway** (most successful example):
- Sovereign wealth fund (Government Pension Fund Global): ~$1.6 trillion (largest in world)
- Constitutional rules limit spending to ~3% of fund value annually
- Transparent governance, low corruption, strong pre-existing institutions
- Oil revenue saved/invested rather than consumed
- Diversified economy maintained alongside oil sector

**Botswana** (diamonds, not oil, but same principle):
- Diamond revenue invested in education, infrastructure, health
- Transparent governance, stable democracy
- GDP per capita grew from $70 (1966) to ~$7,000 (2024)
- Escaped typical African resource-curse outcomes

**Why they succeeded**:
1. **Strong institutions pre-dated resource wealth**: Norway was already a wealthy democracy; Botswana had relatively functional governance at independence
2. **Transparent governance**: Public oversight, rule of law, property rights
3. **Counter-cyclical fiscal policy**: Save during booms, spend during busts
4. **Investment in human capital**: Education and health spending

**Why most fail**: Institutions matter more than resources. Countries with weak institutions before discovering oil rarely develop strong institutions afterward (oil revenue allows authoritarians to avoid governance reforms). Once oil revenue starts flowing, vested interests block reforms.

## Natural Gas Geopolitics

### Pipeline Politics and Strategic Dependence

Natural gas is harder to transport than oil (requires pipelines or liquefaction → LNG). This creates regional markets and strategic dependencies.

**Russia-Europe Energy Relationship** (1970s-2022):

| Pipeline | Capacity (bcm/year) | Route | Commissioning | Status (2024) |
|----------|-------------------|-------|---------------|---------------|
| **Yamal-Europe** | 33 | Russia → Belarus → Poland → Germany | 1999 | Operational, reduced flow |
| **Nord Stream 1** | 55 | Russia → Germany (Baltic Sea, bypass Ukraine/Poland) | 2011 | Sabotaged September 2022 |
| **Nord Stream 2** | 55 | Parallel to NS1 | Completed 2021, never operated | Cancelled after Ukraine invasion |
| **Ukrainian transit** | 100+ | Russia → Ukraine → Europe | Soviet era | Reduced to ~15 bcm/year |
| **Blue Stream** | 16 | Russia → Turkey (Black Sea) | 2003 | Operational |
| **TurkStream** | 31.5 | Russia → Turkey → Europe | 2020 | Operational |

At peak (2021), Russia supplied ~155 bcm (billion cubic meters) of gas to Europe — ~40% of European gas consumption. This created mutual dependence:
- **Europe** relied on Russian gas for heating, power generation, industrial feedstock
- **Russia** relied on European revenue (~$70-100 billion annually, ~10% of government budget)

**The Leverage Trap**: Both sides thought they had leverage. Russia believed energy dependence would constrain European support for Ukraine. Europe believed Russian fiscal dependence on gas revenue would prevent weaponization. Both were wrong.

**Russia-Ukraine War (February 2022) and aftermath**:
1. Russia cut gas flows to Europe (Summer 2022: Nord Stream 1 to 20% capacity, then zero)
2. European gas prices spiked 10x (€20/MWh → €300+/MWh)
3. Europe responded with emergency measures:
   - Industrial curtailment (fertilizer plants shut down, aluminum smelters reduced production)
   - Coal plant restarts
   - Demand reduction mandates (15% reduction target)
   - LNG imports surged (US LNG exports to Europe: ~50 bcm in 2021 → ~100 bcm in 2023)

**Outcome** (as of 2024):
- Russian gas exports to Europe: ~155 bcm (2021) → ~15 bcm (2024) — collapsed 90%
- Europe replaced Russian pipeline gas with Norwegian gas, LNG (US, Qatar, Australia), demand reduction, renewables
- Russia redirected gas exports to China (Power of Siberia pipeline: 38 bcm/year capacity, expanding)
- European industrial competitiveness damaged (high energy costs)

**Strategic lesson**: Energy interdependence is only stabilizing if both sides value the relationship more than other objectives. When geopolitical conflict erupts, energy dependence becomes a weapon, not a bridge.

### LNG: Global Gas Markets

Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) breaks the pipeline constraint, creating global gas markets (though less liquid than oil markets).

**LNG infrastructure costs**:
- Liquefaction terminal: $10-20 billion, 5-10 million tonnes/year capacity
- LNG carrier (ship): $200-250 million each
- Regasification terminal: $1-5 billion
- Total delivered cost: $3-15/MMBtu depending on distance, terminal capacity utilization

**Major LNG trade flows** (2023, million tonnes/year):

**Exporters**:
1. Qatar: ~80 Mt/y (expanding to 126 Mt/y by 2027)
2. Australia: ~80 Mt/y
3. United States: ~85 Mt/y (grew from ~10 Mt/y in 2016 — shale gas enabled rapid expansion)
4. Russia: ~30 Mt/y (Yamal LNG; sanctions limiting expansion)
5. Malaysia, Indonesia, Nigeria, Algeria: ~10-20 Mt/y each

**Importers**:
1. China: ~80 Mt/y (growing ~10%/year)
2. Japan: ~75 Mt/y (post-Fukushima nuclear shutdown increased gas dependence)
3. South Korea: ~45 Mt/y
4. Europe: ~120 Mt/y (up from ~80 Mt/y pre-war)
5. India: ~35 Mt/y (growing rapidly)

**Strategic dynamics**:
- **Flexibility vs cost**: LNG is more expensive than pipeline gas ($3-6/MMBtu for pipeline vs $8-15/MMBtu for LNG delivered) but provides supply diversity
- **Long-term contracts vs spot market**: Traditionally, LNG sold on 20-year contracts tied to oil price. Increasingly, spot market developing (~30% of trade in 2023)
- **US LNG surge**: Shale gas boom enabled US to go from LNG importer (2000s) to world's largest exporter (2023). Provides Europe alternative to Russian gas.

**Geopolitical implications**:
- LNG globalizes gas markets, reducing pipeline leverage
- But LNG still requires multi-billion dollar infrastructure (15-20 year payback periods)
- China's growing LNG imports make it dependent on seaborne trade → US Navy dominance of sea lanes matters
- Qatar's gas reserves (~24 trillion cubic meters, 13% of global) give it outsize geopolitical influence

## Critical Mineral Supply Chains

The energy transition replaces dependence on oil/gas with dependence on critical minerals for batteries, solar panels, wind turbines, and power electronics. These supply chains are even more concentrated than oil.

### The New OPEC: China's Mineral Processing Dominance

China doesn't dominate mining of most critical minerals (except rare earths). But it dominates **refining and processing** — the step that converts ore into battery-grade chemicals or magnets.

| Mineral | Top Miner (% global) | Top Refiner (% global) | Chinese Refining Share | Strategic Vulnerability |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Lithium** | Australia (50%) | China (65%) | 65% | High — alternatives exist but take 5-10 years |
| **Cobalt** | DRC (70%) | China (73%) | 73% | Very High — DRC unstable, Chinese control |
| **Nickel (for batteries)** | Indonesia (37%) | China (35%) | 35% | Medium — Indonesia growing processing |
| **Graphite (natural)** | China (65%) | China (98%) | 98% | Extreme — synthetic alternative expensive |
| **Graphite (synthetic)** | China (100%) | China (100%) | 100% | Extreme — no alternatives at scale |
| **Rare Earths** | China (60%) | China (90%) | 90% | Extreme — alternatives would take 10-15 years |
| **Manganese** | South Africa (29%), Gabon (14%) | China (93%) | 93% | High — critical for batteries and steel |
| **Silicon (metallurgical)** | China (64%) | China (80%) | 80% | High — solar PV production |
| **Gallium** | China (80% mining) | China (95%) | 95% | Extreme — semiconductors, LEDs |
| **Germanium** | China (60%) | China (80%) | 80% | High — fiber optics, solar |

**How China gained dominance**:
1. **Willingness to accept environmental costs**: Refining produces toxic waste (acids, heavy metals, radioactive materials in rare earth processing). China accepted this; Western countries did not.
2. **Vertical integration**: Invested in entire supply chain from mining to processing to manufacturing
3. **30-year head start**: While West deindustrialized, China built expertise
4. **Scale economies**: Dominated market → lower costs → harder for competitors to enter
5. **Strategic subsidies**: Government support for critical industries

**Comparison to OPEC oil**:
- **OPEC** controls ~40% of oil production, ~60% of reserves
- **China** controls 60-100% of refining for key minerals, <50% of reserves
- But mining alone isn't enough — refining capacity takes 5-15 years and billions to build

### Rare Earths: The Quintessential Supply Chain Weapon

Rare earth elements (REEs) are 17 elements (lanthanides + scandium + yttrium) critical for:
- **Permanent magnets** (Nd, Pr, Dy, Tb): Wind turbines, EV motors, hard drives, MRI machines, precision weapons
- **Phosphors** (Eu, Tb, Y): LED lighting, displays
- **Catalysts** (La, Ce): Oil refining, automotive catalytic converters
- **Polishing compounds** (Ce): Semiconductor and glass manufacturing

**Supply chain** (2023):
- Mining: China 60%, US 15%, Myanmar 13%, Australia 6%, others 6%
- Processing/refining: China 90%, US 5%, others 5%
- Magnet manufacturing: China 92%, Japan 4%, others 4%

**Why "rare" earths aren't rare**:
Cerium is more abundant in Earth's crust than copper. Neodymium is more abundant than lead. The "rare" refers to:
1. **Dispersion**: Rarely concentrated enough for economic extraction
2. **Co-occurrence**: Always found together → difficult to separate (requires complex multi-stage chemical processing)
3. **Radioactive contaminants**: Often found with thorium and uranium → radioactive waste disposal challenge

**Processing complexity**:
Typical rare earth refining:
1. Crush ore, leach with concentrated acid (HCl, H₂SO₄)
2. Solvent extraction: 100-200 stages of liquid-liquid separation to isolate individual elements
3. Chemical precipitation to produce oxides
4. Further processing to metals or alloys
5. Waste: 1 tonne of rare earth oxides produces ~2,000 tonnes of toxic wastewater, ~1,000 tonnes of radioactive tailings

This is why China dominates: willingness to accept environmental costs + 30 years of expertise building.

**China's rare earth export restrictions**:
- **2010**: China cut rare earth export quotas by 40%, causing global prices to spike 10x. Triggered by territorial dispute with Japan (Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands).
- **2023**: China restricted exports of gallium and germanium (semiconductors, optics) following US semiconductor export controls
- **Potential future**: Could restrict rare earth exports to pressure Western countries on Taiwan, trade disputes, or other geopolitical issues

**Western response** (building alternative supply chains):
| Project | Location | Status | Capacity (tonnes REO/year) | Timeline to Production |
|---------|----------|--------|------------------------|----------------------|
| **MP Materials (Mountain Pass)** | California, US | Operating (mining), building processing | 35,000 (current ore production) | Processing by 2025-2026 |
| **Lynas** | Australia (mining) + Malaysia (processing) | Operating | 10,500 | Operational |
| **Energy Fuels** | US | Pilot processing | TBD | 2025-2026 |
| **Pensana** | Angola (mining) + UK (processing) | Under development | 12,500 | 2025-2027 |
| **Vital Metals** | Canada | Under development | TBD | 2026+ |

**Timeline to significant Western capacity**: 5-10 years, requiring $5-15 billion investment across projects. Even then, China will retain majority market share through 2030s.

### Cobalt: The DRC Problem

Cobalt is critical for high-energy-density batteries (NMC and NCA chemistries). ~70% mined in Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), particularly Katanga province.

**Supply chain issues**:
1. **Political instability**: DRC has experienced civil wars, coups, ethnic violence. Mining regions are unstable.
2. **Artisanal mining**: 15-30% of Congolese cobalt mined by artisanal miners — small-scale, often hand-dug mines with child labor (~40,000 children estimated), dangerous conditions, no safety equipment
3. **Chinese control of supply chain**: Chinese companies control ~80% of DRC cobalt mining capacity. Ore exported to China for refining (~73% of global refining capacity).
4. **Human rights concerns**: Amnesty International and other NGOs documented child labor, dangerous conditions, environmental damage

**De-cobaltization trend**:
Battery industry is reducing cobalt intensity to address cost, supply risk, and ethics:
- **NMC 111** (33% Ni, 33% Mn, 33% Co) → **NMC 622** (60% Ni, 20% Mn, 20% Co) → **NMC 811** (80% Ni, 10% Mn, 10% Co)
- **LFP** (Lithium Iron Phosphate): **Zero cobalt**. China dominates LFP production (~95% global capacity). Lower energy density than NMC (~130-160 Wh/kg vs 200-260 Wh/kg) but cheaper, safer, longer cycle life. Growing share: ~10% of global EV batteries (2020) → ~40% (2024), driven by Chinese EV market.

**Projection**: Even with 10-15x battery demand growth (2020-2040), cobalt demand may only grow 3-5x due to reduced cobalt content per kWh.

**Alternative sources** (to reduce DRC dependence):
- **Indonesia**: Nickel mining byproduct; ~8% of global cobalt
- **Australia**: ~4% of global production
- **Cuba**: Undeveloped reserves (~500,000 tonnes)
- **Battery recycling**: Could provide ~20-30% of cobalt supply by 2040

But DRC will remain dominant source through 2030s due to geology (largest reserves: ~3.5 million tonnes, ~50% of global).

### Lithium: Brine vs Hard Rock

**Global lithium reserves** (tonnes lithium content):
- Chile: 9.3 million (mostly brine in Atacama Desert)
- Australia: 5.7 million (hard rock, spodumene)
- Argentina: 2.7 million (brine in lithium triangle)
- China: 2 million (brine in Tibet, hard rock)
- US: 1 million (brine in Nevada, Chile, others)
- Other: 4 million

**Current production** (2023): ~130,000 tonnes lithium (content)
**Projected demand** (2030): ~500,000-1,000,000 tonnes

**Extraction methods**:

**Hard rock (spodumene, Australia ~50% of production)**:
- Mine spodumene ore (LiAlSi₂O₆)
- Process: roasting + leaching → lithium carbonate or hydroxide
- Advantages: Faster production (months vs 12-18 months for brine), higher purity, not water-limited
- Disadvantages: Energy-intensive (~60 MJ/kg lithium), higher cost (~$6,000-10,000/tonne lithium carbonate), ~15 tonnes CO₂ per tonne
- Most Australian ore exported to China for processing (China controls ~65% of lithium refining)

**Brine (Chile, Argentina, China ~45% of production)**:
- Pump lithium-rich brine (groundwater) to surface evaporation ponds
- Evaporate for 12-18 months, concentrating lithium
- Process to lithium carbonate/hydroxide
- Advantages: Lower cost (~$3,000-6,000/tonne), lower energy input
- Disadvantages: Very water-intensive (2,000 tonnes water per tonne lithium) in arid regions, slow, weather-dependent
- Environmental concerns: Water depletion in Atacama Desert affecting indigenous communities and ecosystems

**Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE, emerging)**:
- Use ion exchange, adsorption, or membrane technology to extract lithium from brine without evaporation ponds
- Advantages: Hours to days (vs 12-18 months), smaller footprint, less water use, can extract from lower-grade brines
- Disadvantages: Technology still maturing, higher energy input, unproven at large scale
- Potential: Could unlock geothermal brines (California's Salton Sea: ~18 million tonnes lithium), oilfield brines
- Timeline: Commercial deployment 2025-2030

**Geopolitical dimension**:
- Australia mines lithium but ships to China for refining → China captures value-added step
- Chile considering nationalizing lithium (following copper model) → could reduce foreign investment
- US/EU investing in domestic refining (Inflation Reduction Act, EU Critical Raw Materials Act) but 5-10 year timeline
- China's refining dominance gives it leverage similar to rare earths

## Energy Security

### Defining Energy Security

**Four dimensions** (often called the "4 As"):

1. **Availability**: Physical supply exists at the scale needed
   - Global reserves adequate or production capacity sufficient
   - Not artificially constrained by politics, cartels, or conflict

2. **Affordability**: Prices are manageable for the economy
   - Not so high that they crush economic activity or trigger political instability
   - Volatility is moderate (predictable, not sudden 10x price spikes)

3. **Accessibility**: Infrastructure exists to deliver energy where needed
   - Pipelines, LNG terminals, transmission lines, ports, refineries
   - Geopolitical access (not blockaded, sanctioned, or embargoed)

4. **Acceptability**: Social, environmental, and political constraints met
   - Public acceptance (not opposed on environmental or safety grounds)
   - Regulatory compliance (emissions standards, safety, environmental review)
   - Aligns with climate commitments

**Tensions**:
- Availability vs Acceptability: Fossil fuels are available but increasingly unacceptable (climate)
- Affordability vs Acceptability: Coal is cheap but dirty; renewables are clean but initially expensive
- Accessibility vs Availability: Resources exist but infrastructure to move them doesn't (stranded gas reserves)

### Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)

US Strategic Petroleum Reserve: Established 1975 after 1973 oil embargo. Purpose: Buffer against supply disruptions.

**Capacity**: ~714 million barrels (storage in underground salt domes along Gulf Coast)
**Historical peak**: ~727 million barrels (2010)
**Current level** (2024): ~370 million barrels (after 180 million barrel release in 2022 to moderate gasoline prices during Russia-Ukraine war)

**Economics of SPR releases**:
- Released at ~$95-105/barrel (March-October 2022)
- Could refill at ~$70-80/barrel if prices drop (profit to taxpayer + accomplished price stabilization goal)
- Question: Will prices drop enough to refill, or is long-term price floor rising?

**Other countries' strategic reserves** (IEA requirement: 90 days of imports):
- Japan: ~590 million barrels (100+ days of imports — most import-dependent large economy)
- China: ~550 million barrels (building toward 90+ days)
- Germany: ~47 million barrels (~90 days)
- India: ~40 million barrels (growing to 130+ days)

**Effectiveness**: SPR releases can moderate price spikes but can't prevent them if underlying supply disruption is large. 1 million b/d release (~30 million barrels/month) into 100 million b/d global market provides ~1% supply increase — helpful but not decisive.

### Diversification as Core Strategy

**No single source or route should dominate**: The fundamental energy security principle.

**Examples of diversification**:

**Europe post-Russia gas cutoff** (2022-2024):
- **Before**: 40% gas from Russia (mostly via pipelines)
- **After**: ~15% from Russia, replaced by:
  - LNG from US (doubled imports)
  - LNG from Qatar, Algeria, Norway
  - Increased Norwegian pipeline gas
  - Demand reduction (-15%)
  - Coal plants restarted (temporary)
  - Accelerated renewables

**China oil imports** (diversification to avoid Malacca Strait chokepoint):
- Seaborne imports through Malacca Strait (80% of oil)
- Overland pipelines from Russia (Power of Siberia oil pipeline)
- Overland pipeline from Kazakhstan
- Myanmar pipeline (Bay of Bengal to Yunnan, bypassing Malacca)
- Still ~70% seaborne through Malacca as of 2024, but down from ~95% in 2010

**US electricity grid** (fuel diversity):
- Natural gas ~40%, coal ~20%, nuclear ~20%, renewables ~20%
- No single fuel dominates (vs France ~70% nuclear)
- Trade-off: Diversity costs efficiency (can't optimize around one fuel) but provides resilience

**Failure of diversification — Germany's Energiewende miscalculation**:
- Planned to phase out nuclear (by 2022) and coal (by 2030)
- Assumed Russian gas would bridge to renewables
- Assumption failed when Russia weaponized gas (2022)
- Result: Restarted coal plants, extended nuclear (temporary), severe economic pain from high energy prices
- Lesson: Diversify energy sources **and** supply origins

## The Energy Transition

### Decarbonization Pathways

**IEA Net Zero by 2050 Scenario** (requires to limit warming to 1.5°C):

| Sector | 2020 Status | 2030 Target | 2050 Target | Feasibility |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Electricity** | 28% low-carbon | 60% low-carbon | ~90% low-carbon | Challenging but plausible (solar/wind scaling) |
| **Transport** | ~1% EV sales | 60% EV sales | 100% zero-emission | Cars: plausible. Aviation/shipping: very difficult |
| **Buildings** | ~20% electric heat | ~50% electric heat | ~90% electric heat | Requires heat pump deployment, building retrofits |
| **Industry** | 5% low-carbon | 20% low-carbon | ~75% low-carbon | Steel/cement very difficult; hydrogen/CCS unproven at scale |
| **Agriculture** | N/A | ~20% emissions reduction | ~50% reduction | Limited options (CH₄ from cattle, N₂O from fertilizer) |
| **Fossil fuel production** | Growing | Declining | ~90% below 2020 | Requires halting new oil/gas investment (most controversial) |

**Technologies required** (IEA scenario):

| Technology | Current Deployment | 2030 Needed | 2050 Needed | Maturity Level |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Solar PV** | ~1,000 GW capacity | ~5,000 GW | ~14,000 GW | Mature, scaling |
| **Wind** | ~840 GW | ~3,500 GW | ~8,000 GW | Mature, scaling |
| **Nuclear** | 415 GW | ~500 GW | ~900 GW | Mature but slow to build |
| **Battery storage** | ~40 GW / 100 GWh | ~500 GW / ~1,500 GWh | ~3,000 GW / 9,000+ GWh | Scaling rapidly |
| **Hydrogen (low-carbon)** | <1 Mt/y | ~80 Mt/y | ~430 Mt/y | Early stage; cost + infrastructure challenges |
| **CCS** | 40 Mt CO₂/y | 1,600 Mt CO₂/y | 7,600 Mt CO₂/y | Pilot stage; needs 190x scale-up |
| **Direct air capture** | <0.01 Mt CO₂/y | ~60 Mt CO₂/y | ~1,000 Mt CO₂/y | R&D stage; very expensive |
| **EVs** | ~10% new sales | ~60% new sales | 100% new sales | Scaling rapidly (cars); early stage (trucks) |

**Feasibility concerns**:
1. **Material constraints**: Lithium, cobalt, rare earths need 5-40x production increase
2. **Infrastructure**: Grid transmission, hydrogen pipelines, EV charging, CCUS infrastructure all require trillions in investment
3. **Political economy**: Fossil fuel industry employs millions, generates trillions in revenue → political resistance
4. **Developing world**: Need energy for development, lack capital for expensive clean tech
5. **Unproven technologies**: Hydrogen at scale, CCS at scale, direct air capture all unproven economically

**Alternative view** (oil industry, some economists): Net zero by 2050 is unrealistic. More likely: ~2.5-3°C warming with much slower transition driven by economics rather than policy.

### Electrification: The Core Transition Mechanism

**Current energy system**: ~20% of final energy is electricity. ~80% is direct fossil fuel combustion (gasoline in cars, natural gas in furnaces, coal in steel furnaces).

**Net zero scenario**: ~50-60% of final energy is electricity (potentially higher with hydrogen produced via electrolysis).

**Electrification pathways**:

**1. Transport** (currently ~1% electric):
- **Light-duty vehicles (cars, SUVs)**: ~10% EV sales (2022) → 60% (2030 target) → 100% (2035-2040). **Feasible** with battery cost declines.
- **Medium/heavy trucks**: Electric for short-haul (~10% by 2030), hydrogen or catenary for long-haul. **Challenging** due to weight/range.
- **Buses**: ~40% of new urban buses electric in China (2023), ~5% in US/Europe. **Feasible** (return-to-depot charging, predictable routes).
- **Aviation**: Battery-electric unfeasible except small aircraft, short flights. Sustainable aviation fuel (biofuels, synthetic fuels) most likely. **Very difficult**.
- **Shipping**: Ammonia, methanol, hydrogen most likely for deep-sea; electric for ferries, short routes. **Difficult**.

**2. Buildings** (currently ~30% electric heating):
- **Heat pumps** replacing gas furnaces, oil boilers. COP ~3-5 means 3-5x better than resistance heating, competitive with gas on energy basis
- Retrofit challenge: Existing buildings designed for high-temperature heat (gas furnace ~60°C supply) vs heat pumps (40-50°C supply) → requires larger radiators or underfloor heating
- Deployment: ~15% of global heating (2023) → target ~50% (2030) → 90% (2050). Europe accelerating (policy + high gas prices), US slower.

**3. Industry** (currently ~80% fossil fuels):
- **Steel**: Electric arc furnaces from scrap (~30% of global production). Green hydrogen direct reduced iron (H-DRI) for primary steel (pilot stage). Traditional blast furnaces ~70% of production, very difficult to replace.
- **Cement**: Electrification limited (process emissions from limestone chemistry: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂). Requires CCS, novel cements, or accept emissions.
- **Chemicals**: Petrochemicals use oil/gas as feedstock (not just fuel) — need alternative carbon sources (biomass, captured CO₂) or accept continued fossil use.

**Grid implications of high electrification**:
- Global electricity demand could double or triple (2020: ~27,000 TWh/y → 2050: ~60,000-80,000 TWh/y in net zero scenarios)
- Transmission investment: ~$2-3 trillion needed (IEA estimate)
- Storage: ~9,000+ GWh battery storage needed (currently ~100 GWh)
- Peaking capacity: Need dispatchable generation for multi-day wind droughts, winter solar shortfalls

## Industrial Decarbonization: The Hardest Sectors

| Sector | Annual CO₂ Emissions (Gt) | Share of Global | Why Hard | Solution Pathways | Feasibility |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|----------|------------------|------------|
| **Steel** | ~2.6 Gt | 7% | Blast furnaces use coking coal for chemistry (reduce Fe₂O₃ to Fe) + heat (1,500°C) | Green H₂ DRI, electric arc furnaces, CCS | Medium (H₂-DRI pilots exist; cost + H₂ supply challenges) |
| **Cement** | ~2.8 Gt | 8% | Chemistry: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (60% of emissions). Heat: 1,450°C kilns (40%). | CCS, novel cements (limestone-free), electrification (limited) | Low (CCS unproven at scale; novel cements not widely accepted) |
| **Chemicals** | ~1.5 Gt | 4% | Oil/gas as feedstock (raw material) not just fuel. Complex chemistry. | Bio-feedstocks, green H₂, captured CO₂ as carbon source | Low-Medium (expensive, unproven at scale) |
| **Aviation** | ~0.9 Gt | 2.5% | Energy density: jet fuel 46 MJ/kg; best batteries ~1 MJ/kg. Range + payload impossible. | Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF), hydrogen (cryogenic), efficiency | Low (SAF limited by feedstock; H₂ requires aircraft redesign) |
| **Shipping** | ~0.7 Gt | 2% | Energy density + range. Long routes (weeks at sea). | Ammonia, methanol, hydrogen, nuclear (limited) | Medium (fuels feasible; ammonia/methanol pilots underway) |

**Total**: ~8.5 Gt CO₂/year (~23% of global emissions). These are the sectors where decarbonization is technically hardest and most expensive.

### Steel Decarbonization Pathways

**Current production**: ~1.9 billion tonnes/year
- Blast furnace (BF) route: ~70% (uses coking coal)
- Electric arc furnace (EAF) route: ~30% (uses scrap steel or direct reduced iron)

**Blast furnace chemistry**:
```
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂  (iron ore reduction using carbon monoxide from coking coal)
```
Produces ~1.85 tonnes CO₂ per tonne steel. About half from chemistry, half from heat.

**Decarbonization options**:

**1. Hydrogen Direct Reduced Iron (H-DRI)**:
```
Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂ → 2Fe + 3H₂O  (hydrogen replaces carbon as reducing agent)
```
- Produces water instead of CO₂
- Requires ~50-55 kg H₂ per tonne steel (~1.7-2.0 MWh electricity if green H₂ via electrolysis)
- Pilot plants: HYBRIT (Sweden), Thyssen-Krupp (Germany), Arcelor-Mittal (Europe)
- Challenge: Cost (green H₂ at $3-8/kg makes steel $200-400/tonne more expensive vs $600-1,000/tonne current price) + H₂ supply (global H₂ production would need to increase 10-20x)

**2. Electric Arc Furnaces (EAF) from scrap**:
- Melts scrap steel using electric arc (~15 MWh per tonne)
- Emissions: ~0.4 tonnes CO₂/tonne if electricity from renewables
- Limitation: Scrap availability. ~30% of steel is scrap-based already. Can't grow to 100% without massive steel stock (requires decades of accumulation).

**3. Carbon Capture on blast furnaces**:
- Capture ~90% of CO₂ from BF → reduces to ~0.2 tonnes CO₂/tonne steel
- Challenge: Cost ($50-100/tonne CO₂ captured), energy penalty (reduces efficiency), storage infrastructure
- Pilot: COURSE50 (Japan), others in Europe

**Realistic timeline**: 2040-2050 for 50% low-carbon steel. 2060-2070 for 90%+. Requires massive H₂ infrastructure, cheap green electricity, carbon pricing, or mandates.

### Cement Decarbonization

**Process emissions** dominate cement's carbon footprint:
```
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂  (limestone calcination: 60% of emissions, unavoidable chemistry)
Fuel combustion for heat (1,450°C kilns): ~40% of emissions
```

**Options**:

**1. CCS**: Capture both process and combustion CO₂. Only pathway to deeply decarbonize Portland cement.
- Cost: ~$70-120/tonne CO₂
- Few pilots; no commercial scale

**2. Novel cements**: Limestone-free formulations using different chemistries
- Calcium sulfoaluminate, magnesium oxide, geopolymers (alkali-activated)
- Challenge: Performance, durability unknown for many applications. Building codes prohibit in many jurisdictions. Slow adoption.

**3. Blended cements**: Replace 20-40% clinker (the CO₂-intensive component) with fly ash, slag, calcined clay
- Already widely practiced but limited by availability of supplementary materials
- Can reduce emissions ~20-30%, not a path to near-zero

**Realistic assessment**: Cement is hardest major sector to decarbonize. Likely to remain ~1-2 Gt CO₂/year through 2050 unless CCS scales dramatically or construction shifts to alternative materials (timber, recycled steel).

## Key Terms

- **OPEC**: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries — cartel controlling ~40% of oil production
- **OPEC+**: Expanded alliance including Russia and others, controlling ~55% of oil production
- **Petrodollar**: System of pricing oil in US dollars, recycling revenue into US assets
- **Resource Curse (Dutch Disease)**: Paradox where resource wealth leads to poor economic/political outcomes
- **Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)**: Emergency oil storage (US: ~370 million barrels)
- **LNG**: Liquefied Natural Gas — cooled to -162°C for shipping, enabling global gas trade
- **Critical Minerals**: Materials with concentrated supply chains essential for energy transition (Li, Co, REEs, graphite, etc.)
- **Rare Earth Elements (REEs)**: 17 elements critical for magnets, electronics; China processes ~90%
- **Energy Security**: Availability, Affordability, Accessibility, Acceptability of energy supply
- **Green Hydrogen**: H₂ produced via electrolysis with renewable electricity (vs gray H₂ from natural gas)
- **Electrification**: Replacing direct fossil fuel combustion with electricity (EVs, heat pumps, industrial processes)
- **Net Zero**: Balancing CO₂ emissions with removals (CCS, DAC, natural sinks)
- **CCS**: Carbon Capture and Storage — capturing CO₂ from sources or air, storing geologically
- **H-DRI**: Hydrogen Direct Reduced Iron — using H₂ instead of carbon to reduce iron ore for steel
- **Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)**: Biofuels or synthetic fuels for aircraft (reduce emissions vs jet fuel)

## Summary

Energy geopolitics is shifting from oil/gas to critical minerals, but oil remains central through 2030-2040. OPEC/OPEC+ control ~55% of oil production, using production cuts to support prices. But US shale revolution (~13 million b/d) and renewable growth erode OPEC market power long-term. Historical oil crises (1973, 1979, 2014, 2020, 2022) demonstrate oil's economic and political leverage.

The petrodollar system (oil priced in USD, petrodollars recycled to US assets) gives US financial leverage but faces slow erosion as China, Russia, others pursue de-dollarization. Resource curse afflicts most oil-rich countries (corruption, authoritarianism, economic distortion) except those with strong pre-existing institutions (Norway, Botswana).

Natural gas geopolitics centers on pipeline dependencies. Russia-Europe gas relationship collapsed in 2022 war; Europe replaced Russian pipeline gas (~155 bcm/y) with LNG, Norwegian gas, and demand reduction within 18 months — demonstrating rapid supply chain adaptation under crisis. LNG globalizes gas markets but requires multi-billion-dollar infrastructure.

Critical mineral supply chains are more concentrated than oil. China dominates refining: lithium (65%), cobalt (73%), rare earths (90%), graphite (98%). This creates new dependencies analogous to OPEC oil leverage. Building alternative supply chains (US, Australia, Canada, Europe) requires 5-15 years and tens of billions in investment. Rare earths are the most concentrated, with China processing ~90% and manufacturing ~92% of permanent magnets.

Energy transition requires massive electrification: ~20% of final energy is electric today → ~50-60% needed for net zero. This demands doubling or tripling global electricity generation (27,000 TWh/y → 60,000-80,000 TWh/y), plus grid transmission, storage, and EV charging infrastructure. Light-duty transport electrification is progressing; aviation and shipping face severe energy density constraints.

Industrial decarbonization — steel, cement, chemicals, aviation, shipping — represents ~23% of global emissions (~8.5 Gt CO₂/y) and is the hardest challenge. Steel can potentially decarbonize via green hydrogen direct reduced iron (H-DRI) but requires 10-20x increase in global hydrogen production. Cement faces unavoidable process emissions (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂), requiring CCS or novel cements. These sectors likely remain significant CO₂ sources through 2050.

The energy transition doesn't eliminate geopolitical competition — it shifts the battlefield from oil fields to lithium mines, rare earth processing plants, and semiconductor fabs. Whether new dependencies are managed better than old ones depends on institutional responses: diversification, strategic reserves, supply chain redundancy, and international cooperation.
