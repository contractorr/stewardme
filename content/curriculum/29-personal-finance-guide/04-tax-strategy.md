# Tax Strategy

## Overview

Taxes represent one of the largest lifetime expenses for most Americans—often exceeding housing costs over a career. Strategic tax planning can save hundreds of thousands of dollars by minimizing tax liability through legal deductions, credits, and optimization of tax-advantaged accounts. Understanding tax brackets, deductions, tax-deferred and tax-free accounts, and long-term planning strategies is essential for wealth building.

This guide covers federal income tax fundamentals, strategic deduction and credit optimization, tax-advantaged investment vehicles, and planning techniques to reduce lifetime tax burden.

## Federal Income Tax Fundamentals

### Marginal vs. Effective Tax Rates

**Marginal Tax Rate**: Tax rate on your last dollar of income
**Effective Tax Rate**: Total taxes paid ÷ total income (always lower than marginal)

**Why It Matters**: Only income above each bracket threshold is taxed at the higher rate

### Tax Calculation Example

**Single filer, $80,000 income**:

| Income Portion | Tax Rate | Tax Amount |
|----------------|----------|------------|
| $0 - $11,600 | 10% | $1,160 |
| $11,601 - $47,150 | 12% | $4,266 |
| $47,151 - $80,000 | 22% | $7,227 |
| **Total Tax** | - | **$12,653** |

- **Marginal Rate**: 22% (highest bracket reached)
- **Effective Rate**: $12,653 ÷ $80,000 = **15.8%**

**Key Insight**: Even though in 22% bracket, only $32,850 taxed at 22%

- 3.8% surtax on investment income

**Short-Term Capital Gains**: Taxed as ordinary income (same as salary)

## Standard Deduction vs. Itemized Deductions

### Standard Deduction (2025)

| Filing Status | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Single | $15,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $30,000 |
| Head of Household | $22,500 |

**When to Use**: If itemized deductions don't exceed standard deduction

### Itemized Deductions

**Common Deductions**:

**1. State and Local Taxes (SALT)**
- State income taxes OR sales taxes
- Property taxes
- **LIMIT**: $10,000 combined ($5,000 if married filing separately)

**2. Mortgage Interest**
- Interest on mortgage debt up to $750,000 ($375k if married filing separately)
- Must be primary or secondary residence
- Refinanced mortgages qualify

**3. Charitable Contributions**
- Cash donations: up to 60% of AGI
- Appreciated securities: up to 30% of AGI
- Must donate to qualified 501(c)(3) organizations
- Need documentation (receipts, acknowledgment letters)

**4. Medical Expenses**
- Only amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Includes: doctor visits, prescriptions, insurance premiums, dental, vision
- High threshold makes this rarely useful unless catastrophic expenses

**5. Casualty and Theft Losses**
- Only federally declared disaster areas
- Must exceed 10% of AGI
- Rare to qualify

### Itemization Example

**Married couple, $150,000 AGI**:
- State income tax: $8,000
- Property tax: $6,000 (combined SALT = $10,000 due to cap)
- Mortgage interest: $15,000
- Charitable donations: $5,000
- **Total itemized**: $30,000

**Decision**: Use standard deduction ($30,000) since itemized equals it and standard requires no record-keeping

## Tax Credits vs. Deductions

**Deduction**: Reduces taxable income (value = deduction × marginal rate)
**Credit**: Direct reduction in tax owed (dollar-for-dollar savings)

**Example**:
- $1,000 deduction in 22% bracket = $220 savings
- $1,000 credit = $1,000 savings

**Credits are more valuable than deductions**

### Major Tax Credits

**Child Tax Credit**:
- $2,000 per child under 17
- $1,700 refundable (can receive even if no tax liability)
- Phases out: starts $200k (single) / $400k (married)

**Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)**:
- For low-to-moderate income workers
- Up to $7,830 (2025, 3+ children)
- Phases out based on income and children
- Fully refundable

**Child and Dependent Care Credit**:
- Up to $3,000 expense for 1 dependent, $6,000 for 2+
- 20-35% credit based on income
- Max credit: $2,100
- Covers daycare, after-school programs, babysitters

**American Opportunity Tax Credit (Education)**:
- Up to $2,500 per student
- First 4 years of college only
- 100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000 in expenses
- $1,000 refundable

**Lifetime Learning Credit (Education)**:
- Up to $2,000 per tax return (not per student)
- Any level of post-secondary education
- 20% of first $10,000 in expenses
- Non-refundable

**Saver's Credit**:
- For retirement contributions (IRA, 401k)
- Up to $1,000 single / $2,000 married
- Only for low-moderate income
- Phases out: $38,250 (single) / $76,500 (married)

## Tax-Advantaged Accounts

### Traditional 401(k) and IRA

**How They Work**:
- Contributions are **pre-tax** (reduce current taxable income)
- Growth is **tax-deferred** (no taxes on gains until withdrawal)
- Withdrawals taxed as **ordinary income**

**401(k) Limits (2025)**:
- Employee contribution: $23,500
- Age 50+ catch-up: additional $7,500
- Total limit (employee + employer): $70,000

**Traditional IRA Limits (2025)**:
- Contribution: $7,000
- Age 50+ catch-up: additional $1,000
- Deductibility phases out if covered by workplace plan:
  - Single: $77,000 - $87,000
  - Married: $123,000 - $143,000

**Benefits**:
- Immediate tax savings (deduction in current year)
- Tax-deferred growth (compound faster)
- Often lower tax bracket in retirement (pay less tax later)

**Downsides**:
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) at age 73
- Withdrawals before 59½ face 10% penalty + taxes
- Taxes on all withdrawals (even if from growth)

**Best For**: High earners who expect lower retirement tax bracket

### Roth 401(k) and Roth IRA

**How They Work**:
- Contributions are **after-tax** (no current deduction)
- Growth is **tax-free forever**
- Withdrawals are **tax-free** (contributions and gains)

**Roth 401(k) Limits**: Same as traditional 401(k)

**Roth IRA Limits (2025)**:
- Contribution: $7,000
- Age 50+ catch-up: additional $1,000
- Income phase-out:
  - Single: $150,000 - $165,000
  - Married: $236,000 - $246,000

**Benefits**:
- Tax-free growth forever
- Tax-free withdrawals in retirement
- No RMDs during owner's lifetime
- Contributions (not gains) can be withdrawn anytime penalty-free
- Tax diversification (hedge against future rate increases)

**Downsides**:
- No immediate tax deduction
- Income limits for Roth IRA (not 401k)
- Five-year rule for gains

**Best For**: Young investors, low-moderate earners, expect higher future tax bracket

### Health Savings Account (HSA)

**Triple Tax Advantage**:
1. Contributions are **pre-tax** (deduction)
2. Growth is **tax-free**
3. Withdrawals for medical expenses are **tax-free**

**Requirements**:
- Must have High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
- 2025 HDHP definition: $1,650+ individual / $3,300+ family deductible

**Limits (2025)**:
- Individual: $4,300
- Family: $8,550
- Age 55+ catch-up: additional $1,000

**Strategic Use**:
- Pay medical expenses out-of-pocket now
- Let HSA grow tax-free for decades
- Withdraw for past medical expenses anytime (save receipts)
- After 65: can withdraw for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, no penalty)
- **Ultimate retirement account** if maximized

**Example**:
- Contribute $4,300 annually for 30 years
- 8% return
- **$489,000 tax-free** for medical expenses (or general retirement after 65)

### 529 College Savings Plans

**How It Works**:
- Contributions are **after-tax**
- Growth is **tax-free**
- Withdrawals for qualified education expenses are **tax-free**

**Features**:
- No federal contribution limit (state limits typically $300k-500k)
- No income restrictions
- State tax deduction in many states (varies)
- Can change beneficiary to other family members
- $10,000 lifetime limit for K-12 tuition

**Qualified Expenses**:
- Tuition and fees
- Room and board (if at least half-time)
- Books and supplies
- Computers and equipment

**Non-Qualified Withdrawals**:
- Earnings taxed as ordinary income
- 10% penalty on earnings (not contributions)
- Exceptions: scholarship, death, disability

### Flexible Spending Account (FSA)

**Medical FSA**:
- Pre-tax contributions for medical expenses
- 2025 limit: $3,200
- **Use-it-or-lose-it**: Must spend by year-end (some allow $640 carryover or 2.5-month grace period)

**Dependent Care FSA**:
- Pre-tax contributions for child/elder care
- Limit: $5,000 per household
- Alternative to Child Care Credit (choose one)

**Best For**: Predictable annual medical/care expenses

## Tax Optimization Strategies

### 1. Maximize Tax-Advantaged Contributions

**Priority Order**:
1. **401(k) to employer match** (free money, 100% return)
2. **HSA maximum** (triple tax advantage)
3. **Roth IRA maximum** (if eligible)
4. **401(k) to maximum** ($23,500)
5. **Mega Backdoor Roth** (if plan allows)
6. **Taxable brokerage** (after maxing tax-advantaged)

**Example**: $150k income
- 401(k): $23,500 (saves $5,170 in 22% bracket)
- HSA: $4,300 (saves $946)
- Roth IRA: $7,000
- **Total annual savings**: $34,800 ($6,116 tax savings)

### 2. Tax-Loss Harvesting

**Strategy**: Sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains

**How It Works**:
- Sell losing position for $5,000 loss
- Use loss to offset $5,000 gain from winning position
- Net effect: no taxable gain, rebalanced portfolio

**Rules**:
- Can offset unlimited capital gains
- Excess losses offset up to $3,000 ordinary income annually
- Remaining losses carry forward indefinitely
- **Wash Sale Rule**: Can't rebuy "substantially identical" security within 30 days

**Example**:
- $10,000 long-term capital gain (would owe $1,500 at 15%)
- Harvest $10,000 loss from losing position
- **Tax savings**: $1,500
- Immediately buy similar (not identical) fund to maintain exposure

### 3. Asset Location Optimization

**Principle**: Hold tax-inefficient assets in tax-advantaged accounts

**Tax-Inefficient (prioritize for 401k/IRA)**:
- Bonds (interest taxed as ordinary income)
- REITs (most income as ordinary income)
- High-dividend stocks
- Actively managed funds (high turnover = capital gains)

**Tax-Efficient (OK for taxable brokerage)**:
- Index funds (low turnover, minimal distributions)
- Growth stocks (unrealized gains, control timing)
- Municipal bonds (tax-free interest)
- ETFs (more tax-efficient than mutual funds)

**Example Portfolio Split**:
**Tax-Advantaged (401k, IRA)**:
- 100% bonds
- REITs
- Active funds

**Taxable Brokerage**:
- Total stock market index
- International index
- Growth stocks

### 4. Charitable Giving Strategies

**Bunching Donations**:
- Combine multiple years of donations into one
- Itemize in high-donation year, use standard deduction in others
- **Example**: Instead of $10k/year for 3 years, donate $30k in year 1

**Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)**:
- Contribute large amount in one year (immediate deduction)
- Distribute to charities over multiple years
- Contribution can be appreciated stock (avoid capital gains)

**Example**:
- Donate $50,000 of stock with $20,000 cost basis to DAF
- Deduction: $50,000 (saves $11,000 at 22% bracket)
- Avoided capital gains: $4,500 (15% on $30,000 gain)
- **Total tax benefit**: $15,500
- Distribute $10k/year to charities over next 5 years

**Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)**:
- Age 70½+: donate up to $105,000 directly from IRA to charity
- Counts toward RMD
- Excluded from taxable income (better than deduction)

### 5. Roth Conversion Strategy

**What It Is**: Convert traditional IRA to Roth IRA, paying taxes now for tax-free growth

**When It Makes Sense**:
- Low-income years (job transition, sabbatical, early retirement)
- Before RMDs begin (age 73)
- Expect higher future tax rates
- Want to reduce future RMDs

**Example**:
- Convert $50,000 from traditional IRA
- Pay $11,000 tax now (22% bracket)
- $50,000 grows to $200,000 over 20 years
- All $200,000 tax-free in retirement (vs. $44,000 tax at 22%)
- **Savings**: $33,000 in future taxes

**Caution**: Increases current-year income (could affect ACA subsidies, Medicare premiums, etc.)

### 6. Backdoor Roth IRA

**For High Earners Exceeding Roth Income Limits**:

**Steps**:
1. Contribute $7,000 to non-deductible traditional IRA
2. Immediately convert to Roth IRA
3. Pay tax only on any gains between contribution and conversion
4. Repeat annually

**Pro Rata Rule Complication**: If you have existing traditional IRA balances, conversions are partially taxable based on ratio of after-tax to pre-tax money

**Solution**: Roll existing traditional IRAs into 401(k) first

### 7. Tax Bracket Management

**Stay Below Key Thresholds**:
- **Capital gains bracket**: Keep income under $94,050 (married) for 0% rate
- **ACA subsidy cliff**: Keep income under 400% of poverty line ($120,000 family of 4)
- **IRMAA Medicare surcharge**: Avoid $206,000 (married) threshold ($244/month extra)
- **Net Investment Income Tax**: Stay under $250,000 (married) to avoid 3.8% surtax

**Tactics**:
- Maximize pre-tax 401(k) to reduce AGI
- Time capital gains realizations
- Use Roth accounts for tax-free income in retirement
- Manage Roth conversions to stay in target bracket

**Profile**: 55 years old, retired early, $60,000 annual expenses, living off investments

**Strategy**:

- Capital gains: $20,000 gain at 0% rate = $0 tax
- Roth conversion: $30,000 at 12% = $3,600 tax
- **Total tax**: $3,600 on $80,000 income (4.5% effective rate)

**Long-term benefit**: $30,000 moved to Roth grows tax-free; reduces future RMDs

## Key Terms

**Marginal Tax Rate**: Tax rate on the last dollar of income; determines savings from deductions

**Effective Tax Rate**: Total taxes paid divided by total income; actual average rate paid

**Tax Deduction**: Reduces taxable income; value equals deduction amount times marginal rate

**Tax Credit**: Dollar-for-dollar reduction in taxes owed; more valuable than deductions

**Tax-Deferred**: Contributions reduce current taxes; growth untaxed; withdrawals taxed (traditional accounts)

**Tax-Free**: Contributions are after-tax; growth and withdrawals never taxed (Roth accounts)

**AGI (Adjusted Gross Income)**: Total income minus "above-the-line" deductions; determines eligibility for many tax benefits

**MAGI (Modified Adjusted Gross Income)**: AGI with certain deductions added back; used for phase-outs

## Summary

Tax strategy is about legally minimizing lifetime tax burden through smart use of deductions, credits, and tax-advantaged accounts. Understanding marginal vs. effective tax rates is crucial—only income in each bracket is taxed at that rate, not all income.

The standard deduction ($30,000 married, $15,000 single in 2025) eliminates the need for itemization for most taxpayers. Tax credits like Child Tax Credit and education credits provide dollar-for-dollar tax reduction and are more valuable than deductions.

Tax-advantaged accounts are powerful wealth-building tools. Traditional 401(k)/IRA provide immediate tax deductions and tax-deferred growth. Roth 401(k)/IRA offer tax-free growth forever. HSAs provide triple tax advantage—the best retirement account if maxed out. Priority: get 401(k) match, max HSA, max Roth IRA, max 401(k), then taxable accounts.

Advanced strategies include tax-loss harvesting (offsetting gains with losses), asset location optimization (bonds in tax-advantaged, stocks in taxable), Roth conversions in low-income years, and backdoor Roth for high earners. Charitable giving through donor-advised funds can provide multi-year tax benefits in one year.

The key insight: tax planning is about timing—accelerate deductions into high-income years, defer income to low-income years, and use the right account types to minimize taxes over your lifetime. Every $1 saved in taxes compounds over decades.
