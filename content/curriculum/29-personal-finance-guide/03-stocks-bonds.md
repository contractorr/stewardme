# Stocks and Bonds

## Overview

Stocks and bonds are the two primary building blocks of investment portfolios. Stocks represent ownership in companies and offer growth potential through appreciation and dividends. Bonds represent loans to governments or corporations and provide fixed income with lower risk. Understanding how these securities work, how they're valued, and how markets function is essential for effective portfolio construction.

This guide provides in-depth coverage of equity and fixed-income investing, including valuation methods, market mechanics, and strategic considerations.

## Equities (Stocks): Company Ownership

### What Stocks Represent

When you buy stock, you become a partial owner of a company with:
- **Voting rights**: Elect board members, approve major decisions (proportional to shares)
- **Profit participation**: Share in earnings through dividends and appreciation
- **Residual claim**: Last in line for assets in bankruptcy (after bondholders)
- **Limited liability**: Can only lose investment amount, not more

### How Companies Issue Stock

**Initial Public Offering (IPO)**:
- Private company sells shares to public for first time
- Raises capital for expansion, pays early investors
- Investment banks underwrite and market offering
- Example: Airbnb IPO (2020) raised $3.5B at $68/share

**Secondary Offerings**:
- Already-public company issues additional shares
- Dilutes existing shareholders but raises capital
- Market typically reacts negatively (dilution concern)

**Stock Splits**:
- Increases share count, reduces price per share
- 2-for-1 split: own 100 shares at $200 → 200 shares at $100
- No change in total value, improves accessibility
- Example: Apple 4-for-1 split (2020)

### Stock Valuation Methods

**1. Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)**

**Formula**: Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)

**Interpretation**:
- P/E = 15: Market values company at 15× annual earnings
- High P/E (25+): Growth expectations or overvaluation
- Low P/E (under 15): Value opportunity or troubled company
- S&P 500 average: ~20-25

**Example**:
- Stock Price: $100
- EPS: $5
- P/E: 100 ÷ 5 = 20 (market pays $20 for every $1 of earnings)

**2. Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B)**

**Formula**: Stock Price ÷ Book Value Per Share

**Interpretation**:
- P/B < 1: Trading below net asset value (potential value)
- P/B > 3: Premium for intangibles (brand, intellectual property)
- Best for asset-heavy companies (banks, manufacturers)

**3. Dividend Discount Model (DDM)**

**Formula**: Stock Value = Expected Dividend ÷ (Required Return - Dividend Growth Rate)

**Example**:
- Expected dividend: $3/share next year
- Required return: 10%
- Dividend growth: 5% annually
- Fair Value: $3 ÷ (0.10 - 0.05) = $60/share

**4. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)**

Projects future free cash flows and discounts to present value. Most theoretically sound but requires many assumptions.

**Steps**:
1. Project 5-10 years of free cash flows
2. Estimate terminal value (perpetual growth)
3. Discount all cash flows to present using WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)
4. Sum present values = intrinsic stock value

## Fixed Income (Bonds): Lending Money

### What Bonds Represent

A bond is a loan where:
- **Issuer (borrower)**: Government, corporation, or municipality
- **Bondholder (lender)**: Investor who purchases the bond
- **Principal (par value)**: Amount borrowed, repaid at maturity (typically $1,000)
- **Coupon**: Fixed interest payment (e.g., 5% annual = $50/year)
- **Maturity date**: When principal is repaid

### Bond Example

**10-Year Corporate Bond**:
- Par Value: $1,000
- Coupon Rate: 5%
- Maturity: 10 years
- Annual Payment: $50 (5% of $1,000)

**Cash Flows**:
- Years 1-9: Receive $50 annually
- Year 10: Receive $50 + $1,000 principal
- Total received: $1,500 ($500 interest + $1,000 principal)

### Bond Types by Issuer

**1. Treasury Bonds (US Government)**

| Type | Maturity | Risk | Tax Treatment |
|------|----------|------|---------------|
| **T-Bills** | Under 1 year | Virtually none | Federal tax, state/local exempt |
| **T-Notes** | 2-10 years | Virtually none | Federal tax, state/local exempt |
| **T-Bonds** | 20-30 years | Virtually none | Federal tax, state/local exempt |
| **TIPS** | 5-30 years | Virtually none, inflation-protected | Federal tax on inflation adjustments |

**2. Municipal Bonds**

- Issued by state/local governments
- Finance infrastructure (roads, schools, hospitals)
- **Tax-exempt**: Federal tax-free, often state tax-free in issuing state
- Lower yields but tax-equivalent yield often competitive

**Tax-Equivalent Yield Formula**:
Tax-Equiv Yield = Muni Yield ÷ (1 - Tax Rate)

**Example**:
- Municipal bond: 3% yield
- Investor's tax bracket: 32%
- Tax-equivalent yield: 3% ÷ (1 - 0.32) = 4.41%
- Equivalent to 4.41% taxable bond

**3. Corporate Bonds**

| Rating Category | Credit Rating | Default Risk | Typical Yield |
|----------------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Investment-Grade AAA** | Highest quality | Very low | 4-5% |
| **Investment-Grade AA** | High quality | Low | 4.5-5.5% |
| **Investment-Grade A** | Good quality | Moderate | 5-6% |
| **Investment-Grade BBB** | Adequate quality | Moderate | 5.5-6.5% |
| **High-Yield BB** | Speculative | Elevated | 7-9% |
| **High-Yield B** | Highly speculative | High | 9-12% |
| **High-Yield CCC** | Very risky | Very high | 12%+ |

### Bond Pricing and Yields

**Key Principle**: Bond prices move inverse to interest rates

**Why**: If rates rise, existing bonds with lower coupons become less valuable

**Example**:
- You own bond paying 5% ($50/year on $1,000)
- New bonds now pay 6% ($60/year on $1,000)
- Your bond less attractive → price drops to ~$950
- At $950, your $50 payment = 5.3% current yield (more competitive)

### Types of Yield

**1. Coupon Yield**
- Annual interest ÷ par value
- Bond pays $50 on $1,000 par = 5% coupon yield
- Fixed, never changes

**2. Current Yield**
- Annual interest ÷ current market price
- Bond pays $50, priced at $950 = 5.26% current yield
- Changes as price fluctuates

**3. Yield to Maturity (YTM)**
- Total return if held to maturity
- Accounts for: interest payments + capital gain/loss at maturity
- Most comprehensive measure
- Example: $950 bond, 5% coupon, 10 years to maturity = ~5.6% YTM

### Bond Risk Factors

**1. Interest Rate Risk**

Duration measures sensitivity to rate changes:
- **Duration = 5**: 1% rate increase → ~5% price decline
- Longer maturity = higher duration = more rate sensitivity
- 30-year bond much more volatile than 2-year bond

**2. Credit Risk**

Risk that issuer defaults on payments:
- **Investment-grade bonds**: Low default risk (under 1% historically)
- **High-yield bonds**: Higher default risk (5-10% in recessions)
- Diversification critical for corporate bonds

**3. Inflation Risk**

Fixed payments lose purchasing power:
- 5% bond + 3% inflation = 2% real return
- TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) adjust principal for inflation

**4. Reinvestment Risk**

Risk that future coupon payments reinvested at lower rates:
- Buy bond at 6%, rates drop to 3%
- Future coupons earn only 3%, reducing total return

**5. Call Risk**

Issuer redeems bond before maturity (if callable):
- Typically happens when rates fall
- Investor loses higher-yielding bond, must reinvest at lower rates

### Bond Laddering Strategy

**What It Is**: Buying bonds with staggered maturities

**Example Ladder**:
- $10,000 in 1-year bonds
- $10,000 in 3-year bonds
- $10,000 in 5-year bonds
- $10,000 in 7-year bonds
- $10,000 in 10-year bonds

**Benefits**:
- Reduces interest rate risk (not all locked at one rate)
- Provides regular liquidity (bonds mature periodically)
- Averages out rate fluctuations
- Flexibility to reinvest at prevailing rates

## Stock Market Mechanics

### How Exchanges Work

**Primary Market**: Company issues new shares
- IPOs and secondary offerings
- Capital goes to company
- Investment banks facilitate

**Secondary Market**: Investors trade existing shares
- NYSE, NASDAQ, other exchanges
- Capital goes between investors
- Company doesn't receive money

### Order Types

| Order Type | Description | Use Case |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Market Order** | Buy/sell immediately at current price | Quick execution, less control over price |
| **Limit Order** | Buy/sell only at specified price or better | Price control, may not execute |
| **Stop-Loss Order** | Sells when price falls to trigger | Limit losses on declining stock |
| **Stop-Limit Order** | Converts to limit order at trigger | More control but may not execute |

### Bid-Ask Spread

- **Bid**: Highest price buyers willing to pay ($100.00)
- **Ask**: Lowest price sellers willing to accept ($100.05)
- **Spread**: Difference ($0.05)
- Narrow spread = liquid market (easy to trade)
- Wide spread = illiquid market (harder to trade)

### Market Participants

**Retail Investors**: Individual investors (you)
**Institutional Investors**: Mutual funds, pension funds, endowments
**Market Makers**: Firms that provide liquidity by buying and selling
**High-Frequency Traders**: Algorithmic firms making microsecond trades
**Short Sellers**: Bet on price declines by borrowing and selling shares

### Short Selling Explained

**Process**:
1. Borrow shares from broker
2. Sell shares at current price ($100)
3. Hope price falls
4. Buy shares back at lower price ($80)
5. Return shares to broker
6. Keep difference as profit ($20)

**Risks**:
- **Unlimited loss potential**: Stock could rise indefinitely
- **Margin calls**: Must maintain collateral
- **Short squeeze**: Rapid price rise forces covering

### Market Efficiency

**Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)**:
- Stock prices reflect all available information
- Cannot consistently beat market through analysis
- Three forms: weak, semi-strong, strong

**Reality**:
- Markets are mostly efficient (hard to beat)
- Occasional inefficiencies exist (value opportunities)
- Behavioral biases create temporary mispricings
- Long-term index investing wins for most investors

## Advanced Concepts

### Stock Buybacks

Company repurchases own shares:
- Reduces share count
- Increases EPS (fewer shares, same earnings)
- Returns cash to shareholders (alternative to dividends)
- Tax-efficient (shareholders choose when to sell)

**Example**: Apple bought back $90B in stock (2023)

### Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRIPs)

Automatically reinvest dividends to buy more shares:
- Accelerates compound growth
- Often commission-free
- Dollar-cost averaging benefit
- Can buy fractional shares

### Preferred Stock

Hybrid security with bond and stock characteristics:
- Fixed dividend (like bond coupon)
- Priority over common stock (but after bonds)
- Limited appreciation potential
- Less volatility than common stock

### Convertible Bonds

Bonds that can convert to stock:
- Downside protection (bond floor)
- Upside participation (if stock rises)
- Lower yield than regular bonds
- Complex valuation

## Key Terms

**Stock**: Ownership share in a company with potential for appreciation and dividends

**Bond**: Debt security representing a loan to issuer with fixed interest payments

**P/E Ratio**: Price-to-Earnings ratio; valuation metric comparing stock price to per-share earnings

**Yield**: Income return on investment (dividends for stocks, interest for bonds)

**Market Capitalization**: Total value of company's outstanding shares (price × shares)

**Duration**: Measure of bond price sensitivity to interest rate changes

**Credit Rating**: Assessment of borrower's creditworthiness and default risk

**Diversification**: Spreading investments across multiple securities to reduce risk

## Summary

Stocks represent ownership in companies, offering growth through appreciation and dividend income. Returns average 10% historically but with high volatility (30-50% declines possible). Valuation methods include P/E ratios, dividend discount models, and discounted cash flow analysis. Stock types span size (large/mid/small-cap), style (growth/value), and geography (domestic/international).

Bonds represent loans to governments or corporations, providing fixed income with lower risk. Key types include Treasuries (lowest risk), municipal bonds (tax-exempt), and corporate bonds (higher yields, higher risk). Bond prices move inverse to interest rates—when rates rise, existing bond values fall. Duration measures this sensitivity.

Market mechanics involve primary markets (new issuance) and secondary markets (trading among investors). Orders include market orders (immediate execution) and limit orders (price control). The bid-ask spread reflects market liquidity, with narrow spreads indicating easy trading.

Portfolio construction balances stocks and bonds based on age, goals, and risk tolerance. Younger investors favor stocks (80-100%) for growth; near-retirees increase bonds (40-60%) for stability. Diversification across asset classes, sizes, and geographies reduces portfolio volatility while maintaining returns.

The key insight: stocks provide long-term growth but require accepting volatility; bonds provide income and stability but lower returns. Combining both creates balanced portfolios that grow wealth while managing risk appropriately for your timeline and temperament.
