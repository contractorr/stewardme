# Estate Planning

## Overview

Estate planning is the process of arranging for the management and distribution of your assets after death or incapacitation. It ensures your wealth transfers according to your wishes, minimizes taxes and legal complications, protects beneficiaries, and provides for loved ones. Core components include wills, trusts, beneficiary designations, powers of attorney, and healthcare directives. Proper estate planning prevents family conflicts, reduces estate taxes, and ensures efficient asset transfer.

This guide covers essential estate planning documents, trust structures, beneficiary strategies, tax implications, and how to create a comprehensive estate plan.

## Why Estate Planning Matters

### Without an Estate Plan
- **Intestate succession**: State law determines who inherits (may not match your wishes)
- **Probate delays**: 6-24 months before assets distributed
- **Legal costs**: 3-7% of estate value in probate fees
- **Family conflict**: Disputes over assets, guardianship, medical decisions
- **Tax burden**: Missed opportunities for tax minimization
- **Business disruption**: No succession plan for family business

### With Proper Estate Planning
- **Control**: You decide who gets what, when, and how
- **Speed**: Trusts avoid probate; assets transfer immediately
- **Cost savings**: Minimize legal fees and taxes
- **Privacy**: Trusts keep estate private (probate is public record)
- **Protection**: Safeguard assets from creditors, lawsuits, divorcing spouses
- **Peace of mind**: Family knows your wishes and has guidance

### Who Needs Estate Planning?

**Everyone**, but especially:
- Parents with minor children (guardianship designation)
- High net worth individuals (estate tax planning)
- Business owners (succession planning)
- Blended families (ensure fair distribution)
- Special needs dependents (protect government benefits)
- Anyone with specific wishes for asset distribution

## Essential Estate Planning Documents

### 1. Last Will and Testament

**What It Does**:
- Specifies who inherits assets
- Names executor (manages estate settlement)
- Designates guardians for minor children
- Establishes testamentary trusts (created upon death)
- Provides for pet care

**What It Doesn't Do**:
- Avoid probate (will must go through probate)
- Control assets with beneficiary designations (401k, IRA, life insurance bypass will)
- Make medical decisions (need healthcare directive)
- Manage affairs if incapacitated (need power of attorney)

**Key Components**:
| Section | Purpose | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Executor** | Manages estate settlement | "I appoint my spouse, John Smith, as executor" |
| **Beneficiaries** | Who inherits what | "My residence to my spouse; investment accounts split equally among children" |
| **Guardianship** | Who raises minor children | "My sister, Jane Doe, as guardian of my children" |
| **Specific Bequests** | Particular items to individuals | "My 1967 Mustang to my son" |
| **Residuary Clause** | Catches everything not specified | "All remaining assets to my spouse" |

**Common Will Types**:
- **Simple Will**: Basic distribution, one beneficiary or equal split
- **Pour-Over Will**: Transfers assets to existing trust
- **Testamentary Trust Will**: Creates trusts upon death (for minors, special needs)

**Updating Your Will**:
- Review every 3-5 years
- Update after: marriage, divorce, births, deaths, major asset changes, moves to new state
- Use codicil (amendment) for small changes; new will for major revisions

### 2. Revocable Living Trust

**What It Is**: Legal entity that holds assets during lifetime and distributes them after death

**How It Works**:
1. Create trust document naming yourself as trustee
2. Transfer assets into trust (re-title property, accounts)
3. Continue managing assets normally
4. Upon death, successor trustee distributes per trust terms
5. Assets avoid probate

**Benefits**:
- **Avoids probate**: Assets transfer immediately, privately
- **Incapacity planning**: Successor trustee manages if you're unable
- **Privacy**: Trust terms not public (unlike probated will)
- **Multi-state property**: Avoids probate in multiple states
- **Flexibility**: Can modify or revoke anytime
- **Creditor protection**: Some protections for beneficiaries

**Downsides**:
- **Cost**: $1,500-3,000 to establish (vs. $500 for simple will)
- **Complexity**: Must re-title assets into trust
- **Maintenance**: Requires ongoing management
- **No tax savings**: Revocable trusts don't reduce estate taxes

**Assets to Include in Trust**:
- Real estate (primary residence, investment property)
- Investment accounts (brokerage, taxable accounts)
- Business interests
- Personal property (vehicles, collectibles)

**Assets NOT to Put in Trust**:
- Retirement accounts (401k, IRA) - use beneficiary designations
- HSAs - beneficiary designations
- Life insurance - beneficiary designations

### 3. Power of Attorney (POA)

**What It Is**: Document authorizing someone to make financial decisions if you're incapacitated

**Types**:
| Type | When Effective | Duration | Use Case |
|------|---------------|----------|----------|
| **Durable POA** | Immediately or upon incapacity | Until death or revoked | Most common; survives incapacity |
| **Springing POA** | Only when incapacitated | Until recovery or death | Concerns about immediate control |
| **Limited POA** | Specific situations | Defined timeframe | Single transaction (home closing) |

**Powers Granted**:
- Access bank accounts
- Pay bills and taxes
- Manage investments
- Buy/sell property
- Make business decisions
- Access safe deposit box
- Handle insurance claims

**Choosing an Agent**:
- **Trustworthy**: Will act in your best interest
- **Financially responsible**: Good with money
- **Available**: Can dedicate time when needed
- **Organized**: Keeps good records
- Common choices: Spouse, adult child, trusted friend, professional fiduciary

**Important**: POA terminates at death; executor takes over

### 4. Healthcare Directive (Living Will)

**What It Is**: Document specifying medical treatment preferences if unable to communicate

**Components**:
1. **Living Will**: Specific medical instructions (life support, resuscitation, feeding tubes)
2. **Healthcare Proxy/Medical POA**: Names someone to make medical decisions

**Key Decisions**:
- CPR and resuscitation
- Mechanical ventilation
- Tube feeding
- Dialysis
- Organ/tissue donation
- Pain management preferences

**Healthcare Agent Selection**:
- Can handle medical decisions under stress
- Understands and respects your wishes
- Available to make decisions quickly
- Often spouse, adult child, or close family member

**HIPAA Authorization**: Include authorization for agent to access medical records

### 5. Beneficiary Designations

**What They Are**: Forms designating who inherits specific accounts upon death

**Accounts with Beneficiary Designations**:
- Retirement accounts (401k, IRA, 403b)
- Life insurance policies
- HSAs
- Payable-on-death (POD) bank accounts
- Transfer-on-death (TOD) investment accounts

**Why They Matter**:
- **Override will**: Beneficiary designations supersede will instructions
- **Avoid probate**: Assets transfer directly to beneficiaries
- **Speed**: Much faster than probated assets
- **Tax planning**: Different beneficiaries have different tax implications

**Designation Options**:
| Beneficiary Type | Effect | Example Use |
|------------------|--------|-------------|
| **Primary** | First in line to inherit | Spouse |
| **Contingent** | Inherits if primary predeceased | Children |
| **Per Stirpes** | Dead beneficiary's share goes to their descendants | Ensures grandchildren inherit if child predeceases |
| **Per Capita** | Split equally among surviving beneficiaries | Each child gets equal share |

**Common Mistakes**:
- Never updating after life changes (divorce, remarriage, births)
- Naming minor children directly (creates guardianship issues)
- Not naming contingent beneficiaries
- Naming estate as beneficiary (forces probate, loses tax benefits)
- Inconsistent designations across accounts

**Review Annually**: Especially after marriage, divorce, births, deaths

## Trusts: Types and Uses

### Testamentary Trust (Created by Will)

**When Created**: Upon death, through will provisions

**Common Uses**:
- **Minor's Trust**: Holds assets until child reaches age (e.g., 25, 30)
- **Special Needs Trust**: Preserves government benefits eligibility
- **Spendthrift Trust**: Protects against beneficiary's creditors or poor spending

**Features**:
- Goes through probate (part of will)
- Can't be modified before death
- Trustee manages according to will terms

### Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

**Purpose**: Remove life insurance proceeds from taxable estate

**How It Works**:
1. Create irrevocable trust
2. Trust purchases or owns existing life insurance policy
3. Upon death, proceeds paid to trust (not estate)
4. Trust distributes per terms to beneficiaries
5. Proceeds excluded from estate tax calculation

**Benefits**:
- Removes multi-million dollar policies from estate
- Provides liquidity to pay estate taxes
- Protects proceeds from beneficiaries' creditors

**Requirements**:
- Must be irrevocable (can't change or cancel)
- Trust owns policy, not individual
- Transfers of existing policies have 3-year lookback period

**Example**:
- $5M life insurance policy
- Estate worth $10M (above $13.61M exemption after adjustments)
- ILIT removes $5M from estate calculation
- Saves ~$2M in estate taxes (40% rate)

### Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

**Purpose**: Donate appreciated assets, receive income, reduce taxes, support charity

**How It Works**:
1. Transfer appreciated assets (stock, real estate) to irrevocable trust
2. Receive income stream for life or set term (5-10%)
3. Upon death or term end, remainder goes to charity
4. Avoid capital gains tax on asset sale
5. Get immediate charitable deduction

**Benefits**:
- No capital gains tax on sale of appreciated assets
- Income tax deduction for charitable portion
- Income stream for life
- Reduces estate size

**Example**:
- Own stock worth $1M, cost basis $200k
- Transfer to CRT; trust sells stock
- No capital gains tax on $800k gain (saves $240k at 30% combined rate)
- Receive 5% annual income ($50k/year for life)
- Charitable deduction: ~$400k (present value of remainder)
- Tax savings on deduction: ~$160k (at 40% rate)
- **Total tax benefit**: $400k

**Who Benefits**: High net worth, philanthropic individuals with highly appreciated assets

### Special Needs Trust (SNT)

**Purpose**: Provide for disabled beneficiary without disqualifying from government benefits (SSI, Medicaid)

**How It Works**:
- Trust holds assets for beneficiary's benefit
- Trustee has discretion over distributions
- Supplements (doesn't replace) government benefits
- Pays for non-covered expenses: entertainment, education, therapy, vacations

**Types**:
| Type | Who Creates | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **First-Party SNT** | Beneficiary's own assets | Medicaid planning, inheritance protection |
| **Third-Party SNT** | Parents/family | Leave inheritance without benefit loss |
| **Pooled SNT** | Nonprofit manages | For smaller estates, professional management |

**Critical Rules**:
- Can't provide food or shelter directly (reduces benefits)
- Must be properly drafted to avoid benefit disqualification
- Needs experienced trustee who understands rules

**Example**:
- Parent leaves $500k to disabled child
- Without SNT: Disqualifies from $1,200/month SSI, loses Medicaid
- With SNT: Keeps SSI/Medicaid, trust pays for extras (car, travel, therapy, tech)

### Qualified Personal Residence Trust (QPRT)

**Purpose**: Transfer home to heirs at reduced gift tax value while continuing to live there

**How It Works**:
1. Transfer home to irrevocable trust for term of years (10-20)
2. Continue living in home rent-free during term
3. At term end, home passes to beneficiaries
4. Gift tax value discounted because of retained use period

**Benefits**:
- Removes appreciating asset from estate at discounted value
- Continue living in home during trust term
- Future appreciation outside estate

**Risks**:
- If die before term ends, home pulled back into estate (plan fails)
- After term, must pay rent to children or move out
- Irrevocable—can't change mind

**Example**:
- $2M home, 15-year QPRT
- Gift tax value: ~$800k (discounted for 15-year term)
- Uses $800k of gift exemption now vs. $2M+ later
- Home appreciates to $3M; $2.2M removed from estate
- Estate tax savings: ~$880k (at 40%)

## Estate Tax Considerations

### Federal Estate Tax (2025)

**Exemption**: $13.61M per individual ($27.22M per married couple)

**Tax Rate**: 40% on amounts above exemption

**Only 0.1% of estates pay federal estate tax** (99.9% exempt)

**Portability**: Surviving spouse can use deceased spouse's unused exemption (must file election)

**Example Without Portability**:
- Husband dies with $15M estate, all to wife
- Wife's exemption: $13.61M
- Husband's exemption: Unused (all to wife, no tax)
- Wife later dies with $15M + appreciation
- Estate tax on amount over $13.61M

**Example With Portability Election**:
- Husband dies, unused exemption transferred to wife
- Wife's effective exemption: $27.22M
- Wife's $20M estate: No tax

### Estate Tax Planning Strategies

**For Estates Under Exemption**:
- Simple will or revocable trust sufficient
- Focus on beneficiary designations and probate avoidance
- No complex tax planning needed

**For Estates Near or Above Exemption**:

**1. Gifting Strategy**
- Annual exclusion: $18,000 per recipient (2025)
- Lifetime gift exemption: $13.61M (shares exemption with estate)
- Married couple can gift $36,000/year per recipient
- Removes future appreciation from estate

**Example**:
- Gift $1M to child now
- Grows to $3M over 20 years
- Removes $2M appreciation from estate
- Estate tax savings: $800k

**2. Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)**
- Removes life insurance from estate (see earlier section)
- Provides liquidity for estate taxes

**3. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)**
- Transfer appreciating assets to trust
- Receive annuity payments for term
- Excess appreciation passes to beneficiaries gift-tax-free
- Good for volatile assets (stock, business interests)

**4. Family Limited Partnership (FLP)**
- Transfer assets to partnership
- Gift limited partnership interests to children
- Apply valuation discounts (lack of control, marketability)
- 30-40% discount reduces gift tax

**5. Charitable Giving**
- Unlimited charitable deduction
- Reduces estate size
- CRTs, donor-advised funds, direct bequests

| State | Exemption | Top Rate |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Connecticut | $13.61M | 12% |
| Hawaii | $5.49M | 20% |
| Illinois | $4M | 16% |
| Maine | $6.41M | 12% |
| Maryland | $5M | 16% |
| Massachusetts | $2M | 16% |
| Minnesota | $3M | 16% |
| New York | $6.94M | 16% |
| Oregon | $1M | 16% |

**Planning**: High-net-worth individuals in these states need strategies to minimize state taxes

## Beneficiary Planning Strategies

### Inherited IRA Rules (SECURE Act 2.0)

**Pre-2020 Rules**: Beneficiaries could "stretch" withdrawals over lifetime

**Current Rules (2020+)**:

**Eligible Designated Beneficiaries** (can stretch over lifetime):
- Surviving spouse
- Minor children (until age of majority, then 10-year rule)
- Disabled or chronically ill individuals
- Beneficiaries less than 10 years younger

**Non-Eligible Beneficiaries** (10-year rule):
- Adult children
- Most other beneficiaries
- Must empty account within 10 years of death

**Implications**:
- Inherited IRA compressed into 10 years = higher tax brackets
- Roth conversions before death more valuable
- Consider leaving IRAs to charity, Roth to heirs

**Example**:
- Parent dies with $1M traditional IRA, adult child beneficiary
- Child must withdraw $1M within 10 years
- Average $100k/year = likely higher tax bracket
- Total tax: ~$300k+ vs. ~$200k if stretched over lifetime

**Better Strategy**:
- Parent converts traditional IRA to Roth before death (pays tax in lower bracket)
- Child inherits Roth IRA, still 10-year rule but withdrawals tax-free
- Family saves $100k+ in taxes

### Spouse vs. Children Beneficiary Considerations

**Naming Spouse as Primary**:
- **Pros**: Spouse can roll IRA into own IRA; delay RMDs; maximum flexibility
- **Cons**: Remarriage risk; assets may not reach children

**Naming Children as Primary**:
- **Pros**: Ensures children receive assets
- **Cons**: No spousal rollover; spouse may need income

**Common Solution**: Spouse primary, children contingent
- Or: Portion to spouse, portion to children (split IRA in life)

### Trusts as Beneficiaries

**When to Name Trust as IRA Beneficiary**:
- Beneficiary is minor
- Beneficiary has special needs
- Spendthrift concerns (creditor protection)
- Blended family (ensure assets reach your children)
- Control over distributions

**Conduit Trust**:
- RMDs pass directly to beneficiary
- Qualifies as designated beneficiary (10-year rule)
- Less creditor protection

**Accumulation Trust**:
- Trust can accumulate distributions
- More control, more creditor protection
- Higher trust tax rates
- More complex

**Caution**: Naming trust as beneficiary loses some tax benefits; consult attorney

## Incapacity Planning

### Why It Matters

**Without Plan**:
- Court-appointed conservatorship/guardianship
- Public process, expensive ($5k-15k+)
- Court oversight of all financial decisions
- Time-consuming (months to establish)
- Loss of control

**With Plan**:
- Chosen agent steps in immediately
- Private, low-cost
- Flexibility in decision-making
- Avoids family conflict

### Key Documents for Incapacity

**Financial Incapacity**:
1. Durable Power of Attorney (financial decisions)
2. Revocable Living Trust (asset management)

**Medical Incapacity**:
1. Healthcare Directive/Living Will (treatment preferences)
2. Medical Power of Attorney (healthcare proxy)

**Communication**:
- Discuss wishes with family
- Provide copies to agents, doctor, attorney
- Store originals safely (attorney, safe deposit box)
- Keep list of all accounts, passwords (in secure location)

## Real-World Estate Planning Examples

### Example 1: Young Family
**Profile**: Married couple, 32 and 35, two young children (3 and 5), $500k combined assets (house $400k, retirement $100k)

**Estate Plan**:
1. **Simple Will**: Names each other as primary beneficiary, children contingent; designates sister as guardian for children
2. **Life Insurance**: $1M term policy each (ensures children's needs met if parents die)
3. **Power of Attorney**: Each spouse for the other
4. **Healthcare Directive**: Each spouse as healthcare agent
5. **Beneficiary Designations**: Spouse primary, children contingent (in trust until age 25)

**Cost**: ~$1,500-2,500
**Rationale**: Simple plan, focuses on child protection; revocable trust not necessary yet

### Example 2: Established Professional
**Profile**: 48-year-old single parent, one child (15), $3M estate ($800k house, $2M retirement, $200k taxable)

**Estate Plan**:
1. **Revocable Living Trust**: Holds house and taxable accounts; avoids probate
2. **Pour-Over Will**: Catches any assets not in trust
3. **Testamentary Trust**: Child's inheritance held in trust until age 30 (staged distributions at 25, 30)
4. **Power of Attorney**: Trusted sibling
5. **Healthcare Directive**: Sibling as agent
6. **Beneficiary Designations**: Trust as contingent (child is minor)

**Cost**: ~$3,000-5,000
**Rationale**: Trust avoids probate, provides control over when child receives assets

### Example 3: High Net Worth Couple
**Profile**: Married couple, both 60, children grown, $20M estate ($5M home, $12M investments, $3M business)

**Estate Plan**:
1. **Revocable Living Trust**: Holds real estate, taxable investments
2. **Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust**: $5M life insurance policy (removes from estate)
3. **Family Limited Partnership**: Business and some investments (valuation discounts)
4. **Charitable Remainder Trust**: $2M appreciated stock (income for life, remainder to charity)
5. **Annual Gifting**: $36k/year to each child and grandchild (12 recipients = $432k/year)
6. **Will**: Pour-over will
7. **POA and Healthcare Directive**: Each other, children as successors

**Tax Planning**:
- Estate: $20M - $5M (ILIT) - $2M (CRT) - ~$4M (gifting over years) = $9M
- Under $13.61M exemption: No federal estate tax
- Without planning: ~$2.5M estate tax

**Cost**: $20,000-50,000+ (complex planning)
**Rationale**: Sophisticated plan minimizes estate taxes, provides income, supports charity

### Example 4: Blended Family
**Profile**: Married couple (second marriage), 55 and 58, each with adult children from prior marriages, $4M estate

**Challenges**:
- Ensure current spouse provided for
- Ensure each person's children ultimately receive inheritance
- Avoid conflict between stepparent and stepchildren

**Estate Plan**:
1. **QTIP Trust** (Qualified Terminable Interest Property): Assets in trust for surviving spouse's life, remainder to deceased spouse's children
2. **Separate Trusts**: Each spouse has trust for their children
3. **Clear Documentation**: Specify what happens to joint vs. separate property
4. **Life Insurance**: Each owns policy benefiting own children (prevents disputes over estate assets)

**Example Structure**:
- Husband dies: $2M to QTIP trust (wife receives income for life, then to his children)
- Wife continues managing her $2M for her children
- Both sets of children know what they'll receive; reduces conflict

**Cost**: $5,000-10,000
**Rationale**: Protects both spouse and children, reduces family conflict

## Estate Planning Checklist

### Essential Actions

- [ ] Create or update will
- [ ] Establish revocable living trust (if estate over $500k or multi-state property)
- [ ] Designate executor/trustee
- [ ] Name guardians for minor children
- [ ] Establish power of attorney (financial)
- [ ] Create healthcare directive and medical POA
- [ ] Review beneficiary designations on all accounts
- [ ] Organize financial documents and passwords
- [ ] Purchase adequate life insurance
- [ ] Consider disability insurance
- [ ] Store documents safely (attorney, safe deposit box, fireproof safe)
- [ ] Provide copies to agents, executor, attorney
- [ ] Discuss wishes with family

### Review Triggers

- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption of children
- Death of beneficiary or executor
- Significant change in assets (inheritance, business sale)
- Move to different state
- Change in tax laws
- Every 3-5 years (general review)

## Key Terms

**Probate**: Court-supervised process of validating will and distributing assets; public and time-consuming

**Executor**: Person named in will to manage estate settlement and distribute assets

**Trustee**: Person or institution managing trust assets according to trust terms

**Beneficiary**: Person or entity designated to receive assets from estate, trust, or account

**Power of Attorney**: Legal document authorizing someone to make financial decisions on your behalf

**Living Will**: Document specifying medical treatment preferences if unable to communicate

**Intestate**: Dying without a valid will; state law determines asset distribution

**QTIP Trust**: Qualified Terminable Interest Property Trust; provides for spouse while ultimately preserving assets for designated heirs

## Summary

Estate planning ensures your wealth transfers according to your wishes while minimizing taxes, costs, and family conflict. Essential documents include a will (specifies distribution and guardianship), powers of attorney (financial and medical decisions if incapacitated), healthcare directive (medical treatment preferences), and beneficiary designations (bypass probate).

Revocable living trusts avoid probate, provide incapacity planning, and offer privacy. They hold real estate, investment accounts, and personal property—transferring immediately upon death without court involvement. Unlike wills, trusts don't reduce taxes but provide control and efficiency.

For high-net-worth estates above the $13.61M federal exemption ($27.22M married), tax planning becomes critical. Strategies include gifting during life ($18k annual exclusion), irrevocable life insurance trusts (remove insurance from estate), charitable remainder trusts (donate appreciated assets, receive income), and specialized trusts for business succession or family dynamics.

Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, and TOD/POD accounts supersede wills and avoid probate. Review annually, especially after life changes. The SECURE Act's 10-year rule for inherited IRAs makes Roth conversions before death more valuable—beneficiaries empty accounts faster, increasing tax burden.

The key insight: everyone needs basic estate planning (will, POA, healthcare directive). Those with children, significant assets, or complex family situations benefit from trusts and specialized planning. Review every 3-5 years or after major life events. Proper planning provides control, reduces costs by 70-90%, and gives peace of mind.
