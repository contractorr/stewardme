# Retirement Planning

## Overview

Retirement planning is the process of determining retirement income goals and creating strategies to achieve them. It involves estimating expenses, calculating required savings, maximizing tax-advantaged accounts, optimizing Social Security benefits, and developing sustainable withdrawal strategies. Starting early and understanding the retirement savings landscape—401(k)s, IRAs, pensions, and Social Security—is critical for financial independence in later years.

This guide covers retirement account types, contribution strategies, Social Security optimization, withdrawal planning, and how to calculate your retirement number.

## Why Retirement Planning Matters

### The Retirement Crisis
- **Life expectancy**: Average American lives to 78-80 (many into 90s)
- **Retirement duration**: 20-30+ years without employment income
- **Social Security gap**: Replaces only ~40% of pre-retirement income
- **Pension decline**: Only 14% of private-sector workers have defined-benefit pensions
- **Healthcare costs**: Medicare doesn't cover everything; out-of-pocket $300k+ per couple

### The Power of Starting Early

**$500/month invested from age 25 to 65 at 10% return**:
- Total invested: $240,000
- Final value: **$3,162,000**

**$1,500/month invested from age 45 to 65 at 10% return**:
- Total invested: $360,000
- Final value: **$1,144,000**

**Starting 20 years earlier with 1/3 the monthly contribution yields 2.8× more wealth**

### Common Retirement Mistakes
1. Starting too late (missing compound growth years)
2. Undersaving (failing to reach 15-20% savings rate)
3. Not maximizing employer match (leaving free money on table)
4. Poor Social Security timing (claiming too early)
5. Withdrawing retirement savings early (penalties + lost growth)
6. No withdrawal strategy (paying unnecessary taxes)
7. Underestimating healthcare costs
8. Not accounting for inflation

## Retirement Account Types

### 401(k) Plans

**What It Is**: Employer-sponsored retirement plan allowing pre-tax or Roth contributions

**2025 Contribution Limits**:
| Type | Under 50 | Age 50+ | Total (employee + employer) |
|------|----------|---------|---------------------------|
| Employee Elective Deferral | $23,500 | $31,000 | - |
| Total Contribution Limit | - | - | $70,000 |

**Traditional 401(k)**:
- **Contributions**: Pre-tax (reduces current taxable income)
- **Growth**: Tax-deferred
- **Withdrawals**: Taxed as ordinary income
- **Best for**: High earners expecting lower tax bracket in retirement

**Roth 401(k)**:
- **Contributions**: After-tax (no current deduction)
- **Growth**: Tax-free
- **Withdrawals**: Tax-free (contributions and gains after 5 years)
- **Best for**: Young workers, those expecting higher future tax rates
- **No income limits** (unlike Roth IRA)

**Key Features**:
- **Employer match**: Free money (typically 50% or 100% of first 3-6% contributed)
- **Vesting schedule**: May need to stay 2-6 years to keep employer contributions
- **Investment options**: Limited to plan's fund lineup
- **Loan provision**: Can borrow 50% or $50k (whichever is less)—not recommended
- **Hardship withdrawals**: Allowed but subject to 10% penalty + taxes if under 59½

**Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)**:
- Must begin at age 73 (raised from 72 in 2023)
- Annual withdrawal = account balance ÷ life expectancy factor
- Penalty for missing: 25% of required amount (recently reduced from 50%)

### Individual Retirement Account (IRA)

**2025 Contribution Limits**:
- Under 50: $7,000
- Age 50+: $8,000

**Traditional IRA**:
- **Contributions**: Pre-tax if income below thresholds and no workplace plan
- **Deduction phase-out** (2025, with workplace retirement plan):
  - Single: $77,000 - $87,000
  - Married: $123,000 - $143,000
- **Withdrawals**: Taxed as ordinary income
- **RMDs**: Required at age 73

**Roth IRA**:
- **Contributions**: After-tax
- **Income limits** (2025):
  - Single: Phase-out $150,000 - $165,000
  - Married: Phase-out $236,000 - $246,000
- **Growth**: Tax-free
- **Withdrawals**: Tax-free after 5 years and age 59½
- **No RMDs**: Can leave money growing forever
- **Flexibility**: Can withdraw contributions (not gains) anytime without penalty

**Traditional vs. Roth Decision Framework**:

| Factor | Traditional | Roth |
|--------|-------------|------|
| **Current tax bracket** | High (24%+) | Low-Moderate (12-22%) |
| **Expected retirement bracket** | Lower | Same or higher |
| **Age** | Older (40+) | Younger (under 40) |
| **Tax rate outlook** | Expect lower future rates | Expect higher future rates |
| **Need immediate deduction** | Yes | No |
| **Want RMD flexibility** | No | Yes |

### Solo 401(k) for Self-Employed

**What It Is**: 401(k) for self-employed individuals with no employees (except spouse)

**Contribution Limits (2025)**:
- **Employee deferral**: $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+)
- **Employer contribution**: Up to 25% of compensation
- **Total limit**: $70,000 ($77,500 if 50+)

**Example**:
- Self-employment income: $150,000
- Employee deferral: $23,500
- Employer contribution: $30,000 (20% of $150k for self-employed)
- **Total contribution**: $53,500

**Advantages**:
- Much higher contribution limits than regular IRA
- Allows both Roth and traditional contributions
- Can take loans (unlike SEP IRA)

### SEP IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)

**What It Is**: Employer-funded retirement plan for self-employed or small businesses

**Contribution Limits (2025)**:
- Up to 25% of compensation (20% for self-employed)
- Maximum: $70,000

**Features**:
- Easy to set up and maintain
- Employer contributions only (no employee deferrals)
- Must contribute same percentage for all eligible employees
- Immediately vested

**Best For**: Self-employed with fluctuating income (can vary contribution each year)

### Pension Plans (Defined Benefit)

**What It Is**: Employer-funded plan promising specific monthly benefit in retirement

**Formula**: Typically based on salary and years of service
- Example: 1.5% × years of service × final salary
- 30 years, $100k salary: 1.5% × 30 × $100k = $45,000/year

**Types**:
- **Single-life**: Highest payment, stops at death
- **Joint-and-survivor**: Lower payment, continues to spouse
- **Period certain**: Guarantees payments for specific period

**Considerations**:
- Rare in private sector (mostly government/unions)
- Not portable (lose benefit if leave early)
- Company bankruptcy risk (PBGC insurance covers some)
- No control over investments

**Pension vs. Lump Sum Decision**:
| Factor | Take Pension | Take Lump Sum |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Health/longevity** | Good health, long-lived family | Poor health, short life expectancy |
| **Investment skill** | Prefer guaranteed income | Confident investor |
| **Spouse** | Want survivor benefits | Can manage lump sum |
| **Legacy** | Don't need to leave inheritance | Want to pass wealth to heirs |
| **Interest rates** | When rates low (annuity more valuable) | When rates high (lump sum better) |

## Social Security Fundamentals

### How Social Security Works

**Funding**: Payroll taxes (6.2% employee + 6.2% employer on first $168,600 in 2025)

**Benefit Calculation**:
1. Index earnings for inflation across 35 highest-earning years
2. Calculate Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)
3. Apply formula to get Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)

**PIA Formula (2025)**:
- 90% of first $1,226 of AIME
- 32% of AIME between $1,226 and $7,391
- 15% of AIME above $7,391

**Example**:
- AIME: $8,000/month
- Benefit: (90% × $1,226) + (32% × $6,165) + (15% × $609) = $3,166/month at Full Retirement Age

### Claiming Age Strategy

**Full Retirement Age (FRA)**: Age for 100% of benefit (66-67 depending on birth year)

**Impact of Claiming Age**:
| Age | Percentage of Full Benefit | Monthly Benefit (if FRA = $3,000) |
|-----|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| 62 | 70% | $2,100 |
| 64 | 80% | $2,400 |
| 66 | 93% (if FRA is 67) | $2,800 |
| 67 | 100% (for those born 1960+) | $3,000 |
| 68 | 108% | $3,240 |
| 69 | 116% | $3,480 |
| 70 | 124% | $3,720 |

**Key Insights**:
- **Claiming early (62)**: 30% permanent reduction, break-even at ~78-80
- **Delaying to 70**: 24% increase over FRA, 77% higher than age 62, break-even at ~82-84
- **Each year delayed**: ~8% benefit increase from FRA to 70
- **After 70**: No further increase, no reason to delay

### Optimal Claiming Strategies

**For Singles**:
- **Good health/longevity**: Delay to 70 (maximize lifetime benefits)
- **Poor health/short life expectancy**: Claim at 62 or FRA
- **Need income**: Claim when needed, but consider other sources first

**For Married Couples**:
**Scenario 1: Similar Earnings**
- Both delay to 70 if possible (maximize both benefits)

**Scenario 2: One High Earner, One Low/No Earner**
- **Higher earner**: Delay to 70 (survivor benefits)
- **Lower earner**: Can claim earlier (around FRA)
- **Rationale**: Survivor gets higher of the two benefits; maximizing high earner's benefit protects survivor

**Scenario 3: Significant Age Gap**
- Older spouse delays to 70
- Younger spouse claims earlier if needed for income

**Spousal Benefits**:
- Spouse can receive up to 50% of worker's FRA benefit
- Must wait until worker claims
- Doesn't reduce worker's benefit
- At spouse's FRA: 50%; if claim early: reduced

**Survivor Benefits**:
- Widow/widower receives higher of own benefit or 100% of deceased's benefit
- Can claim survivor benefit at 60 (reduced) or own benefit at 62
- Strategy: Claim one at 60, switch to higher benefit later

### Working While Receiving Benefits

**Before Full Retirement Age**:
- Earn over $22,320 (2025): Lose $1 in benefits for every $2 earned
- Year reaching FRA: Lose $1 for every $3 over $59,520
- Benefits recalculated at FRA to account for withholding (not permanently lost)

**After Full Retirement Age**:
- No earnings limit
- Increased benefits to account for months withheld

## Calculating Your Retirement Number

### The 25x Rule (4% Rule)

**Concept**: Need 25 times annual expenses saved to withdraw 4% annually without running out

**Formula**: Annual Expenses × 25 = Retirement Nest Egg Needed

**Examples**:
| Annual Expenses | Nest Egg Needed (25×) |
|----------------|----------------------|
| $40,000 | $1,000,000 |
| $60,000 | $1,500,000 |
| $80,000 | $2,000,000 |
| $100,000 | $2,500,000 |

**Why It Works**:
- 4% withdrawal rate historically sustained 30+ year retirements
- Balances growth with inflation protection
- Based on historical stock/bond returns

**Limitations**:
- Assumes consistent expenses (doesn't account for sequence risk)
- May be too conservative (could leave large estate)
- May be too aggressive in low-return environments
- Doesn't account for Social Security, pensions

### Comprehensive Retirement Calculation

**Step 1: Estimate Annual Retirement Expenses**

Include:
- Housing (mortgage/rent, insurance, taxes, maintenance)
- Healthcare (insurance premiums, out-of-pocket, long-term care)
- Food and groceries
- Transportation
- Entertainment and travel
- Utilities
- Discretionary spending

Adjust for retirement:
- **Eliminate**: Payroll taxes, retirement savings, work expenses, mortgage (if paid off)
- **Reduce**: Clothing, transportation (no commute)
- **Increase**: Healthcare, travel, hobbies

**Typical Rule**: Need 70-80% of pre-retirement income

**Step 2: Calculate Guaranteed Income Sources**

- Social Security (estimate at ssa.gov)
- Pension payments
- Annuities
- Rental income

**Step 3: Calculate Gap**

Annual Expenses - Guaranteed Income = Gap to Fill from Portfolio

**Step 4: Apply 25x Rule to Gap**

Gap × 25 = Required Portfolio

### Retirement Calculation Example

**Couple, age 65, retiring**:

**Annual Expenses**: $90,000
- Housing: $20,000
- Healthcare: $15,000 (premiums + out-of-pocket)
- Food: $12,000
- Travel: $15,000
- Transportation: $8,000
- Other: $20,000

**Guaranteed Income**:
- Social Security (both): $60,000 ($3,000 + $2,000/month × 12)
- No pension

**Gap**: $90,000 - $60,000 = $30,000

**Required Portfolio**: $30,000 × 25 = **$750,000**

**Reality Check**:
- 4% withdrawal: $30,000 (covers gap)
- Social Security: $60,000
- Total: $90,000 ✓

## Withdrawal Strategies

### The 4% Rule in Detail

**Original Research** (Bengen, 1994; Trinity Study):
- 50% stocks / 50% bonds
- 4% initial withdrawal, adjusted annually for inflation
- 30-year retirement period
- 95% success rate historically

**Example**:
- Portfolio: $1,000,000
- Year 1 withdrawal: $40,000
- Year 2 (3% inflation): $41,200
- Year 3: $42,436
- Continue adjusting for inflation

**Criticisms**:
- Developed during high-return period
- Current low yields may require 3-3.5%
- Doesn't account for flexibility (can reduce spending in bear markets)

### Dynamic Withdrawal Strategies

**1. Guardrails Strategy**
- Set upper and lower spending thresholds
- Increase spending if portfolio outperforms (e.g., 20% above target)
- Decrease if underperforms (e.g., 20% below target)
- More spending in good markets, belt-tightening in bad

**2. Variable Percentage Withdrawal**
- Withdraw fixed % of current portfolio value each year
- Example: 5% of balance
- Year 1: $1M × 5% = $50,000
- Year 2 (market down to $900k): $900k × 5% = $45,000
- Year 3 (market up to $1.1M): $1.1M × 5% = $55,000
- **Benefit**: Never runs out
- **Downside**: Variable income (could be very low in crashes)

**3. Bucket Strategy**
- **Bucket 1 (0-2 years)**: Cash/CDs - immediate needs
- **Bucket 2 (3-10 years)**: Bonds - medium-term
- **Bucket 3 (10+ years)**: Stocks - long-term growth
- Refill buckets from longer-term buckets periodically
- Provides spending certainty and allows growth

### Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Order

**General Priority**:
1. **Taxable accounts first** (most flexible, lowest tax often)
2. **Tax-deferred (401k, traditional IRA)** (fill lower brackets)
3. **Tax-free (Roth)** (save for last or high-expense years)

**Rationale**:
- Deplete taxable first preserves tax-advantaged growth
- Control when to pay taxes on traditional accounts
- Roth as backstop or for large expenses

**Refinements**:
- If in low bracket: Convert traditional → Roth (fill bracket)
- If have long-term capital gains: May be taxed at 0% if under threshold
- After RMD age (73): Must take RMDs from traditional accounts

**Example Age 65-75 Strategy**:
- 65-72: Withdraw from taxable accounts + Roth conversions
- 73+: Take RMDs from traditional + fill remaining from taxable/Roth
- Large expense year: Use Roth to avoid bracket jump

## Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

### RMD Basics

**When**: Must begin at age 73 (for those turning 72 after 2022)

**Which Accounts**:
- Traditional IRAs
- SEP IRAs
- SIMPLE IRAs
- Traditional 401(k)s
- 403(b)s

**Exempt**:
- Roth IRAs (during owner's lifetime)
- Roth 401(k)s *must* take RMDs unless rolled to Roth IRA

### RMD Calculation

**Formula**: Account Balance (Dec 31 prior year) ÷ Life Expectancy Factor

**Life Expectancy Factors** (IRS Uniform Lifetime Table):
| Age | Factor | Percentage |
|-----|--------|-----------|
| 73 | 26.5 | 3.77% |
| 75 | 24.6 | 4.07% |
| 80 | 20.2 | 4.95% |
| 85 | 16.0 | 6.25% |
| 90 | 12.2 | 8.20% |
| 95 | 9.1 | 10.99% |

**Example**:
- Age 75, IRA balance: $800,000
- Factor: 24.6
- RMD: $800,000 ÷ 24.6 = **$32,520**
- Must withdraw by Dec 31 (or April 1 of following year for first RMD)

### RMD Strategies

**Minimize RMDs Before Age 73**:
- Roth conversions in low-income years
- Reduces future traditional IRA balance
- Smaller RMDs means more control

**Satisfy RMDs Efficiently**:
- Donate directly to charity (Qualified Charitable Distribution)
- Use in-kind transfers (transfer securities, don't sell)
- Coordinate across multiple accounts (can take from one, but calculate for each)

**Penalty for Missing RMD**: 25% of amount not withdrawn (reduced from 50% in 2023)

## Key Terms

**Full Retirement Age (FRA)**: Age at which you receive 100% of Social Security benefit (66-67 depending on birth year)

**Required Minimum Distribution (RMD)**: Mandatory annual withdrawal from tax-deferred accounts starting at age 73

**4% Rule**: Guideline suggesting 4% annual withdrawal rate from retirement portfolio sustains 30-year retirement

**Vesting**: Process of earning ownership of employer contributions to retirement accounts over time

**Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)**: Full Social Security benefit amount at Full Retirement Age

**Sequence of Returns Risk**: Danger of poor market returns early in retirement depleting portfolio

## Summary

Retirement planning requires estimating expenses, calculating required savings, and creating income strategies for 20-30+ years without employment. The foundation: maximize tax-advantaged accounts early—401(k)s, IRAs, and HSAs—to harness compound growth over decades.

The 25x rule provides a starting point: save 25 times annual expenses to withdraw 4% annually. For example, $80,000 annual expenses requires $2 million saved. This simplified calculation should be adjusted for Social Security, pensions, and other guaranteed income sources.

Social Security timing is critical. Delaying from 62 to 70 increases benefits by 77%, with break-even around age 82. For married couples, the higher earner should typically delay to 70 to maximize survivor benefits. Working in early retirement reduces benefits but they're recalculated at FRA.

Withdrawal strategy matters as much as accumulation. The 4% rule—withdrawing 4% of starting portfolio adjusted for inflation—has historically sustained 30-year retirements. Tax-efficient withdrawal order: deplete taxable accounts first, tax-deferred second, Roth last. After age 73, Required Minimum Distributions force withdrawals from traditional accounts.

The key insight: start early, save consistently (15-20% of income), maximize employer matches (free money), and let compound interest work for 30-40 years. A 25-year-old saving $500/month reaches $3.2M by 65; a 45-year-old needs $1,500/month for just $1.1M. Time is more powerful than amount—start now.
