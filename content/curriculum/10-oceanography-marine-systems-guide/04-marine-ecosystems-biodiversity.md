# Marine Ecosystems & Biodiversity

## Overview

Oceans contain ~15% of known species but represent fundamentally different environments than land — from sunlit coral reefs to pitch-black hydrothermal vents. Marine ecosystems span 11 km vertically (surface to Mariana Trench) and range from tropics to poles. Understanding marine biodiversity essential for fisheries management, pharmaceutical discovery, and climate resilience.

---

## Key Concepts

### Marine Biodiversity by the Numbers

| Metric | Value | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Described marine species | ~240,000 | Likely 10-30% of actual total |
| Estimated total species | 700,000 - 1 million+ | Many microbes, deep-sea organisms undiscovered |
| Marine vs terrestrial | 15% vs 85% (described) | Oceans less explored than land |
| Marine prokaryotes | 10²⁸ - 10²⁹ cells | More than stars in observable universe |
| Coral reef species | 25% of marine species | Despite covering <0.1% of ocean area |
| Deep-sea diversity | Possibly exceeds shallow | High species richness but low abundance |

**Why Marine Biodiversity Lower (in absolute species count)?**
- Water is thermally stable → less isolation/speciation pressure
- Larvae disperse long distances → gene flow prevents divergence
- Marine habitats more continuous (vs terrestrial islands/mountains)
- **But:** Marine life spans more phylogenetic diversity (more phyla than land)

### Major Marine Ecosystems

#### 1. Coral Reefs

**Characteristics:**
- Tropical (18-30°C); shallow (<50m); clear water; sunlight-dependent
- Built by corals (polyps + symbiotic zooxanthellae algae)
- 25% of marine species despite <0.1% of ocean area
- "Rainforests of the sea"

**Productivity:**
- High primary production despite nutrient-poor water (algae + efficient recycling)
- Support complex food webs; hundreds of fish species per reef

**Distribution:**
- Indo-Pacific: Great Barrier Reef, Coral Triangle (highest diversity)
- Caribbean: Mesoamerican Reef, Florida Keys
- Red Sea, Persian Gulf (extreme temperatures; hardy corals)

**Ecosystem Services:**
- **Fisheries:** 25% of catch in developing countries
- **Tourism:** $36 billion/year globally
- **Coastal protection:** Break waves; reduce storm damage
- **Pharmaceuticals:** Source of novel compounds (anticancer, antibiotic)

**Threats:**
- **Bleaching:** 1-2°C warming → coral expels algae → starvation; 50% of reefs bleached since 1980s
- **Acidification:** Lower pH → harder to build calcium carbonate skeleton
- **Overfishing:** Removes herbivorous fish → algae overgrow corals
- **Pollution:** Sediment, nutrients smother reefs
- **Destructive fishing:** Dynamite, cyanide, bottom trawling physically destroy

#### 2. Kelp Forests

**Characteristics:**
- Temperate/polar coasts; cold water (<20°C)
- Giant kelp (Macrocystis) grows up to 45m tall; up to 60 cm/day (fastest-growing organism)
- Three-dimensional structure like terrestrial forests

**Productivity:**
- Very high; kelp photosynthesizes, decays → fuels food web
- Supports 1,000+ species: sea otters, seals, fish, invertebrates

**Distribution:**
- US/Canada West Coast, South America, South Africa, southern Australia, New Zealand

**Ecosystem Services:**
- **Fisheries:** Nursery habitat for commercial species (rockfish, lobster)
- **Carbon sequestration:** Captures CO₂; some sinks to deep ocean
- **Coastal protection:** Dampens waves

**Threats:**
- **Sea urchin overgrazing:** When otter/predator populations decline, urchins explode → eat kelp → barren seafloor
- **Marine heatwaves:** 2014-16 Pacific blob killed kelp; recovery slow
- **Pollution:** Sediment reduces light; nutrients favor algae over kelp

#### 3. Mangrove Forests

**Characteristics:**
- Tropical/subtropical; coastal; salt-tolerant trees
- Grow in intertidal zones (submerged at high tide)
- Unique adaptations: aerial roots (gas exchange), salt excretion/exclusion

**Productivity:**
- High; leaf litter → detritus → food web
- Nursery habitat for fish, shrimp, crabs

**Distribution:**
- 118 countries; largest in Indonesia, Brazil, Nigeria, Australia, Mexico
- 150,000 km² globally (declining)

**Ecosystem Services:**
- **Coastal protection:** Reduce storm surge, erosion; saved lives in 2004 tsunami
- **Carbon sequestration:** Store 3-5× more carbon per area than terrestrial forests (in sediment)
- **Fisheries:** 80% of commercial fish spend part of life cycle in mangroves
- **Water filtration:** Trap sediment, filter pollutants

**Threats:**
- **Conversion:** Shrimp farms, coastal development, agriculture
- **Logging:** Fuel wood, construction timber
- **Sea level rise:** Drowning if landward migration blocked
- **Pollution:** Oil spills, chemical runoff

#### 4. Seagrass Meadows

**Characteristics:**
- Flowering plants (not algae) adapted to marine life
- Shallow coastal waters (<30m); clear water; temperate to tropical
- Form dense underwater meadows

**Productivity:**
- High; basis for food web (grazers: sea turtles, manatees, dugongs)
- Stabilize sediment with roots

**Distribution:**
- Widespread in coastal zones globally; 300,000 km² total

**Ecosystem Services:**
- **Fisheries:** Nursery habitat; food source
- **Carbon sequestration:** "Blue carbon"; store carbon in sediment
- **Water quality:** Filter sediment, absorb nutrients
- **Coastal protection:** Dampen waves, prevent erosion

**Threats:**
- **Pollution:** Sediment blocks light; nutrient runoff causes algae blooms
- **Coastal development:** Dredging, land reclamation destroy habitat
- **Boat anchors/propellers:** Physical damage
- **Climate change:** Heatwaves kill seagrass; sea level rise alters habitat

#### 5. Open Ocean (Pelagic)

**Characteristics:**
- Largest ecosystem by area (~65% of ocean surface)
- Nutrient-poor (oligotrophic) except upwelling zones
- Deep; photic zone = thin surface layer

**Productivity:**
- Low per-area but high total (due to vast area)
- Phytoplankton → zooplankton → small fish → large fish → apex predators

**Key Species:**
- **Phytoplankton:** Diatoms, dinoflagellates, cyanobacteria (Prochlorococcus = most abundant photosynthesizer on Earth)
- **Zooplankton:** Copepods, krill (Antarctic krill = 500M tonnes biomass)
- **Fish:** Tuna, marlin, flying fish, lanternfish (most abundant vertebrate)
- **Marine mammals:** Whales, dolphins
- **Seabirds:** Albatross, petrels, shearwaters

**Threats:**
- **Overfishing:** Tuna, sharks, swordfish depleted
- **Bycatch:** Dolphins, turtles, seabirds caught in nets/longlines
- **Plastic pollution:** Gyres accumulate debris; microplastics ubiquitous
- **Ocean acidification:** Affects pteropods (key zooplankton with shells)
- **Climate change:** Warming, stratification reduce productivity

#### 6. Deep Sea (Abyssal & Hadal)

**Characteristics:**
- >1000m depth; pitch black; cold (2-4°C); high pressure
- Low productivity; food scarce (relies on sinking organic matter)
- Slow growth, long lifespans, low reproduction rates

**Key Ecosystems:**
- **Abyssal plains:** Soft sediment; sea cucumbers, brittle stars, polychaete worms
- **Hydrothermal vents:** Superheated water (400°C); chemosynthetic bacteria → unique food web (tube worms, giant clams)
- **Cold seeps:** Methane/sulfide seepage; similar chemosynthetic communities
- **Seamounts:** Underwater mountains; often rich in fish, corals
- **Trenches:** Deepest ocean; amphipods, snailfish, microbial mats

**Unique Adaptations:**
- **Bioluminescence:** 90% of organisms produce light (communication, hunting, camouflage)
- **Pressure tolerance:** Flexible bodies, no air spaces, specialized proteins
- **Slow metabolism:** Long lifespans (some sponges 10,000+ years)
- **Gigantism:** Some species (giant squid, isopods) much larger than shallow relatives

**Threats:**
- **Deep-sea fishing:** Bottom trawling destroys coral gardens, seamount ecosystems
- **Mining:** Polymetallic nodules, seafloor massive sulfides; impacts unknown but likely severe
- **Plastic pollution:** Found in Mariana Trench; ingested by deep-sea organisms
- **Climate change:** Oxygen minimum zones expanding; acidification affects deep-sea corals

## Marine Food Webs

### Trophic Structure

**Primary Producers (Phytoplankton):**
- Convert sunlight + CO₂ + nutrients → organic matter
- Produce 50-80% of atmospheric oxygen
- Total biomass: ~1-2 billion tonnes (low compared to land plants)
- Turnover: Days to weeks (vs years for trees)

**Primary Consumers (Zooplankton):**
- Graze on phytoplankton
- Copepods, krill, jellyfish, larval fish
- Krill = keystone species in Antarctic (supports whales, seals, penguins)

**Secondary/Tertiary Consumers:**
- Small fish, squid → large fish → apex predators (sharks, tuna, marine mammals)
- Energy loss: ~90% per trophic level → only ~10% passed up

**Decomposers:**
- Bacteria, fungi break down dead organic matter
- Critical for nutrient recycling
- "Marine snow": Sinking detritus; feeds deep-sea life

**Keystone Species:**
- **Sea otters:** Eat sea urchins → prevent kelp overgrazing
- **Sharks:** Control prey populations; maintain ecosystem balance
- **Krill:** Link phytoplankton to whales, penguins, seals
- Removal → trophic cascades → ecosystem collapse

## Real-World Examples

### Great Barrier Reef Decline

- World's largest reef system (344,000 km²); 2,900 reefs, 900 islands
- 50% of coral cover lost since 1990s
- **Causes:** Bleaching (2016, 2017, 2020), crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks, cyclones, pollution
- **Bleaching mechanism:** 1-2°C warming → coral expels zooxanthellae → white skeleton visible
- **Economic:** $6 billion/year tourism; 64,000 jobs
- **Outlook:** Grim without rapid emissions cuts; some corals adapting but slowly

### Sea Otter Keystone Species

- Pacific coast kelp forests; otters eat sea urchins (2-3 kg/day)
- 1900s: Fur trade drove otters near extinction → urchin explosion → kelp deforestation → "urchin barrens"
- Reintroduction: Otters recovered → urchins controlled → kelp forests rebounded
- **Lesson:** Apex/keystone predators structure entire ecosystems
- **Modern threat:** Sea stars (urchin predators) decimated by wasting disease → urchins exploding again in some areas

### Coral Triangle

- Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, Timor-Leste
- 6 million km²; 76% of coral species; 37% of coral reef fish species
- Highest marine biodiversity on Earth
- 120 million people depend on reefs for food, income
- **Threats:** Overfishing, destructive fishing (dynamite/cyanide), pollution, coastal development
- **Conservation:** Coral Triangle Initiative (regional cooperation)

### Deep-Sea Hydrothermal Vents

- Discovered 1977; revolutionized biology
- Superheated water (400°C) + chemicals (H₂S, CH₄) from Earth's interior
- Chemosynthetic bacteria convert chemicals to energy (no photosynthesis)
- Support unique life: giant tube worms (2m), giant clams, eyeless shrimp
- **Significance:** Life without sunlight → implications for origin of life, astrobiology (Europa, Enceladus)
- **Threat:** Deep-sea mining targets similar areas (seafloor massive sulfides)

## Conservation Challenges

### Overfishing

- 34% of fish stocks overfished; 60% fully fished; only 6% underfished
- Bycatch: 10-20% of catch discarded dead (dolphins, turtles, seabirds)
- Bottom trawling: Destroys seafloor habitats; like clearcutting forests
- Illegal fishing: 20-30% of catch; undermines management

### Habitat Destruction

- Coral reefs: 50% degraded or lost
- Mangroves: 35% lost since 1980s (shrimp farming, development)
- Seagrass: 29% lost since 1879; accelerating
- Deep-sea corals: Bottom trawling destroys centuries-old growth in minutes

### Pollution

- **Plastic:** 8 million tonnes/year enter ocean; 150M tonnes currently in ocean; microplastics in all marine life
- **Nutrients:** Runoff → algal blooms → dead zones (400+ globally)
- **Chemicals:** Pesticides, heavy metals bioaccumulate in food web
- **Oil:** Spills (Deepwater Horizon) + chronic leakage from ships

### Climate Change

- **Warming:** Species shifting poleward; coral bleaching; reduced productivity in tropics
- **Acidification:** Shells/skeletons harder to build; pteropods (key zooplankton) threatened
- **Sea level rise:** Drowns coastal ecosystems if landward migration blocked
- **Deoxygenation:** Expanding oxygen minimum zones; dead zones growing

### Invasive Species

- Ballast water transport; aquaculture escapes; aquarium releases
- Lionfish (Caribbean): Native to Indo-Pacific; outcompete native fish; no predators
- Comb jellies (Black Sea): Collapsed anchovy fishery
- Green crabs (US): Destroy eelgrass, shellfish beds

## Key Terms

**Phytoplankton:** Microscopic photosynthetic organisms (algae, cyanobacteria); base of marine food web

**Zooplankton:** Drifting animals (copepods, krill, jellyfish); graze on phytoplankton

**Zooxanthellae:** Symbiotic algae living in coral tissue; provide energy via photosynthesis

**Coral Bleaching:** Expulsion of zooxanthellae from stressed coral; turns white; often leads to death

**Keystone Species:** Species with disproportionate impact on ecosystem structure (sea otters, sharks)

**Trophic Cascade:** Domino effect through food web when top predator removed

**Bycatch:** Non-target species caught incidentally in fisheries

**Blue Carbon:** Carbon stored in coastal/marine ecosystems (mangroves, seagrass, salt marshes)

**Eutrophication:** Excess nutrients → algal blooms → oxygen depletion

**Marine Protected Area (MPA):** Zone where human activity restricted to conserve ecosystems

## Summary

Marine ecosystems span from sunlit coral reefs to pitch-black hydrothermal vents, containing 25% of marine species in reefs despite <0.1% of ocean area. Coral reefs provide fisheries, tourism, coastal protection but 50% degraded from bleaching, acidification, overfishing. Kelp forests, mangroves, seagrass meadows are highly productive coastal ecosystems providing nurseries, carbon storage, coastal protection. Open ocean is largest ecosystem by area; phytoplankton produce 50-80% of atmospheric oxygen but low nutrient availability limits productivity except in upwelling zones. Deep sea (>1000m) is cold, dark, high-pressure; relies on sinking organic matter; hosts unique chemosynthetic ecosystems at hydrothermal vents. Marine food webs lose ~90% energy per trophic level; keystone species like sea otters structure entire ecosystems. Threats include overfishing (34% stocks depleted), habitat destruction, pollution (8M tonnes plastic/year), climate change (warming, acidification, deoxygenation). Understanding marine biodiversity essential for conservation, sustainable fisheries, and climate resilience.
