# Ocean Circulation & Climate

## Overview

Ocean currents are Earth's thermal regulation system. Surface currents driven by wind redistribute heat from equator to poles. Deep currents driven by density differences (thermohaline circulation) operate on millennial timescales. Together, oceans transport more heat than atmosphere, regulate regional climates, drive weather patterns, and determine marine ecosystem productivity.

---

## Key Concepts

### Surface Currents

**Mechanism:** Driven by persistent wind patterns (trade winds, westerlies), modified by Coriolis effect and continental boundaries

**Major Surface Currents:**

| Current | Ocean | Speed | Function | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gulf Stream | Atlantic | 2 m/s (~7 km/h) | Carries warm water northeast from Gulf of Mexico | Warms Western Europe by 5-10°C |
| Kuroshio | Pacific | 1-2 m/s | Western boundary current; flows northeast past Japan | Warms Japan; supports fisheries |
| Antarctic Circumpolar | Southern | 0.2-0.5 m/s | Only current circling globe; no land barriers | Isolates Antarctica; drives global circulation |
| California Current | Pacific | 0.2-0.4 m/s | Eastern boundary; cold water flows south | Cools US West Coast; supports fisheries |
| Humboldt (Peru) | Pacific | 0.3-0.6 m/s | Cold upwelling current off South America | World's richest fishery (anchovies); Atacama desert |

**Gyres:** Large circular current systems in ocean basins
- **5 major gyres:** North/South Atlantic, North/South Pacific, Indian Ocean
- **Subtropical gyres:** Clockwise (Northern Hem), counterclockwise (Southern Hem)
- **Centers:** Accumulate floating debris (Great Pacific Garbage Patch)
- **Western intensification:** Western boundary currents (Gulf Stream, Kuroshio) faster/narrower than eastern

**Coriolis Effect:** Earth's rotation deflects moving water
- Rightward in Northern Hemisphere, leftward in Southern
- Creates gyre rotation patterns
- Critical for western boundary current intensification

### Thermohaline Circulation (Global Conveyor Belt)

**Mechanism:** Density differences drive deep circulation; temperature and salinity control density

**The Loop (simplified):**
1. **North Atlantic:** Cold, salty water sinks near Greenland/Labrador Sea
2. **Deep Southward:** Flows south as North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW)
3. **Antarctic:** Mixes with Antarctic Bottom Water (coldest, densest)
4. **Northward in Indian/Pacific:** Slowly rises as it warms, freshens
5. **Surface Return:** Warm surface water flows westward through Indonesian Throughflow, around Africa, back to North Atlantic
6. **Timescale:** 1,000-1,600 years for complete circuit

**Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC):**
- Atlantic branch of thermohaline circulation
- Transports ~1.3 petawatts of heat northward (= 100× global energy consumption)
- Keeps Western Europe 5-10°C warmer than same latitudes elsewhere
- Weakening 15-20% since 1950; projected further decline
- **Risk:** AMOC collapse could crash European temperatures by 5-10°C within decades

**Why It Matters:**
- **Climate Regulation:** Distributes heat globally; regional climates depend on it
- **Carbon Storage:** Sinking water carries dissolved CO₂ to deep ocean (centuries of storage)
- **Nutrient Cycling:** Deep water accumulates nutrients; upwelling returns them to surface
- **Paleoclimate:** Rapid AMOC changes triggered past abrupt climate shifts (Younger Dryas 12,000 years ago)

### Upwelling

**Mechanism:** Wind-driven or topography-driven vertical transport of deep water to surface

**Types:**

**Coastal Upwelling:**
- Wind blows parallel to coast (e.g., California, Peru)
- Coriolis effect pushes surface water offshore
- Deep water rises to replace it
- Brings cold, nutrient-rich water → high productivity

**Equatorial Upwelling:**
- Trade winds drive surface water away from equator
- Deep water rises along equatorial zone
- Creates band of high productivity across Pacific

**Divergence Upwelling:**
- Antarctic Circumpolar Current: Diverging currents pull up deep water
- Southern Ocean extremely productive despite cold

**Why Upwelling Matters:**
- **Fisheries:** Upwelling zones = <1% of ocean area but ~50% of fish catch
- **Productivity:** Nutrients fuel phytoplankton → supports food web
- **Climate:** Cold upwelled water cools overlying air; affects regional weather
- **El Niño:** Suppressed upwelling during El Niño collapses Peruvian fisheries

**Major Upwelling Zones:**
- **Peru/Humboldt:** World's most productive fishery (anchovies)
- **California:** Supports sardines, salmon, diverse marine life
- **Canary (West Africa):** Sardines, horse mackerel
- **Benguela (South Africa):** Sardines, anchovies
- **Arabian Sea:** Monsoon-driven upwelling; seasonal productivity spike

### El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO)

**Normal Conditions (La Niña or Neutral):**
- Trade winds blow west across Pacific
- Warm surface water piles up in western Pacific (Indonesia, Philippines)
- Cold water upwells off Peru → cold, productive
- Indonesia gets heavy rain; Peru gets drought

**El Niño:**
- Trade winds weaken or reverse
- Warm water sloshes eastward across Pacific
- Suppresses upwelling off Peru → fishery collapses
- Peru gets heavy rain/floods; Indonesia/Australia get drought/fires
- Alters jet stream → global weather disruption

**Southern Oscillation:** Atmospheric pressure seesaw between eastern/western Pacific; coupled with ocean temperature

**ENSO Cycle:**
- Oscillates every 2-7 years
- Strong El Niños: 1982-83, 1997-98, 2015-16
- Impacts: Droughts in Australia/Indonesia/southern Africa; floods in Peru/California; hurricane patterns shift; coral bleaching; agricultural losses

**Teleconnections:** ENSO affects weather globally via atmospheric wave patterns
- North America: Warm/dry Southwest; cool/wet Southeast during El Niño
- South America: Floods in Peru; droughts in Brazil/Colombia
- Africa: East Africa floods; southern Africa droughts
- Asia: Indian monsoon weakens; Australian drought

**Predictability:** Can forecast 6-9 months ahead → disaster preparedness, agriculture planning

### Ocean Heat Transport

**Scale:** Oceans transport ~2 petawatts from tropics to poles (atmosphere transports ~3 PW)

**Mechanism:**
- Warm water flows poleward in surface currents
- Cold water returns equatorward in deep currents
- Heat released to atmosphere at high latitudes

**Regional Climate Impacts:**

| Region | Ocean Influence | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Western Europe | Gulf Stream/North Atlantic Drift | 5-10°C warmer than same latitude in North America |
| US East Coast | Gulf Stream proximity | Warmer winters; hurricanes intensify offshore |
| US West Coast | California Current (cold) | Cool summers; marine layer fog; stable atmosphere |
| Japan | Kuroshio Current (warm) | Milder winters than expected; typhoon intensification |
| UK/Ireland | North Atlantic Drift | Ice-free ports despite 50-60°N latitude |

**Without Ocean Heat Transport:**
- Tropics would be ~5°C warmer
- High latitudes would be ~20°C colder
- Larger temperature gradients → more violent storms

### Ocean-Atmosphere Interactions

**Heat Exchange:**
- **Evaporation:** Ocean loses heat; atmosphere gains moisture + latent heat
- **Sensible heat:** Direct conduction (small compared to evaporation)
- **Longwave radiation:** Ocean radiates infrared; absorbed by greenhouse gases
- Net effect: Tropics heat atmosphere; high latitudes cool slightly

**Moisture Source:**
- 86% of global evaporation from oceans
- Transported over land via atmospheric circulation
- Supports terrestrial precipitation
- Without oceans: Deserts everywhere except near large lakes

**Hurricane Formation:**
- Requires sea surface temp >26.5°C
- Evaporation provides energy (latent heat release)
- Coriolis effect provides rotation (can't form within 5° of equator)
- Ocean heat content determines intensity
- Warming oceans → more intense hurricanes (Cat 4-5 increasing)

**Monsoons:**
- Seasonal wind reversal driven by land-ocean temperature contrast
- Summer: Land heats faster → low pressure → draws moist ocean air inland → heavy rain
- Winter: Land cools faster → high pressure → dry air flows from land to ocean
- Asian monsoon affects 4 billion people; Indian agriculture depends on timing/intensity

## Real-World Examples

### Gulf Stream Slowdown

- **Observation:** AMOC weakened 15-20% since 1950 (proxy reconstructions + direct measurements)
- **Mechanism:** Greenland ice melt adds freshwater → reduces surface salinity → prevents sinking
- **Risks:**
  - European temperatures could drop 5-10°C if AMOC collapses
  - US East Coast sea level rise accelerated (weakened Gulf Stream = water backs up)
  - Shifts in hurricane tracks
  - Disruption of African/Indian monsoons
- **Tipping point:** Some models suggest collapse possible this century if emissions high
- **Paleoclimate analog:** Younger Dryas (12,900 years ago) — AMOC shutdown → Europe froze in decades

### 1997-98 El Niño

- Strongest El Niño on record (at the time; 2015-16 similar)
- **Impacts:**
  - Indonesia: Drought + fires; smoke pollution affected 70M people
  - Peru: Flooding; infrastructure damage; fisheries collapsed
  - East Africa: Flooding; cholera outbreaks
  - North America: Warm winter; reduced heating demand but increased tornado activity
  - Global: Warmest year on record (1998); coral bleaching events
- **Economic:** $35-45 billion in damages globally
- **Forecast:** Successfully predicted 6-9 months ahead → some disaster mitigation possible

### Humboldt Current & Peruvian Anchovy

- Cold upwelling off Peru = world's most productive fishery
- Anchovy catch: 8-10 million tonnes/year (15% of global wild catch)
- Supports fishmeal industry (aquaculture feed, fertilizer)
- During El Niño: Upwelling suppressed → warm water → anchovies move/die → fishery collapses
- 1972 El Niño + overfishing → population crashed; took decade to recover
- **Lesson:** Climate variability + overfishing = double jeopardy

### Antarctic Circumpolar Current

- Only current flowing unobstructed around planet
- Transports 130 Sverdrups (1 Sv = 1 million m³/s) — 100× all world's rivers combined
- Isolates Antarctica → keeps it cold → ice sheets stable
- Drives global thermohaline circulation
- Blocks warm water from reaching Antarctic ice shelves (mostly)
- **Risk:** Strengthening/shifting with climate change → uncertain impacts on ice sheet stability

## Climate Change Impacts

### Ocean Warming

- **Heat Content:** 0-2000m depth warmed by 0.075°C/decade (since 1960s); accelerating
- **Top 700m:** Absorbed 1.5×10²⁴ J since 1990 (= 5 Hiroshima bombs/second for 30 years)
- **Consequences:**
  - Thermal expansion → 40% of sea level rise
  - Stronger hurricanes (more energy available)
  - Coral bleaching (temperature stress)
  - Species range shifts (fish moving poleward)
  - Stratification increases → reduced mixing → nutrient depletion in surface

### Sea Level Rise

- **Current Rate:** 3.4 mm/year (2006-2015); accelerating from 1.4 mm/year (20th century avg)
- **Since 1900:** ~20 cm rise
- **Contributors:**
  - 40%: Thermal expansion
  - 30%: Glacier melt
  - 20%: Greenland ice sheet
  - 10%: Antarctica ice sheet
- **Projections:** 30-110 cm by 2100 (depends on emissions scenario)
- **Risks:** 1 billion people live <10m above sea level; coastal cities, islands, deltas vulnerable

### AMOC Weakening

- 15-20% decline since 1950
- Projected: 25-45% decline by 2100 (medium emissions); possible collapse (low probability but catastrophic)
- **Mechanisms:** Freshwater from Greenland melt; increased precipitation at high latitudes
- **Consequences:** European cooling; US East Coast sea level rise; monsoon shifts

### Ocean Acidification

- pH: 8.2 → 8.1 since 1750 (30% more acidic)
- Rate: 10× faster than any time in 66 million years
- Projected: pH 7.8 by 2100 (high emissions) = 150% more acidic than pre-industrial
- **Consequences:** Shell/skeleton formation harder for corals, mollusks, pteropods; food web disruption

## Key Terms

**Gyre:** Large circular ocean current system; 5 major subtropical gyres

**Western Boundary Current:** Fast, narrow current on western side of ocean basin (Gulf Stream, Kuroshio)

**Thermohaline Circulation:** Global deep ocean circulation driven by density differences (temp + salinity)

**AMOC (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation):** Atlantic branch of thermohaline circulation; transports heat northward

**Upwelling:** Vertical movement of deep, cold, nutrient-rich water to surface

**ENSO (El Niño Southern Oscillation):** Quasi-periodic climate pattern; oscillates every 2-7 years; major global weather driver

**Sverdrup:** Unit of ocean current transport; 1 Sv = 1 million m³/s

**Teleconnection:** Linkage between weather/climate patterns in distant regions

**Ekman Transport:** Net water movement perpendicular to wind direction (due to Coriolis); drives coastal upwelling

**Mixed Layer:** Surface layer homogenized by wind/waves; typically 50-200m deep

## Summary

Ocean currents are Earth's thermal regulation system, transporting more heat than atmosphere from tropics to poles. Surface currents driven by wind create gyres and western boundary currents like Gulf Stream, which warms Western Europe by 5-10°C. Thermohaline circulation operates on millennial timescales, with cold, salty water sinking at poles and returning via deep circulation. AMOC (Atlantic branch) is weakening 15-20% since 1950, risking abrupt climate change. Upwelling zones bring nutrient-rich deep water to surface, supporting 50% of global fish catch despite covering <1% of ocean area. ENSO cycles every 2-7 years, causing global weather disruptions from Peru floods to Australian droughts. Ocean warming is accelerating, driving 40% of sea level rise via thermal expansion, intensifying hurricanes, and bleaching corals. Understanding ocean circulation essential for predicting regional climate, managing fisheries, and preparing for climate change impacts.
