# Fisheries & Marine Resources

## Overview

Oceans provide 20% of animal protein for 3.3 billion people. Wild capture fisheries plateau at ~90M tonnes/year; aquaculture surpassed wild catch in 2013, now ~130M tonnes/year. Fisheries employ 60 million people directly, support 200+ million livelihoods. Yet 34% of stocks overfished, many iconic species collapsed. The challenge: feed growing population without destroying marine ecosystems.

---

## Key Concepts

### Global Fish Production

| Source | Production (M tonnes/year) | Trend |
|---|---|---|
| Wild capture (marine) | ~80M | Plateaued since 1990s |
| Wild capture (freshwater) | ~12M | Slowly growing |
| Aquaculture (marine) | ~55M | Rapid growth (+4-5%/year) |
| Aquaculture (freshwater) | ~75M | Rapid growth (+5-6%/year) |
| **Total** | **~220M** | Growing ~2-3%/year |

**Fish for Human Consumption:** ~160M tonnes/year (rest = fishmeal, fish oil)

**Protein Contribution:**
- Global average: 17% of animal protein from seafood
- Coastal developing countries: Often >50%
- Small island states: Often >70%
- Inland/wealthy countries: Often <10%

### Fishery Types

#### By Target Species

**Pelagic (Open Water):**
- **Small pelagics:** Anchovies, sardines, herring, mackerel (30% of catch)
- **Large pelagics:** Tuna, billfish, swordfish (high value)
- **Characteristics:** Schooling behavior; short-lived; population fluctuates with climate

**Demersal (Bottom-Dwelling):**
- Cod, haddock, flatfish (flounder, sole), rockfish
- Longer-lived; slower growth; more vulnerable to overfishing

**Invertebrates:**
- Shrimp/prawns (15% of value despite 5% of volume — most valuable fishery)
- Crabs, lobsters, squid, octopus, sea cucumbers
- Shellfish: Clams, mussels, oysters, scallops

#### By Method

**Purse Seining:**
- Encircles school with net; draws bottom closed like purse
- Efficient for tuna, sardines, anchovies
- Issue: Bycatch (dolphins caught with tuna; FADs attract juveniles)

**Trawling:**
- Drags net through water (midwater) or along bottom (bottom trawl)
- Catches cod, shrimp, flatfish
- **Bottom trawling:** Destroys seafloor habitats; like clearcutting forests

**Longlining:**
- Lines up to 100 km with thousands of baited hooks
- Targets tuna, swordfish, halibut
- Issue: High bycatch (sharks, turtles, seabirds)

**Gillnets:**
- Vertical curtain of net; fish swim into it, get tangled
- Cheap, effective
- Issue: "Ghost nets" continue fishing if lost; entangle whales, turtles

**Pole-and-Line:**
- Individual hooks; fishers throw bait, pull fish in
- Labor-intensive; selective (low bycatch)
- Premium market (certified sustainable)

**Traps/Pots:**
- Cages with bait; catches crabs, lobsters
- Selective; low habitat damage
- Issue: Lost traps continue "ghost fishing"

### Stock Assessment

**Goal:** Determine sustainable catch level — maximum that can be removed without depleting population

**Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY):**
- Largest catch that can be taken year after year without reducing stock size
- Typically ~30-50% of virgin biomass
- Theoretical ideal; rarely achieved in practice

**Key Metrics:**

| Metric | Definition | Status Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Biomass (B) | Total weight of fish in stock | B < 0.5 B_MSY = overfished |
| Fishing Mortality (F) | % of stock removed by fishing | F > F_MSY = overfishing |
| Recruitment | New fish entering fishable population | Poor recruitment = future collapse |
| Total Allowable Catch (TAC) | Max catch allowed in year | Set based on stock assessment |

**Stock Status (FAO 2020):**
- 34% overfished (fishing above sustainable level)
- 60% fully fished (at or near sustainable limit)
- 6% underfished (could catch more sustainably)

**Assessment Challenges:**
- Data-poor fisheries: 80% of stocks lack adequate data (mostly developing countries)
- Illegal/unreported catch: 20-30% of total; undermines management
- Ecosystem interactions: Predator-prey dynamics complicate single-species models
- Climate variability: Shifting baselines; species moving with warming

### Fisheries Collapses (Case Studies)

#### 1. Grand Banks Cod (Newfoundland)

- Historically one of richest fisheries; supported 500 years of fishing
- 1960s: Factory trawlers from Europe/Asia; catch soared to 800,000 tonnes/year
- 1992: Biomass <1% of historical; Canada declared moratorium
- 30+ years later: Still not recovered
- **Lessons:** Overfishing + poor management + ignored warnings = collapse; recovery slow/uncertain

#### 2. Peruvian Anchoveta

- World's largest single-species fishery (8-10M tonnes/year)
- Cold upwelling off Peru; nutrient-rich; supports massive anchovy schools
- 1972: El Niño + overfishing → population crashed from 20M tonnes to 2M tonnes
- Took decade to recover
- Modern management: Conservative quotas; ban during El Niño
- **Lessons:** Climate variability + overfishing = double jeopardy; adaptive management essential

#### 3. Bluefin Tuna (Atlantic/Mediterranean)

- Apex predator; highly valuable (single fish sold for $3.1M in Japan)
- 1960s-2000s: Overfishing + illegal catch → population dropped 85%
- 2010: Proposed CITES listing (failed); strengthened management instead
- Recent recovery: Biomass increasing under strict quotas
- **Lessons:** High value drives overfishing; international cooperation essential; recovery possible if managed

#### 4. Orange Roughy (New Zealand/Australia)

- Deep-sea fish; lives 100+ years; matures at 20-30 years; low reproduction
- 1970s-80s: New fishery; catch peaked quickly then collapsed
- Fish aggregations targeted → removed centuries of accumulated biomass in years
- Recovery impossible on human timescales (100+ years)
- **Lessons:** Deep-sea fish extremely vulnerable; short-term profits = long-term loss

### Aquaculture (Fish Farming)

**Growth:** Fastest-growing food sector; 130M tonnes/year (59% of fish consumption)

**Types:**

| System | Environment | Species | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Marine cages | Open ocean/coastal | Salmon, tuna, bream | High productivity; pollution/disease/escape risks |
| Freshwater ponds | Land-based | Tilapia, catfish, carp | Low-tech; widespread; water use issues |
| Recirculating systems | Land-based; closed | Salmon, barramundi | Low impact; high cost; energy-intensive |
| Shellfish | Coastal waters | Oysters, mussels, clams | Filter feeders; improve water quality; low impact |
| Seaweed | Coastal waters | Kelp, nori | Carbon sink; no feed needed; growing sector |

**Advantages:**
- Relieves pressure on wild stocks
- Predictable supply; year-round production
- Can grow low on food chain (tilapia, carp, shellfish)
- Economic development for coastal communities

**Challenges:**
- **Pollution:** Excess feed, feces degrade water quality; eutrophication
- **Disease:** High density → rapid spread; antibiotics overused
- **Escapes:** Farmed fish interbreed with wild; reduce genetic fitness
- **Feed:** Carnivorous fish (salmon, tuna) require fishmeal from wild-caught fish → pressure on forage species
- **Habitat destruction:** Mangrove clearing for shrimp ponds (30% of mangrove loss)
- **Social conflict:** Coastal communities displaced; traditional fishers lose access

**Innovations:**
- **Feed efficiency:** Insect-based, algae-based, synthetic proteins reduce fishmeal dependence
- **Offshore cages:** Deeper water; stronger currents dilute waste
- **Integrated multi-trophic aquaculture (IMTA):** Combine fish, shellfish, seaweed → shellfish/seaweed filter fish waste
- **Recirculating systems:** Closed loops; clean water; high cost but zero pollution

**Future:** Offshore expansion; lower trophic species (seaweed, shellfish, herbivorous fish); alternative feeds

## Fisheries Management

### Management Approaches

**Traditional (Single-Species):**
- Set TAC (Total Allowable Catch) based on stock assessment
- Allocate via quotas (individual, vessel, national)
- Enforce via monitoring, observers, inspections
- **Limitations:** Ignores ecosystem interactions, bycatch, habitat damage

**Ecosystem-Based Fisheries Management (EBFM):**
- Considers entire ecosystem: predator-prey, habitat, climate
- Protects forage fish (base of food web)
- Accounts for non-target species, bycatch
- Harder to implement; data-intensive

**Rights-Based Management:**
- **Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs):** Fishers own % of TAC; can trade
- **Catch shares:** Similar; align incentives for long-term stewardship
- **Evidence:** Reduces race to fish; fewer bycatch; improves profitability; stocks recover
- **Criticism:** Concentrates wealth; excludes small-scale fishers

**Community-Based Management:**
- Local communities set/enforce rules
- Territorial use rights (TURFs): Community controls fishing area
- **Success:** Pacific islands, Chile, Japan
- **Requirements:** Strong social cohesion, clear boundaries, external support

**Marine Protected Areas (MPAs):**
- No-take zones where fishing prohibited
- Biomass increases inside; spillover benefits adjacent areas
- ~8% of ocean in MPAs; <3% fully protected
- **Goal:** 30% by 2030 (UN biodiversity target)

### Enforcement Challenges

**Illegal, Unreported, Unregulated (IUU) Fishing:**
- 20-30% of global catch
- $10-23 billion/year
- Undermines management; depletes stocks; unfair competition

**Mechanisms:**
- Fishing in closed areas/seasons
- Using banned gear (driftnets, dynamite)
- Exceeding quotas
- Transshipment at sea (hides catch origin)
- Flag-of-convenience vessels (register in weak-regulation countries)

**Solutions:**
- Satellite monitoring (VMS: Vessel Monitoring Systems)
- Port state measures (inspect foreign vessels)
- Traceability (chain of custody from catch to plate)
- Regional Fisheries Management Organizations (RFMOs) for high seas

## Economic & Social Dimensions

**Employment:**
- 60 million directly employed in fisheries/aquaculture
- 200+ million livelihoods depend on fisheries (processing, distribution, support)
- 90% in small-scale fisheries (developing countries)

**Value:**
- First-sale value: ~$150 billion/year (wild capture)
- Retail value: ~$400 billion/year
- Shrimp = most valuable traded seafood (~$30 billion/year)

**Trade:**
- 38% of fish production traded internationally
- Flows: Developing countries export high-value (shrimp, tuna); import low-value (small pelagics, fishmeal)
- Net benefit to developing countries: ~$40 billion/year

**Food Security:**
- Critical for coastal developing countries (Bangladesh, Indonesia, Philippines)
- Small pelagics (sardines, anchovies) = cheap protein for poor
- Fishmeal/fish oil → aquaculture, poultry, pigs (competes with human consumption)

**Subsistence Fishing:**
- Small-scale, artisanal fishers feed families/local communities
- Not captured in statistics
- Vulnerable to competition from industrial fleets, coastal degradation

## Real-World Examples

### Iceland's ITQ System

- 1980s: Cod stocks collapsing; unrestricted access
- 1984: Introduced ITQs; capped total catch; allocated quotas to vessels
- **Results:** Stocks recovered; profitability increased; fleet consolidated
- **Criticism:** "Cod lords" — few companies own most quotas; small-scale fishers excluded
- **Lesson:** Rights-based management can work but equity matters

### Chilean Sea Bass (Patagonian Toothfish)

- Deep-sea fish; lives 50+ years; high value
- 1990s: Unregulated; IUU fishing decimated population
- 2000s: Strict management; catch documentation scheme; satellite tracking
- Certified sustainable in some fisheries
- **Lesson:** High-value species vulnerable; enforcement + traceability essential

### Hilsa (Bangladesh)

- National fish; supports 0.5M livelihoods
- Overfishing + habitat degradation → declining catch
- 2015: Banned fishing during spawning season; gear restrictions; protected areas
- **Results:** Catch doubled; fisher incomes increased
- **Success factors:** Government buy-in, alternative livelihoods during ban, enforcement

### Seafood Fraud

- 20-30% of seafood mislabeled globally
- Red snapper in US: 87% mislabeled (cheaper tilapia, rockfish substituted)
- Tuna: Species substitution (cheaper species labeled as expensive)
- **Drivers:** Complex supply chains; high-value species; weak traceability
- **Solutions:** DNA testing, blockchain traceability, port inspections

## Future Challenges

### Climate Change Impacts

- **Warming:** Fish moving poleward; tropical countries lose productivity
- **Acidification:** Shellfish, pteropods (forage base) threatened
- **Oxygen loss:** Expanding dead zones; habitat compression
- **Extreme events:** Marine heatwaves kill fish, kelp; disrupt recruitment

### Growing Demand

- Population: 10 billion by 2050
- Middle-class growth: More fish consumption (Asia)
- Need: 50% more fish by 2050

### Solutions

**Better Management:**
- Enforce quotas; end overfishing (could add 16M tonnes/year)
- Rebuild depleted stocks (could add 10M tonnes/year)
- Reduce bycatch/discards (10-20% of catch wasted)

**Aquaculture Expansion:**
- Grow lower trophic species (shellfish, seaweed, herbivorous fish)
- Improve feed efficiency; alternative proteins
- Offshore systems (less habitat impact)

**Reduce Waste:**
- 35% of fish lost/wasted post-catch
- Better cold chains, storage, processing

**Shift Diets:**
- Eat lower on food chain (anchovies, sardines, mussels vs tuna, salmon)
- Seaweed = high protein, zero feed inputs, carbon sink

## Key Terms

**Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY):** Largest catch possible without depleting stock

**Overfishing:** Fishing mortality above sustainable level; biomass declining

**Overfished:** Stock biomass below sustainable threshold

**Bycatch:** Non-target species caught incidentally; often discarded

**IUU Fishing:** Illegal, unreported, unregulated fishing; 20-30% of global catch

**Total Allowable Catch (TAC):** Annual catch limit for stock

**Individual Transferable Quota (ITQ):** Tradable share of TAC allocated to fisher/vessel

**Catch Share:** Percentage-based allocation of catch rights

**Marine Protected Area (MPA):** Zone with restricted human activity to conserve ecosystems

**Aquaculture:** Fish farming; freshwater or marine; 130M tonnes/year

## Summary

Fisheries provide 20% of animal protein for 3.3 billion people. Wild capture plateaued at ~90M tonnes/year since 1990s; 34% of stocks overfished. Iconic collapses (Grand Banks cod, Peruvian anchoveta) show overfishing + climate variability = high risk. Aquaculture surpassed wild catch in 2013, now 130M tonnes/year; relieves pressure on wild stocks but creates pollution, disease, habitat destruction challenges. Management approaches include quotas (TAC), rights-based systems (ITQs), ecosystem-based, community-based, and marine protected areas. IUU fishing (20-30% of catch) undermines management. Climate change threatens fisheries via warming (poleward shifts), acidification (shellfish impacts), and deoxygenation (expanding dead zones). Meeting 2050 demand requires better management (rebuild stocks), aquaculture expansion (lower trophic species), reduced waste (35% lost post-catch), and dietary shifts (eat lower on food chain). Understanding fisheries essential for food security, livelihoods, and ocean health.
