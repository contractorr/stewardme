# Ocean Physics & Chemistry

## Overview

Ocean properties — salinity, temperature, density, pressure — govern circulation, climate, and life. Water's unique molecular properties make liquid oceans possible. Understanding physical and chemical characteristics essential for predicting climate, managing resources, and comprehending marine ecosystems.

---

## Key Concepts

### Water's Unique Properties

**High Heat Capacity**
- Water requires 4,186 J to raise 1 kg by 1°C (vs 1,005 J for air)
- Oceans store 1,000× more heat than atmosphere despite similar mass
- Result: Oceans stabilize global climate; coastal regions have milder temperatures
- Practical: Ocean warming lags atmospheric warming by decades; thermal inertia

**High Latent Heat**
- Evaporation requires 2,260 kJ/kg (phase change without temp increase)
- Hurricanes extract this energy when water vapor condenses
- Drives massive heat transport from tropics to poles via atmosphere
- Practical: Evaporative cooling dominates ocean-atmosphere heat exchange

**Density Anomaly**
- Ice less dense than liquid water (unusual for solids)
- Ice floats → insulates water below → prevents ocean from freezing solid
- Without this: Life on Earth might not exist
- Practical: Icebergs, sea ice dynamics, polar ecosystems

**Solvent Properties**
- Polar molecule; dissolves salts, gases, nutrients
- Enables dissolved CO₂ uptake → ocean carbon sink
- Enables nutrient distribution → supports marine life
- Practical: Ocean chemistry complex; sensitive to pH changes

### Salinity

**Definition:** Concentration of dissolved salts in seawater; measured in practical salinity units (PSU) or parts per thousand (ppt)

**Average:** 35 PSU (3.5% dissolved salts)

**Composition (by mass):**
| Ion | % of Total Salts |
|---|---|
| Chloride (Cl⁻) | 55.0% |
| Sodium (Na⁺) | 30.6% |
| Sulfate (SO₄²⁻) | 7.7% |
| Magnesium (Mg²⁺) | 3.7% |
| Calcium (Ca²⁺) | 1.2% |
| Potassium (K⁺) | 1.1% |
| Others (trace) | 0.7% |

**Spatial Variation:**
- **Surface:** 32-37 PSU (affected by evaporation, precipitation, ice formation/melting, river input)
- **Highest salinity:** Red Sea (~41 PSU), Persian Gulf (~40 PSU) — high evaporation, restricted circulation
- **Lowest salinity:** Baltic Sea (~7 PSU), estuaries (<5 PSU) — freshwater input
- **Deep ocean:** Relatively uniform (~34.7 PSU); slow circulation mixes

**Why Salinity Matters:**
- **Density:** Higher salinity → higher density → affects vertical mixing, stratification
- **Freezing point:** Seawater freezes at ~-1.9°C (vs 0°C for freshwater)
- **Thermohaline circulation:** Sinking of cold, salty water at poles drives global conveyor belt
- **Marine life:** Organisms osmoregulate; salinity changes stress or kill
- **Corrosion:** Saltwater highly corrosive → challenges for ships, infrastructure

### Temperature

**Range:** -1.9°C (polar surface) to 30°C (tropical surface)

**Vertical Profile (Tropical Ocean):**
| Layer | Depth | Temperature | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mixed layer | 0-50m | ~28°C | Wind/waves mix; uniform temp |
| Thermocline | 50-1000m | 28°C → 4°C | Sharp temp drop; acts as barrier |
| Deep ocean | 1000m+ | ~2-4°C | Cold, stable; slowly circulating |

**Thermocline:** Sharp temperature gradient; separates warm surface from cold deep; inhibits vertical mixing; nutrient barrier.

**Seasonal Thermocline:** Mid-latitudes develop shallow thermocline in summer (heating); disappears in winter (cooling + storms mix water).

**Heat Content vs Temperature:**
- Ocean warming: Top 2000m gained 1.5×10²⁴ J since 1990
- Equivalent to: ~5 Hiroshima bombs/second for 30 years
- Small temp change in ocean = massive energy storage

**Why Temperature Matters:**
- **Climate:** Ocean heat capacity buffers atmospheric warming; heat release drives weather
- **Sea level:** Thermal expansion accounts for ~40% of recent rise
- **Ecosystems:** Coral bleaching at 1-2°C above normal; species range shifts
- **Hurricanes:** Require >26.5°C to form; warmer = more intense
- **Fisheries:** Temperature affects fish metabolism, distribution, breeding

### Pressure

**Formula:** Pressure increases ~1 atm per 10m depth

| Depth | Pressure | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Sea level | 1 atm | Standard atmospheric pressure |
| 100m | 11 atm | Scuba diving limit (recreational) |
| 500m | 51 atm | Sperm whale dive depth |
| 1000m | 101 atm | Abyssal zone begins |
| 4000m | 401 atm | Average ocean floor |
| 11,000m | 1,100 atm | Mariana Trench (Challenger Deep) |

**Engineering Challenges:**
- Submersibles require thick hulls; implosion risk
- Sensors, cameras must be pressure-rated
- Human diving: Direct exposure lethal beyond ~100m (nitrogen narcosis, oxygen toxicity, barotrauma)
- ROVs/AUVs: Specialized materials, electronics, seals

**Biological Adaptations:**
- Deep-sea organisms: Flexible bodies, no air spaces, pressure-stable proteins
- Lipid composition changes → membranes remain fluid
- Piezophiles (pressure-loving microbes) at hydrothermal vents

---

### Density

**Definition:** Mass per unit volume; typically 1020-1030 kg/m³ for seawater (vs 1000 kg/m³ for freshwater)

**Controlled by:** Temperature (decreases density), salinity (increases density), pressure (increases density slightly)

**Density Stratification:**
- Less dense water floats on denser water
- Thermocline/halocline = density gradients
- Prevents vertical mixing → nutrients trapped in deep ocean unless upwelling occurs

**Pycnocline:** Layer of rapidly changing density; inhibits mixing between surface and deep

**Practical:**
- Ships float higher in seawater than freshwater (higher density)
- Submarines adjust buoyancy via ballast tanks
- Ocean mixing critical for nutrient cycling, heat distribution

### Light in the Ocean

**Absorption:** Water absorbs light; red wavelengths first, blue last

| Depth | Light Penetration | Color | Photosynthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-10m | 100-50% | Full spectrum | High |
| 10-100m | 50-1% | Blue-green | Moderate |
| 100-200m | 1-0.01% | Deep blue | Minimal (euphotic limit) |
| 200-1000m | <0.01% | Twilight | None (mesopelagic) |
| 1000m+ | None | Pitch black | None (aphotic) |

**Photic Zone (Euphotic):** 0-200m; where photosynthesis possible; contains 90%+ of marine life

**Turbidity:** Suspended particles (sediment, plankton) reduce clarity; affects light penetration

**Bioluminescence:** 90% of deep-sea organisms produce light; communication, camouflage, hunting

**Why Light Matters:**
- Photosynthesis confined to surface → primary production in thin layer
- Red fish appear black at depth (no red light to reflect)
- Camera/vision systems limited; sonar/acoustics essential for deep mapping

### Sound in the Ocean

**Speed:** ~1,500 m/s (vs 343 m/s in air); increases with temp, salinity, pressure

**Why Faster in Water:**
- Water denser, less compressible → sound waves propagate more efficiently
- Long-range communication possible (whale songs travel 1000+ km)

**SOFAR Channel (Sound Fixing and Ranging):**
- Depth ~1000m where sound speed minimized
- Sound waves trapped, refract back toward channel → minimal attenuation
- Used by whales for long-distance communication
- Military submarines use for covert communication

**Acoustic Applications:**
- **Sonar (echo location):** Ship navigation, submarine detection, fish finding
- **Seismic surveys:** Map seafloor geology for oil/gas, tectonic structure
- **Ocean tomography:** Measure temperature, currents via sound speed changes
- **Wildlife tracking:** Tag marine mammals, fish migration

**Noise Pollution:**
- Shipping, seismic surveys, naval sonar disrupt marine life
- Whale strandings linked to military sonar
- Masking of biological signals → affects communication, hunting

### Ocean Chemistry

**pH:** 8.1 (slightly alkaline); was 8.2 pre-industrial
- Dropped 0.1 units = 30% increase in acidity (pH is logarithmic)
- Fastest change in 66 million years
- Driven by dissolved CO₂ forming carbonic acid

**Ocean Acidification Mechanism:**
```
CO₂ (atm) → CO₂ (dissolved)
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate)
```
- Increased H⁺ = lower pH = more acidic
- Reduced carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) → harder for organisms to build shells/skeletons

**Carbonate System:** Ocean's chemical buffering system
- Absorbs ~25% of anthropogenic CO₂
- Slows atmospheric warming but acidifies ocean
- Buffering capacity decreasing → future CO₂ absorption slower

**Dissolved Oxygen:**
- Surface: ~6-8 mg/L (via air exchange, photosynthesis)
- Oxygen minimum zones (OMZ): 200-1000m depth; low O₂ due to decomposition, poor ventilation
- Expanding OMZs: Warming reduces O₂ solubility + increases stratification
- Dead zones: Hypoxic areas (O₂ <2 mg/L); driven by nutrient pollution, algal blooms

**Nutrients:** Essential for life
- Nitrate (NO₃⁻), phosphate (PO₄³⁻), silicate (SiO₄⁴⁻), iron (Fe²⁺)
- Surface waters nutrient-depleted (consumed by phytoplankton)
- Deep waters nutrient-rich (from decomposition of sinking organic matter)
- Upwelling brings nutrients to surface → high productivity

## Real-World Examples

### Dead Zones: Gulf of Mexico

- Mississippi River drains 40% of US; delivers fertilizer runoff (nitrogen, phosphorus)
- Nutrients fuel algal blooms → algae die → decomposition consumes oxygen
- Hypoxic zone: 15,000-20,000 km² each summer (size of New Jersey)
- Fish/shrimp flee or die; economic impacts on fisheries

### Hydrothermal Vents

- Discovered 1977 near Galápagos; revolutionized understanding of life's limits
- Superheated water (400°C) jets from seafloor; rich in minerals, sulfur compounds
- Chemosynthetic bacteria convert chemicals to energy (no sunlight needed)
- Support unique ecosystems: tube worms, giant clams, eyeless shrimp
- Demonstrate life possible without photosynthesis → implications for astrobiology

### Great Barrier Reef Bleaching

- Coral = symbiosis between polyp and zooxanthellae (photosynthetic algae)
- 1-2°C warming → coral expels algae → turns white (bleaching)
- Without algae, coral starves; prolonged bleaching = death
- 2016, 2017, 2020 mass bleaching events → 50% of reef dead or damaged
- Driven by ocean warming; acidification makes recovery harder

### Mediterranean Salinity Crisis

- 5.96 million years ago, strait of Gibraltar closed
- Mediterranean evaporated (nearly); deposited salt layers km thick
- Salinity reached >150 PSU before reopening
- Demonstrates: Ocean basins can change radically; salt deposits reveal past

## Key Terms

**Salinity:** Concentration of dissolved salts; average ~35 PSU

**Thermocline:** Layer of rapid temperature decrease with depth

**Halocline:** Layer of rapid salinity change with depth

**Pycnocline:** Layer of rapid density change with depth

**Photic Zone:** Surface layer where sunlight enables photosynthesis (0-200m)

**Aphotic Zone:** Lightless deep ocean (>200m)

**SOFAR Channel:** Depth where sound speed minimized; traps sound waves

**Ocean Acidification:** Decrease in pH from dissolved CO₂

**Upwelling:** Vertical movement of deep, cold, nutrient-rich water to surface

**Hypoxia:** Low oxygen (<2 mg/L); creates "dead zones"

---

## Summary

Ocean physics and chemistry govern circulation, climate, and life. Water's high heat capacity makes oceans Earth's climate stabilizer, absorbing 90% of warming energy. Salinity and temperature control density, which drives thermohaline circulation. Pressure increases ~1 atm per 10m, requiring specialized engineering for deep exploration. Light penetrates only the top 200m, confining photosynthesis and most life to surface. Sound travels 4× faster than in air, enabling long-range communication for marine mammals and sonar applications. Ocean chemistry is changing rapidly: pH dropped 0.1 units (30% more acidic) from absorbed CO₂, threatening shell-forming organisms. Dissolved oxygen declining in expanding dead zones from warming and nutrient pollution. Understanding these physical and chemical properties essential for predicting climate change impacts, managing fisheries, and engineering ocean technologies.
