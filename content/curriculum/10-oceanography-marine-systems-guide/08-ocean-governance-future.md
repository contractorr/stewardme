# Ocean Governance & Future Challenges

## Overview

62% of ocean is "high seas" — beyond national jurisdiction. Governance gaps enable overfishing, IUU fishing, plastic pollution, unregulated deep-sea mining. UN Convention on Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982) is "constitution for oceans" but enforcement weak. Climate change accelerating: warming, acidification, deoxygenation, sea level rise. Ocean health declining but not yet irreversible. The challenge: coordinate 195 countries to manage shared global commons.

---

## Key Concepts

### Maritime Zones (UNCLOS)

| Zone | Distance from Coast | Sovereign Rights | Access |
|---|---|---|---|
| Internal Waters | Landward of baseline | Full sovereignty | Coastal state controls |
| Territorial Sea | 0-12 nautical miles | Full sovereignty (with innocent passage) | Ships can transit |
| Contiguous Zone | 12-24 nm | Customs, immigration, sanitation enforcement | Open to all |
| Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) | 0-200 nm | Rights to resources (fish, oil, minerals) | Open to navigation |
| Continental Shelf | Variable (200-350 nm) | Rights to seabed resources | Coastal state extracts minerals |
| High Seas | Beyond EEZ | No state sovereignty | Freedom of navigation, fishing, research |
| The Area | Seafloor beyond jurisdiction | "Common heritage of mankind" | Managed by ISA |

**EEZ Coverage:**
- 38% of ocean surface
- Contains 90%+ of fisheries
- Contains 95%+ of known oil/gas

**High Seas:**
- 62% of ocean surface
- 10% of fish catch (but includes high-value tuna, squid)
- Legally complex; governance weak

---

### UN Convention on Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)

**Adopted:** 1982; entered force 1994

**Signatories:** 168 countries (notably NOT USA — signed but not ratified by Senate)

**Key Provisions:**
- Established maritime zones (territorial sea, EEZ, high seas)
- Freedom of navigation (ships can transit EEZs)
- Innocent passage (ships can transit territorial seas if not threatening)
- Archipelagic waters (island nations can draw boundaries around island groups)
- Dispute resolution (International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea)

**Gaps:**
- **Enforcement:** Countries must enforce within EEZs; many lack capacity
- **High seas fisheries:** Open to all; tragedy of the commons
- **Deep-sea mining:** "Area" managed by ISA but rules incomplete
- **Climate change:** Not addressed (pre-climate awareness)
- **Plastic pollution:** Not addressed

**Why USA Not Ratified:**
- Senate objections to ISA (seabed mining authority); seen as ceding sovereignty
- Navy supports (freedom of navigation benefits US interests)
- US follows UNCLOS customarily but not legally bound

---

### International Seabed Authority (ISA)

**Created:** 1994 (UNCLOS)

**Role:** Regulate mineral extraction in "the Area" (seafloor beyond national jurisdiction)

**Headquarters:** Jamaica

**Members:** 168 countries

**Mandate:**
- Issue exploration licenses (30 issued for polymetallic nodules)
- Develop mining code (environmental standards, benefit-sharing)
- Protect marine environment

**Status (2024):**
- Exploration licenses issued; no commercial extraction yet
- Mining code incomplete; environmental regs weak
- Nauru triggered "two-year rule" (2021) → ISA must finalize regs or allow mining
- Scientists call for moratorium; ISA under pressure

**Criticism:**
- **Capture:** Mining companies influence ISA; weak environmental protections
- **Rush to mine:** Two-year rule forces premature decision
- **Benefit-sharing unclear:** How will profits be distributed? (developing countries should benefit per UNCLOS)

---

### Regional Fisheries Management Organizations (RFMOs)

**Purpose:** Manage fish stocks in high seas or straddling stocks (cross EEZ boundaries)

**Examples:**

| RFMO | Region | Target Species | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| ICCAT | Atlantic | Tuna, swordfish | Mixed; bluefin recovery but yellowfin overfished |
| WCPFC | Western/Central Pacific | Tuna, billfish | Weak; overfishing continues |
| NAFO | Northwest Atlantic | Cod, flatfish | Improving after collapse |
| CCAMLR | Antarctic | Krill, toothfish | Strong; ecosystem-based management |
| NEAFC | Northeast Atlantic | Redfish, blue whiting | Improving |

**Challenges:**
- **Consensus required:** All members must agree; one country can block
- **Weak enforcement:** Relies on flag states (often flags of convenience)
- **IUU fishing:** 20-30% of catch; undermines quotas
- **Non-members:** Countries can fish outside RFMOs if not members
- **Bycatch/ecosystem:** RFMOs slow to adopt ecosystem approach

**Success Story:** CCAMLR (Antarctica)
- Ecosystem-based: Protects krill (keystone species for whales, penguins)
- Marine protected areas: Ross Sea MPA (1.6M km², 2016)
- Precautionary: Conservative quotas; adapts to climate change

---

### Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)

**Definition:** Ocean zones where human activity restricted to conserve ecosystems

**Types:**

| Type | Protection Level | Activities Allowed |
|---|---|---|
| No-take reserve | Highest | None; total protection |
| Multiple-use | Moderate | Sustainable fishing, tourism, research |
| Marine park | Variable | Zoning; some areas protected, others open |
| Particularly Sensitive Sea Area (PSSA) | Low | Navigation restricted; pollution controls |

**Coverage:**
- 8% of ocean in MPAs (2024)
- <3% fully protected (no-take)
- **Goal:** 30% by 2030 (UN biodiversity target); 10% fully protected

**Effectiveness:**
- **Inside MPAs:** Biomass increases 2-10× after 5-10 years; larger fish; more species
- **Spillover:** Adults/larvae export to adjacent areas → benefits fisheries
- **Resilience:** Protects ecosystems from overfishing; aids climate resilience
- **Paper parks:** Some MPAs lack enforcement; fishing continues illegally

**Examples:**

**Papahānaumokuākea (Hawaii):**
- 1.5M km² (larger than all US national parks combined)
- No-take; protects coral reefs, seabirds, monk seals, green turtles
- World Heritage Site

**Great Barrier Reef Marine Park (Australia):**
- 344,000 km²
- Zoned: 33% no-take; rest allows sustainable use
- Tourism generates $6 billion/year; balancing protection vs economics

**Ross Sea MPA (Antarctica):**
- 1.6M km²; 70% no-take
- Protects krill, penguins, seals, toothfish
- CCAMLR achievement; first high-seas MPA

**Chagos Marine Reserve (Indian Ocean):**
- 640,000 km²; no-take
- UK designated 2010; legal dispute with Mauritius over sovereignty
- Demonstrates: MPAs entangled with geopolitics

**High Seas MPAs:**
- Currently <1% of high seas protected
- New treaty (BBNJ, 2023) enables high-seas MPAs; entering force soon
- Potential: Protect migratory routes, seamounts, hydrothermal vents

---

### High Seas Biodiversity Treaty (BBNJ)

**Official Name:** Agreement on Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction

**Adopted:** 2023; entering force 2025

**Purpose:** Fill governance gap in high seas (62% of ocean)

**Key Provisions:**
- **Marine Protected Areas:** Process to designate MPAs in high seas
- **Environmental Impact Assessments:** Required for activities (fishing, mining, shipping)
- **Marine Genetic Resources:** Benefit-sharing from discoveries (pharmaceuticals, biotech)
- **Capacity-building:** Support developing countries

**Significance:**
- First new ocean treaty in 40 years (since UNCLOS)
- Enables 30×30 target (30% protected by 2030)
- Addresses climate, biodiversity, equity

**Challenges:**
- Slow ratification; needs 60 countries (15 as of 2024)
- Implementation uncertain; funding unclear
- Overlap with RFMOs, ISA; coordination needed

## Climate Change Impacts (Detailed)

### 1. Ocean Warming

**Status:**
- 0-2000m: +0.075°C/decade (1960-2023)
- Top 700m: Absorbed 1.5×10²⁴ J since 1990 (= 5 Hiroshima bombs/second for 30 years)
- Accelerating: 90% of warming energy goes into ocean

**Impacts:**
- **Thermal expansion:** 40% of sea level rise
- **Stratification:** Warmer surface water less dense → reduced mixing → nutrient depletion → productivity decline
- **Species shifts:** Fish moving poleward (10-100 km/decade); tropics losing productivity
- **Coral bleaching:** 1-2°C above normal = bleaching; 4-6 week exposure = death; 50% of reefs bleached since 1980s
- **Marine heatwaves:** Increasing frequency/intensity; 2014-16 Pacific "blob" killed kelp; 2020 Siberia heat melted sea ice

**Tipping Points:**
- **Coral reefs:** 1.5°C warming = 70-90% loss; 2°C = 99%+ loss (IPCC)
- **AMOC collapse:** 15-20% weakened; could collapse this century (low probability but catastrophic)
- **Arctic ice-free summer:** Projected 2040s-2050s; albedo feedback accelerates warming

---

### 2. Ocean Acidification

**Status:**
- pH: 8.2 (pre-industrial) → 8.1 (2024) = 30% more acidic
- Projected: pH 7.8 by 2100 (high emissions) = 150% more acidic
- Rate: 10× faster than any time in 66 million years

**Mechanism:**
```
CO₂ (atm) → CO₂ (dissolved)
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃
H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
Result: Lower pH, less CO₃²⁻ (carbonate)
```

**Impacts:**
- **Shellfish:** Harder to build calcium carbonate shells (oysters, clams, mussels)
- **Corals:** Skeleton formation harder; combined with warming = double stress
- **Pteropods:** "Sea butterflies"; key zooplankton with fragile shells; food for salmon, whales; dissolving in some areas
- **Food web disruption:** Base of food web (pteropods, forams) threatened → cascades up
- **Economic:** Oyster aquaculture failures (Pacific Northwest US); $100M+ losses

**Uncertain Impacts:**
- Fish behavior: Impaired sensory systems (smell, hearing); affects predator avoidance, homing
- Seagrass, algae: May benefit (more CO₂ for photosynthesis) but uncertain

---

### 3. Deoxygenation

**Status:**
- Ocean oxygen declined 2% since 1960 (0-1000m)
- Oxygen minimum zones (OMZs) expanding
- Dead zones: 400+ coastal areas; 245,000 km² (size of UK)

**Mechanisms:**
- **Warming:** Reduces O₂ solubility; less dissolved oxygen in warm water
- **Stratification:** Reduced mixing → surface/deep don't exchange oxygen
- **Eutrophication:** Nutrient pollution → algal blooms → decomposition consumes O₂

**Impacts:**
- **Habitat compression:** Fish confined to surface; increased predation, fishing vulnerability
- **Dead zones:** Hypoxia (O₂ <2 mg/L); fish/shrimp flee or die; economic losses
- **Species shifts:** Low-oxygen tolerant species (jellyfish, microbes) increase
- **Mass mortality:** Sudden upwelling of hypoxic water kills marine life

**Worst Cases:**
- Gulf of Mexico: 15,000-20,000 km² each summer (Mississippi River nutrient runoff)
- Baltic Sea: 70,000 km² permanent dead zone
- East China Sea: Expanding; threatens fisheries

---

### 4. Sea Level Rise

**Status:**
- Current rate: 3.4 mm/year (2006-2015); accelerating from 1.4 mm/year (20th century)
- Since 1900: ~20 cm rise
- Projected: 30-110 cm by 2100 (depends on emissions; IPCC 2021)

**Contributors:**
- 40%: Thermal expansion
- 30%: Glacier melt
- 20%: Greenland ice sheet
- 10%: Antarctica ice sheet

**Impacts:**
- **Coastal flooding:** 1 billion people live <10m above sea level
- **Saltwater intrusion:** Contaminates freshwater aquifers; threatens agriculture
- **Erosion:** Beaches, cliffs disappearing; property loss
- **Ecosystem loss:** Mangroves, salt marshes drowned if landward migration blocked
- **Economic:** $1 trillion+ in coastal property at risk by 2050; insurance crisis

**Hotspots:**
- **Small island states:** Maldives, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands (existential threat)
- **Deltas:** Ganges-Brahmaputra (Bangladesh), Mekong (Vietnam), Nile (Egypt) — densely populated, subsiding land
- **Coastal megacities:** Miami, New York, Shanghai, Jakarta, Lagos

**Wild Cards:**
- **Ice sheet collapse:** West Antarctic Ice Sheet vulnerable; could add 3-5m (centuries)
- **Greenland melt:** Could add 7m (millennia)
- **Marine ice cliff instability:** Uncertain mechanism; could accelerate dramatically

---

### 5. Extreme Events

**Marine Heatwaves:**
- Days with extreme sea temp; increasing 50% since 1980s
- 2014-16 Pacific blob: 3-6°C above normal; killed kelp, seabirds, mammals; shifted fisheries
- 2019-20 Australia: 6-8°C above normal; bleached Great Barrier Reef
- Projected: 10-100× more frequent by 2100

**Hurricanes:**
- Warmer ocean → more intense (higher winds, more rainfall)
- Category 4-5 hurricanes increasing
- Rapid intensification more common (Katrina, Maria, Ida)
- Projected: Fewer total but more intense

**Compound Events:**
- Heatwave + acidification + deoxygenation = triple stress; mass mortality
- Example: 2019 Gulf of Alaska; seabirds, fish die-offs

## Pollution Challenges

### 1. Plastic

**Scale:**
- 8 million tonnes/year enter ocean
- 150 million tonnes currently in ocean
- Projected: 250 million tonnes by 2050 (BAU)

**Sources:**
- Rivers: 80% from land (10 rivers contribute 90%)
- Fishing gear: 10-20% (nets, lines, buoys)
- Litter: Bottles, bags, packaging

**Impacts:**
- **Entanglement:** Seals, turtles, whales die in ghost nets
- **Ingestion:** 90% of seabirds, 50% of turtles have plastic in gut; starvation, toxins
- **Microplastics:** <5mm particles; in all marine life; human food chain; health impacts unknown
- **Habitat:** Smothers coral reefs, seagrass
- **Transport:** Vectors for invasive species, pathogens

**Gyres:**
- Great Pacific Garbage Patch: 1.6M km² (3× France); 80,000 tonnes plastic
- 5 major gyres accumulate plastic

**Solutions:**
- **Reduce:** Single-use plastic bans; redesign packaging
- **Capture:** River interception (Ocean Cleanup Interceptors)
- **Cleanup:** Controversial (energy-intensive, ineffective vs reduction)
- **Treaty:** Global Plastics Treaty negotiating (2024); aim for 2025

---

### 2. Chemical Pollution

**Types:**
- **Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs):** DDT, PCBs; banned but persist; bioaccumulate
- **Heavy metals:** Mercury, lead, cadmium; biomagnify; neurotoxins
- **Oil:** Spills (rare but severe) + chronic leakage (shipping, runoff)
- **Pharmaceuticals:** Antibiotics, hormones; endocrine disruptors
- **Microplastics:** Contain additives (plasticizers, flame retardants); release toxins

**Bioaccumulation:**
- Concentrates up food chain
- Top predators (tuna, swordfish, orcas) have highest levels
- Humans exposed via seafood

**Examples:**
- **Minamata disease (Japan, 1950s):** Mercury poisoning from industrial discharge; 1,000s affected
- **Orcas (Salish Sea):** Highest PCB levels of any marine mammal; suppressing reproduction

---

### 3. Nutrient Pollution (Eutrophication)

**Sources:**
- Agricultural runoff (fertilizer: nitrogen, phosphorus)
- Sewage (untreated/poorly treated)
- Atmospheric deposition (burning fossil fuels)

**Mechanism:**
1. Excess nutrients → algal blooms
2. Algae die → decomposition consumes oxygen
3. Hypoxia (dead zones)

**Scale:**
- 400+ coastal dead zones
- 245,000 km² hypoxic area globally

**Solutions:**
- Reduce fertilizer use (precision agriculture)
- Buffer strips (vegetation between fields and water)
- Sewage treatment upgrades
- Wetland restoration (filters nutrients)

---

### 4. Noise Pollution

**Sources:**
- Shipping (engine, propeller noise)
- Seismic surveys (airguns; 250+ dB)
- Military sonar (active; 200+ dB)
- Pile-driving (offshore wind construction)

**Impacts:**
- **Whales:** Communication masked; behavioral changes; strandings (sonar-related)
- **Fish:** Hearing damage; stress; reduced reproduction
- **Invertebrates:** Larvae disoriented; reduced settlement

**Solutions:**
- Quieter propellers (expensive)
- Speed restrictions (slower = quieter)
- Temporal/spatial restrictions (avoid sensitive areas/seasons)

## Geopolitical Tensions

### South China Sea

**Disputes:** China, Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, Taiwan claim overlapping EEZs

**China's Position:**
- "Nine-dash line" claims 90% of South China Sea (based on historical maps)
- Built artificial islands on reefs; militarized
- **Spratly Islands, Paracel Islands:** Occupied, fortified

**Stakes:**
- Fisheries: $100 billion/year catch
- Oil/gas: Potential reserves (uncertain)
- Shipping: $3.4 trillion/year trade passes through

**Legal:**
- 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling: China's claims lack legal basis (UNCLOS)
- China rejected ruling

**Tensions:**
- US Navy "freedom of navigation" operations
- Coast guard standoffs (China-Philippines)
- Risk of miscalculation → conflict

---

### Arctic

**Changes:**
- Ice extent: -13% per decade (summer)
- Northwest Passage, Northern Sea Route opening

**Stakes:**
- Resources: 13% of undiscovered oil, 30% of gas (USGS)
- Shipping: 40% shorter Asia-Europe route
- Fisheries: Opening as ice retreats

**Claimants:**
- Arctic 8: Canada, Denmark (Greenland), Finland, Iceland, Norway, Russia, Sweden, USA
- China: Self-declared "near-Arctic state"; builds icebreakers; invests in infrastructure

**Disputes:**
- Northwest Passage: Canada claims as internal waters; US/EU claim as international strait
- Lomonosov Ridge: Russia, Canada, Denmark claim continental shelf extension
- Svalbard: Norway sovereign but treaty requires access for all signatories; fishing disputes

**Militarization:**
- Russia: Reopening Cold War bases; largest icebreaker fleet
- US: Increasing presence; new icebreaker construction (behind Russia)
- NATO: Exercises increasing

---

### Maritime Chokepoint Vulnerability

**Hormuz (21% of oil):**
- Iran periodically threatens closure
- US Navy maintains carrier group nearby
- Closure would spike oil prices globally (2019 tanker attacks, 2022 tensions)

**Malacca (25% of trade):**
- China's "Malacca dilemma": Energy imports vulnerable
- Response: Overland pipelines (Myanmar, Pakistan), Arctic routes, strategic ports

**Suez (12% of trade):**
- 2021 Ever Given blockage: $10 billion/day delayed
- Egypt's revenue source; political leverage

**Risk:** Chokepoints = single points of failure; no alternatives for most traffic

## Future Scenarios

### Blue Economy Growth

**Definition:** Sustainable ocean-based economic activity

**Sectors:**
- Fisheries/aquaculture: $240 billion/year
- Shipping: $380 billion/year
- Tourism: $390 billion/year
- Offshore wind: $1 trillion investment (2020-2030)
- Marine biotech: $6 billion/year (growing 5-10%/year)
- Deep-sea mining: 0 currently; $10-30 billion/year potential

**Potential:** $3 trillion/year by 2030 (OECD)

**Sustainability Challenge:** Growth vs conservation — can "blue economy" be truly sustainable?

---

### Ocean-Based Climate Solutions

**Blue Carbon:**
- Mangroves, seagrass, salt marshes store 3-5× more carbon per area than forests
- Restoration: 50 million hectares proposed
- Potential: 1 Gt CO₂/year sequestration

**Seaweed Aquaculture:**
- Fast growth; no feed/fertilizer; absorbs CO₂
- Sink to deep ocean (controversial; uncertain permanence)
- Potential: 0.5-1 Gt CO₂/year

**Alkalinity Enhancement:**
- Add crushed silicate rocks to ocean → neutralizes acidity; absorbs CO₂
- Potential: Gigaton-scale
- Risks: Uncertain ecosystem impacts; expensive

**Marine Cloud Brightening:**
- Spray seawater into clouds → increases reflectivity → cools ocean
- Geoengineering; highly controversial
- Risks: Unintended consequences; governance nightmare

---

### 30×30 Target

**Goal:** Protect 30% of ocean by 2030 (UN biodiversity target)

**Status:** 8% currently; <3% fully protected

**Pathway:**
- Expand EEZ MPAs (easier; within national jurisdiction)
- High-seas MPAs (harder; requires BBNJ treaty implementation)
- Priorities: Migration corridors, breeding grounds, biodiversity hotspots, carbon stores

**Challenges:**
- Enforcement (many MPAs "paper parks")
- Funding ($20-30 billion/year needed)
- Equity (displace small-scale fishers?)
- High seas (governance weak; compliance voluntary)

## Key Terms

**UNCLOS:** UN Convention on Law of the Sea; "constitution for oceans"

**EEZ (Exclusive Economic Zone):** 0-200 nm from coast; coastal state has resource rights

**High Seas:** Ocean beyond EEZs; no state sovereignty; 62% of ocean

**ISA (International Seabed Authority):** Regulates deep-sea mining in international waters

**RFMO (Regional Fisheries Management Organization):** Manages fish stocks in high seas

**MPA (Marine Protected Area):** Zone with restricted activity to conserve ecosystems

**BBNJ Treaty:** Agreement on Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction; enables high-seas MPAs

**Blue Carbon:** Carbon stored in coastal ecosystems (mangroves, seagrass, salt marshes)

**30×30:** Goal to protect 30% of ocean by 2030

**AMOC:** Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation; transports heat northward

## Summary

UNCLOS (1982) established maritime zones: EEZs (38% of ocean, 90%+ fisheries), high seas (62%, governance weak). ISA regulates deep-sea mining in "the Area"; mining code incomplete; scientists call for moratorium. RFMOs manage high-seas fisheries but enforcement weak; IUU fishing (20-30% of catch) undermines management. MPAs cover 8% of ocean (<3% fully protected); 30×30 target requires BBNJ treaty implementation (adopted 2023). Climate change accelerating: warming (0.075°C/decade in 0-2000m), acidification (pH 8.2→8.1, 30% more acidic), deoxygenation (2% O₂ loss, 400+ dead zones), sea level rise (3.4 mm/year, accelerating). Pollution: 8M tonnes plastic/year, 150M tonnes in ocean; microplastics in all marine life; eutrophication creates dead zones. Geopolitical tensions: South China Sea disputes (9-dash line), Arctic militarization (ice retreat opens resources/routes), chokepoint vulnerability (Hormuz, Malacca, Suez). Future: Blue economy growing ($3T/year by 2030); ocean-based climate solutions (blue carbon, seaweed); 30×30 requires funding, enforcement, equity. Understanding ocean governance essential for sustainable use, conservation, and climate resilience.
