# Accounting  Crash Course
## What is Accounting?
Accounting is a standardised set of rules to assess a company's financial performance. Used by internal officers, investors, lenders and general public. It's useful for making corporate and investment decisions because it creates a standardised mechanism enabling comparison across different firms.

Accounting is used by:
- Government
- Non-profit
- For-profit
  - Sole Proprietorship
  - Partnership
  - Corporation
    - Private
    - Public

In the US, the SEC authorises the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) to determine the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP). GAAP is composed of individual Statements of Financial Accounting Standards (SFAS), issued by the FASB. These are the standardised set of rules used by US firms. 

SFAS are constantly evolving. In addition to SFAS, the FASB also pronounces:
- Interpretations: modifications or extensions to existing SFAS
- Financial Accounting Concepts: Objects for establishment of future SFAS
- Emerging Insues Task Force Statements: Identifying new financial transaction types and establishing reporting practices for them.

Most of the rest of the world uses International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). US GAAP is very similar to IFRS. 

## Basic Accounting Principles 
### Assumptions
- Accounting entity: A company is a separate 'living' engerprise spearate from its' owners. It has:
  - A name
  - Incorporation date (birth date)
  - Incorporation place (birth place)
  - Engaged in clearly-defined activities
  - Regularly reports financial reports 
  - Pays taxes
  - Files lawsuits 
- Going Concern: This is when we assume the company will exist indefinitely
  - This allows us to assume the company will not need to sell assets at liquidation or fire sale prices. How we value and price it's assets and liabilities is therefore different to whether the company was in distress
- Measurement: Financial statements must be reported in the national monetary unit 
  - A consequences it that financial statements can only show measurable activities (money, taxes). They exclude things like employee satisfaction, customer loyalty, etc.
- Periodicity: Companies must report quarterly and annual financial reports
  - Fiscal year can vary from company to company. Many stick with the calendar year.

### Major underlying accounting principles
1. Historical Cost: Resources are reported at book value instead of fair value. E.g. If a company bought land at $1m 10 years ago that is worth $100m today, it's book value is $1m but it's fair value is $100m. The financial statement will report $1m. 
     - This is easier, since it does not require re-evaluation/appraisal of the company's assets
     - Avoids any subjectivity
     - IFRS allows reporting of fair value. High cost of appraisals nevertheless limits adoption of this.
2. Accrual Accounting - Revenue Recognition: Accrual basis of accounting states that revenues must be recorded when earned and measurable
     - 1st Jan 2025: Amazon purchases book from a publisher for $10. 
     - 1st Jan 2026: Amazon receives $20 credit card order for the book from a buyer
     - 1st Feb 2026: Book is shipped to the customer
     - 1st Mar 2026: Amazon.com receives cash 
     - The revenues were **earned** on 1st of Feb 2026, so this is when we must be record our revenue of $20
     - Unlike IFRS, US GAAP has very specific guidelines for each industry 
3. Accrual Accounting - Matching Principle: Costs associated with making a product must be recorded during the same period as revenue generated from that product 
     - Since we recorded the revenue on 1st of Feb 2026, we must also recorded the $10 expense on this data 
4. Full Disclosure: Companies must reveal all relevant economic information
     - Financial Statements
     - Notes to Financial Statements
     - Supplementary Information 

### Major accounting constraints 
1. Estimates: Use conservative estimates and judgements where uncertainty exists. E.g. if estimating money lost due to customers purchasing goods on credit and later deciding not to pay, the company must make a conservative estimate based on past experience
2. Materiality: Inclusion of transactions depends on their size and effect on the company performing them. 
3. Consistency: Companies must stick to assumptions used in the past, for consistent reporting across periods. E.g. if you value inventory based on last-in-first-out, you cannot later switch to first-in-first-out.
4. Conservative bias: Understate assets and revenues and overstate expenses and liabilities

### Form 10-K
Publicly-traded companies have to file a 10-K report at the end of each fiscal year. These:
- Include a business overview as well as financial statements. 
- Must be filed within 60d of year end for large companies or within 90d of year end for smaller (non-accelerated) companies
- Detailed and mandatory. Careful not to confuse with an 'Annual Report', which might be a marketing document different from the 10-k
- 10k also includes Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A)

### Form 10-Q
- At the end of each quarter of their fiscal year, companies must also file a 10-Q within 40-45d.
- Also contains financial statements, important footnotes and management commentary, but less detailed than 10-K
- 10-k by comparison has more details on stock options, fixed and intangible assets, debt, future expectations, etc. 
- Unlike a 10-K, a 10-Q is unaudited
- Public companies must generate three 10-Qs and one 10-K per year

### Other Forms
- 8-K: required any time company undergoes materially significant event (earnings press release, disposal of assets, bankruptcy, acquisition, etc.). These must be fined within 4d of event (usually next day). 
- 14-A: Also known as the Proxy Statement, this includes information on top officers and their compensation. Required filing before the annual shareholder meeting. This form often solicits shareholder votes (proxies) for Board nominees and other things requiring votes. 
  - Especially useful when analysing an acquisition because companies provide shareholders with technical details regarding the deal
- S-1: registration filed by a company when it decisdes to go public and sell securities and an IPO. Includes the prospectus, outlining company's operations to help investors make a decision on investment
- S-4: filed to register a securities offering in mergers/reorganisations
- 20-F: annual report filed by certain foreign issuers of securities trading in the US. The equivalent to the 10-K

## Income Statement
- An income statement reports the revenues and expences for a given period (quarter or year). It deducts one from the other to arrive at the profit (income). 
- Income Statements are often referred to as:
  - Consolidated Statement of Earnings
  - Profit & Loss (P&L)
  - Statement of Revenues and Expenses
- Used to assess growth prospects, cost structure and profitability
  
### Components
  - Net Revenue: total payment for goods and services over time period. Also called Net Sales, Turnover or just Revenue. Also called a company's "top-line". 
  - Cost of Goods Sold: Manufacture or procurement costs for goods and services
    - **Gross Profit: Net Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold**
  - Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A) Expenses: Operating costs not directly associated with the production or procurement of the product or service the company sells to generate revenue. Includes Payroll, commissions, meal/travel expenses, stationary, advertising, marketing etc. May be called marketing expenses, etc.
  - R&D Expenses 
    - **EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization: Gross Profit - SG&A - R&D.**
  - Depreciation * Amortization (D&A): Allocation of cost over a fixed asset's useful life in order to match the timing of the cost of the asset with when it is expected to generate revenue benefits
  - Other Operating Expenses/Income: Anything that does not fall into expenses buckets above
    - **Operating profit (EBIT): Earnings before interest * Taxes: EBITDA - D&A - Other Operating expenses/Income**
  - Interest Expense: what the company has to pay on debt owed (to bondholders or banks)
    - **EBT**: Earnings before taxes = EBIT - Interest Expense
  - Interest Income: any income from cash holdings or investments
  - Non-operating Income: Items peripheral to core operations, e.g. gains/losses on investments and revaluations of financial assets and debt obligations
  - Income Tax Expense: tax liability 
    - **Net Income: EBIT - Net Interest Expense - Other Non-operating Income - Taxes**
  - Basic earnings per share (EPS): Net income/Basic weighted average shares outstanding
  - Diluted EPS: Net income/Diluted weighted average shares outstanding

### Net Revenue and Revenue Recognition
Net revenue excludes money from other income sources unrelated to main operations (legal settlements, interest income from investments, cash holdings etc.). E.g. If a company:

- Collects $450 in cash
- Sells $500 in merchandise
- Sells $100 in prescriptions
- Wins a $400 legal settlement
- Collects $20 in interest income from a bank account
  
The total revenue is $600 from the merchandise and prescriptions. Cash does not constitute revenue under accrual accounting, since it is recorded when earned. It cannot be recorded as revenue until the good or service has been delivered and the collection of that cash is reasonably assured (finalised). 

For sales of bundled products, companies must assign individual values to each bundled component (e.g. hardware + software). 
- Apple sels their iPhone for $499
- Apple estimates the standalone selling price for the unspecified software upgrade rights implicit when a customer buys an iPhone to be $25
- Apple therefore immediately recognise $474 ($499-$25)
- Apple recognise remaining $25 in revenue after several years 

For long-term projects, you can recognise revenue ased on percentage of completion or completed contract methods
- Completed Contract Method: Allows revenue recognition only once entire project has been completed (rare) 
- Percentage of Completion Method: Revenues recognised on the basis of percentage of total work completed during the accounting period
  - E.g. On 12 Jan 2015 Boeing agrees to deliver 6 airplanes to Bavaria Aircraft Leasing for $330m. Delivery of the airplanes begins in July 2015 and extends through 2017. Boing is paid upon delivery of each plane. 
  - Boeing should recognise the revenue throughout 2015-2017 as each plane is delivered to the customer.
  - Any expenses should be matched to the revenue, even if the cost has been incurred prior. 
  - If we used cash accounting, we would recognise costs when they are incurred (paid out) and revenue when cash is received (paid in). If we did that:
    - Boeing would recognise the purchase of raw materials some time before any revenue from it's contract with Bavaria Leasing
    - This would show a company with high costs and no revenues, leading analysts to conclude that Boeing is not profitable 
    - However Boeing is in fact profitable as it only bought the raw materials to fulfill an order that will generate future revenue 
    - The matching principle therefore is in place to allow analysts to accurately assess a company's operating results
  - Cons of accrual accounting:
    - There is some subjectivity around which period revenues and expenses are allocated
    - Cashflow is not clear and a separate cashflow statement is therefore required 
  - Cash accounting allowed for business with up to $1m in revenue (sometimes up to $10m). 

#### Revenue Manipulation
- For bundled products and long-term projects, you must estimate future revenues and costs. The estimation methods are explained in 10-K footnotes, but must be read carefully
- E.g. Software maker TSAI had falling revenues
  - It sold a 5-year license
  - Prior to 1998, the company recorded revenues only when the customer was billed
  - Post 1998 the company's sales declined
  - The company started recording 5 years' of revenue upfront, artificially boosting sales

### Cost of Goods Sold 
This is the company's direct cost of manufacture (for manufacturing) or procurement (for merchandisers) of a good or service that the company sells to generate revenue. 

It can include, for example:
- Merchandise inventory(e.g. cost for Walmart to purchase a TV from their supplier)
- Manufactured goods inventory
  - Raw material costs
  - Direct labour costs
  - Factory overhead (heat, rent, administrative salaries, equipment maintenance, custodial costs)
- Shipping and delivery costs
- Other costs directly associated with revenue generation
- Depreciation of fixed assets
- Direct service costs 
  - e.g. Google's payments to it's adversiting network
  - e.g. Compensation of BCG consultants that directly generate consulting fees

Due to matching principle, this form of cost is only recognised when revenue is recognised. 

### Gross Profit 
This is the revenue - COGS. 

E.g. Tire producer recorded $100m in net revenues in 2015 to 8000 customers. That year, they incurred:
- $60m cost of rubber raw materials
- $7m factory overhead
- $5m direct labour
- $40m tire inventories that were used up in the sales that generated $100m in revenue. Of these
  - $30m were raw material costs
  - $5m factory overhead
  - $5m direct labour
- $4m office supplies
- $2m R&D
- $2m shipping
Gross Profit = $100m - $40m - $2m = $58m

Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit/Revenue. Most 'margins' are jsut that value divided by revenue (e.g. operating profit margin, operating expense margin). 

### Accrual Accounting and Depreciation 
Where we have a cost (marketing, office, equipment maintenance, etc.), we try to match it to the revenue according to the matching principle. To do this in the case of physical assets (plants and buildings, equipment, furniture and fixtures etc.), we spread the cost evenly over the life of the asset, so that the costs match the period when revenue is earned as a result of using the asset. This is what is known as Depreciation. 

For example:
- GE buys a sensor for $35m (upfront cash). 
- The equipment has a useful life of 10 years after which it will be sold for parts
- The salvage value of the sensor after 10 years is estimated to be $5m  
- The depreciable cost is therefore $35m - $5m = $30m
- GE will recognise an annual depreciation expense of $3m for the next 10 years
- This is why we need a cashflow statement:
  - Looking at just the income statement alone can be misleading, because GE will spend $35m but that year the income statement will only state an expense of $3m
  - Alternatively, GE will state an expense of $3m in subsequent years even though there is no outflow in those years

Land is a fixed asset but is not depreciated. 

Depreciation does not get a separate line-item on the income statement. Instead, it is included in the stated COGS or SG&A. The nature of the asset determines whether it contributes to COGS or SG&A. 

Depreciation is always stated explicitly in the cashflow statement. 

The balance sheet tracks the value of the asset over time. For example if a tire maker spends $100,000 in 2014 on equipment that is likely to be productive for 5 years after which it is estimated to have a salvage cost of $20,000, then your income statement COGS value will include the depreciation ($16,000) while the balance sheet will track the accumulated depreciation as well as the latest value of the asset:

| Year | Depreciation expense | Accumulated depreciation (end of year) | Book value (end of year) |
|------|----------------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------------|
|      |                      |                                        | 100,000 (at purchase)    |
| 2014 | 16,000               | 16,000                                 | 84,000                   |
| 2015 | 16,000               | 32,000                                 | 68,000                   |
| 2016 | 16,000               | 48,000                                 | 52,000                   |
| 2017 | 16,000               | 64,000                                 | 36,000                   |
| 2018 | 16,000               | 80,000                                 | 20,000                   |

Here we have straight-line depreciation, but sometimes companies can choose accelerated depreciation, where there is greater depreciation in earlier years than later years. 

### Amortization Expense
Very similar to depreciation, often stated together. Whereas depreciation is for fixed assets, amortization is for intangible assets (e.g. customer lists, franchise, licenses, memberships, patents). 

Trademark and goodwill are intangible assets with an indefinite useful life, and are therefore not subject to amortization. They are the equivalent of land. 

Internally-generated assets (patents, trademarks, etc.), while may accrue value, cannot be amortized or stated on an income statement due to the historical cost principle. 

### Stock-Based Compensation Expense
Salaries are embedded within the expense categories based on job function. E.g. software engineer salaries will be under R&D while sales salaries will be under SG&A. 

When the employee receives stock-based compensation (SBC), it is recognised when it is earned. E.g. if the employee has a salary of $100,000 and earns SBC of $25,000, the total expense captured will be $125,000, even though the stocks will be exercised and the payment made at a later date. 

The SBC will also therefore be hidded in the expense bucket to which the employee is allocated. You can find the value of the SBC as a non-cash item in the cash flow statement.

## Quick Reference: Financial Close Process

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  TRANSACTION PROCESSING                      │
│  Daily: AP, AR, Expense, Payroll, Journal entries           │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   PERIOD CLOSE                               │
│  Cut-off → Accruals → Reconciliations → Adjustments        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  CONSOLIDATION                               │
│  Entity close → Intercompany → Elimination → FX translation │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              ↓
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   REPORTING                                  │
│  Trial balance → Financial statements → Analysis → Filing   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```
