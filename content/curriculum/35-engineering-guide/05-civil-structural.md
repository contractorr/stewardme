# Civil and Structural Engineering

## Overview

Civil engineering designs, builds, and maintains physical infrastructure—buildings, bridges, roads, dams, water systems, and tunnels. It is humanity's oldest engineering discipline, transforming landscapes and enabling cities, transportation networks, and water supply that billions depend on daily. Structural engineering, a core subdiscipline, ensures structures safely resist loads from gravity, wind, earthquakes, and use.

## Structural Engineering Fundamentals

### Load Types

**Dead load**: Permanent, static weight
- Structure self-weight
- Fixed equipment, facades
- **Predictable, constant**

**Live load**: Temporary, variable loads
- Occupants, furniture, vehicles, snow
- Design values from building codes (offices: 50 psf, residential: 40 psf)

**Environmental loads**:
- **Wind**: Pressure on surfaces, increases with height and velocity²
- **Seismic**: Earthquake ground motion
- **Snow**: Varies by climate, roof slope
- **Temperature**: Expansion/contraction

**Load combinations**: Structures designed for most critical combination
- Dead + Live
- Dead + Wind
- Dead + Earthquake
- Partial factors for probability

### Structural Materials

**Concrete**:
- **Compressive strength**: Excellent (20-100+ MPa)
- **Tensile strength**: Poor (10% of compressive)
- **Solution**: Steel reinforcement in tension zones
- **Advantages**: Durable, fire-resistant, moldable, cheap
- **Disadvantages**: Heavy, cracks, slow construction

**Structural steel**:
- **Strength-to-weight**: Excellent
- **Ductility**: Deforms before failure (warning, earthquake resistance)
- **Speed**: Prefabricated, rapid erection
- **Disadvantages**: Expensive, fire protection needed, corrosion

**Timber**:
- **Renewable**, carbon-negative during growth
- Good strength-to-weight
- Limited spans (20-30 ft typical beams)
- Fire and moisture concerns
- **Engineered lumber** (glulam, CLT) enables larger structures

**Masonry**:
- Brick, concrete block, stone
- Compressive only (like unreinforced concrete)
- Traditional construction, still used for walls

### Structural Systems

**Beams and columns**: Post-and-beam construction
- Vertical loads on beams → columns → foundation
- Simple, versatile
- Practical spans: 20-40 ft (wood), 30-60 ft (steel/concrete)

**Trusses**: Triangulated frameworks
- Members in pure tension or compression (no bending)
- Efficient for long spans (100+ ft)
- Bridges, roof structures, towers

**Frames**: Rigid connections resist bending
- Moment-resisting frames: Beams and columns resist lateral loads together
- **Portal frames**: Common in warehouses, hangars

**Arches**: Compressive structure following load path
- Ancient form (Roman aqueducts)
- Stone/concrete ideal (strong in compression)
- Long spans possible (bridges, hangars)

**Shells and domes**: Curved surfaces resist through membrane action
- Efficient use of material
- Eggshell strength analogy
- Examples: Pantheon dome (43m, 125 CE), Sydney Opera House

**Suspended structures**:
- **Suspension bridge**: Cables in tension, deck hangs from cables
- **Cable-stayed**: Cables directly from towers to deck
- **Tension structures**: Fabric/membrane (stadiums, temporary structures)

## Building Design

### Skyscrapers

**Vertical load**: Cumulative weight increases downward
- Lower columns carry entire building
- Tapering or stronger materials at base

**Lateral load** (wind, earthquake): **Dominates design** for tall buildings
- Bending moment increases with height²
- Drift (sideways movement) must be limited for comfort and cladding

**Structural systems for height**:

| Height Range | System | Example |
|--------------|--------|---------|
| 20-40 floors | Moment frame | Typical office tower |
| 40-60 floors | Shear walls + frame | Residential towers |
| 60-100 floors | Core + outriggers | Willis Tower |
| 100+ floors | Bundled tubes, braced megatubes | Burj Khalifa (163 floors) |

**Burj Khalifa** (828m, 2010):
- Buttressed core structure
- Y-shaped plan (wind load reduction)
- High-performance concrete (80 MPa)
- 330,000 m³ concrete, 39,000 tons steel rebar
- Pumped concrete to 600m (world record)

**Wind engineering**: Wind tunnel testing scale models
- Vortex shedding can cause oscillations
- Tuned mass damper: Heavy mass on springs/dampers at top, counteracts sway
  - Taipei 101: 660-ton damper

**Foundation**: Transfer loads to ground
- **Shallow** (spread footings): Competent soil near surface
- **Deep** (piles, caissons): Poor surface soil; transfer load to rock/dense soil at depth
- Burj Khalifa: 43m deep piles into rock

### Earthquake Engineering

**Seismic loading**: Ground acceleration causes inertia forces (F = ma)

**Design philosophy**:
- **Life safety**: Building may be damaged but shouldn't collapse
- **Ductility**: Plastic deformation absorbs energy
- **Redundancy**: Multiple load paths

**Seismic-resistant systems**:
- **Moment frames**: Ductile beam-column connections
- **Shear walls**: Reinforced concrete walls resist lateral forces
- **Braced frames**: Diagonal members resist lateral loads
- **Base isolation**: Building sits on bearings that decouple from ground motion (hospitals, data centers)
- **Dampers**: Viscous or friction devices dissipate energy

**Codes**: Region-specific (California stringent, low seismic areas less so)

**Historic failures**: Mexico City 1985 (resonance with soft soil), Kobe 1995 (brittle concrete columns)

## Bridge Engineering

### Bridge Types

**Beam bridge**: Simplest; deck supported on piers
- **Spans**: <50m practical (steel/concrete)
- Highway overpasses

**Arch bridge**: Compressive structure
- **Spans**: 100-500m possible
- Beautiful, efficient for stone/concrete

**Truss bridge**: Triangulated members
- **Spans**: 50-500m
- Railroad bridges (heavy loads)

**Suspension bridge**: Cables in tension support deck
- **Longest spans**: 500-2000m
- Golden Gate (1280m main span), Akashi Kaikyo (1991m)
- Requires strong foundations/anchorages for cable tension

**Cable-stayed bridge**: Cables from towers to deck
- **Spans**: 200-1000m
- More economical than suspension for medium spans
- Faster construction

### Design Considerations

**Loads**:
- Dead: Bridge self-weight
- Live: Vehicles (design trucks, train loads)
- Wind: Can be enormous for long spans
- Seismic: Especially in active zones
- Temperature: Expansion joints, bearings
- Impact: Vehicle collision with piers

**Materials evolution**:
- Stone/brick arches → iron → steel → reinforced concrete → prestressed concrete → composites
- Stronger materials → longer spans

**Corrosion**: Major maintenance issue
- Salt (deicing, seawater)
- **Solutions**: Stainless rebar, galvanizing, coatings, cathodic protection, weathering steel

**Inspection**: Regular monitoring
- Visual, ultrasonic, radiographic
- Drones, robots for hard-to-reach areas

**I-35W Bridge collapse** (Minneapolis, 2007): Gusset plate design flaw + heavy construction load
- 13 killed
- **Lesson**: Importance of inspection, design review, maintenance

## Transportation Engineering

### Highway Design

**Geometric design**:
- **Horizontal alignment**: Curves, superelevation (banking)
- **Vertical alignment**: Grades, sight distance
- **Cross-section**: Lane widths, shoulders, drainage

**Design speed**: Determines minimum curve radius, stopping sight distance
- Interstate: 70 mph design speed
- Urban: 30-40 mph

**Pavement design**:
- **Flexible** (asphalt): Multiple layers, distributes load
  - Cheaper, easier to repair, but requires more maintenance
- **Rigid** (concrete): Thick slab, high durability
  - Expensive initially, long lifespan (30-40 years)

**Traffic engineering**:
- Signal timing optimization
- Capacity analysis (vehicles/hour per lane)
- Level of Service (LOS): A (free flow) to F (gridlock)

### Railway Engineering

**Track structure**:
- **Rails**: Steel, standardized profiles
- **Ties** (sleepers): Distribute load, maintain gauge (spacing)
  - Wood (traditional), concrete (modern, longer life)
- **Ballast**: Crushed stone, drainage, flexibility
- **Subgrade**: Compacted soil foundation

**High-speed rail**: Special requirements
- Very precise alignment (minimize curves)
- Continuously welded rail (no joints)
- Grade separated (no level crossings)
- Advanced signaling (ERTMS, ACSES)

**Freight rail**: Heavy axle loads
- North America: 315 kN (70,000 lb) per axle typical
- Requires strong track structure
- Slow speeds acceptable (freight vs. passenger priorities)

## Water Resources Engineering

### Dams

**Purposes**: Flood control, water supply, irrigation, hydropower, recreation

**Types**:

**Gravity dam**: Mass resists water pressure
- Concrete or rock-fill
- Stable from sheer weight
- Grand Coulee, Hoover Dam

**Arch dam**: Curves upstream, transfers load to canyon walls
- Requires strong rock canyon walls
- Very efficient use of concrete
- Tallest: Jinping-I (305m, China)

**Embankment dam**: Earth or rock-fill with impervious core
- Uses local materials
- More tolerant of foundation settlement
- Most common type worldwide

**Design considerations**:
- **Hydrostatic pressure**: Increases with depth
- **Seepage**: Water through/under dam (erosion risk)
- **Overtopping**: Spillway must handle floods
- **Earthquakes**: Liquefaction, structural damage
- **Failure consequences**: Catastrophic flooding downstream

**Spillways**: Release excess water safely
- Overflow, gate-controlled, chute
- Energy dissipation (stilling basin) prevents downstream erosion

**Oroville Dam crisis** (California, 2017): Spillway erosion, 180,000 evacuated
- **Lesson**: Maintenance critical, aging infrastructure vulnerable

### Water Treatment

**Drinking water treatment**:
1. **Coagulation/flocculation**: Chemicals cause particles to clump
2. **Sedimentation**: Flocs settle
3. **Filtration**: Sand/activated carbon remove remaining particles
4. **Disinfection**: Chlorine, UV, ozone kill pathogens
5. **pH adjustment**: Prevent pipe corrosion

**Wastewater treatment**:
1. **Primary**: Screening, grit removal, sedimentation (removes ~60% solids)
2. **Secondary**: Biological process (activated sludge, trickling filter) removes organic matter
3. **Tertiary**: Advanced treatment (nutrient removal, disinfection)
4. **Sludge treatment**: Digestion (biogas production), dewatering, disposal/reuse

**Design flow**: Population × per capita usage + industrial/commercial
- Design for peak flow (3-4× average)

**Regulatory**: Meet EPA standards (Safe Drinking Water Act, Clean Water Act)

## Geotechnical Engineering

### Soil Mechanics

**Soil types**:
- **Gravel/sand**: Cohesionless (friction); good bearing capacity, free-draining
- **Silt**: Intermediate properties
- **Clay**: Cohesive; plastic, low permeability, swells/shrinks with moisture

**Properties**:
- **Bearing capacity**: Load soil can support without failure
  - Rock: Very high (500+ kPa)
  - Dense sand/gravel: 200-400 kPa
  - Stiff clay: 100-200 kPa
  - Soft clay: <50 kPa (pile foundation required)
- **Settlement**: Vertical compression under load
  - Immediate (elastic) + consolidation (pore water squeeze out)
- **Shear strength**: Resistance to sliding failure

**Site investigation**: Borings, test pits, geophysical
- Soil profile, groundwater level
- Lab testing (strength, compressibility)

### Foundations

**Spread footings**: Distribute load over area
- Shallow, economical
- Requires competent soil

**Piles**: Deep foundations
- **Driven**: Steel, concrete, or timber piles hammered into ground
  - End bearing (rest on rock/dense layer) or friction (skin friction)
- **Drilled**: Concrete cast in drilled hole (caissons)
- Transfer load to depth

**Mats/rafts**: Single large foundation for entire structure
- Heavy loads, weak soil
- Reduces differential settlement

**Retaining walls**: Hold back soil
- Gravity (mass resists), cantilever, anchored
- Drainage critical (prevent hydrostatic pressure buildup)

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Dead load** | Permanent weight (structure, fixed equipment) |
| **Live load** | Temporary, variable loads (people, vehicles, snow) |
| **Moment** | Rotational force (bending moment in beams) |
| **Shear** | Force parallel to surface (causes sliding) |
| **Reinforced concrete** | Concrete with steel bars providing tensile strength |
| **Factor of safety** | Ratio of capacity to demand |
| **Ductility** | Ability to deform plastically before failure |
| **Bearing capacity** | Load soil can support |
| **Settlement** | Vertical compression of soil/structure |
| **Seismic** | Related to earthquakes |

## Summary

Civil and structural engineering creates the physical infrastructure enabling modern civilization. Structural engineers design buildings and bridges to safely resist gravity, wind, and seismic loads using materials—concrete, steel, timber—each with distinct advantages. Load paths through beams, columns, trusses, arches, or cables transfer forces to foundations and ultimately the ground.

Skyscrapers require sophisticated lateral load systems as height increases, with wind and earthquakes dominating design. Bridges span obstacles using beams, arches, trusses, suspension, or cable-stayed systems, with spans from meters to kilometers. Transportation engineers design highways, railways, and airports balancing safety, capacity, and cost.

Water resources engineers dam rivers for flood control, supply, and power, while treating water for safe drinking and wastewater disposal before environmental release. Geotechnical engineers characterize soil and design foundations—shallow footings for competent ground, deep piles for weak soils—preventing settlement and failure.

From ancient Roman aqueducts to modern supertall towers, civil engineering transforms landscapes and builds infrastructure society depends on. Every building, bridge, road, dam, and water system exists because civil engineers translated physics and materials science into designs that safely, economically, and sustainably serve humanity.
