### Workflow & Decision Making

```
PENSION FUND BUYING PROCESS
───────────────────────────

├── Primary: Head of PE / Alternatives CIO
├── Influencers: Investment team members
├── Technical: IT / Operations
├── Approval: CIO, potentially Board
└── Procurement: Formal RFP process often required

PROCESS:
1. Need identified by investment team
2. Internal requirements gathering
3. RFP issued (public process often required)
4. Vendor presentations
5. Due diligence / reference checks
6. Negotiation
7. Legal/procurement review
8. Contract execution

• Willing to pay: $100K - $500K+ annually

• May require competitive bidding
• Budget cycles are annual

KEY DECISION FACTORS:
├── Data integration capabilities
├── Reporting flexibility
├── Security/compliance
├── References from peer institutions
├── Implementation support
└── Long-term vendor viability

## 14.3 Sovereign Wealth Funds

### Profile

```
SOVEREIGN WEALTH FUNDS
──────────────────────

EXAMPLES:
• Norway Government Pension Fund ($1.4T+)
• China Investment Corporation ($1.2T+)
• Abu Dhabi Investment Authority ($900B+)
• GIC Singapore ($700B+)
• Kuwait Investment Authority ($700B+)
• Saudi PIF ($600B+)

CHARACTERISTICS:
├── Total AUM: $100B - $1T+
├── Alternatives allocation: 10-40%
├── PE allocation: $10B - $100B+
├── Number of GP relationships: 50-200+
├── Internal team: 50-500+ total
├── PE/alternatives team: 20-100+
├── Direct investments: Significant
└── Co-investments: Very active
```

### Unique Characteristics

```
SWF DISTINGUISHING FEATURES
───────────────────────────

1. DIRECT INVESTING CAPABILITY
   • In-house deal teams
   • Compete with GPs on deals
   • Need deal-level analytics
   • Track record building

2. CO-INVESTMENT SCALE
   • $100M-$1B+ co-investments
   • Separate tracking from fund investments
   • Different fee structures
   • Attribution complexity

3. GLOBAL MANDATE
   • Investments across all geographies
   • Currency complexity
   • Multiple time zones
   • Local regulatory requirements

4. LONG TIME HORIZON
   • Perpetual capital
   • Inter-generational mandate
   • Can accept illiquidity
   • Focus on absolute returns

5. GOVERNANCE COMPLEXITY
   • Government oversight
   • Political considerations
   • Santiago Principles adherence
   • Transparency requirements vary by country

6. SOPHISTICATED ANALYTICS
   • Risk factor analysis
   • Scenario modeling
   • Total portfolio perspective
   • Factor exposure management
```

### Pain Points

```
SWF SPECIFIC PAIN POINTS
────────────────────────

1. UNIFIED VIEW ACROSS STRATEGIES
   • Fund investments + directs + co-invests
   • Different data sources
   • Attribution across categories
   • Total PE exposure calculation
   Pain level: ████████████████████ CRITICAL

2. DIRECT INVESTMENT TRACKING
   • Deal pipeline management
   • Internal valuation process
   • Board/IC materials
   • Exit tracking
   Pain level: ████████████████████ CRITICAL

3. GLOBAL REPORTING REQUIREMENTS
   • Multiple stakeholder reports
   • Different formats by region
   • Currency consolidation
   • Regulatory variations
   Pain level: ████████████████░░░░ HIGH

4. BENCHMARK CONSTRUCTION
   • Custom benchmarks needed
   • Direct vs fund comparison
   • Strategy-specific benchmarks
   • Geographic benchmarks
   Pain level: ███████████████░░░░░ HIGH

5. RISK ANALYTICS
   • Factor exposure
   • Concentration risk
   • Correlation analysis
   • Scenario planning
   Pain level: ██████████████░░░░░░ MEDIUM-HIGH
```

### Buying Behavior

```
SWF BUYING PROCESS
──────────────────

TIMELINE: 12-24 months

CHARACTERISTICS:
• Highly methodical
• Extensive due diligence
• Security paramount
• Custom requirements common
• Willing to pay premium for quality

BUDGET:
• Willing to pay: $500K - $2M+ annually

```

### Pain Points

```
CORPORATE PENSION PAIN POINTS
─────────────────────────────

1. LIMITED STAFF FOR COMPLEXITY
   • 2-5 people managing $5B+ portfolio
   • PE is one of many responsibilities
   • Can't dedicate specialist resources
   Pain level: ████████████████████ CRITICAL

2. ERISA COMPLIANCE
   • Fiduciary documentation
   • Fee reasonableness
   • Prohibited transaction rules
   • DOL audit risk
   Pain level: ████████████████░░░░ HIGH

3. LIABILITY-DRIVEN CONTEXT
   • PE is part of return-seeking portfolio
   • Funded status is key metric
   • Asset-liability matching
   • Risk budget constraints
   Pain level: ████████████████░░░░ HIGH

4. PENSION RISK TRANSFER TREND
   • Plans being terminated/transferred
   • Need clean data for transfer
   • Valuation accuracy critical
   • Time pressure
   Pain level: ███████████████░░░░░ HIGH

5. CORPORATE OVERSIGHT
   • CFO/audit committee questions
   • Board reporting requirements
   • Corporate governance standards
   • Internal audit scrutiny
   Pain level: ██████████████░░░░░░ MEDIUM-HIGH
```

### Buying Behavior

```
CORPORATE PENSION BUYING PROCESS
────────────────────────────────

TIMELINE: 3-9 months

INFLUENCERS:
• If internal: CIO / Head of Investments
• If OCIO: The OCIO firm (key influencer)
• Corporate procurement involved
• IT security review

BUDGET:
• Willing to pay: $30K - $150K annually
• More price-sensitive than public pensions
• Corporate budget constraints
• Need to justify ROI

BUYING TRIGGERS:
├── Staff departure / knowledge loss
├── ERISA audit findings
├── Board/committee pressure
├── Plan merger/acquisition
└── OCIO recommendation

NOTE ON OCIO:
If plan uses OCIO, the OCIO controls:
• Technology decisions
• Reporting format
• Data aggregation
Your customer may be the OCIO, not the plan
```

## 14.6 Insurance Companies

### Profile

```
INSURANCE COMPANY INVESTORS
───────────────────────────

EXAMPLES:
• Life insurers: MetLife, Prudential, Principal
• P&C insurers: AIG, Chubb, Travelers
• Reinsurers: Swiss Re, Munich Re
• Non-US: Allianz, AXA, Ping An

CHARACTERISTICS:

├── Separate accounts: Client-specific
├── Total alternatives: $1B - $50B+
├── PE allocation: $500M - $20B
├── Number of GP relationships: 30-150
├── Internal team: 5-30+ (alternatives)
└── Heavy regulatory burden
```

### Unique Regulatory Context

```
INSURANCE-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS
───────────────────────────────

STATUTORY ACCOUNTING (SAP):
• Different from GAAP
• Conservative valuations
• Admitted vs non-admitted assets
• Schedule BA for PE holdings

RISK-BASED CAPITAL (RBC):
• Capital charge for each asset
• PE typically 30% charge
• Affects investment capacity
• Need to track RBC impact

INVESTMENT LIMITS:
• State-level regulations
• Asset class limits
• Single issuer limits
• Quality requirements

SOLVENCY II (European):
• Look-through required
• SCR calculations
• Detailed reporting templates
• Matching adjustment portfolios

REPORTING CADENCE:
• Quarterly statutory filings
• Annual statement
• State exam preparation
• Rating agency reviews
```

### Pain Points

```
INSURANCE COMPANY PAIN POINTS
─────────────────────────────

1. DUAL ACCOUNTING (GAAP + STAT)
   • Maintain both bases
   • Different valuations
   • Reconciliation burden
   • Two sets of reports
   Pain level: ████████████████████ CRITICAL

2. LOOK-THROUGH REQUIREMENTS
   • Need underlying holdings
   • GPs often don't provide
   • Solvency II complexity
   • SCR calculation data
   Pain level: ████████████████████ CRITICAL

3. REGULATORY REPORTING
   • Schedule BA detail
   • State filing requirements
   • NAIC format compliance
   • Admitted asset tracking
   Pain level: ████████████████░░░░ HIGH

4. RBC IMPACT ANALYSIS
   • Capital consumption tracking
   • Return on capital analysis
   • Limit utilization
   • Optimization
   Pain level: ████████████████░░░░ HIGH

5. RATING AGENCY REQUIREMENTS
   • S&P, Moody's, Fitch requests
   • Different analytics needed
   • Stress testing
   • Concentration analysis
   Pain level: ███████████████░░░░░ HIGH
```

### Buying Behavior

```

────────────────────────

STAKEHOLDERS:

## Knowledge Check

1. Why do insurance companies have different needs than pension funds?
2. What makes Fund of Funds both a customer and a unique segment?
3. Why might an OCIO relationship change how you sell to corporate pensions?
4. What are the key differences between large and small endowments as customers?
5. Why is family office sophistication so variable?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Insurance companies must maintain dual accounting (GAAP and Statutory), face specific regulatory requirements (Schedule BA, RBC calculations, Solvency II), and need look-through data for capital calculations. Their regulatory burden is much higher and more specific.

2. FoFs are customers who need to aggregate data from 100s of GPs (like other LPs), but they're also GPs themselves who need to report to their own LPs. They have massive scale, deep sophistication, and may build their own tools - making them both customers and potential competitors.

3. If a corporate pension uses an OCIO, the OCIO controls technology decisions and reporting. The actual buyer becomes the OCIO, not the plan. You may need to sell to OCIOs rather than the underlying plans.

4. Large endowments have dedicated PE teams (5-15 people), significant co-investments, and need sophisticated analytics. Small endowments often have 1-2 people total, rely heavily on consultants, use Fund of Funds, and are very price-sensitive with limited bandwidth.

5. Family offices range from ex-institutional professionals managing billions with full teams to family members managing a few hundred million with little investment experience. There's no regulatory framework enforcing standards, and structures range from highly institutionalized to completely informal.

</details>
