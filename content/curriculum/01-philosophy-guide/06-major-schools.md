# Major Philosophical Schools and Thinkers

## Overview

Philosophy's history spans over 2,500 years across multiple cultures. This guide focuses on influential Western philosophers and schools that shaped philosophical thought, from ancient Greece through contemporary philosophy. Each thinker and movement built upon, reacted to, or revolutionized previous ideas, creating an ongoing dialogue across centuries.

## Ancient Greek Philosophy (600 BCE - 400 CE)

### Pre-Socratic Philosophy (600-400 BCE)

The first Western philosophers sought natural explanations for the cosmos.

**Thales (c. 624-546 BCE)**
- First philosopher: Sought natural, not mythological explanations
- Claimed water is the fundamental substance
- Predicted solar eclipse (585 BCE)

**Heraclitus (c. 535-475 BCE)**
- "Everything flows" (panta rhei)
- Reality is constant change
- Unity of opposites
- Famous: "You cannot step in the same river twice"

**Parmenides (c. 515-450 BCE)**
- Opposite of Heraclitus: Change is illusion
- Reality is unchanging, eternal, indivisible Being
- Reason over senses
- Influenced Plato's theory of Forms

**Democritus (c. 460-370 BCE)**
- Atomism: Reality composed of indivisible atoms and void
- Early materialist view
- Influenced modern atomic theory

### Socrates (469-399 BCE)

**Life**: Athenian philosopher who never wrote; known through Plato's dialogues

**Key Ideas:**

**Socratic Method (Elenchus)**:
- Question and answer dialogue
- Expose contradictions in beliefs
- Goal: Discover truth through critical examination
- "I know that I know nothing"

**Ethics**:
- "The unexamined life is not worth living"
- Virtue is knowledge; no one does wrong knowingly
- Care for the soul above all
- Questioned traditional Athenian values

**Death**: Executed by Athens for impiety and corrupting youth; chose death over exile

**Legacy**: Shifted philosophy from nature to ethics and self-knowledge

### Plato (428-348 BCE)

**Life**: Student of Socrates; founded Academy; teacher of Aristotle

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Theory of Forms**
- Two realms: World of Forms (perfect, eternal, unchanging) and physical world (imperfect, changing copies)
- Forms are true reality; physical objects participate in Forms
- **Example**: Many beautiful things, but one Form of Beauty
- Knowledge is recollection of Forms known before birth

**2. Epistemology**
- Divided Line: Levels of knowledge from illusion to pure reason
- Allegory of the Cave: Prisoners see shadows, mistake for reality; philosopher escapes to see true reality
- True knowledge (episteme) vs. opinion (doxa)

**3. Politics**
- *The Republic*: Ideal state ruled by philosopher-kings
- Three classes: Guardians (rulers), Auxiliaries (soldiers), Producers (workers)
- Justice: Each class doing its proper function
- Communal property and child-rearing for guardians

**4. Soul**
- Tripartite: Reason (head), Spirit (chest), Appetite (belly)
- Virtue: Each part functioning properly under reason's rule
- Immortal soul in mortal body

**5. Metaphysics**
- Idealism: Forms are more real than physical objects
- Demiurge (craftsman god) shaped physical world after Forms

**Major Dialogues**: *Euthyphro*, *Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*, *Republic*, *Symposium*, *Timaeus*

### Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

**Life**: Student of Plato; tutor of Alexander the Great; founded Lyceum

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Metaphysics**
- Rejected separate realm of Forms
- Forms (essence) exist in physical objects, not separate realm
- Four causes: Material (matter), Formal (essence), Efficient (agent), Final (purpose)
- Substance (ousia) is primary reality

**2. Logic**
- Founded formal logic
- Syllogistic reasoning
- Categories of being
- Law of non-contradiction

**3. Ethics**
- *Nicomachean Ethics*: Virtue ethics
- Eudaimonia (flourishing) is ultimate goal
- Virtue is excellence of character, developed through habit
- Doctrine of the Mean: Virtue between extremes
- Practical wisdom (phronesis) guides action

**4. Politics**
- Humans are "political animals"
- State exists for good life, not just survival
- Various forms of government; preferred mixed constitution

**5. Natural Philosophy**
- Emphasis on observation and classification
- Biology: Studied hundreds of species
- Teleology: Nature acts for purposes

**6. Psychology**
- *De Anima*: Soul is form of body
- Three types: Vegetative, Animal, Rational
- Rational soul distinguishes humans

**Major Works**: *Organon* (logic), *Physics*, *Metaphysics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Politics*, *Poetics*

**Legacy**: Dominated medieval philosophy; father of many sciences

### Hellenistic Philosophy (300 BCE - 100 CE)

**Stoicism (Founded by Zeno of Citium)**

**Key Figures**: Epictetus, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius

**Core Ideas**:
- Live according to nature and reason
- Virtue is sufficient for happiness
- Four cardinal virtues: Wisdom, courage, justice, temperance
- Dichotomy of control: Focus on what you can control (judgments, actions), accept what you can't (external events)
- Cosmopolitanism: All humans are citizens of the world
- Apatheia: Freedom from destructive emotions through rational understanding

**Famous Quotes**:
- "It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters" (Epictetus)
- Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*: Personal philosophical diary

**Epicureanism (Founded by Epicurus)**

**Core Ideas**:
- Pleasure is the highest good (hedonism)
- But: Emphasis on moderate, lasting pleasures (friendship, tranquility)
- Avoid pain and anxiety
- Ataraxia: Peace of mind, absence of disturbance
- Atomism: Universe made of atoms; no afterlife to fear
- "Death is nothing to us" (when we exist, death is not; when death is, we are not)
- Withdraw from politics; cultivate friendships

**Skepticism (Pyrrho, Sextus Empiricus)**

**Core Ideas**:
- Suspend judgment (epoché) on all matters
- No belief is more justified than its opposite
- Goal: Ataraxia through suspension of judgment
- Ten modes for suspending judgment (e.g., variation in perception)

## Medieval Philosophy (500-1400 CE)

Integration of Greek philosophy (especially Aristotle) with monotheistic religions.

### Augustine of Hippo (354-430 CE)

**Life**: Converted to Christianity; Bishop of Hippo; influential theologian

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Faith and Reason**
- "Believe in order to understand"
- Reason illuminated by divine grace
- Integrated Platonism with Christianity

**2. Problem of Evil**
- Evil is privation (absence of good), not positive entity
- Free will defense: Evil results from human free choice
- Original sin inherited from Adam

**3. Time**
- *Confessions*: What is time? Time is a distension of the mind
- Past (memory), present (attention), future (anticipation)

**4. Politics**
- *City of God*: Two cities—earthly and heavenly
- Earthly politics inevitably imperfect

**Major Works**: *Confessions*, *City of God*, *On Free Choice of the Will*

### Anselm (1033-1109)

**Ontological Argument** for God's existence:
1. God is "that than which nothing greater can be conceived"
2. Existence in reality is greater than existence in understanding alone
3. Therefore, God must exist in reality

**Criticized by**: Gaunilo (Perfect Island objection), Kant (existence not a predicate)

### Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)

**Life**: Dominican friar; synthesized Aristotle with Christianity

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Five Ways** (arguments for God's existence):
- First Mover: Everything in motion moved by another; must be first mover (God)
- First Cause: Chain of causes requires first cause
- Contingency: Contingent beings require necessary being
- Gradation: Degrees of perfection imply perfect being
- Teleology: Order in nature implies intelligent designer

**2. Faith and Reason**
- Both paths to truth; compatible but distinct
- Reason can prove God's existence; faith reveals more (Trinity, Incarnation)
- Natural law: Moral law based on human nature

**3. Metaphysics**
- Essence and existence distinct (except in God)
- Act and potency
- Hylomorphism: Matter and form

**Major Work**: *Summa Theologica*

**Legacy**: Official philosophy of Catholic Church; natural law theory

## Modern Philosophy (1600-1900)

### René Descartes (1596-1650)

**Life**: French mathematician and philosopher; "Father of Modern Philosophy"

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Method of Doubt**
- Systematically doubt everything possible to find certainty
- Senses deceive; dreams indistinguishable from waking; evil demon hypothesis
- **Cogito ergo sum**: "I think, therefore I am"
- Cannot doubt own existence while doubting

**2. Dualism**
- Mind (res cogitans—thinking substance) and body (res extensa—extended substance) are distinct
- Mind is indivisible, body is divisible
- Interaction problem: How do they interact?
- Pineal gland hypothesis (largely rejected)

**3. God**
- Ontological argument (similar to Anselm)
- Trademark argument: Idea of perfect God must come from perfect being
- God guarantees clear and distinct ideas are true

**4. Epistemology**
- Rationalism: Reason primary source of knowledge
- Innate ideas exist
- Mathematics as model of knowledge

**Major Works**: *Meditations on First Philosophy*, *Discourse on Method*, *Principles of Philosophy*

### Baruch Spinoza (1632-1677)

**Life**: Dutch-Jewish philosopher; excommunicated for heretical views

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Monism**
- Only one substance: God/Nature (Deus sive Natura)
- Everything is mode (modification) of one substance
- Pantheism: God and universe are identical

**2. Determinism**
- Everything follows necessarily from God's nature
- No free will in libertarian sense
- Freedom is understanding necessity

**3. Ethics**
- Emotions arise from inadequate understanding
- Freedom through rational understanding
- Intellectual love of God: Understanding the whole

**4. Politics**
- Early advocate of democracy and religious freedom

**Major Work**: *Ethics* (written in geometric style)

### John Locke (1632-1704)

**Life**: English philosopher; major figure in empiricism and liberalism

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Epistemology**
- Mind is "blank slate" (tabula rasa) at birth
- All ideas come from experience (sensation and reflection)
- No innate ideas (contra Descartes)
- Primary qualities (size, shape) vs. secondary qualities (color, taste)

**2. Political Philosophy**
- *Two Treatises of Government*
- Natural rights: Life, liberty, property
- Social contract: Government by consent to protect rights
- Right to revolution if government violates contract
- Separation of powers

**3. Religious Toleration**
- *Letter Concerning Toleration*
- Government shouldn't enforce religious belief

**Major Works**: *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, *Two Treatises of Government*

**Legacy**: Influenced American Revolution, liberalism, empiricism

### David Hume (1711-1776)

**Life**: Scottish philosopher; radical empiricist and skeptic

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Epistemology**
- All ideas derive from impressions (sensory experiences)
- No impression of self; self is "bundle of perceptions"
- Knowledge divided: Relations of ideas (a priori, certain) vs. matters of fact (a posteriori, probable)

**2. Problem of Induction**
- Past experience doesn't logically guarantee future
- Induction rests on custom and habit, not reason
- Undermines scientific method's logical foundation

**3. Causation**
- No direct perception of causation
- Causation is constant conjunction + psychological expectation
- No necessary connection observed

**4. Ethics**
- Moral sentiments, not reason, motivate action
- "Reason is slave of the passions"
- Sympathy (empathy) basis of morality

**5. Religion**
- Skeptical of arguments for God
- Critique of design argument
- Critique of miracles: More likely testimony is false than miracle occurred

**Major Works**: *A Treatise of Human Nature*, *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*

### Immanuel Kant (1724-1804)

**Life**: German philosopher; synthesized rationalism and empiricism; "Copernican Revolution" in philosophy

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Epistemology** (*Critique of Pure Reason*)
- Synthetic a priori knowledge is possible
- Mind actively structures experience through categories (causation, substance, etc.)
- We know phenomena (appearances), not noumena (things-in-themselves)
- Space and time are forms of intuition, not external realities

**2. Ethics** (*Critique of Practical Reason*, *Groundwork*)
- Categorical Imperative (see Ethics guide)
- Autonomy and dignity of rational beings
- Good will is only thing good without qualification

**3. Aesthetics** (*Critique of Judgment*)
- Beauty is subjective but universal (paradox)
- Sublime: Overwhelmingly large or powerful

**4. Religion**
- Cannot prove God's existence (critique of traditional arguments)
- God, freedom, immortality are postulates of practical reason
- Needed to make sense of morality

**Major Works**: Three Critiques (*Pure Reason*, *Practical Reason*, *Judgment*), *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*

**Legacy**: One of most influential philosophers; shaped subsequent philosophy

### G.W.F. Hegel (1770-1831)

**Life**: German idealist; complex, systematic philosopher

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Dialectic**
- Thesis → Antithesis → Synthesis
- History progresses dialectically
- Contradictions drive development

**2. Absolute Idealism**
- Reality is manifestation of Absolute Spirit (Geist)
- "The real is rational, the rational is real"
- Mind and world not separate

**3. History**
- History is progress of freedom
- Spirit realizes itself through history
- World-historical individuals (Napoleon) embody Spirit

**4. Phenomenology**
- *Phenomenology of Spirit*: Development of consciousness from sense-certainty to absolute knowing
- Master-slave dialectic: Recognition and self-consciousness

**Major Works**: *Phenomenology of Spirit*, *Science of Logic*, *Philosophy of Right*

**Legacy**: Influenced Marx, existentialism, Continental philosophy

### Karl Marx (1818-1883)

**Life**: German philosopher, economist, revolutionary; "Marxism"

**Key Ideas:**

**1. Historical Materialism**
- Material conditions (economic base) determine ideas (superstructure)
- History driven by class struggle
- Stages: Primitive communism → Slavery → Feudalism → Capitalism → Socialism → Communism

**2. Critique of Capitalism**
- *Das Kapital*: Analysis of capitalist economy
- Exploitation: Workers produce more value than they receive (surplus value)
- Alienation: Workers alienated from product, process, others, self
- Class conflict: Bourgeoisie (owners) vs. Proletariat (workers)

**3. Revolution**
- Capitalism contains seeds of own destruction
- Proletarian revolution will establish classless society

**4. Ideology**
- Dominant ideology serves ruling class
- "Religion is opium of the people"

**Major Works**: *The Communist Manifesto* (with Engels), *Das Kapital*

**Legacy**: Enormous political influence; Soviet Union, China; debates continue

### Friedrich Nietzsche (1844-1900)

**Life**: German philosopher; iconoclastic, poetic style

**Key Ideas:**

**1. God is Dead**
- Modern world has lost religious foundation
- Nihilism: Absence of objective meaning and values
- Need to create new values

**2. Will to Power**
- Fundamental drive in humans and nature
- Not domination but self-overcoming, creativity

**3. Master-Slave Morality**
- Master morality: Good/bad (noble/base)
- Slave morality: Good/evil (Christian morality)
- Slave morality born of ressentiment (resentment)

**4. Übermensch (Overman/Superman)**
- Ideal: Create own values, embrace life
- Goes beyond good and evil
- Affirm life including suffering

**5. Eternal Recurrence**
- Thought experiment: Live same life infinitely
- Test: Can you affirm your life completely?

**6. Perspectivism**
- No objective truth; only interpretations
- Multiple perspectives, no "view from nowhere"

**Major Works**: *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, *Beyond Good and Evil*, *On the Genealogy of Morality*

**Legacy**: Influenced existentialism, postmodernism; misappropriated by Nazis

## Contemporary Philosophy (1900-Present)

### Analytic Tradition

Emphasis on clarity, logic, language analysis, science.

**Bertrand Russell (1872-1970)**
- Logic and foundations of mathematics (*Principia Mathematica* with Whitehead)
- Theory of descriptions: Logical analysis of language
- Logical atomism
- Pacifism and social activism

**Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889-1951)**

**Early Wittgenstein** (*Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*):
- Picture theory: Propositions picture facts
- Logical structure of language mirrors structure of world
- "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent"

**Later Wittgenstein** (*Philosophical Investigations*):
- Meaning is use in language-games
- No private language
- Philosophical problems arise from misunderstanding language
- "Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language"

**Logical Positivism (Vienna Circle)**
- Only verifiable statements are meaningful
- Verification principle: Meaning = method of verification
- Elimination of metaphysics
- Unity of science
- **Problem**: Principle self-refuting (can't verify verification principle)

**W.V.O. Quine (1908-2000)**
- "Two Dogmas of Empiricism": Critique of analytic/synthetic distinction
- Web of belief: Holistic view of knowledge
- Ontological relativity
- Naturalized epistemology

**John Rawls (1921-2002)**
- *A Theory of Justice*
- Veil of ignorance thought experiment
- Justice as fairness
- Two principles: Equal basic liberties; fair equality of opportunity and difference principle

### Continental Tradition

Emphasis on human experience, history, interpretation, critique of Western metaphysics.

**Phenomenology**

**Edmund Husserl (1859-1938)**
- Founded phenomenology: Study of structures of consciousness
- "To the things themselves"
- Intentionality: Consciousness is always of something
- Bracketing (epoché): Suspend assumptions about external world
- Transcendental phenomenology

**Martin Heidegger (1889-1976)**
- *Being and Time*: Question of Being
- Dasein: Being-in-the-world (human existence)
- Existential structures: Being-toward-death, care, anxiety
- Authenticity vs. inauthenticity
- Critique of Western metaphysics as forgetting Being
- Later work: Poetry, technology, language ("language is house of Being")

**Existentialism**

**Jean-Paul Sartre (1905-1980)**
- "Existence precedes essence"
- Radical freedom: We are "condemned to be free"
- Bad faith: Denying own freedom
- "Hell is other people" (*No Exit*)
- Engaged philosophy: Writers must be politically committed

**Albert Camus (1913-1960)**
- Absurdism: Life has no inherent meaning
- *The Myth of Sisyphus*: "Must imagine Sisyphus happy"
- Response to absurd: Revolt, freedom, passion
- Opposed to Sartre on political grounds

**Simone de Beauvoir (1908-1986)**
- *The Second Sex*: Foundational feminist text
- "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman"
- Women defined as "Other" to male subject
- Ambiguity of human existence

**Poststructuralism/Postmodernism**

**Michel Foucault (1926-1984)**
- Power/knowledge: Knowledge and power intertwined
- Disciplinary power: Surveillance, normalization (panopticon)
- Genealogical method: Historical analysis showing contingency of present
- Studies of madness, prisons, sexuality
- "Death of Man": Human as recent invention

**Jacques Derrida (1930-2004)**
- Deconstruction: Reading strategy revealing text's contradictions
- Critique of logocentrism and presence
- Différance: Meaning deferred and differed
- Binary oppositions (speech/writing) are hierarchical and unstable
- "There is nothing outside the text"

**Gilles Deleuze (1925-1995)**
- Philosophy of difference and becoming
- Rhizome vs. tree structure
- Nomadic thought
- Capitalism and schizophrenia (with Guattari)

**American Pragmatism**

**Charles Sanders Peirce (1839-1914)**
- Pragmatic maxim: Meaning = practical consequences
- Fallibilism: Knowledge always revisable
- Semiotics

**William James (1842-1910)**
- Pragmatic theory of truth: Truth is what works
- Radical empiricism
- *The Varieties of Religious Experience*: Psychology of religion
- "Will to believe": Sometimes justified to believe without sufficient evidence

**John Dewey (1859-1952)**
- Instrumentalism: Ideas are instruments for solving problems
- Democracy and education
- Naturalism: Continuity with science
- Experience-centered philosophy

## Comparison of Major Schools

| School | Period | Key Focus | Method | Representatives |
|--------|--------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Ancient Greek** | 600 BCE-400 CE | Nature, ethics, Forms | Dialectic, logic | Socrates, Plato, Aristotle |
| **Medieval** | 500-1400 | God, faith and reason | Scholasticism | Augustine, Aquinas |
| **Rationalism** | 1600-1700 | Reason, innate ideas | Deduction | Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz |
| **Empiricism** | 1600-1800 | Experience, observation | Induction | Locke, Berkeley, Hume |
| **German Idealism** | 1780-1830 | Mind, freedom, history | Dialectic | Kant, Hegel |
| **Existentialism** | 1900s | Existence, freedom, meaning | Phenomenology | Heidegger, Sartre, Camus |
| **Analytic** | 1900-present | Language, logic, science | Analysis | Russell, Wittgenstein, Quine |
| **Continental** | 1900-present | Experience, interpretation | Phenomenology, hermeneutics | Husserl, Foucault, Derrida |

## Key Terms

- **Pre-Socratic**: Early Greek philosophers before Socrates
- **Socratic Method**: Question-and-answer dialogue to expose contradictions
- **Forms (Plato)**: Perfect, eternal, unchanging realities
- **Rationalism**: Knowledge primarily through reason
- **Empiricism**: Knowledge primarily through experience
- **Phenomenology**: Study of structures of consciousness and experience
- **Existentialism**: Philosophy emphasizing individual existence, freedom, and meaning
- **Analytic Philosophy**: Emphasis on logic, language, and analysis
- **Continental Philosophy**: Emphasis on experience, history, and interpretation
- **Dialectic**: Process of thesis, antithesis, synthesis

## Summary

Western philosophy progresses through dialogue and debate across centuries. Ancient Greek philosophy established foundations: Socrates emphasized self-knowledge, Plato posited ideal Forms, Aristotle developed systematic empiricism and virtue ethics. Medieval philosophers integrated Greek thought with Christianity. Modern philosophy split into rationalism (Descartes, Spinoza) emphasizing reason and empiricism (Locke, Hume) emphasizing experience; Kant synthesized both. Hegel's idealism and dialectic influenced Marx's materialism. Nietzsche challenged traditional morality and values. Contemporary philosophy divided into analytic tradition (Russell, Wittgenstein) focusing on language and logic, and continental tradition (Heidegger, Sartre, Foucault) exploring existence, power, and interpretation. Each philosopher and movement contributed enduring questions and insights that continue to shape philosophical inquiry and inform how we understand knowledge, reality, ethics, and human existence.
