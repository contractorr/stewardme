# Epistemology: Theory of Knowledge

## Overview

Epistemology (from Greek "episteme" = knowledge, "logos" = study) is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, limits, and justification of knowledge. It asks: What can we know? How do we know it? What distinguishes knowledge from mere belief? These questions underlie all intellectual inquiry, from science to everyday decision-making.

## What is Knowledge?

### The Classical Definition: Justified True Belief (JTB)

Traditionally, knowledge has been defined as:

**S knows that P if and only if:**
1. P is true
2. S believes that P
3. S is justified in believing that P

**Example:**
- Sarah knows the bank is open on Saturday if:
  1. The bank IS open on Saturday (truth)
  2. Sarah believes the bank is open (belief)
  3. Sarah has good reason for this belief, like checking the website (justification)

### The Gettier Problem

In 1963, Edmund Gettier showed that JTB is insufficient with counterexamples:

**Gettier Case Example:**
- Smith believes "The person who will get the job has 10 coins in their pocket"
- Smith is justified because the boss told him Jones will get the job, and Smith counted 10 coins in Jones's pocket
- But Smith gets the job, and by coincidence, Smith also has 10 coins
- Smith's belief is justified and true, but it seems wrong to say he "knows" it

This suggests knowledge requires something beyond JTB—perhaps the right kind of connection between belief and truth.

### Theories Beyond JTB

| Theory | Addition to JTB | Example |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| **No-False-Lemmas** | Justification must not rely on false steps | No false intermediate beliefs |
| **Causal Theory** | Belief must be causally connected to fact | Seeing the fire causes belief in fire |
| **Reliabilism** | Belief formed through reliable process | Perception in good conditions |
| **Virtue Epistemology** | Belief results from intellectual virtues | Careful observation, critical thinking |

## Types of Knowledge

### 1. Propositional Knowledge (Know-That)

Knowledge of facts and truths.
- "I know that Paris is in France"
- "I know that 2+2=4"
- Subject of most epistemological discussion

### 2. Procedural Knowledge (Know-How)

Knowledge of how to do something.
- "I know how to ride a bike"
- "I know how to solve quadratic equations"
- May not be reducible to propositional knowledge

### 3. Knowledge by Acquaintance

Direct experiential knowledge.
- "I know what pain feels like"
- "I know Paris" (have been there)
- Phenomenological and subjective

### 4. A Priori vs. A Posteriori Knowledge

| A Priori | A Posteriori |
|----------|--------------|
| Independent of experience | Dependent on experience |
| Known through reason alone | Known through observation |
| "All bachelors are unmarried" | "The sky is blue" |
| Necessarily true | Contingently true |
| Mathematical truths | Scientific facts |

## Sources of Knowledge

### 1. Perception (Empirical Evidence)

Knowledge through the five senses.

**Strengths:**
- Direct contact with the world
- Testable and repeatable
- Foundation of science

**Challenges:**
- Sensory illusions (e.g., optical illusions)
- Perceptual relativity (different observers, different experiences)
- Question: Can we trust our senses?

### 2. Reason (Rational Insight)

Knowledge through logical thinking and deduction.

**Strengths:**
- Provides certain knowledge (mathematics, logic)
- Not dependent on potentially deceptive senses
- Universal truths

**Challenges:**
- Limited to abstract truths
- Cannot tell us about the physical world alone
- Question: Are all truths accessible to reason?

### 3. Testimony

Knowledge from what others tell us.

**Strengths:**
- Most of our knowledge comes from testimony
- Allows accumulation of knowledge across generations
- Efficient learning

**Challenges:**
- Depends on others' reliability
- Can propagate errors
- Question: When should we trust others?

### 4. Memory

Knowledge retained from past experience.

**Strengths:**
- Essential for learning and identity
- Allows use of past knowledge

**Challenges:**
- Memories can be false or distorted
- Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive
- Question: How reliable is memory?

### 5. Introspection

Knowledge of one's own mental states.

**Strengths:**
- Direct access to own thoughts
- Cartesian certainty about own existence

**Challenges:**
- Self-deception is possible
- Unconscious mental processes
- Question: Do we have privileged access to our minds?

## Truth

### What is Truth?

**Correspondence Theory**: Truth is correspondence with reality
- "Snow is white" is true if and only if snow is white
- Most intuitive theory

**Coherence Theory**: Truth is coherence with a system of beliefs
- A belief is true if it fits consistently with other accepted beliefs
- Problem: Which system?

**Pragmatic Theory**: Truth is what works in practice
- True beliefs lead to successful action
- Problem: Something can work without being true

**Deflationary Theory**: "True" adds nothing to a statement
- Saying "it's true that snow is white" = saying "snow is white"
- Truth is not a substantial property

## Belief

### What is Belief?

Mental state of accepting a proposition as true.

**Characteristics:**
- **Degrees**: Beliefs can be held with varying confidence
- **Voluntary?**: Can we choose what to believe?
- **Rational**: Should be proportioned to evidence

### Belief vs. Knowledge

| Belief | Knowledge |
|--------|-----------|
| Subjective mental state | Belief + truth + justification |
| Can be false | Must be true |
| No justification required | Requires justification |
| "I believe it's raining" | "I know it's raining" |

## Rationalism vs. Empiricism

Two competing theories about the primary source of knowledge.

### Rationalism

**Core Claim**: Reason is the primary source of knowledge; significant knowledge can be gained independent of sensory experience.

**Key Philosophers:**
- **Plato**: Knowledge is recollection of innate ideas; Forms accessible through reason
- **Descartes**: "Cogito ergo sum" (I think, therefore I am); clear and distinct ideas
- **Spinoza**: Geometric method; all knowledge derived from first principles
- **Leibniz**: Innate ideas; necessary truths known through reason

**Arguments For:**
- Mathematical and logical truths known without experience
- Universal truths transcend particular experiences
- Senses can deceive, reason provides certainty

**Arguments Against:**
- How do we access innate ideas?
- How does pure reason connect to physical world?
- Seems to limit knowledge to abstract truths

### Empiricism

**Core Claim**: Experience is the primary source of knowledge; all knowledge ultimately derives from sensory experience.

**Key Philosophers:**
- **Aristotle**: Knowledge begins with observation
- **Locke**: Mind is a "blank slate" (tabula rasa); no innate ideas
- **Berkeley**: "To be is to be perceived"; all reality is mental
- **Hume**: All ideas derive from impressions; skepticism about causation

**Arguments For:**
- Scientific method based on observation
- Children learn through experience
- Abstract ideas ultimately trace to sensory experience

**Arguments Against:**
- Mathematical truths seem independent of experience
- Cannot explain necessity and universality
- Problem of induction (Hume)

### Comparison Table

| Issue | Rationalism | Empiricism |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Primary source | Reason | Experience |
| Innate ideas | Yes | No |
| Knowledge of world | Through reason | Through senses |
| Mathematical knowledge | Paradigm of knowledge | Abstraction from experience |
| Certainty | Possible through reason | Difficult to achieve |
| Method | Deduction | Induction |

### Synthesis: Kant's Critical Philosophy

Kant attempted to synthesize rationalism and empiricism:
- **A priori** concepts (categories) organize **a posteriori** sensory data
- "Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind"
- We have knowledge of how things appear (phenomena), not things-in-themselves (noumena)

## Skepticism

The view that knowledge is impossible or that we should suspend judgment.

### Types of Skepticism

**Global Skepticism**: We can know nothing
- Most extreme form
- Self-defeating? (To know we know nothing is to know something)

**Local Skepticism**: We cannot know specific domains
- Skepticism about the external world
- Skepticism about other minds
- Skepticism about the future

**Methodological Skepticism**: Temporarily doubt everything to find certainty
- Descartes's method
- Tool for inquiry, not a conclusion

### Skeptical Challenges

**1. The Dream Argument (Descartes)**
- How do you know you're not dreaming right now?
- Dreams can feel real while we're in them
- Response: Coherence, continuity, testability distinguish waking

**2. The Evil Demon/Brain in a Vat**
- What if an evil demon is deceiving you about everything?
- What if you're a brain in a vat receiving simulated experiences?
- Response: These scenarios are unfalsifiable but unproductive

**3. Problem of Induction (Hume)**
- Past experience doesn't logically guarantee future
- The sun has risen every day, but that doesn't prove it will tomorrow
- Response: Induction is pragmatically justified even if not logically

**4. The Regress Problem**
- Justification requires further justification, which requires further justification...
- Three responses:
  - **Foundationalism**: Some beliefs are basic, need no justification
  - **Coherentism**: Beliefs justify each other in a web
  - **Infinitism**: Infinite regress is acceptable

### Real-World Example: Climate Change Skepticism

Different types of skepticism apply:
- **Reasonable skepticism**: Examining evidence carefully, checking sources
- **Unreasonable skepticism**: Rejecting overwhelming evidence without justification
- Epistemology helps distinguish legitimate questioning from denial

## Justification

What makes a belief justified?

### Foundationalism

**Structure**: Pyramid
- Basic beliefs at foundation (self-evident, incorrigible)
- Non-basic beliefs justified by basic beliefs
- **Example**: Descartes's "I think, therefore I am"

**Strengths**: Avoids infinite regress, provides certainty
**Challenges**: What counts as basic? Are basic beliefs really unjustified?

### Coherentism

**Structure**: Web or network
- Beliefs justified by mutual support within system
- No foundational beliefs
- **Example**: Scientific theories supported by multiple pieces of evidence

**Strengths**: Reflects holistic nature of knowledge
**Challenges**: Coherent systems can be false; which system?

### Reliabilism

**Structure**: Process-based
- Belief justified if produced by reliable process
- Focus on how belief is formed, not internal states
- **Example**: Vision in good lighting is reliable

**Strengths**: Explains animal and child knowledge
**Challenges**: Defining "reliable"; lacks internal perspective

### Internalism vs. Externalism

| Internalism | Externalism |
|-------------|-------------|
| Justification accessible to knower | Justification need not be accessible |
| Can determine if justified through reflection | External factors determine justification |
| Emphasizes reasons | Emphasizes reliability |
| Coherentism, Foundationalism | Reliabilism |

## Contextualism

Knowledge claims depend on context.

**Example**: "I know I have hands"
- True in ordinary context
- Questionable in philosophical skepticism context
- Different standards apply in different situations

**Application**: Legal contexts require higher standards than casual conversation.

## Key Terms

- **Epistemology**: Theory of knowledge
- **Justified True Belief**: Classical definition of knowledge
- **Gettier Problem**: Challenge to JTB showing it's insufficient
- **A Priori**: Knowledge independent of experience
- **A Posteriori**: Knowledge dependent on experience
- **Rationalism**: Reason is primary source of knowledge
- **Empiricism**: Experience is primary source of knowledge
- **Skepticism**: Doubt or denial that knowledge is possible
- **Foundationalism**: Justification rests on basic beliefs
- **Coherentism**: Justification through mutual support of beliefs
- **Reliabilism**: Justification through reliable processes

## Summary

Epistemology explores the nature, sources, and limits of human knowledge. The classical definition of knowledge as justified true belief faces challenges from Gettier cases, leading to refined theories. Knowledge can be a priori or a posteriori, propositional or procedural. The rationalist-empiricist debate concerns whether reason or experience is the primary source of knowledge—Kant synthesized both views. Skepticism, from ancient to modern forms, questions whether knowledge is possible at all, raising challenges like the dream argument and problem of induction. Theories of justification—foundationalism, coherentism, and reliabilism—offer different accounts of what makes beliefs warranted. Understanding epistemology sharpens our ability to evaluate claims, recognize the basis of our beliefs, and appreciate both the power and limits of human knowledge.
