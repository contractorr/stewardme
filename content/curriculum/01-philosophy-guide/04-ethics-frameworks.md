# Ethics: Moral Frameworks

## Overview

Ethics (moral philosophy) is the systematic study of what makes actions right or wrong, what makes people good or bad, and how we ought to live. Unlike descriptive ethics (what people believe) or meta-ethics (what moral terms mean), normative ethics prescribes how we should act. This guide covers major ethical frameworks that provide different answers to moral questions.

## The Central Questions of Ethics

1. **What should I do?** (Normative ethics)
2. **What kind of person should I be?** (Virtue ethics)
3. **What is the good life?** (Well-being and human flourishing)
4. **What do I owe to others?** (Moral obligations)
5. **How do we resolve moral conflicts?** (Moral reasoning)

## Major Ethical Theories

| Theory | Focus | Key Question | Decision Method |
|--------|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Utilitarianism** | Consequences | What produces most happiness? | Maximize overall well-being |
| **Deontology** | Duties/Rules | What is my duty? | Follow moral rules |
| **Virtue Ethics** | Character | What would a virtuous person do? | Cultivate virtues |
| **Social Contract** | Agreement | What would we agree to? | Mutual benefit and fairness |
| **Care Ethics** | Relationships | How do I care for others? | Maintain relationships |

## Consequentialism and Utilitarianism

### Core Principle

**Right action = action that produces the best consequences**

The morality of an act depends entirely on its outcomes, not on the act itself or the actor's intentions.

### Classical Utilitarianism

**Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)**
- Principle of utility: Greatest happiness for greatest number
- Happiness = pleasure, unhappiness = pain
- **Hedonic calculus**: Quantify pleasure and pain by intensity, duration, certainty, etc.
- All pleasures equal in quality ("pushpin is as good as poetry")

**John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)**
- Refined Bentham: Some pleasures are higher quality
- "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied"
- Qualitative distinction: Intellectual/moral pleasures > bodily pleasures
- Developed harm principle: Liberty until it harms others

### Types of Utilitarianism

**Act Utilitarianism**
- Evaluate each act by its consequences
- Do whatever produces most utility in this situation
- Flexible but potentially time-consuming

**Rule Utilitarianism**
- Evaluate rules by their consequences
- Follow rules that generally produce most utility
- More practical, respects moral rules

**Preference Utilitarianism (Peter Singer)**
- Maximize satisfaction of preferences, not just pleasure
- Respects individual autonomy
- Can accommodate diverse values

### Applying Utilitarian Reasoning

**Example 1: Organ Donation**
- Five patients need organs to live; one healthy person could provide all five
- Act utilitarian calculation: 5 lives saved > 1 life lost = harvest organs?
- Most would reject this, showing limits of simple utility

**Example 2: Lie to Spare Feelings**
- Friend asks if their artwork is good; it's terrible
- Utilitarian calculus: Small lie prevents significant pain
- Truth might cause short-term pain but long-term benefit
- Rule utilitarian: General rule against lying produces better consequences

**Example 3: Environmental Policy**
- Carbon tax causes short-term economic pain
- Long-term consequences: Prevent climate disaster
- Utilitarian: Massive future suffering prevented justifies current cost
- Must weigh: certainty, temporal distance, distribution

### Strengths of Utilitarianism

1. **Impartial**: Everyone's well-being counts equally
2. **Rational**: Based on measurable outcomes
3. **Flexible**: Adapts to circumstances
4. **Progressive**: Challenges traditional prejudices
5. **Practical**: Widely used in policy analysis (cost-benefit analysis)

### Criticisms of Utilitarianism

1. **Demandingness**: Requires constant self-sacrifice for greater good
2. **Calculation Problems**: Can't always predict consequences
3. **Rights Violations**: Could justify punishing innocents if it maximizes utility
4. **Justice Problems**: Might justify unequal distribution
5. **Integrity**: Ignores personal projects and commitments
6. **Measurement**: How do we quantify happiness across people?

**Bernard Williams's Integrity Objection**:
If utilitarianism demands you violate your core commitments for marginal utility gains, it denies your integrity and makes you merely a vehicle for producing good.

## Deontological Ethics (Kant)

### Core Principle

**Right action = action that follows moral duty, regardless of consequences**

Morality based on rules, duties, and rights. Some acts are inherently right or wrong.

### Immanuel Kant (1724-1804)

**Key Concepts:**

**1. Good Will**
- Only thing good without qualification
- Acting from duty, not merely in accordance with duty
- Motive matters more than outcome

**2. The Categorical Imperative**

Three formulations (all equivalent for Kant):

**Formula of Universal Law**: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."

**Example: Lying**
- Maxim: "When it benefits me, I'll lie"
- Universalize: Everyone lies when beneficial
- Result: Trust breaks down, lying becomes pointless
- Conclusion: Lying is wrong because the maxim is self-contradictory when universalized

**Formula of Humanity**: "Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of another, always at the same time as an end and never simply as a means."

**Example: Using People**
- Wrong: Manipulating someone for your benefit (treating as mere means)
- Right: Asking someone for help, respecting their choice (treating as end)
- Humans have dignity, not just price

**Formula of Autonomy**: "Act as if you were legislating universal laws for a kingdom of ends."
- Moral agents are self-governing
- Laws we'd agree to if everyone was rational and free

**3. Autonomy and Reason**
- Humans are rational beings capable of self-governance
- Morality comes from reason, not desire or emotion
- Freedom is acting according to rational moral law

### Perfect vs. Imperfect Duties

| Perfect Duties | Imperfect Duties |
|----------------|------------------|
| Strict, no exceptions | Allow discretion in how fulfilled |
| Negative (don't do X) | Positive (do Y) |
| Don't lie, don't kill | Help others, develop talents |
| Specific actions prohibited | General principles to follow |

### Applying Kantian Ethics

**Example 1: Breaking a Promise**
- Maxim: "Break promises when inconvenient"
- Universalized: Promises become meaningless
- Conclusion: Keep promises (self-contradictory to break)

**Example 2: Suicide**
- Kant: Using self-preservation instinct to destroy yourself is contradiction
- Treating yourself as mere means to end suffering
- Controversial: Critics question whether autonomy includes right to end life

**Example 3: Helping Others**
- Imperfect duty to beneficence
- Can't universalize maxim of never helping (society couldn't function)
- But have discretion in how and when to help

### Strengths of Deontology

1. **Respects Rights**: People have dignity, not just utility
2. **Clear Principles**: Categorical rules guide action
3. **Intentions Matter**: Recognizes moral worth of motive
4. **Universal**: Applies to all rational beings
5. **Justice**: Treats people as equals, not sacrificing some for others

### Criticisms of Deontology

1. **Rigidity**: Absolute rules seem inflexible (lie to save a life?)
2. **Conflicting Duties**: What when duties conflict?
3. **Consequences Ignored**: Shouldn't outcomes matter at all?
4. **Abstract**: Difficult to apply in concrete situations
5. **Derivation Problems**: How do we derive specific duties from categorical imperative?

**Example of Conflict**: Nazi at door asking if you're hiding Jews
- Kant notoriously said you must tell truth
- Critics: Surely lying is right here?
- Modern Kantians: More sophisticated interpretation of duties

## Virtue Ethics

### Core Principle

**Right action = what a virtuous person would do; focus on character, not rules or consequences**

Ethics about being a good person, not just doing good acts.

### Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

**Key Concepts:**

**1. Eudaimonia (Human Flourishing)**
- Ultimate goal: Living well, flourishing
- Not just happiness (feeling) but objective well-being
- Achieved through virtuous life

**2. Virtue (Arete)**
- Excellence of character
- Acquired through practice and habituation
- Like learning an instrument: practice until it's second nature

**3. The Doctrine of the Mean**
- Virtue is the middle ground between two vices
- Not mathematical average, but appropriate response
- Requires practical wisdom (phronesis)

**Examples of Virtues:**

| Vice (Deficiency) | Virtue (Mean) | Vice (Excess) |
|-------------------|---------------|---------------|
| Cowardice | Courage | Recklessness |
| Stinginess | Generosity | Wastefulness |
| Self-deprecation | Proper pride | Arrogance |
| Insensibility | Temperance | Self-indulgence |
| Surliness | Friendliness | Obsequiousness |

**4. Practical Wisdom (Phronesis)**
- Ability to deliberate well about what is good
- Not just theoretical knowledge but practical judgment
- Learned through experience
- Necessary for all other virtues

**5. The Good Life**
- Rational activity of the soul in accordance with virtue
- Requires external goods: health, friends, reasonable wealth
- Social and political: Humans are "political animals"

### Modern Virtue Ethics

**Alasdair MacIntyre**
- Virtues defined by social practices and traditions
- Critique of modern morality as fragmented

**Rosalind Hursthouse**
- Neo-Aristotelian approach
- Virtues benefit both possessor and community

**Martha Nussbaum**
- Capabilities approach
- Universal human functions and needs

### Applying Virtue Ethics

**Example 1: Workplace Dilemma**
- Discover colleague stealing
- What would honest, loyal, fair person do?
- Consider relationships, circumstances, character development
- Balance: Loyalty (to colleague) vs. Justice (to company)

**Example 2: Environmental Action**
- Not just "is this required?" but "what kind of person do I want to be?"
- Cultivate virtue of stewardship, temperance
- Actions flow from character, not calculation

**Example 3: Social Media**
- How should virtuous person engage?
- Temperance: Not excessive scrolling
- Honesty: Authentic self-presentation
- Kindness: Thoughtful communication

### Strengths of Virtue Ethics

1. **Holistic**: Concerned with whole life, not just isolated acts
2. **Practical**: Based on human psychology and development
3. **Flexible**: Context-sensitive through practical wisdom
4. **Motivating**: Aspire to be excellent, not just follow rules
5. **Relational**: Emphasizes relationships and community

### Criticisms of Virtue Ethics

1. **Guidance**: What should I do right now? (Less action-guiding)
2. **Disagreement**: Which traits are virtues? (Cultural variation)
3. **Circularity**: Virtuous act = what virtuous person does; who is virtuous?
4. **Moral Dilemmas**: Doesn't resolve conflicts between virtues
5. **Privilege**: Assumes circumstances that allow virtue development

## Applied Ethics: Case Studies

### Bioethics

**Euthanasia:**
- **Utilitarian**: Does it reduce suffering overall?
- **Kantian**: Autonomy vs. sanctity of life; using person as means?
- **Virtue**: What does compassion require? What about integrity of medicine?

**Allocation of Scarce Medical Resources:**
- **Utilitarian**: Save most lives or most life-years
- **Kantian**: Respect equal dignity; lottery or first-come-first-serve?
- **Virtue**: What does justice and care require?

### Business Ethics

**Whistleblowing:**
- **Utilitarian**: Harm prevented vs. harm caused (job loss, company damage)
- **Kantian**: Duty to truth; complicity in wrongdoing
- **Virtue**: Courage to speak up; loyalty to employer vs. broader community

**Corporate Responsibility:**
- **Utilitarian**: Maximize stakeholder well-being
- **Kantian**: Don't use employees/customers merely as means to profit
- **Virtue**: What does corporate character look like? Integrity, trustworthiness

### Environmental Ethics

**Climate Change:**
- **Utilitarian**: Massive future suffering vs. current economic costs
- **Kantian**: Duty not to harm; respect for future generations' autonomy
- **Virtue**: Stewardship, temperance, practical wisdom

**Animal Rights:**
- **Utilitarian** (Singer): Animals feel pain; equal interests count equally
- **Kantian**: Indirect duties (animals not rational); but don't be cruel
- **Virtue**: Compassion extends to animals; what does kindness require?

### Technology Ethics

**AI and Privacy:**
- **Utilitarian**: Benefits of data collection vs. privacy harms
- **Kantian**: Respect for persons requires respecting privacy
- **Virtue**: What does trustworthiness require of tech companies?

## Other Ethical Frameworks

### Care Ethics

**Developed by**: Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings

**Core Ideas:**
- Morality rooted in relationships and caring
- Context-specific, particularist
- Critique of abstract, impartial principles
- Emphasis on empathy, compassion, responsibility

**Example**: Caring for aging parent requires attention to specific needs and relationship, not just applying rules.

### Social Contract Theory

**Key Thinkers**: Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls

**Core Ideas:**
- Morality based on agreement among rational agents
- What rules would we agree to?
- Balance of self-interest and cooperation

**Rawls's Veil of Ignorance**:
- Choose principles of justice not knowing your position in society
- Leads to: Equal basic liberties, fair equality of opportunity, help worst-off

### Rights-Based Ethics

**Core Ideas:**
- Individuals have fundamental rights (life, liberty, property)
- Rights create duties in others
- Rights trump utility considerations

**Example**: Even if torturing suspect would save lives (utilitarian calculus), right against torture is inviolable.

### Divine Command Theory

**Core Ideas:**
- Morally right = commanded by God
- Morally wrong = forbidden by God

**Euthyphro Dilemma**: Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it's good?

## Comparing Frameworks

**Scenario**: Should you steal medicine to save a dying person?

| Framework | Analysis | Likely Conclusion |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **Act Utilitarian** | Life saved > property taken | Yes, steal |
| **Rule Utilitarian** | Rule against stealing produces better consequences overall | Probably no |
| **Kantian** | Can't universalize stealing; but also duty to help? | Conflicted (maybe treat as imperfect duty) |
| **Virtue Ethics** | What would compassionate, just person do in context? | Depends on circumstances, relationships |
| **Care Ethics** | Relationship to dying person, context of care | Likely yes if person you care for |

## Moral Relativism vs. Moral Objectivism

**Moral Relativism**: Moral truths are relative to culture, individual, or context
- **Cultural**: What's right varies by culture
- **Individual**: Each person determines their own morality

**Moral Objectivism**: Some moral truths are universal
- **Absolutism**: Moral rules have no exceptions
- **Moderate**: Universal principles but context matters

**Arguments for Relativism:**
- Cultural diversity in moral beliefs
- No way to prove moral claims like factual claims

**Arguments Against Relativism:**
- Some practices (slavery, genocide) seem objectively wrong
- Relativism seems self-defeating (is relativism itself relative?)
- Moral progress and reform require objective standards

## Key Terms

- **Ethics**: Study of morality and how we should live
- **Utilitarianism**: Right action maximizes overall well-being
- **Deontology**: Right action follows moral duty regardless of consequences
- **Categorical Imperative**: Kant's principle for determining moral duties
- **Virtue Ethics**: Focus on character and what a virtuous person would do
- **Eudaimonia**: Human flourishing or living well (Aristotle)
- **Doctrine of the Mean**: Virtue as the middle ground between extremes
- **Care Ethics**: Morality rooted in relationships and caring
- **Moral Relativism**: Morality is relative to culture or individual
- **Applied Ethics**: Application of ethical theory to specific issues

## Summary

Ethics provides frameworks for determining right and wrong action. Consequentialism, particularly utilitarianism, judges acts by their outcomes—the greatest good for the greatest number. Deontological ethics, exemplified by Kant, focuses on duty and universal moral laws derived from reason, treating persons as ends in themselves. Virtue ethics shifts focus from acts to character, asking what kind of person we should be and emphasizing practical wisdom and human flourishing. Each framework has strengths: utilitarianism's impartiality and practicality, deontology's respect for rights and dignity, virtue ethics' holistic view of moral life. Each also faces criticisms regarding demandingness, rigidity, or lack of action-guidance. In applied ethics—bioethics, business, environmental issues—these frameworks provide different perspectives on concrete dilemmas. No single framework resolves all moral questions, but together they provide tools for moral reasoning and help us think more carefully about how to live ethical lives.
