# Philosophy Glossary: Key Terms Quick Reference

## Overview

This glossary provides concise definitions of essential philosophical terms organized by subject area. Use this as a quick reference when reading philosophical texts or reviewing concepts.

---

## General Philosophical Terms

**Philosophy**: Love of wisdom; systematic study of fundamental questions about reality, knowledge, values, reason, mind, and existence.

**Argument**: Set of statements (premises) offered to support a conclusion.

**Premise**: Statement offered as evidence or reason in an argument.

**Conclusion**: Statement that premises are intended to support.

**Valid**: Property of deductive argument where conclusion necessarily follows from premises.

**Sound**: Valid argument with all true premises.

**Cogent**: Strong inductive argument with true premises.

**Fallacy**: Error in reasoning that undermines logical validity.

**Dialectic**: Method of philosophical investigation through dialogue and questioning.

**Analytic Statement**: True by definition; meaning contained in the terms ("All bachelors are unmarried").

**Synthetic Statement**: Truth depends on facts about the world ("The sky is blue").

**Necessary Truth**: Could not be false; true in all possible worlds ("2+2=4").

**Contingent Truth**: Could be false; depends on how world happens to be ("It's raining").

**Thought Experiment**: Hypothetical scenario used to test philosophical ideas.

## Logic and Reasoning

**Logic**: Study of correct reasoning and valid inference.

**Deductive Reasoning**: Reasoning from general to specific; aims for certainty.

**Inductive Reasoning**: Reasoning from specific to general; aims for probability.

**Abductive Reasoning**: Inference to best explanation.

**Validity**: In deductive logic, conclusion follows necessarily from premises.

**Soundness**: Valid deductive argument with true premises.

**Syllogism**: Three-part deductive argument with major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.

**Modus Ponens**: If P then Q; P; therefore Q.

**Modus Tollens**: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.

**Truth Table**: Chart showing all possible truth values for logical propositions.

**Ad Hominem**: Fallacy attacking person rather than argument.

**Strawman**: Fallacy misrepresenting opponent's argument.

**Slippery Slope**: Fallacy claiming small step leads inevitably to extreme outcome.

**Begging the Question**: Circular reasoning where premise assumes conclusion.

**False Dilemma**: Fallacy presenting only two options when more exist.

**Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**: Fallacy assuming causation from temporal succession ("after this, therefore because of this").

**Red Herring**: Fallacy introducing irrelevant information to distract.

**Appeal to Authority**: Fallacy citing inappropriate or false authority.

**Hasty Generalization**: Fallacy drawing conclusion from insufficient sample.

## Epistemology (Theory of Knowledge)

**Epistemology**: Branch of philosophy studying nature, sources, and limits of knowledge.

**Knowledge**: Traditionally, justified true belief (with Gettier complications).

**Belief**: Mental state of accepting proposition as true.

**Truth**: Correspondence with reality (correspondence theory); coherence with belief system (coherence theory); practical success (pragmatic theory).

**Justification**: Reasons or evidence supporting a belief.

**Gettier Problem**: Challenge showing justified true belief insufficient for knowledge.

**A Priori Knowledge**: Knowledge independent of experience (mathematics, logic).

**A Posteriori Knowledge**: Knowledge dependent on experience (empirical facts).

**Rationalism**: View that reason is primary source of knowledge; significant knowledge can be gained independent of experience.

**Empiricism**: View that experience is primary source of knowledge; all knowledge derives from sensory experience.

**Skepticism**: View that knowledge is impossible or we should suspend judgment.

**Foundationalism**: Theory that knowledge has foundation of basic beliefs requiring no justification.

**Coherentism**: Theory that beliefs are justified by mutual support within a system.

**Reliabilism**: Theory that beliefs are justified if produced by reliable cognitive processes.

**Internalism**: Justification must be accessible to the knower's consciousness.

**Externalism**: Justification can depend on factors outside the knower's awareness.

**Contextualism**: Knowledge claims depend on conversational context and standards.

**Phenomenology**: Study of structures of consciousness and experience from first-person perspective.

**Propositional Knowledge**: Knowledge that something is the case (know-that).

**Procedural Knowledge**: Knowledge of how to do something (know-how).

**Knowledge by Acquaintance**: Direct experiential knowledge.

## Metaphysics

**Metaphysics**: Branch of philosophy investigating fundamental nature of reality.

**Ontology**: Theory of what exists; study of being.

**Being**: Existence; what it means to be.

**Substance**: That which exists independently; bearer of properties.

**Property/Attribute**: Quality or characteristic of a thing.

**Essence**: What makes something the kind of thing it is.

**Accident**: Property not essential to a thing.

**Existence**: The state of being real or actual.

**Reality**: All that exists; the totality of what is real.

**Materialism/Physicalism**: View that only physical matter exists.

**Idealism**: View that reality is fundamentally mental or idea-based.

**Dualism**: View that two fundamentally different kinds of things exist (typically mind and matter).

**Monism**: View that only one kind of thing exists.

**Pluralism**: View that multiple fundamentally different kinds of things exist.

**Particulars**: Individual, concrete things (this apple, that person).

**Universals**: General properties or types (redness, humanity) that can be instantiated by multiple particulars.

**Realism (about universals)**: Universals exist independently of particulars.

**Nominalism**: Only particulars exist; universals are just names.

**Conceptualism**: Universals exist as concepts in minds.

**Causation**: Relation between cause and effect; how one event brings about another.

**Determinism**: View that all events are caused by prior events according to natural laws.

**Indeterminism**: Some events are not determined by prior causes.

**Free Will**: Ability to act without being determined by prior causes.

**Compatibilism**: Free will and determinism are compatible.

**Libertarian Free Will**: Free will is incompatible with determinism; we have genuine free will.

**Hard Determinism**: Determinism is true and incompatible with free will; therefore no free will.

**Identity**: What makes something the same thing over time.

**Persistence**: How things remain the same through change.

**Temporal Parts**: Theory that objects have different parts at different times.

## Philosophy of Mind

**Philosophy of Mind**: Study of nature of mind, mental states, consciousness, and relation to body.

**Mind-Body Problem**: How mental relates to physical.

**Consciousness**: Subjective experience; what it's like to be someone.

**Qualia**: Subjective, qualitative aspects of experience (what red looks like, what pain feels like).

**Hard Problem of Consciousness**: Explaining why there is subjective experience at all.

**Intentionality**: Aboutness; mental states are directed toward objects or states of affairs.

**Substance Dualism**: Mind and body are separate substances (Descartes).

**Property Dualism**: One substance with mental and physical properties.

**Identity Theory**: Mental states are identical to brain states.

**Functionalism**: Mental states defined by functional role, not physical makeup.

**Eliminative Materialism**: Folk psychology will be eliminated; no such things as beliefs, desires.

**Panpsychism**: Consciousness is fundamental feature of all matter.

**Zombie**: Hypothetical being physically identical to human but lacking consciousness.

**Chinese Room**: Thought experiment challenging strong AI (Searle).

## Ethics

**Ethics**: Branch of philosophy studying morality, right and wrong, good and bad, virtue and vice.

**Morality**: Principles distinguishing right from wrong, good from bad.

**Normative Ethics**: Study of how we ought to act; prescriptive ethical theories.

**Meta-Ethics**: Study of nature of moral judgments and properties.

**Applied Ethics**: Application of ethical theory to specific issues.

**Value**: What is good, worthwhile, or desirable.

**Virtue**: Excellence of character; trait disposed toward good action.

**Vice**: Character flaw or deficiency.

**Right Action**: Morally correct or permissible action.

**Wrong Action**: Morally incorrect or prohibited action.

**Obligation/Duty**: What morality requires us to do.

**Supererogatory**: Actions beyond duty; praiseworthy but not required.

**Moral Agent**: Being capable of moral action and responsibility.

**Moral Patient**: Being toward whom we have moral obligations.

**Consequentialism**: Right action determined by consequences.

**Utilitarianism**: Right action maximizes overall happiness or well-being.

**Act Utilitarianism**: Evaluate each individual act by its consequences.

**Rule Utilitarianism**: Follow rules that generally produce best consequences.

**Greatest Happiness Principle**: Actions are right insofar as they promote happiness, wrong as they produce opposite.

**Hedonism**: Pleasure is the only intrinsic good.

**Deontology**: Right action based on duty and rules, regardless of consequences.

**Categorical Imperative**: Kant's principle for determining moral duties; act only on maxims you can will as universal law.

**Hypothetical Imperative**: Command conditional on desires ("If you want X, do Y").

**Kingdom of Ends**: Ideal community where everyone treats everyone as end, not mere means.

**Autonomy**: Self-governance; ability to act according to rational principles one gives oneself.

**Dignity**: Inherent worth requiring respect; humans have dignity, not price.

**Virtue Ethics**: Ethics focused on character and what virtuous person would do.

**Eudaimonia**: Human flourishing or living well (Aristotle).

**Arete**: Excellence or virtue.

**Phronesis**: Practical wisdom; knowing how to act virtuously in particular situations.

**Doctrine of the Mean**: Virtue as middle ground between extremes (Aristotle).

**Care Ethics**: Morality rooted in relationships, care, and responsibility to particular others.

**Social Contract Theory**: Morality and political legitimacy based on agreement among rational agents.

**Divine Command Theory**: Right action is what God commands.

**Moral Relativism**: Moral truths are relative to culture, society, or individual.

**Moral Objectivism**: Some moral truths are universal and independent of belief.

**Moral Absolutism**: Some moral rules have no exceptions.

## Political Philosophy

**Political Philosophy**: Study of government, justice, rights, law, and political obligation.

**State**: Political organization with sovereignty over territory.

**Government**: Institutions and processes for making and enforcing collective decisions.

**Legitimacy**: Right to rule; justified authority.

**Authority**: Right to command and be obeyed.

**Power**: Ability to influence or control others.

**Sovereignty**: Supreme authority within territory.

**State of Nature**: Hypothetical pre-political condition without government.

**Social Contract**: Agreement (actual or hypothetical) establishing political society and obligations.

**Civil Society**: Sphere of voluntary associations distinct from state and family.

**Justice**: Fair and appropriate treatment; giving each their due.

**Distributive Justice**: Fair distribution of goods, resources, and opportunities.

**Retributive Justice**: Fair punishment for wrongdoing.

**Procedural Justice**: Fairness of processes and rules.

**Rights**: Valid claims imposing duties on others.

**Natural Rights**: Rights inherent in human nature, independent of law.

**Human Rights**: Universal moral rights based on human dignity.

**Legal Rights**: Rights granted by law.

**Negative Rights**: Rights to non-interference (freedom from).

**Positive Rights**: Rights to provision of goods or services (freedom to).

**Liberty**: Freedom from constraint or interference.

**Negative Liberty**: Freedom from external interference.

**Positive Liberty**: Freedom to self-govern and fulfill potential.

**Equality**: Equal treatment, status, or distribution.

**Formal Equality**: Same rules for everyone.

**Substantive Equality**: Equal outcomes or real opportunities.

**Equality of Opportunity**: Equal chance to achieve positions.

**Equality of Outcome**: Equal distribution of goods.

**Democracy**: Government by the people, typically through majority rule and voting.

**Republic**: Government based on law and elected representatives rather than direct democracy.

**Liberalism**: Political philosophy emphasizing individual rights, liberty, and limited government.

**Conservatism**: Political philosophy emphasizing tradition, order, and gradual change.

**Libertarianism**: Political philosophy emphasizing maximum individual liberty and minimal state.

**Socialism**: Political and economic system with collective ownership of means of production.

**Communitarianism**: Political philosophy emphasizing community, tradition, and common good over individual rights.

**Cosmopolitanism**: View that all humans belong to single community with shared morality.

**Nationalism**: Priority to nation; identification with one's nation.

**Patriotism**: Love of or devotion to one's country.

**Law**: System of rules enforced through social institutions.

**Natural Law**: Moral law knowable through reason, reflecting human nature.

**Legal Positivism**: Law is what is posited (created) by legitimate authority, not necessarily moral.

**Rule of Law**: Governance by law rather than arbitrary decisions; laws apply equally to all.

**Separation of Powers**: Division of government into branches (legislative, executive, judicial).

**Civil Disobedience**: Deliberate, public violation of law to protest injustice.

## Major Philosophers and Schools (Quick Reference)

**Socrates** (469-399 BCE): Socratic method; examined life; virtue is knowledge.

**Plato** (428-348 BCE): Theory of Forms; ideal state; immortal soul.

**Aristotle** (384-322 BCE): Virtue ethics; logic; empirical observation; eudaimonia.

**Epicureanism**: Pursuit of pleasure (tranquility); atomism; withdraw from politics.

**Stoicism**: Live according to nature; focus on what you control; cosmopolitanism.

**Augustine** (354-430): Faith and reason; problem of evil; time; Christian Platonism.

**Aquinas** (1225-1274): Five Ways; natural law; synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity.

**Descartes** (1596-1650): Cogito ergo sum; methodological doubt; mind-body dualism; rationalism.

**Spinoza** (1632-1677): Monism; God/Nature; determinism; freedom through understanding.

**Locke** (1632-1704): Blank slate; empiricism; natural rights; social contract; limited government.

**Hume** (1711-1776): Radical empiricism; problem of induction; skepticism about causation; moral sentiments.

**Kant** (1724-1804): Categorical imperative; synthetic a priori; phenomena/noumena; transcendental idealism.

**Hegel** (1770-1831): Dialectic; Absolute Spirit; history as progress of freedom; idealism.

**Mill** (1806-1873): Utilitarianism; harm principle; liberty; higher and lower pleasures.

**Marx** (1818-1883): Historical materialism; class struggle; alienation; critique of capitalism.

**Nietzsche** (1844-1900): God is dead; will to power; master-slave morality; Übermensch; eternal recurrence.

**Russell** (1872-1970): Logic; logical atomism; analytic philosophy; philosophy of mathematics.

**Wittgenstein** (1889-1951): Early: Picture theory; Later: Language-games; meaning as use.

**Heidegger** (1889-1976): Being and Time; Dasein; authenticity; Being question.

**Sartre** (1905-1980): Existence precedes essence; radical freedom; bad faith; existentialism.

**Rawls** (1921-2002): Justice as fairness; veil of ignorance; difference principle.

**Analytic Philosophy**: Emphasis on logic, language, clarity, and scientific method (Russell, Wittgenstein, Quine).

**Continental Philosophy**: Emphasis on experience, interpretation, history, and critique (Heidegger, Sartre, Foucault, Derrida).

**Pragmatism**: Truth is what works; emphasis on practical consequences (Peirce, James, Dewey).

**Existentialism**: Individual existence, freedom, and meaning; existence precedes essence (Kierkegaard, Sartre, Camus).

**Phenomenology**: Study of structures of consciousness and experience (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty).

**Postmodernism/Poststructuralism**: Critique of grand narratives; deconstruction; power/knowledge (Foucault, Derrida, Lyotard).

## Specialized Terms by Topic

### Logic Symbols

- **∧**: And (conjunction)
- **∨**: Or (disjunction)
- **¬**: Not (negation)
- **→**: If...then (conditional)
- **↔**: If and only if (biconditional)
- **∀**: For all (universal quantifier)
- **∃**: There exists (existential quantifier)

### Time and Modality

**Presentism**: Only the present exists.

**Eternalism**: Past, present, and future all equally real (block universe).

**Growing Block**: Past and present exist; future doesn't.

**A-Theory**: Tensed time; objective past, present, future; time flows.

**B-Theory**: Tenseless time; only "earlier than" and "later than" relations; no flow.

**Possible Worlds**: Ways world could have been; used to analyze modality.

**Modal Realism**: All possible worlds are equally real (David Lewis).

**Necessary**: True in all possible worlds.

**Contingent**: True in some but not all possible worlds.

**Possible**: True in at least one possible world.

**Impossible**: True in no possible worlds.

### Philosophy of Language

**Meaning**: What a word or sentence signifies.

**Reference**: Object or entity a term stands for.

**Sense**: Mode of presentation of reference (Frege).

**Speech Act**: Action performed through utterance (Austin, Searle).

**Performative**: Utterance that performs action ("I promise").

**Analytic/Synthetic Distinction**: Analytic truths true by meaning; synthetic truths depend on facts.

**Use Theory of Meaning**: Meaning is use in language (Wittgenstein).

**Semantic**: Relating to meaning.

**Pragmatic**: Relating to use and context.

**Syntax**: Grammatical structure.

### Philosophy of Science

**Scientific Method**: Observation, hypothesis, testing, theory.

**Induction**: Inferring general principles from observations.

**Deduction**: Deriving predictions from general principles.

**Hypothesis**: Proposed explanation for phenomena.

**Theory**: Well-confirmed, systematic explanation.

**Law of Nature**: Regular pattern in nature; scientific generalization.

**Falsifiability**: Ability to be proven false; criterion of scientific theories (Popper).

**Paradigm**: Framework of concepts and methods in science (Kuhn).

**Paradigm Shift**: Revolutionary change in scientific framework.

**Reductionism**: Complex phenomena explainable by simpler components.

**Emergence**: Whole has properties not possessed by parts.

### Aesthetics

**Aesthetics**: Philosophy of art and beauty.

**Beauty**: Quality arousing pleasure, admiration, or satisfaction.

**Sublime**: Quality inspiring awe through vastness or power.

**Taste**: Faculty or standard for judging aesthetic value.

**Art**: Creative expression; culturally significant objects or performances.

**Aesthetic Experience**: Appreciation or contemplation of beauty or art.

**Formalism**: Aesthetic value in formal properties (shape, color, structure).

**Expression Theory**: Art expresses emotion.

**Institutional Theory**: Art is what art institutions designate as art.

## Common Latin Terms in Philosophy

**A fortiori**: With even stronger reason; "all the more so."

**A posteriori**: Based on experience.

**A priori**: Independent of experience.

**Ad hoc**: For this specific purpose; improvised.

**Ad infinitum**: To infinity; without end.

**Cogito ergo sum**: "I think, therefore I am" (Descartes).

**Ceteris paribus**: All other things being equal.

**De facto**: In fact; actual.

**De jure**: By law; rightful.

**Ex nihilo**: Out of nothing.

**Ipso facto**: By the fact itself; thereby.

**Modus ponens**: Mode of affirming (if P then Q; P; therefore Q).

**Modus tollens**: Mode of denying (if P then Q; not Q; therefore not P).

**Non sequitur**: "It does not follow"; conclusion doesn't follow from premises.

**Per se**: By itself; intrinsically.

**Petitio principii**: Begging the question; circular reasoning.

**Prima facie**: At first glance; on its face.

**Qua**: In the capacity of; as.

**Reductio ad absurdum**: Reduction to absurdity; proof by showing consequence is absurd.

**Sui generis**: Of its own kind; unique.

**Tabula rasa**: Blank slate (Locke's view of mind at birth).

**Terra incognita**: Unknown territory.

**Via**: By way of; through.

## Summary

This glossary covers essential philosophical terminology across major branches: logic, epistemology, metaphysics, ethics, political philosophy, and philosophy of mind. Understanding these terms provides foundation for reading philosophical texts and engaging with philosophical arguments. Philosophy builds on precise definitions and careful distinctions, so familiarity with key terms is essential for serious study. Use this glossary as ongoing reference when encountering unfamiliar terms or when you need quick clarification. Many terms have contested meanings or multiple interpretations—this glossary provides standard, widely-accepted definitions while recognizing ongoing philosophical debates about concepts themselves.
