# Climate Drivers

## Overview

Climate is determined by multiple natural and anthropogenic drivers operating across timescales from years to millions of years. Natural drivers include solar radiation variations, Earth's orbital cycles, volcanic eruptions, and ocean circulation patterns. Contemporary climate change is primarily driven by human greenhouse gas emissions overwhelming natural variability. Understanding these drivers helps distinguish natural climate variations from human-caused warming and predict future changes.

## Key Concepts

### Solar Radiation

**Solar Constant**
- Sun delivers ~1361 watts per square meter at Earth's distance (top of atmosphere)
- Earth's spherical shape and rotation mean average is ~340 W/m² when averaged globally
- About 30% reflected by clouds, ice, and surface; 70% absorbed

**Solar Cycles**
- 11-year sunspot cycle varies solar output by ~0.1%
- Climate influence: ~0.1°C temperature variation
- Too small to explain 20th-21st century warming
- Last solar maximum was 2014; declining phase since then while temperatures continue rising

**Solar Forcing vs Greenhouse Forcing**
| Factor | Change Since 1750 | Radiative Forcing |
|--------|------------------|------------------|
| Solar irradiance | +0.05% | +0.05 W/m² |
| Greenhouse gases | CO₂: 280→420 ppm | +3.0 W/m² |
| **Ratio** | | **Greenhouse gases 60x stronger** |

### Milankovitch Cycles

Long-term orbital variations affecting climate over tens of thousands of years:

**Eccentricity (100,000-year cycle)**
- Earth's orbit varies from nearly circular to slightly elliptical
- Currently moderate eccentricity, slowly decreasing
- Changes distance from Sun by ~6%, affecting seasonal intensity
- Weakest of three cycles but dominates ice age timing

**Axial Tilt/Obliquity (41,000-year cycle)**
- Tilt of Earth's axis varies between 22.1° and 24.5°
- Currently 23.5°, decreasing
- Greater tilt = more extreme seasons (hot summers, cold winters)
- Affects ice sheet growth/retreat by changing summer melting

**Precession (26,000-year cycle)**
- Direction of tilt wobbles like spinning top
- Changes which hemisphere has summer during closest approach to Sun
- Currently Northern Hemisphere summer occurs at farthest point (aphelion)
- In 13,000 years will reverse, intensifying NH summers

**Ice Age Connection**
- Cycles combine to control high-latitude summer insolation
- Cool summers prevent winter snow from melting → ice sheet growth
- Last glacial maximum: 20,000 years ago
- Current interglacial (Holocene) began 11,700 years ago
- Natural cycle suggests slow cooling toward next ice age over millennia
- Human greenhouse gas emissions overriding this natural trend

### Volcanic Activity

**Cooling Mechanism**
- Explosive eruptions inject sulfur dioxide into stratosphere
- Forms sulfate aerosol layer
- Reflects incoming solar radiation
- Cools surface but warms stratosphere

**Temporal Effects**
| Eruption Size | SO₂ Injected | Cooling | Duration |
|---------------|--------------|---------|----------|
| Small | <1 Mt | Negligible | Months |
| Moderate (Pinatubo 1991) | 20 Mt | 0.5°C | 1-2 years |
| Large (Tambora 1815) | 60 Mt | 0.7°C | 2-3 years |
| Super-eruption | >1000 Mt | >5°C | Decade+ |

**Cannot Explain Current Warming**
- Volcanic cooling is temporary (aerosols settle out within years)
- No unusual volcanic activity in recent decades
- Net volcanic effect is slight cooling, opposite of observed warming
- CO₂ from volcanoes (~0.3 Gt C/year) is ~3% of human emissions

### Ocean Circulation

**Thermohaline Circulation (Global Conveyor Belt)**
- Density-driven circulation connecting all ocean basins
- Cold, salty water sinks in North Atlantic and around Antarctica
- Drives deep ocean currents; surfaces in Pacific and Indian Oceans
- Full cycle takes ~1000 years
- Transports heat from equator toward poles

**El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO)**
- Irregular oscillation (2-7 years) between warm (El Niño) and cool (La Niña) phases
- Affects tropical Pacific Ocean temperatures and global weather

| Phase | Eastern Pacific | Global Effects |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| El Niño | Warm | Warmer global temps, droughts in Australia/Indonesia, floods in Americas |
| La Niña | Cool | Cooler global temps, wet in Australia/SE Asia, dry in Americas |
| Neutral | Average | Typical patterns |

- 2023-2024 strong El Niño contributed to record global temperatures
- Natural variability superimposed on warming trend
- Warmest El Niño years now hotter than past La Niña years

**Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)**
- Part of thermohaline circulation; transports warm water northward in Atlantic
- Releases heat, warming Europe by ~5°C compared to similar latitudes
- Sinking in Nordic seas drives circulation
- Freshwater from melting Greenland ice sheet could slow AMOC
- Slowdown would cool North Atlantic region, disrupt monsoons, shift tropical rainfall
- Evidence suggests AMOC weakening ~15% since mid-20th century

**Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO)**
- 20-30 year cycle of North Pacific temperature patterns
- Affects regional climate, fish populations, North American precipitation
- Interacts with ENSO; can enhance or dampen effects

### Greenhouse Gas Forcing

**Radiative Forcing Contributions (2019 vs 1750)**

| Driver | Forcing (W/m²) | Contribution |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| CO₂ | +2.16 | 66% |
| Methane | +0.54 | 17% |
| Halocarbons | +0.36 | 11% |
| Nitrous oxide | +0.21 | 6% |
| Aerosols | -0.5 | Cooling |
| Land use (albedo) | -0.15 | Cooling |
| **Net anthropogenic** | **+3.0** | **Warming** |

**Non-linear Relationships**
- Doubling CO₂ (e.g., 280→560 ppm) causes consistent warming (~3°C equilibrium)
- Each additional CO₂ molecule has slightly less effect than previous (logarithmic)
- But each ton emitted still adds to warming; no "safe" threshold where emissions don't matter

**Residence Times and Commitment**
- CO₂: 300-1000 years (complex; no single lifetime)
- Methane: 12 years (but converts to CO₂)
- N₂O: 114 years
- Some halocarbons: 1000+ years
- Past emissions commit to decades/centuries of future warming even if emissions stop

## Real-World Examples

### The Little Ice Age (1300-1850)

Cooler period demonstrating natural variability:
- Northern Hemisphere temperatures ~0.6°C cooler than 20th century
- Multiple causes: reduced solar activity (Maunder Minimum 1645-1715), volcanic eruptions, ocean circulation changes
- Glaciers advanced in Alps and Norway
- Thames River in London froze regularly
- Shorter growing seasons, crop failures, social impacts
- Shows climate can vary naturally, but current warming far exceeds Little Ice Age variations

### 1991 Mount Pinatubo Eruption

Largest eruption since 1912; provided climate science test case:
- June 15, 1991 in Philippines
- Injected 20 million tons SO₂ into stratosphere
- Created sulfate aerosol layer circling globe within weeks
- Global temperatures dropped 0.5°C in 1992-1993
- Climate models accurately predicted magnitude and timing
- Validated understanding of aerosol cooling effect
- Warming resumed once aerosols settled out
- Demonstrated volcanic effects are temporary, unlike greenhouse gases

### 2015-2016 El Niño

One of strongest El Niños on record:
- Global temperature spike: 2016 became warmest year on record (at the time)
- Added ~0.2°C to background warming trend
- Droughts: Indonesia (forest fires), Ethiopia, Zimbabwe
- Floods: Peru, East Africa
- Coral bleaching: Great Barrier Reef lost 30% of corals
- Demonstrated how natural variability (ENSO) combines with long-term warming trend
- Subsequent La Niña cooled slightly but didn't return to pre-El Niño baseline

### Ice Core Evidence from Vostok, Antarctica

800,000-year ice core record reveals drivers:
- CO₂ and temperature closely correlated over glacial cycles
- CO₂ ranged 180-280 ppm naturally
- Temperature varied ~8°C between glacials and interglacials
- Milankovitch cycles pace ice ages; CO₂ amplifies changes
- Current CO₂ (420 ppm) is 50% higher than any time in 800,000 years
- Rate of CO₂ increase (~2 ppm/year) unprecedented in ice core record

## Key Terms

**Radiative Forcing**: Change in energy balance at top of atmosphere; positive = warming, negative = cooling

**Insolation**: Incoming solar radiation; varies with latitude, season, and orbital parameters

**Milankovitch Cycles**: Long-term variations in Earth's orbit affecting climate over tens of thousands of years

**ENSO**: El Niño-Southern Oscillation; irregular Pacific Ocean temperature cycle affecting global weather

**Thermohaline Circulation**: Density-driven ocean circulation transporting heat globally

**Albedo**: Reflectivity; ice/clouds reflect solar radiation (high albedo), ocean/forest absorb it (low albedo)

**Stratospheric Aerosols**: Fine particles in stratosphere reflecting sunlight, causing temporary cooling

**Climate Sensitivity**: Global temperature increase from doubling atmospheric CO₂ (~3°C equilibrium)

**Orbital Forcing**: Climate changes driven by variations in Earth's orbit and axial tilt

**Teleconnection**: Climate pattern in one region influencing weather in distant regions

## Summary

Climate is driven by multiple factors operating across different timescales. Solar radiation provides fundamental energy input, with small variations having minor climate effects. Milankovitch cycles pace ice ages over tens of thousands of years by altering seasonal insolation patterns at high latitudes. Volcanic eruptions cause temporary cooling lasting 1-3 years through stratospheric aerosols. Ocean circulation patterns like ENSO create interannual variability, while longer cycles like PDO and thermohaline circulation operate over decades to centuries, redistributing heat globally. Current climate change is dominated by anthropogenic greenhouse gas forcing (+3.0 W/m²), far exceeding natural variations. CO₂ accounts for 66% of warming, with methane, halocarbons, and nitrous oxide contributing additional forcing. Understanding these drivers reveals that contemporary warming cannot be explained by natural factors - solar output has been flat or declining since 1980, no unusual volcanic activity has occurred, and Milankovitch cycles predict slight cooling. Human emissions are the dominant driver of 20th-21st century climate change.
