# Carbon Cycle

## Overview

The carbon cycle describes movement of carbon through Earth's systems - atmosphere, oceans, land, and living organisms. Carbon atoms cycle through multiple reservoirs on timescales from days to millions of years. This cycle regulates atmospheric CO₂ concentration, making it central to climate regulation. Human activities have disrupted the natural carbon cycle, creating net flow from long-term geological storage into atmosphere and oceans.

## Key Concepts

### Carbon Reservoirs

Carbon is stored in multiple reservoirs with vastly different capacities and residence times:

| Reservoir | Carbon Amount (Gt C) | Average Residence Time |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Atmosphere | 880 | 3-5 years |
| Ocean surface | 900 | Years to decades |
| Ocean deep | 37,000 | Centuries to millennia |
| Soil | 2,300 | Years to centuries |
| Vegetation | 550 | Years to decades |
| Permafrost | 1,700 | Millennia (if stable) |
| Fossil fuels | 4,000 | Millions of years (geological) |
| Limestone/sediments | 75,000,000 | Millions of years |

**Key insight**: Atmosphere is small reservoir but critically important for climate; fossil fuels represent geological carbon being rapidly transferred to atmosphere.

### Natural Carbon Fluxes

**Photosynthesis and Respiration**
- Photosynthesis: Plants/algae absorb ~120 Gt C/year from atmosphere
- Respiration: Organisms release ~60 Gt C/year back to atmosphere
- Plant/soil respiration: Additional ~60 Gt C/year
- Roughly balanced annually, but seasonal variations create atmospheric CO₂ oscillations

**Ocean-Atmosphere Exchange**
- Oceans absorb ~90 Gt C/year; release ~88 Gt C/year (natural)
- Net absorption currently ~2.5 Gt C/year (absorbing human emissions)
- Cold water absorbs more CO₂; warm water releases it
- Ocean circulation brings CO₂-rich deep water to surface (upwelling)

**Rock Weathering and Sedimentation**
- Chemical weathering of silicate rocks consumes atmospheric CO₂ over millennia
- Carbon buried in ocean sediments eventually forms limestone
- Volcanic activity and metamorphism release CO₂ back to atmosphere
- Operates on million-year timescales; geological thermostat

### Anthropogenic Carbon Inputs

**Fossil Fuel Combustion**
- ~10 Gt C/year released (36.6 Gt CO₂)
- Coal, oil, natural gas burned for energy
- Transfers carbon from geological storage to atmosphere
- Accounts for ~65% of human CO₂ emissions

**Land Use Change**
- ~1.5 Gt C/year released
- Primarily deforestation in tropics
- Converts carbon-storing forests to agriculture
- Reduces ongoing photosynthetic CO₂ removal
- Accounts for ~11% of human CO₂ emissions

**Cement Production**
- ~1.5 Gt C/year
- Heating limestone (CaCO₃) releases CO₂
- Accounts for ~4% of human CO₂ emissions

**Total Human Impact**:
- ~11 Gt C/year added to atmosphere
- Of this, ~5 Gt C stays in atmosphere (causes CO₂ rise)
- ~2.5 Gt C absorbed by oceans
- ~3.5 Gt C absorbed by land (vegetation/soil)
- Oceans and land absorb ~57% of emissions (natural carbon sinks)

### Ocean Carbon Chemistry

**Physical Solubility Pump**
- Cold polar waters absorb CO₂
- Dense water sinks, carrying carbon to deep ocean
- Centuries before this water returns to surface
- Climate warming reduces efficiency (warmer water holds less CO₂)

**Biological Pump**
- Phytoplankton photosynthesize, incorporating CO₂ into organic matter
- When organisms die, some sink to deep ocean
- Decomposes or gets buried in sediments
- Removes carbon from surface ocean/atmosphere interaction
- Strengthened by nutrient upwelling; weakened by stratification

**Ocean Acidification**
- Absorbed CO₂ reacts with water: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
- Increases hydrogen ions, lowering pH
- Ocean pH dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 since pre-industrial (0.1 unit = 30% more acidic)
- Threatens shell-forming organisms (corals, mollusks, plankton)
- Carbonate ion concentration decreases, making shell formation harder

### Terrestrial Carbon Cycle

**Forest Carbon Storage**
- Tropical rainforests: 250-300 Gt C stored
- Boreal forests: 270 Gt C (trees + soil)
- Temperate forests: 120 Gt C
- Forests are net carbon sinks when growing; sources when clearing/burning

**Soil Carbon**
- Largest terrestrial reservoir (2,300 Gt C)
- Organic matter from decomposing plants/organisms
- Microbes respire, releasing CO₂
- Warming accelerates decomposition, releasing more carbon (positive feedback)
- Agricultural practices can increase or decrease soil carbon

**Permafrost**
- Frozen soil containing 1,700 Gt C (ancient organic matter)
- Arctic warming causes thawing
- Microbes decompose previously frozen material
- Releases CO₂ (aerobic) or CH₄ (anaerobic) - both greenhouse gases
- Major potential positive feedback to warming

### Feedback Loops

**Positive Feedbacks (Amplifying)**
1. **Permafrost thaw**: Warming → thawing → decomposition → more greenhouse gases → more warming
2. **Soil respiration**: Warming → faster decomposition → more CO₂ → more warming
3. **Ocean saturation**: Warming oceans → reduced CO₂ absorption → more atmospheric CO₂ → more warming
4. **Forest dieback**: Drought/heat → tree mortality → carbon release → warming → more stress

**Negative Feedbacks (Dampening)**
1. **CO₂ fertilization**: Higher CO₂ → enhanced photosynthesis → more carbon storage (limited by nutrients/water)
2. **Enhanced weathering**: Warming → faster rock weathering → CO₂ consumption (very slow)

**Current state**: Positive feedbacks increasingly dominating, reducing effectiveness of natural carbon sinks.

## Real-World Examples

### Amazon Rainforest Carbon Flip

Recent research shows concerning transition:
- Historically: Net carbon sink, absorbing ~500 million tons CO₂/year
- 2010-2019: Southeastern Amazon became net carbon source
- Causes: Deforestation, fires, drought stress from climate change
- Trees dying faster than regrowth can compensate
- Demonstrates vulnerability of assumed permanent carbon sinks
- Could release 90 Gt C if transitions to savanna

### Ocean Acidification in Pacific Northwest

Oyster hatcheries experienced massive die-offs (2005-2009):
- Upwelling brought deep, CO₂-rich, acidic water to surface
- Combined with additional atmospheric CO₂ absorption
- Larval oysters couldn't form shells in corrosive water
- Economic impact on aquaculture industry
- Now monitor water chemistry and adjust intake timing
- Preview of broader ocean acidification impacts

### Permafrost Thermokarst Lakes

Arctic Alaska and Siberia show dramatic changes:
- Permafrost thaw creates collapsed terrain and lakes
- Lakes emit methane from anaerobic decomposition
- Some lakes emit 5-10 times more methane than terrestrial surfaces
- "Abrupt thaw" can release carbon much faster than gradual thaw
- Represents potential climate tipping point
- Some regions emitting carbon centuries ahead of climate model projections

### Peatland Carbon Storage

Northern peatlands demonstrate long-term carbon accumulation:
- Cover 3% of land surface but store 550 Gt C (1/3 of soil carbon)
- Formed over 10,000 years since last ice age
- Waterlogged conditions slow decomposition
- Drainage for agriculture releases stored carbon
- Degraded peatlands emit ~1.9 Gt CO₂/year (4% of human emissions)
- Restoration can reverse emissions and rebuild storage

## Key Terms

**Carbon Reservoir**: Location where carbon is stored (atmosphere, ocean, biosphere, etc.)

**Carbon Flux**: Rate of carbon transfer between reservoirs

**Residence Time**: Average time carbon atom remains in given reservoir

**Carbon Sink**: Reservoir absorbing more carbon than it releases

**Carbon Source**: Reservoir releasing more carbon than it absorbs

**Biological Pump**: Process transporting carbon from surface to deep ocean via marine organisms

**Ocean Acidification**: Decrease in ocean pH from absorbing atmospheric CO₂

**Permafrost**: Permanently frozen ground containing organic carbon

**Sequestration**: Long-term carbon storage, removing it from atmospheric circulation

**Net Primary Productivity (NPP)**: Rate of carbon uptake by photosynthesis minus respiration

## Summary

The carbon cycle governs movement of carbon through atmosphere, oceans, land, and biosphere on timescales from years to millions of years. Naturally, photosynthesis and respiration exchange ~120 Gt C/year between atmosphere and biosphere in roughly balanced flows. Oceans exchange ~90 Gt C/year with atmosphere, with cold regions absorbing CO₂ and warm regions releasing it. Geological processes operate on million-year timescales. Human activities add ~11 Gt C/year through fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and cement production - disrupting this ancient balance. Approximately 57% is absorbed by ocean and land sinks, while 43% accumulates in atmosphere, driving climate change. Ocean absorption causes acidification, threatening marine life. Positive feedbacks like permafrost thaw and reduced sink efficiency risk accelerating carbon buildup. Understanding carbon cycle dynamics is essential for predicting future climate change and designing effective mitigation strategies like protecting forests, restoring wetlands, and reducing fossil fuel emissions.
