# Ecosystems and Biodiversity

## Overview

Ecosystems are communities of interacting organisms and their physical environment. They provide essential services supporting human civilization - food production, water purification, climate regulation, pollination. Biodiversity (variety of life) underpins ecosystem resilience and functionality. Climate change threatens ecosystems by altering temperature, precipitation, and seasonal patterns faster than many species can adapt, risking ecosystem collapse at critical tipping points.

## Key Concepts

### Major Biomes

Biomes are large-scale ecosystem types characterized by climate, vegetation, and organisms:

| Biome | Climate | Key Features | Climate Threat |
|-------|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Tropical Rainforest** | Hot, wet year-round | Highest biodiversity; dense canopy | Drought, fire risk, temperature stress |
| **Temperate Forest** | Four seasons; moderate precip | Deciduous/coniferous; fall colors | Shifting ranges, pest outbreaks, fire |
| **Boreal Forest (Taiga)** | Cold winters, cool summers | Coniferous; low diversity; peat soils | Warming, fire, permafrost thaw |
| **Grassland/Savanna** | Seasonal rainfall; fire-adapted | Grazing animals; grass dominance | Desertification, altered fire regimes |
| **Desert** | Very low precipitation | Sparse vegetation; extreme temps | Expanding in some regions, flooding in others |
| **Tundra** | Cold, short growing season | Permafrost; low-growing plants | Rapid warming, shrub expansion, permafrost thaw |
| **Freshwater** | Rivers, lakes, wetlands | Critical water source; high productivity | Warming, changing precipitation, drought |
| **Marine** | Oceans, coral reefs, coasts | Highest diversity in coral reefs | Warming, acidification, deoxygenation |

### Biodiversity Components

**Species Diversity**
- 8.7 million estimated species (±1.3 million described)
- Insects: ~5.5 million species
- Plants: ~400,000 species
- Vertebrates: ~70,000 species
- Tropical regions contain highest diversity

**Genetic Diversity**
- Variation within species
- Enables adaptation to changing conditions
- Small populations lose genetic diversity, reducing adaptability
- Example: Cheetahs have low genetic diversity from past bottleneck

**Ecosystem Diversity**
- Variety of habitats and ecological communities
- Different ecosystems provide different services
- Loss of one ecosystem type affects entire landscape

### Ecosystem Services

**Provisioning Services** (direct products)
- Food: Crops, fish, livestock, wild foods
- Fresh water: Drinking, irrigation, industrial use
- Fiber/fuel: Wood, cotton, biofuels
- Biochemicals: Medicines (25% of drugs from plants), genetic resources

**Regulating Services** (process regulation)
- Climate regulation: Forests store carbon, affect rainfall
- Water purification: Wetlands filter pollutants
- Pollination: Bees, butterflies, bats pollinate 75% of crop species
- Flood control: Wetlands, forests absorb excess water
- Disease regulation: Biodiversity reduces disease transmission
- Pest control: Natural predators reduce crop pests

**Supporting Services** (enabling other services)
- Nutrient cycling: Decomposers recycle nutrients
- Soil formation: Takes centuries to millennia
- Primary production: Photosynthesis creates energy base

**Cultural Services** (non-material benefits)
- Recreation: Tourism, hiking, wildlife viewing
- Aesthetic: Natural beauty, inspiration
- Spiritual: Sacred sites, traditional practices
- Educational: Scientific knowledge

**Economic Value**
- Estimates range from $125-145 trillion per year globally
- Exceeds global GDP (~$100 trillion)
- Most services not priced in markets but essential for survival

### Climate Change Impacts on Ecosystems

**Direct Impacts**

1. **Temperature Stress**
   - Species have thermal tolerance limits
   - Tropical species often near upper limits already
   - Cold-adapted species losing habitat (Arctic species)
   - Coral bleaching at 1-2°C above normal

2. **Precipitation Changes**
   - Droughts stress water-dependent ecosystems
   - Altered seasonality disrupts life cycles
   - Flash floods damage habitats

3. **Ocean Changes**
   - Warming: Species shifting poleward; coral bleaching
   - Acidification: Threatens shell-forming organisms
   - Deoxygenation: Expanding dead zones

**Indirect Impacts**

1. **Phenology Shifts**
   - Earlier spring events (flowering, migration, breeding)
   - Mismatches: Pollinators emerging before flowers, or vice versa
   - Caterpillar peaks before migratory bird arrival

2. **Range Shifts**
   - Species moving poleward (~6 km/decade average)
   - Upslope in mountains (~11 m/decade)
   - Some species unable to disperse fast enough
   - Habitat fragmentation blocks migration routes

3. **Ecosystem Composition Changes**
   - Invasive species thrive in disturbed conditions
   - Fire regimes altered (more frequent/intense)
   - Forest composition shifts as species respond differently

### Tipping Points

Thresholds beyond which rapid, potentially irreversible changes occur:

**Amazon Rainforest to Savanna**
- Tipping point: 20-25% deforestation + 2.5-4°C warming
- Current: ~17% deforested
- Mechanism: Reduced rainfall → more fire → tree mortality → less rainfall (positive feedback)
- Consequence: Release 90 Gt C; regional climate shift

**Coral Reef Collapse**
- Tipping point: 1.5-2°C warming + acidification
- Current: 1.1°C warming; frequent bleaching events
- Mechanism: Bleaching → mortality → reef degradation → reduced reef-building
- Consequence: Loss of fisheries, coastal protection, tourism

**Boreal Forest Shift**
- Tipping point: Unclear; 2-4°C warming
- Mechanism: Warming → fire/pest outbreaks → tree mortality → grassland/shrubland
- Consequence: Release stored carbon; albedo change (dark trees → lighter vegetation)

**Arctic Ecosystem Transformation**
- Tipping point: Already beginning; accelerates beyond 2°C
- Mechanism: Sea ice loss → open water → warming → habitat loss for ice-dependent species
- Consequence: Polar bears, seals, walruses declining; fish species moving north

### Biodiversity Loss

**Extinction Rates**
- Background rate: ~1 species per million per year
- Current rate: 100-1000 times background (sixth mass extinction)
- Climate change accelerates other threats (habitat loss, overexploitation, pollution, invasives)

**Threatened Species** (IUCN Red List)
- 28% of assessed species threatened with extinction
- Amphibians: 41% threatened (climate + disease)
- Reef-building corals: 33% threatened
- Birds: 14% threatened
- Mammals: 25% threatened

**Functional Extinction**
- Populations too small to perform ecosystem role
- Example: Large predators removed → herbivore population explosions → overgrazing

## Real-World Examples

### Great Barrier Reef Bleaching

Demonstrates ecosystem climate vulnerability:
- 2016-2017: Back-to-back mass bleaching events
- 2020: Third mass bleaching
- Mechanism: 1-2°C above normal summer temps → coral expels symbiotic algae → white "bleached" appearance
- If prolonged, corals starve and die
- 50% of corals lost since 1995
- Affects 1,500 fish species, tourism ($6 billion/year), coastal protection
- Projected: Annual bleaching by 2040s even with emissions reductions

### Monarch Butterfly Decline

Shows climate-migration-ecosystem interactions:
- Eastern North American population declined 80% since 1990s
- Multi-generational migration: Mexico (winter) → Texas → Canada (summer)
- Climate threats: Extreme weather during migration, drought in breeding grounds, winter storm intensity
- Habitat loss: Milkweed decline from agriculture
- 2020 winter: Lowest population on record (1.9 hectares vs 45 hectares in 1990s)
- Demonstrates vulnerability of species with complex life cycles

### Bark Beetle Outbreaks in Western North America

Climate-pest-forest interaction:
- Mountain pine beetles killed 18 million hectares of forest (2000-2020)
- Mechanism: Warmer winters → increased beetle survival → population explosions
- Previously: Cold snaps killed overwintering beetles, limiting populations
- Dead trees: Release carbon, fuel wildfires, reduce water uptake
- Ecosystem shift from forest to open woodland
- Economic losses in timber industry; reduced tourism

### Kelp Forest Collapse (Northern California)

Tipping point example:
- Bull kelp forests thrived for decades along coast
- 2014-2016: Marine heat wave + sea star wasting disease
- Sea stars died → urchin populations exploded (predator release)
- Urchins overgrazed kelp → barren landscape
- 95% kelp loss in some areas
- Has not recovered; stuck in degraded state
- Loss of fish habitat, carbon storage, coastal protection

### Sahel Greening

Unexpected ecosystem response:
- Sahel region (southern Sahara) greening since 1980s
- Increased rainfall + CO₂ fertilization → vegetation expansion
- Complicates simple narratives; climate change has variable regional effects
- However, human conflict over resources, locust outbreaks continue
- Future projections uncertain; could reverse with further warming

## Key Terms

**Biome**: Large-scale ecosystem type characterized by climate and dominant vegetation

**Biodiversity**: Variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels

**Ecosystem Services**: Benefits humans obtain from ecosystems

**Tipping Point**: Threshold beyond which rapid, hard-to-reverse changes occur

**Phenology**: Timing of seasonal biological events (flowering, migration, etc.)

**Range Shift**: Geographic movement of species distribution in response to climate

**Keystone Species**: Species with disproportionate ecosystem impact relative to abundance

**Functional Diversity**: Variety of ecological roles species perform

**Resilience**: Ecosystem's ability to absorb disturbance and maintain function

**Ecological Niche**: Environmental conditions and resources a species requires

## Summary

Ecosystems are complex communities providing essential services - food, water, climate regulation, pollination, cultural value - worth more than global GDP. Biodiversity underpins ecosystem functionality and resilience. Climate change threatens ecosystems through direct temperature and precipitation stress, altered phenology creating mismatches, forced range shifts, and changed ecosystem composition. Multiple ecosystems approach tipping points: Amazon rainforest risks transition to savanna, coral reefs face collapse from bleaching and acidification, boreal forests threatened by fire and pests. Current extinction rates are 100-1000 times background levels, qualifying as sixth mass extinction. Examples like Great Barrier Reef bleaching, monarch butterfly decline, bark beetle outbreaks, and kelp forest collapse demonstrate real-world ecosystem vulnerability. Protecting ecosystems requires both climate mitigation to limit warming and direct conservation actions like habitat protection, restoration, and connectivity to enable species migration. Ecosystem collapse would undermine human wellbeing through loss of food, water, climate stability, and cultural values.
