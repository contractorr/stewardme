# Mitigation and Adaptation

## Overview

Addressing climate change requires two complementary approaches: mitigation (reducing greenhouse gas emissions and enhancing carbon sinks) and adaptation (adjusting systems to minimize harm and exploit opportunities). Limiting warming to 1.5-2°C requires rapid emissions reductions across all sectors - energy, transport, buildings, industry, agriculture. Even with aggressive mitigation, adaptation is necessary due to committed warming from past emissions. This section examines technologies, policies, and strategies for both approaches.

## Key Concepts

### Emissions Reduction Pathways

**Climate Targets**

| Target | Cumulative CO₂ Budget from 2020 | Probability | Required Actions |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------|------------------|
| 1.5°C | 500 Gt CO₂ | 50% | Net-zero by ~2050; rapid immediate cuts |
| 1.5°C | 400 Gt CO₂ | 67% | Net-zero by ~2040; very rapid cuts |
| 2.0°C | 1,350 Gt CO₂ | 67% | Net-zero by ~2070; rapid cuts |

**Current trajectory**: 2.7-3.0°C by 2100 based on current policies

**Required Emission Reductions (for 1.5°C)**
- 2030 target: -45% vs 2010 levels
- 2040 target: -75% vs 2010 levels
- 2050 target: Net-zero CO₂; ~80% reduction all greenhouse gases

**Sectoral Requirements**
- Electricity: 100% renewable/zero-carbon by 2040
- Transport: 60-80% electric vehicles by 2040
- Buildings: Deep energy efficiency retrofits; electrified heating
- Industry: Efficiency, electrification, hydrogen, carbon capture
- Agriculture: Reduced emissions; improved practices; dietary shifts

### Energy Sector Transformation

**Renewable Energy**

| Technology | 2021 Global Capacity | Cost Trend | Key Advantages | Challenges |
|------------|---------------------|------------|----------------|-----------|
| Solar PV | 940 GW | -90% since 2010 | Rapidly deployable; modular | Intermittency; land use |
| Wind | 840 GW | -70% since 2010 | Mature technology; high capacity factor (offshore) | Intermittency; visual impact |
| Hydropower | 1,360 GW | Mature/stable | Dispatchable; storage | Limited sites; environmental impact |
| Geothermal | 16 GW | Stable | Baseload power | Geographic constraints |
| Bioenergy | 140 GW | Variable | Dispatchable; negative emissions potential | Land competition; sustainability concerns |

**Growth Trajectory**
- Solar/wind installations accelerating: 350 GW added in 2021
- Renewables now cheapest electricity source in most regions
- Projections: 70-85% renewable electricity by 2050 for 1.5°C pathway

**Supporting Technologies**

*Energy Storage*
- Lithium-ion batteries: Cost down 90% since 2010; 4-hour duration typical
- Long-duration storage: Pumped hydro, compressed air, hydrogen, flow batteries
- Grid-scale deployment accelerating; 100+ GWh installed globally

*Grid Modernization*
- Smart grids: Real-time demand response; distributed generation integration
- High-voltage DC transmission: Long-distance renewable energy transport
- Vehicle-to-grid: EVs as distributed storage resources

*Nuclear Energy*
- Current: 440 reactors; 390 GW globally
- Role: Low-carbon baseload power
- Challenges: Cost, construction time, waste, public acceptance
- Advanced reactors: Small modular reactors (SMRs), fusion (future)

### Transport Decarbonization

**Electric Vehicles (EVs)**
- 2021: 16 million EVs on road (2% of global fleet)
- 2030 target: 300-600 million EVs (30-60% of fleet) for climate goals
- Battery cost: From $1,200/kWh (2010) to $130/kWh (2021); approaching parity
- Range: 300-500 km typical; fast charging enables long trips
- Lifecycle emissions: 50-70% lower than gasoline even with current grids; improving as grids decarbonize

**Other Transport Solutions**

| Mode | Solutions | Timeline |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Aviation | Sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs), hydrogen, electric (short routes) | 2030-2050 |
| Shipping | Ammonia, hydrogen, electrification (short routes), wind assist | 2030-2050 |
| Rail | Electrification, hydrogen | Mature (electric); 2025-2030 (hydrogen) |
| Urban | Public transit, cycling, walking, compact development | Immediate |

**Co-benefits**
- Air quality: Major reduction in urban air pollution
- Health: Fewer respiratory illnesses; 4-9 million premature deaths/year from air pollution
- Noise: Electric vehicles quieter

### Building Efficiency and Electrification

**Energy Efficiency**
- Buildings: 30% of global energy use; 26% of emissions
- Efficiency potential: 50-70% reduction via retrofits
- Measures: Insulation, efficient windows, LED lighting, smart thermostats, heat recovery

**Electrification**
- Heat pumps: 3-5x more efficient than gas furnaces; cost-competitive in many regions
- Induction cooking: Efficient; improves indoor air quality
- Heat pump water heaters: 2-3x more efficient than resistance electric

**Net-Zero Buildings**
- On-site solar + batteries + efficiency = net-zero or positive energy
- Cost premium decreasing; mandatory in some jurisdictions
- Passive house standard: 90% heating/cooling reduction

### Industrial Decarbonization

**Cement (8% of global emissions)**
- Alternative fuels: Biomass, waste replacing coal
- Alternative materials: Supplementary cementitious materials reducing clinker
- Carbon capture: Capture process CO₂ (60% of cement emissions)
- Novel chemistries: Calcium silicate alternatives

**Steel (7% of global emissions)**
- Hydrogen direct reduction: Replace coking coal with hydrogen
- Electric arc furnaces: Use recycled steel; renewable electricity
- Efficiency: Improved processes reduce energy intensity

**Chemicals**
- Process electrification where feasible
- Biomass feedstocks replacing fossil inputs
- Circular economy: Plastics recycling reducing virgin production

**Cross-cutting**
- Energy efficiency: Often 20-40% savings cost-effective
- Electrification: Renewable-powered electric processes
- Circular economy: Reuse, remanufacturing, recycling reducing emissions

### Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)

Required to reach net-zero and potentially net-negative emissions:

| Method | Scale (2021) | Potential | Cost | Permanence | Co-benefits/Concerns |
|--------|--------------|-----------|------|------------|---------------------|
| Afforestation/Reforestation | 2 Gt CO₂/yr | 3-6 Gt/yr | Low-Medium | Decades-centuries | Biodiversity; land competition |
| Soil carbon sequestration | <0.5 Gt/yr | 2-5 Gt/yr | Low | Decades | Improved agriculture; reversible |
| Biochar | <0.01 Gt/yr | 1-2 Gt/yr | Medium | Centuries | Soil improvement |
| Bioenergy + CCS (BECCS) | <0.01 Gt/yr | 3-8 Gt/yr | Medium-High | Millennia | Land competition; water use |
| Direct air capture (DAC) | <0.001 Gt/yr | 5-40 Gt/yr | High | Millennia | Energy intensive; scalable |
| Enhanced weathering | Pilot | 2-4 Gt/yr | Medium | Millennia | Ocean alkalinity; ecosystem impacts? |

**Challenges**
- Scale: Need several Gt CO₂/yr removal by 2050; currently ~2 Gt/yr (mostly forestry)
- Cost: Many methods expensive; need innovation
- Permanence: Some methods (forests, soil) reversible by fire, land use change
- Side effects: Land/water requirements; ecological impacts need assessment

### Adaptation Strategies

**Water Resources**
- Infrastructure: Reservoirs, desalination, wastewater recycling
- Efficiency: Drip irrigation, water-saving appliances, leak reduction
- Watershed management: Protect forests/wetlands for water filtration and storage
- Drought planning: Water allocation systems, emergency reserves

**Agriculture and Food Security**
- Crop breeding: Heat/drought tolerant varieties; faster development cycles
- Diversification: Multiple crops reduce risk from specific climate impacts
- Irrigation: Efficient systems; tap groundwater/stored water
- Forecasting: Seasonal predictions inform planting decisions
- Insurance: Index-based insurance transfers risk

**Coastal Protection**
- Hard defenses: Seawalls, levees, storm surge barriers
- Nature-based: Mangrove/wetland restoration; dune preservation
- Accommodation: Elevated buildings; flood-resistant design
- Retreat: Managed relocation from highest-risk areas

**Health Systems**
- Heat action plans: Early warning systems; cooling centers; vulnerable population outreach
- Disease surveillance: Monitor vector-borne disease ranges
- Infrastructure: Air conditioning for vulnerable populations; hospital climate resilience

**Urban Planning**
- Green infrastructure: Trees, parks, green roofs reduce heat island effect
- Permeable surfaces: Reduce flooding; recharge groundwater
- Compact development: Reduces transport emissions; enables efficient services
- Building codes: Require climate-resilient design

**Ecosystem-based Adaptation**
- Maintain/restore ecosystems providing natural services
- Coastal wetlands buffer storms; filter water
- Forests regulate water cycles; prevent erosion
- Often lower cost than engineered solutions; multiple benefits

### Policy Approaches

**Carbon Pricing**

*Carbon Tax*
- Fee per ton CO₂ emitted
- Implemented: Canada, Nordic countries, parts of US
- Advantages: Price certainty; simple; economy-wide
- Challenges: Political resistance; distributional impacts; determining appropriate price

*Cap-and-Trade*
- Emission limit (cap); tradeable allowances
- Implemented: EU ETS, California, RGGI, China
- Advantages: Emission certainty; market efficiency
- Challenges: Price volatility; setting appropriate cap; free allocation debates

**Regulations and Standards**
- Renewable portfolio standards: Mandate renewable energy percentage
- Vehicle emission standards: Fleet average requirements
- Building codes: Energy efficiency requirements
- Industrial regulations: Technology mandates; emission limits

**Subsidies and Incentives**
- Renewable energy: Tax credits; feed-in tariffs
- EV subsidies: Purchase incentives; charging infrastructure
- Efficiency programs: Rebates for insulation, appliances
- R&D funding: Support innovation in clean technologies

**International Agreements**
- Paris Agreement (2015): Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs); 1.5-2°C goal
- Montreal Protocol: CFCs phase-out; now HFC phase-down
- Sectoral agreements: Aviation (CORSIA), shipping (IMO)

## Real-World Examples

### Denmark Wind Energy Transformation

Demonstrates renewable transition success:
- 1980s: <5% renewable electricity
- 2021: 80% wind + renewables; 50% wind alone
- Strategies: Offshore wind; interconnections with neighbors; flexible demand
- Result: Low-carbon electricity; net exporter; energy security
- Lessons: Long-term policy commitment; public acceptance; grid integration

### Costa Rica Renewable Electricity

Small country achieving high renewable share:
- 99% renewable electricity (hydropower, geothermal, wind, solar)
- Goal: Carbon neutral by 2050
- Strategies: Protected forests (payment for ecosystem services); renewable investment
- Challenges: Transport still fossil-dependent (addressing with EV adoption)
- Demonstrates renewable electricity feasible even in developing economy

### Netherlands Climate Adaptation

Low-lying country proactively adapting:
- "Room for the River": Give rivers flooding space rather than higher levees
- Floating houses: Adapt to water rather than fight it
- Water plazas: Dual-use spaces storing stormwater/recreation
- Integrated planning: Climate adaptation in all urban development
- Shows adaptation can enhance livability while managing risk

### China Renewable Energy Scale-Up

Demonstrates speed of transition possible:
- 2010-2021: Solar capacity 0.8 GW → 308 GW (385x growth)
- Wind: 45 GW → 328 GW (7x growth)
- EVs: <1% of sales (2015) → 16% (2021)
- Still coal-dependent but building renewable capacity faster than all other countries combined
- Shows rapid deployment possible with policy support and manufacturing scale

### Managed Retreat: Isle de Jean Charles, Louisiana

Difficult but necessary adaptation:
- Island lost 98% of land since 1955 (subsidence + sea level rise)
- 2016: $48 million federal grant for community relocation
- First US climate relocation program
- Challenges: Cultural loss; community cohesion; compensation adequacy
- Preview of difficult decisions coastal communities worldwide will face

## Key Terms

**Mitigation**: Actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions or enhance sinks

**Adaptation**: Adjustments to minimize climate change harm or exploit opportunities

**Net-Zero**: Balance between emissions and removals from atmosphere

**Carbon Pricing**: Policy putting cost on carbon emissions (tax or cap-and-trade)

**Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)**: Extracting CO₂ from atmosphere for storage

**Renewable Energy**: Energy from sources that naturally replenish (solar, wind, hydro, etc.)

**Energy Efficiency**: Using less energy to provide same service

**Nature-Based Solutions**: Using ecosystems to address climate change

**Circular Economy**: Minimizing waste through reuse, remanufacturing, recycling

**Maladaptation**: Adaptation action that increases vulnerability or has adverse outcomes

## Summary

Limiting warming to 1.5-2°C requires rapid, deep emissions reductions across all sectors plus carbon dioxide removal. Energy transformation to 70-85% renewables by 2050 is technically and economically feasible; solar and wind are now cheapest electricity sources. Transport electrification is accelerating as EV costs reach parity with combustion vehicles. Buildings need efficiency retrofits and electrified heating. Industrial decarbonization requires efficiency, electrification, hydrogen, and carbon capture. Agriculture needs emission reductions and improved practices. Carbon dioxide removal through reforestation, soil sequestration, and emerging technologies like direct air capture will be necessary to reach net-zero. Even with mitigation, adaptation is essential due to committed warming. Water management, agricultural adjustments, coastal protection, health system preparation, and ecosystem-based approaches can reduce climate risks. Effective policy mixes combine carbon pricing, regulations, subsidies, and international cooperation. Real-world examples show renewable transitions are achievable (Denmark, Costa Rica), rapid scale-up is possible (China), and adaptation can enhance resilience (Netherlands), though difficult choices like managed retreat may be necessary in extreme cases. Comprehensive approach combining ambitious mitigation and proactive adaptation offers path to climate-resilient, sustainable future.
