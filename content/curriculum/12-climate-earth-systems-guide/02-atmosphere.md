# Atmosphere

## Overview

The atmosphere is Earth's protective gaseous envelope, extending from surface to approximately 10,000 km altitude. It regulates temperature, protects from harmful radiation, enables weather systems, and hosts the greenhouse effect that makes Earth habitable. Atmospheric composition and circulation patterns fundamentally determine climate.

## Key Concepts

### Atmospheric Composition

Modern atmosphere consists of:

| Gas | Volume % | Role |
|-----|----------|------|
| Nitrogen (N₂) | 78.08% | Inert; essential for proteins via nitrogen fixation |
| Oxygen (O₂) | 20.95% | Respiration; produced by photosynthesis |
| Argon (Ar) | 0.93% | Inert noble gas |
| Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) | 0.042% (420 ppm) | Greenhouse gas; photosynthesis substrate |
| Water Vapor (H₂O) | 0-4% | Most important greenhouse gas; highly variable |
| Trace gases | <0.01% | Methane, ozone, nitrous oxide - potent greenhouse gases |

**Note**: While CO₂ is small by volume, its climate impact is enormous due to infrared absorption properties.

### Atmospheric Layers

**Troposphere (0-12 km)**
- Contains 80% of atmospheric mass and virtually all water vapor
- Temperature decreases with altitude (~6.5°C per km)
- All weather occurs here
- Top boundary is tropopause

**Stratosphere (12-50 km)**
- Contains ozone layer (15-35 km) absorbing UV radiation
- Temperature increases with altitude due to UV absorption
- Very stable; little vertical mixing
- Commercial aircraft cruise in lower stratosphere

**Mesosphere (50-85 km)**
- Temperature decreases with altitude (coldest atmospheric layer)
- Meteors burn up here
- Difficult to study (too high for aircraft, too low for satellites)

**Thermosphere (85-600 km)**
- Temperature increases dramatically (can exceed 1500°C)
- Extremely low density; would feel cold due to few molecules
- Aurora occur here
- International Space Station orbits in thermosphere

**Exosphere (600-10,000 km)**
- Transitions to outer space
- Extremely tenuous; atoms can escape Earth's gravity

### The Greenhouse Effect

**Natural Greenhouse Effect**

Critical for life - without it, Earth's average temperature would be -18°C instead of +15°C.

**Mechanism**:
1. Sun emits shortwave radiation (visible light)
2. Earth's surface absorbs energy, warms up
3. Warm surface emits longwave radiation (infrared)
4. Greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit infrared in all directions
5. Some radiation goes back to surface, warming it further

**Key Greenhouse Gases and Properties**:

| Gas | Atmospheric Lifetime | Warming Potential (vs CO₂) | Main Sources |
|-----|---------------------|---------------------------|--------------|
| Water vapor | Days | Feedback amplifier | Evaporation |
| Carbon dioxide | 300-1000 years | 1 (baseline) | Fossil fuels, deforestation |
| Methane | 12 years | 28-34 (over 100 years) | Agriculture, natural gas leaks |
| Nitrous oxide | 114 years | 265-298 | Agriculture, combustion |
| CFCs | 45-1700 years | 4,000-14,000 | Industrial (now banned) |

**Enhanced Greenhouse Effect**

Human activities increase greenhouse gas concentrations, intensifying natural effect:
- CO₂ increased from 280 ppm (pre-industrial) to 420 ppm (2024) - 50% increase
- Methane increased 150% since pre-industrial times
- Result: Additional 1.1°C of global warming since 1880

### Atmospheric Circulation

**Hadley Cells**
- Tropical circulation pattern
- Warm air rises at equator, moves poleward at high altitude
- Descends around 30° latitude, creating subtropical deserts
- Returns to equator at surface (trade winds)

**Ferrel Cells**
- Mid-latitude circulation (30-60°)
- Driven by polar and Hadley cells
- Associated with temperate climate zones and storm tracks

**Polar Cells**
- High-latitude circulation (60-90°)
- Cold air descends at poles, moves equatorward at surface
- Creates polar easterlies

**Jet Streams**
- Fast-flowing air currents at tropopause (~10 km altitude)
- Subtropical jets at ~30° latitude
- Polar jets at ~60° latitude (200-400 km/h winds)
- Steer weather systems; meandering pattern influences regional weather

**Coriolis Effect**
- Earth's rotation deflects moving air
- Rightward in Northern Hemisphere, leftward in Southern Hemisphere
- Creates rotating weather systems (cyclones/anticyclones)
- Drives trade winds, westerlies, and polar easterlies

## Real-World Examples

### The Asian Monsoon

Demonstrates atmospheric circulation principles:
- Summer: Land heats faster than ocean; warm air rises over continent
- Creates low pressure that draws moist ocean air inland
- Rising air produces heavy rainfall (80% of India's annual precipitation)
- Winter: Pattern reverses; dry air flows from continent to ocean
- Affects 4 billion people; agricultural calendar depends on monsoon timing

### Ozone Hole Discovery

Antarctic ozone hole (discovered 1985) showed atmospheric vulnerability:
- CFCs from aerosols/refrigerants reached stratosphere
- Polar stratospheric clouds provided surface for chemical reactions
- Springtime conditions destroyed ozone, allowing UV radiation through
- Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs
- Ozone layer recovering; hole expected to heal by 2070s
- Success story for international environmental cooperation

### Urban Heat Islands

Cities demonstrate local atmospheric effects:
- Dark surfaces (asphalt, buildings) absorb more solar radiation than vegetation
- Reduced evapotranspiration decreases cooling
- Waste heat from vehicles, HVAC systems adds energy
- Cities can be 1-7°C warmer than surrounding rural areas
- Affects local weather patterns, air quality, energy demand

### Volcanic Eruptions and Climate

Mount Pinatubo eruption (1991) demonstrated atmosphere-climate connection:
- Injected 20 million tons of sulfur dioxide into stratosphere
- Formed sulfate aerosol layer that reflected sunlight
- Global temperatures dropped 0.5°C for 1-2 years
- Shows atmosphere can respond rapidly to perturbations
- Also demonstrates that aerosols (cooling) and greenhouse gases (warming) have opposing effects

## Key Terms

**Greenhouse Gas**: Gas that absorbs and emits infrared radiation, warming Earth's surface

**Tropopause**: Boundary between troposphere and stratosphere

**Ozone Layer**: Stratospheric region with high O₃ concentration; absorbs harmful UV radiation

**Jet Stream**: Fast-flowing air current at tropopause boundary; steers weather systems

**Coriolis Effect**: Deflection of moving objects (including air masses) due to Earth's rotation

**Albedo**: Reflectivity of surface; clouds have high albedo, dark ocean has low albedo

**Radiative Forcing**: Change in energy balance at top of atmosphere; positive = warming

**Atmospheric Pressure**: Weight of air column above a point; decreases with altitude

**Adiabatic Process**: Temperature change from expansion/compression without heat exchange

**Convection**: Vertical air movement driven by temperature differences

## Summary

The atmosphere is multilayered gaseous envelope with distinct thermal and compositional properties. Troposphere hosts weather and contains most atmospheric mass, while stratosphere contains protective ozone layer. Natural greenhouse effect makes Earth habitable by trapping infrared radiation, but human emissions of CO₂, methane, and other greenhouse gases enhance this effect, causing warming. Atmospheric circulation driven by solar heating and Earth's rotation creates distinct climate zones - rising air at equator, descending air at 30° creating deserts, mid-latitude storm tracks, and polar regions. Jet streams at boundaries between circulation cells steer weather systems. Understanding atmospheric composition, structure, and circulation is fundamental to comprehending climate change, as atmosphere is primary medium through which energy imbalances manifest as warming.
