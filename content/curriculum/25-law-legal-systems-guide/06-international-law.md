# International Law

## What Is International Law?

**Definition**: Rules governing relations between states (and increasingly, international organizations and individuals).

**Distinguishing features**:
- **No world legislature**: No global parliament enacting laws
- **No centralized enforcement**: No world police or government
- **Consent-based**: States bound only by what they accept
- **Horizontal structure**: Sovereign states (not hierarchical)

**Is it really "law"?** Debate persists:
- **Skeptics**: Without enforcement, just moral exhortation
- **Defenders**: States comply most of the time; enforcement exists (reciprocity, reputation, sanctions, international courts)

### Why International Law Matters

**Coordination**: Facilitates cooperation (trade, travel, communications, environment).

**Predictability**: Establishes rules so states can plan.

**Legitimacy**: Provides framework for justifying action (or condemning violations).

**Peace and security**: Constrains use of force; provides dispute resolution.

**Human rights**: Protects individuals from state abuses.

## Sources of International Law

**Article 38, Statute of International Court of Justice** (widely accepted hierarchy):

### 1. Treaties (Conventional International Law)

**Definition**: Written agreements between states (or international organizations).

**Names vary**: Treaty, convention, protocol, covenant, charter, statute.

**Binding**: Only on parties (pacta sunt servanda—agreements must be kept).

**Examples**: UN Charter, Geneva Conventions, Paris Agreement, WTO agreements, bilateral investment treaties.

**Types**:

**Bilateral**: Between two states.
- **Example**: US-Japan Treaty of Mutual Cooperation and Security

**Multilateral**: Many states.
- **Example**: UN Charter (193 parties)

**Law-making vs contractual**: Some create general rules (Geneva Conventions); some specific exchanges (trade agreements).

### 2. Customary International Law

**Definition**: General practice accepted as law.

**Elements**:
1. **State practice**: Consistent, general practice by states (what states do)
2. **Opinio juris**: States believe practice is legally required (not just courtesy or habit)

**Examples**:
- **Diplomatic immunity**: Diplomats immune from host state prosecution
- **Freedom of high seas**: Ships can navigate international waters
- **Prohibition on torture**: Recognized as customary (even without treaty)
- **Prohibition on genocide**: Jus cogens (see below)

**Persistent objector**: State that consistently objects during formation of custom not bound.
- **Example**: If state consistently objected to 12-mile territorial sea while custom forming, might not be bound. But once custom solidified, new states bound.

**Instant custom**: In rare cases (nuclear weapons, space law), rapid state acceptance forms custom quickly.

**Bind all states**: Unlike treaties (only parties), custom binds all (except persistent objectors).

### 3. General Principles of Law

**Definition**: Principles common to major legal systems (civil law, common law, etc.).

**Examples**:
- **Good faith**: Parties must act honestly
- **Res judicata**: Final judgments binding
- **Estoppel**: Can't contradict prior position to another's detriment
- **Equity**: Fairness considerations

**Function**: Fill gaps when treaty/custom silent.

### 4. Judicial Decisions and Scholarly Writings (Subsidiary Means)

**Not binding** but persuasive.

**ICJ decisions**: Bind only parties to that case, but influential precedent.

**Treatises**: International law scholars' writings (especially "publicists of various nations").

### Jus Cogens (Peremptory Norms)

**Definition**: Fundamental norms from which no derogation permitted (even by treaty).

**Examples**:
- Prohibition on genocide
- Prohibition on slavery
- Prohibition on torture
- Prohibition on aggressive war
- Prohibition on crimes against humanity

**Effect**: Treaty violating jus cogens is void.

**Example**: Treaty authorizing genocide would be void.

**Controversially enforced**: Few courts strike down treaties as violating jus cogens.

## International Organizations

### United Nations (UN)

**Created**: 1945 (after WWII; replaced League of Nations)

**Members**: 193 states (nearly universal)

**Purposes** (Article 1, UN Charter):
1. Maintain international peace and security
2. Develop friendly relations
3. Achieve international cooperation (economic, social, cultural, humanitarian)
4. Harmonize actions of nations

**Principal organs**:

#### Security Council

**Composition**: 15 members—5 permanent (P5: US, UK, France, Russia, China) + 10 elected (2-year terms).

**Functions**: Primary responsibility for peace and security.
- Investigate disputes
- Recommend settlement
- Authorize sanctions
- Authorize use of force (Chapter VII)

**Veto power**: P5 each have veto; any can block substantive resolutions.
- **Criticism**: Paralyzes UN when P5 interests conflict (Russia vetoes Ukraine resolutions; US vetoes Israel resolutions)

**Binding decisions**: Security Council resolutions binding on all members (Article 25).

**Chapter VII**: If threat to peace, breach of peace, or act of aggression, Security Council can:
- Impose economic sanctions (freeze assets, trade embargoes)
- Authorize military force

**Examples**: Authorized force in Korea (1950), Gulf War (1991), Libya (2011).

#### General Assembly

**Composition**: All 193 members; one state, one vote.

**Functions**:
- Discuss any matter within UN scope
- Make recommendations (non-binding)
- Approve budget
- Elect Security Council non-permanent members, ICJ judges

**Non-binding**: Resolutions are recommendations (no enforcement power).

**"Uniting for Peace"**: If Security Council paralyzed, General Assembly can recommend collective measures (including force). Rarely used.

#### International Court of Justice (ICJ)

**Location**: The Hague, Netherlands

**Composition**: 15 judges (9-year terms; staggered).

**Jurisdiction**:
- **Contentious cases**: Disputes between states (only states can be parties)
- **Advisory opinions**: Legal questions from UN organs/agencies

**Consent required**: States must consent to jurisdiction.
- **Ways to consent**:
  1. Special agreement (submit specific dispute)
  2. Treaty clause (many treaties include ICJ jurisdiction for disputes)
  3. Optional clause declaration (accept ICJ jurisdiction generally; ~1/3 of states)

**Binding**: Decisions bind parties (but enforcement difficult; Security Council can enforce, but P5 veto).

**Examples**:
- **Nicaragua v. US (1986)**: US mining Nicaraguan harbors violated international law. US withdrew from Optional Clause; didn't comply.
- **Genocide cases**: Bosnia v. Serbia, Gambia v. Myanmar (ongoing).

#### Secretariat

**Head**: Secretary-General (elected by General Assembly on Security Council recommendation; 5-year term).

**Functions**: Administrative; implement UN programs; bring matters to Security Council attention.

**Current**: António Guterres (Portugal; since 2017).

#### Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC)

**Coordinates**: Economic, social, environmental, humanitarian work.

**Subsidiary bodies**: Many (e.g., Commission on Human Rights, now replaced by Human Rights Council).

#### Trusteeship Council

**Purpose**: Oversee decolonization.

**Status**: Suspended operations (1994) after last trust territory (Palau) independent.

### International Criminal Court (ICC)

**Created**: 2002 (Rome Statute)

**Location**: The Hague

**Jurisdiction**: Prosecute individuals for:
1. **Genocide**
2. **Crimes against humanity**
3. **War crimes**
4. **Crime of aggression** (added 2018)

**Complementarity**: ICC only acts if national courts unwilling or unable.

**Members**: 123 states (notable non-members: US, Russia, China, India, Israel).

**Prosecutor**: Can initiate investigations (with Pre-Trial Chamber approval).

**Defendants**: Individuals (not states).

**Arrest**: ICC has no police; relies on states to arrest.

**Criticisms**:
- **Africa bias**: Most cases from Africa (though many referred by African states)
- **Politicized**: Some arrests seen as selective (Putin indicted for Ukraine but not Western leaders for Iraq/Afghanistan)
- **Ineffective**: Many indictees remain at large (Sudan's Bashir evaded arrest for years before overthrow)

**US opposition**: Fears politically motivated prosecutions of US soldiers/officials. Passed ASPA (American Service-Members' Protection Act)—authorizes using force to free US citizens detained by ICC ("Hague Invasion Act").

### World Trade Organization (WTO)

**Created**: 1995 (successor to GATT—General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade)

**Members**: 164

**Purpose**: Reduce trade barriers; provide dispute resolution.

**Principles**:
- **Most-favored-nation (MFN)**: Treat all members equally (can't give one preferential tariff without giving all)
- **National treatment**: Treat foreign goods same as domestic (once tariff paid)
- **Tariff binding**: Once reduced, can't raise (without compensation)

**Exceptions**: Free trade agreements (FTAs), developing countries (special treatment), national security.

**Dispute Settlement**: Panels hear complaints; Appellate Body reviews (though Appellate Body non-functional since 2019—US blocked appointments).

**Enforcement**: If violation found and not remedied, complainant can impose retaliatory tariffs.

**Examples**: US-China disputes over subsidies, IP theft; EU-US disputes over aircraft subsidies (Boeing vs Airbus).

**Doha Round**: Negotiations (started 2001) stalled (developed vs developing countries on agriculture, services).

### Regional Organizations

**European Union (EU)**:
- **Supranational**: Member states delegate sovereignty
- **Institutions**: European Commission, European Parliament, Council of EU, European Court of Justice
- **Single market**: Free movement of goods, services, capital, people
- **Euro**: Common currency (19 of 27 members)
- **Brexit**: UK left (2020)

**African Union (AU)**: 55 members; promotes integration, peace, development.

**Organization of American States (OAS)**: 35 members (Americas); promotes democracy, human rights, security.

**ASEAN** (Association of Southeast Asian Nations): 10 members; economic integration, stability.

## Treaty Law

**Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT)** (1969): Codifies treaty law.

### Treaty Formation

**Negotiation**: States negotiate text.

**Signature**: Indicates agreement to text (but not yet binding).

**Ratification**: State consents to be bound (domestic process varies).
- **US**: President signs; Senate ratifies (2/3 vote); President ratifies.
- **Parliamentary systems**: Executive ratifies (sometimes requires legislature approval).

**Entry into force**: Treaty specifies (e.g., "30 days after 50th ratification").

**Executive agreements** (US): President makes without Senate; binding domestically (but less stable—next President can withdraw).

### Reservations

**Definition**: Unilateral statement excluding/modifying treaty's legal effect.

**Permissible if**:
- Treaty allows, OR
- Not incompatible with object and purpose

**Example**: US ratified Genocide Convention with reservation excluding ICJ jurisdiction for disputes.

**Effect**: Reservation modifies treaty between reserving state and others (unless others object).

### Interpretation

**VCLT Article 31**: Interpret in good faith according to:
1. **Ordinary meaning** of terms
2. **Context** (preamble, annexes)
3. **Object and purpose**

**Supplementary means** (Article 32): Preparatory work (travaux préparatoires) if ambiguous.

**Example**: Defining "investment" in investment treaty. Look at ordinary meaning, treaty's purpose (protecting investors), prior negotiations.

### Termination and Suspension

**Ways to end treaty**:
1. **Provisions in treaty**: Expiration date, withdrawal clause
2. **Consent of all parties**
3. **Material breach**: If party materially breaches, others can suspend/terminate (VCLT Article 60)
4. **Fundamental change of circumstances** (rebus sic stantibus): Unforeseen change undermining basis (rare; narrowly construed)
5. **Impossibility**: Performance impossible (e.g., treaty about island that sinks)
6. **New jus cogens**: If treaty conflicts with new peremptory norm, void

**Notice required**: Can't withdraw overnight (usually).

**US treaty withdrawals**: Paris Agreement (Trump, 2020; Biden rejoined 2021), Iran nuclear deal (Trump, 2018), INF Treaty (Trump, 2019).

## Law of Armed Conflict / International Humanitarian Law (IHL)

**Applies**: During armed conflict (war).

**Sources**: Geneva Conventions (1949), Additional Protocols (1977), customary law.

**Principles**:

### Distinction

**Combatants vs civilians**: Must distinguish; target combatants, not civilians.

**Military objectives vs civilian objects**: Attack only military objectives.

**Indiscriminate attacks**: Prohibited (e.g., carpet bombing city).

**Combatants**: Lawful if part of armed forces, carry arms openly, wear uniforms/distinctive sign, obey laws of war.

**Unlawful combatants**: Don't meet criteria (e.g., blend with civilians). Can be prosecuted (but still entitled to humane treatment).

### Proportionality

**Rule**: Incidental civilian harm can't be excessive relative to military advantage anticipated.

**Not**: Ban on all civilian casualties; requires balancing.

**Example**: Bombing munitions factory in city. If military advantage significant and civilian precautions taken, proportionate. If bomb entire neighborhood for marginal advantage, disproportionate.

### Humanity

**Prohibitions**:
- Torture, cruel treatment
- Outrages upon personal dignity
- Hostage-taking
- Summary executions
- Use of certain weapons (chemical, biological, anti-personnel mines, cluster munitions in some treaties)

**Wounded and sick**: Must be collected, cared for.

**Prisoners of war (POWs)**: Humane treatment, no coercion, repatriated after hostilities.

### Necessity

**Only military necessity justifies harm**: Can't destroy for destruction's sake.

**Prohibited**: Pillage, wanton destruction.

### Geneva Conventions (1949)

**Nearly universal**: 196 parties (all UN members + Palestine, Holy See, Cook Islands).

**Four Conventions**:
1. **GC I**: Wounded/sick in armed forces in field
2. **GC II**: Wounded/sick/shipwrecked at sea
3. **GC III**: Prisoners of war
4. **GC IV**: Civilians

**Common Article 3**: Minimum protections in non-international armed conflicts (civil wars).
- No violence to life/person
- No hostages
- No degrading treatment
- No summary executions
- Judicial guarantees

**Grave breaches**: War crimes (willful killing, torture, inhuman treatment of protected persons).

**Additional Protocols**:
- **Protocol I (1977)**: International armed conflicts (further protections for civilians)
- **Protocol II (1977)**: Non-international armed conflicts (civil wars)

**US position**: Ratified GCs but not Protocols I/II (claims Protocol I favors guerrillas).

### Use of Force (Jus ad Bellum)

**UN Charter Article 2(4)**: "All Members shall refrain... from the threat or use of force against the territorial integrity or political independence of any state."

**Exceptions**:

**1. Self-defense** (Article 51): "Inherent right of individual or collective self-defense if an armed attack occurs."
- **Anticipatory self-defense**: Controversial (Caroline doctrine—imminent threat). Israel invoked (Six-Day War, 1967).
- **Collective self-defense**: Can defend another state if invited (NATO invoked after 9/11).

**2. Security Council authorization** (Chapter VII): SC can authorize force to maintain/restore peace.

**Humanitarian intervention**: Using force to stop atrocities (without SC authorization). Controversial.
- **NATO in Kosovo (1999)**: No SC authorization (Russia/China would veto). Claimed humanitarian justification. Legally dubious but "legitimate" (Kofi Annan).
- **Responsibility to Protect (R2P)**: Emerging norm—if state fails to protect population from genocide/crimes against humanity, international community can intervene. But requires SC authorization (R2P document, 2005).

**Preemptive war**: Attacking to prevent future threat (even if not imminent). Controversial; most see as illegal.
- **Iraq War (2003)**: US claimed WMD threat. No SC authorization. Widely seen as illegal (no imminent threat).

## Human Rights Law

**Premise**: Individuals have rights governments must respect (regardless of citizenship).

**Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)** (1948): Non-binding but foundational.
- **Rights**: Life, liberty, security, freedom from slavery/torture, equality, fair trial, privacy, freedom of thought/expression/religion/assembly, education, work, adequate standard of living.

### Major Treaties

**International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)** (1966):
- Rights: Life, freedom from torture, liberty, fair trial, privacy, freedom of thought/expression/religion/assembly, political participation
- **Monitoring**: Human Rights Committee (reviews state reports, hears individual complaints if state accepts Optional Protocol)
- **173 parties** (US ratified 1992)

**International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)** (1966):
- Rights: Work, fair wages, social security, adequate standard of living, health, education
- **Progressive realization**: States must take steps (given resources)
- **171 parties** (US signed but never ratified)

**Convention Against Torture (CAT)** (1984):
- Prohibits torture, cruel/inhuman/degrading treatment
- No derogation (even in emergency)
- **173 parties** (US ratified 1994 with reservations)

**Convention on Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)** (1979):
- Equality in political, economic, social, cultural, civil life
- **189 parties** (US signed but never ratified)

**Convention on Rights of the Child (CRC)** (1989):
- Child's best interests paramount
- **196 parties** (US signed but never ratified—only country not to)

**Regional systems**:

**European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)** (1950):
- **European Court of Human Rights** (Strasbourg): Individuals can sue states
- **Binding decisions**: States comply (usually)
- **47 members** (Council of Europe)

**American Convention on Human Rights** (1969):
- **Inter-American Court of Human Rights**: Contentious cases
- **25 parties** (US not party)

**African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights** (1981):
- Includes peoples' rights (self-determination, development)
- **African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights**

### Enforcement Challenges

**Sovereignty concerns**: States resist external human rights scrutiny.

**Weak enforcement**: Most treaties rely on reporting (no sanctions).

**Reservations**: States ratify with reservations undermining obligations.

**Domestic implementation**: Treaties require domestic incorporation (US: non-self-executing treaties need legislation).

**Selective enforcement**: Powerful states (P5) rarely held accountable (veto).

**Success stories**: European Court effective; some shaming works (reputation matters).

## International Trade Law

### WTO Law

(See WTO section above.)

**GATT** (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade): Covers goods.

**GATS** (General Agreement on Trade in Services): Covers services.

**TRIPS** (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights): Minimum IP protections.

**Dispute Settlement Understanding (DSU)**: Binding arbitration.

### Free Trade Agreements (FTAs)

**Bilateral/regional agreements** eliminating/reducing tariffs.

**Examples**:
- **USMCA** (US-Mexico-Canada Agreement): Replaced NAFTA (2020)
- **CPTPP** (Comprehensive and Progressive Trans-Pacific Partnership): 11 Asia-Pacific countries (US withdrew)
- **RCEP** (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership): 15 Asia-Pacific countries (China-led)

**"Spaghetti bowl"**: Overlapping FTAs with different rules (complexity).

**Race to bottom concerns**: FTAs pressure countries to lower labor/environmental standards to compete.

## Investment Law

**Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs)**: ~3,000 worldwide.

**Purpose**: Protect foreign investors from host state misconduct.

**Protections**:
- **Expropriation**: Compensation required (direct or indirect taking)
- **Fair and equitable treatment (FET)**: Stable, predictable legal framework
- **Full protection and security**: Physical security for investments
- **National treatment**: Treat foreign investors same as domestic
- **Most-favored-nation (MFN)**: Treat all foreign investors equally

**Investor-State Dispute Settlement (ISDS)**:
- Foreign investors can sue host state in international arbitration (ICSID, UNCITRAL)
- Bypass domestic courts

**Controversies**:
- **Regulatory chill**: Fear of ISDS discourages health/environmental regulation
- **Asymmetry**: Investors can sue states; states can't sue investors
- **Legitimacy**: Private arbitrators decide public policy disputes
- **Example**: Philip Morris sued Australia, Uruguay over cigarette plain packaging (lost)
- **Example**: Occidental sued Ecuador for terminating oil contract; won $1.8 billion

**Reform**: EU phasing out ISDS in favor of Investment Court System (ICS); USMCA limits ISDS.

## Law of the Sea

**UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)** (1982):
- **"Constitution for the oceans"**
- **168 parties** (US signed but Senate never ratified; US treats as customary law)

### Maritime Zones

**Internal waters**: Landward of baseline (full sovereignty).

**Territorial sea**: Up to 12 nautical miles from baseline.
- Coastal state sovereignty (but innocent passage allowed for foreign ships)

**Contiguous zone**: Up to 24 nautical miles.
- Coastal state can enforce customs, fiscal, immigration, sanitary laws

**Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ)**: Up to 200 nautical miles.
- Coastal state has sovereign rights over resources (fishing, oil/gas)
- Other states have freedom of navigation, overflight

**Continental shelf**: Natural prolongation of land (min 200 nm, max ~350 nm if geologically extended).
- Coastal state has rights over seabed/subsoil resources

**High seas**: Beyond EEZ.
- Freedom of navigation, fishing, overflight, laying cables
- No state sovereignty

**Deep seabed** ("the Area"): Beyond national jurisdiction.
- "Common heritage of mankind"
- International Seabed Authority (ISA) regulates mining

### Disputes

**Island vs rock**: Island generates EEZ; rock doesn't (UNCLOS Article 121).
- **South China Sea arbitration** (Philippines v. China, 2016): China's "nine-dash line" invalid; artificial islands don't generate EEZ. China rejected ruling.

**Straits**: Innocent passage (territorial sea) vs transit passage (international straits—can't suspend).

**EEZ freedoms**: Can military ships/aircraft operate in other's EEZ? US says yes (freedom of navigation); China says no (military activities require consent). US conducts Freedom of Navigation Operations (FONOPs) to challenge excessive claims.

## Sovereignty and Its Limits

**Sovereignty**: Supreme authority within territory; independence from external authority.

**Westphalian system** (1648): States equal, sovereign, non-interference.

**Limits emerging**:

**Human rights**: Can't treat own citizens however wished (human rights treaties, R2P).

**International crimes**: Individuals prosecuted for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity (ICC, ad hoc tribunals).

**Humanitarian intervention**: Debated whether force allowed to stop atrocities (Kosovo, Libya).

**Economic interdependence**: Trade agreements constrain domestic policy.

**Environment**: Transboundary pollution (Trail Smelter principle—can't use territory to harm others).

**Responsibility to Protect (R2P)**: If state unable/unwilling to protect population from mass atrocities, international community has responsibility (pillars: state responsibility, international assistance, timely response including force if needed).

**Critics**: R2P used selectively (Libya yes, Syria no); masks regime change (Libya).

## Sanctions as Enforcement Tool

**Types**:

**Unilateral sanctions**: One state (or bloc) imposes (US most frequent user).
- **Example**: US sanctions Iran (nuclear program), Russia (Ukraine invasion), Venezuela (authoritarianism)

**Multilateral sanctions**: UN Security Council authorizes.
- **Example**: North Korea (nuclear weapons), Libya (Qaddafi era), Iraq (Saddam era)

**Forms**:
- **Economic**: Trade embargoes, asset freezes
- **Financial**: Cut off from banking systems (SWIFT)
- **Travel bans**: Restrict officials' movement
- **Arms embargoes**: Prohibit weapons sales

**Effectiveness**:
- **Mixed record**: Iran came to negotiating table (JCPOA, 2015); Cuba/North Korea endured decades without regime change
- **Humanitarian concerns**: Hurt civilian population (Iraq sanctions, 1990s, estimated hundreds of thousands of child deaths)
- **Smart sanctions**: Targeted at elites (asset freezes, travel bans) to minimize humanitarian impact

**Evasion**: Sanctions busting common (smuggling, front companies, sympathetic states).

## Jurisdiction

**Basis for exercising authority**:

**Territorial**: Conduct within state's territory.

**Nationality**: Conduct by state's nationals (anywhere).

**Passive personality**: Conduct against state's nationals (controversial; few use).

**Protective**: Conduct threatening state's security (counterfeiting currency, espionage).

**Universal**: Certain crimes (piracy, genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, torture, slavery) can be prosecuted by any state (regardless of location, perpetrator/victim nationality).
- **Rationale**: Crimes so heinous, any state has interest in prosecuting.
- **Example**: Belgium tried Rwandan génocidaires; Spain tried Argentine junta members.

**Conflicts**: Multiple states may have jurisdiction; priority unclear (usually first to arrest).

## Key Terms

- **Treaty**: Binding written agreement between states
- **Customary international law**: General practice accepted as law
- **Jus cogens**: Peremptory norm (no derogation)
- **UN Security Council**: 15 members (P5 have veto); primary peace/security responsibility
- **ICJ**: International Court of Justice (state-to-state disputes)
- **ICC**: International Criminal Court (prosecute individuals for atrocity crimes)
- **WTO**: World Trade Organization (trade liberalization, dispute settlement)
- **Geneva Conventions**: Core IHL treaties (protect civilians, POWs, wounded in war)
- **UDHR**: Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
- **ICCPR**: International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights
- **ICESCR**: International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights
- **UNCLOS**: UN Convention on the Law of the Sea

- **ISDS**: Investor-State Dispute Settlement (foreign investors sue host states)
- **R2P**: Responsibility to Protect (intervene to stop mass atrocities)
- **Sanctions**: Economic/political penalties for violations
- **Universal jurisdiction**: Any state can prosecute certain international crimes

## Summary

International law governs relations between states through treaties, customary law, and general principles. Unlike domestic law, it lacks centralized enforcement, relying instead on consent, reciprocity, reputation, and institutional mechanisms.

The United Nations, created in 1945, maintains peace through the Security Council (with P5 veto power), provides dispute resolution via the International Court of Justice, and coordinates development through various organs. The International Criminal Court prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity, though facing legitimacy challenges and non-participation by major powers.

Treaty law, codified in the Vienna Convention, governs formation, interpretation, and termination of agreements. States can make reservations (if compatible with object and purpose) and withdraw (if treaty permits or fundamental change occurs).

International humanitarian law, embodied in the Geneva Conventions, regulates armed conflict through principles of distinction, proportionality, humanity, and necessity. The UN Charter prohibits use of force except in self-defense or with Security Council authorization, though humanitarian intervention and responsibility to protect remain contentious.

Human rights law, grounded in the Universal Declaration and implemented through treaties like the ICCPR and ICESCR, protects individuals from state abuses, though enforcement remains weak outside Europe. Regional systems (European Court of Human Rights) show greater effectiveness.

International trade law operates through the WTO's dispute settlement system, while bilateral investment treaties provide investor-state arbitration (ISDS), raising concerns about regulatory chill. The Law of the Sea Convention establishes maritime zones (territorial sea, EEZ, high seas) and dispute resolution for overlapping claims.

Sovereignty faces limits from human rights obligations, international criminal prosecution, humanitarian intervention norms, and economic interdependence. Sanctions—unilateral or multilateral—serve as primary enforcement tools, though effectiveness varies and humanitarian costs arise.

International law's binding force stems not from coercion but from states' interests in cooperation, predictability, and legitimacy. While imperfect and often violated, it shapes state behavior and provides frameworks for addressing global challenges from climate change to pandemics to armed conflict.
