# Contract & Property Law

## Contract Law: Foundation of Market Economies

Contracts are legally enforceable agreements. Contract law provides predictability, enabling people to plan, invest, and cooperate.

### Why Contracts Matter

**Economic function**:
- **Facilitate exchange**: Trade goods/services across time and space
- **Allocate risk**: Parties assign risks (price changes, performance failure)
- **Enable credit**: Lenders loan based on enforceable repayment promises
- **Coordinate production**: Complex supply chains require enforceable commitments

**Without enforceable contracts**: Only spot transactions; no investment in relationship-specific assets; economic stagnation.

## Contract Formation

A valid contract requires:
1. **Offer**
2. **Acceptance**
3. **Consideration**
4. (Sometimes) **Writing** (Statute of Frauds)
5. **Capacity**
6. **Legality**

### 1. Offer

**Definition**: Manifestation of willingness to enter bargain, made so other party understands acceptance will conclude deal.

**Requirements**:
- **Definite terms**: Who, what, when (price, quantity, subject matter)
- **Intent to be bound**: Objective test—would reasonable person believe offeror intended commitment?

**Examples**:
- **Offer**: "I'll sell you my car for $5,000" (specific terms, intent)
- **Not offer**: "I'm thinking about selling my car" (no commitment)
- **Not offer**: Advertisement (usually invitation to make offer, not offer itself)

**Exception**: **Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store (1957)**: Ad saying "1 fur coat, worth $139.50, first come first served, $1" was offer (clear terms, limited quantity).

**Termination of offer**:
- **Revocation**: Offeror withdraws before acceptance
- **Rejection**: Offeree says no
- **Counteroffer**: Changes terms (rejects original, makes new offer)
- **Lapse**: Time expires
- **Death/incapacity**: Of either party

**Option contract**: Offeree pays to keep offer open; offeror can't revoke during period.

### 2. Acceptance

**Definition**: Manifestation of assent to terms of offer.

**Mirror image rule** (common law): Acceptance must match offer exactly; any variation is counteroffer.
- **Example**: Offer to sell for $5,000; response "I'll buy for $4,500" is counteroffer (not acceptance).

**UCC § 2-207** (for sale of goods): Additional/different terms don't necessarily prevent acceptance.
- **Battle of the forms**: Buyer's purchase order vs seller's confirmation often have conflicting boilerplate.
- **Rule**: Contract formed if parties intend; conflicting terms drop out; UCC gap-fillers apply.

**Method of acceptance**:
- **Can be any reasonable method** unless offer specifies
- **Unilateral contract**: Acceptance by performance (e.g., reward offer)
- **Bilateral contract**: Acceptance by promise

**Mailbox rule**: Acceptance effective when sent (if reasonable method); revocation effective when received.
- **Example**: Offer mailed Monday. Acceptance mailed Wednesday. Revocation mailed Tuesday but received Thursday. Contract formed Wednesday (acceptance mailed before revocation received).

**Silence**: Generally not acceptance (except if prior dealings establish pattern).

### 3. Consideration

**Definition**: Bargained-for exchange; each party gives something of value.

**Elements**:
1. **Benefit to promisor OR detriment to promisee**
2. **Bargained-for** (not gift)

**Examples**:
- **Consideration exists**: "I'll pay you $100 to mow my lawn" (promise for performance)
- **No consideration**: "I promise to give you $100" (gift promise, not enforceable)
- **No consideration**: Past consideration—"Thanks for helping me move last month; I promise to pay you $50" (not bargained-for)

**Adequacy**: Courts don't inquire if exchange is fair (freedom of contract).
- **Hamer v. Sidway (1891)**: Uncle promised nephew $5,000 to abstain from drinking/smoking until 21. Nephew's forbearance was consideration (even if beneficial to nephew).

**Nominal consideration**: "Peppercorn" ($1 for house worth $500,000) may not suffice if court finds it's pretextual (sham).

**Pre-existing duty rule**: Promise to do what you're already obligated to do isn't consideration.
- **Example**: Contractor agrees to build house for $200,000. Midway through, demands $250,000 to finish. Homeowner agrees. Modification unenforceable (contractor already obligated).
- **Modern trend** (UCC § 2-209): Modifications don't need consideration if in good faith.

**Illusory promise**: Appears to commit but doesn't ("I'll buy if I feel like it"). Not consideration.

### 4. Statute of Frauds

**Requires writing** for certain contracts (to prevent fraud/perjury):

**MY LEGS** (mnemonic):
- **M**arriage (promises in consideration of marriage)
- **Y**ear (contracts not performable within one year)
- **L**and (sale or transfer of interest in real property)
- **E**xecutor (promises to pay estate debts from own funds)
- **G**oods over $500 (UCC § 2-201; now often $5,000 in revised UCC)
- **S**urety (promises to pay another's debt)

**Writing requirement**:
- **Not full contract**: Just memorandum signed by party to be charged, with essential terms
- **Electronic**: E-signatures generally valid (UETA, E-SIGN Act)

**Exceptions** (enforceable despite no writing):
- **Part performance** (real estate): Buyer paid, took possession, made improvements
- **Merchant confirmation** (UCC): Between merchants, confirmation sent, no objection within 10 days
- **Specially manufactured goods** (UCC): Seller began manufacturing for buyer
- **Admission in court**: Party admits contract exists

### 5. Capacity

Certain persons lack capacity to contract:
- **Minors** (under 18): Can disaffirm contracts (voidable, not void). Exception: necessaries (food, shelter, clothing)—liable for reasonable value.
- **Mental incapacity**: If unable to understand nature/consequences, can disaffirm.
- **Intoxication**: If so intoxicated can't understand, and other party knows, can disaffirm.

**Voidable**: Party lacking capacity can enforce or cancel; other party bound.

### 6. Legality

Contracts for illegal purposes unenforceable.

**Examples**:
- Contract to sell illegal drugs
- Contract to commit crime/tort
- Usurious loan (interest above legal limit)
- Non-compete agreements (enforceable only if reasonable in scope/time/geography)

**Public policy**: Some legal contracts unenforceable if violate public policy (e.g., exculpatory clause for gross negligence).

## Contract Interpretation

What do contract's words mean?

### Plain Meaning Rule

**Start with text**: If unambiguous, enforce plain meaning.
- **Objective theory**: What would reasonable person understand, not secret intent.

**Ambiguity**: If term susceptible to multiple reasonable interpretations, ambiguous.
- **Contra proferentem**: Construe against drafter (especially in adhesion contracts).

### Parol Evidence Rule

**Rule**: If parties intended writing to be complete and final (integrated contract), prior/contemporaneous oral statements can't contradict writing.

**Rationale**: Parties chose to reduce agreement to writing; should rely on that.

**Exceptions**:
- **Explain ambiguity**: Parol evidence admissible to clarify unclear terms
- **Show defect in formation**: Fraud, duress, mistake, lack of consideration
- **Subsequent modification**: Agreements after contract signed
- **Collateral agreement**: Separate deal on different subject

**Example**: Written contract says "Seller will deliver 1,000 widgets." Buyer claims seller orally promised delivery by June 1. Parol evidence rule bars testimony (contradicts/adds to writing). BUT if writing is ambiguous about date, parol evidence admissible to clarify.

### Trade Usage and Course of Dealing

**UCC**: Interpret contracts considering:
- **Course of performance**: How parties acted under this contract
- **Course of dealing**: How parties acted in prior transactions
- **Trade usage**: Industry custom

**Example**: Contract for "dozen" widgets. In widget industry, "dozen" means 13 (baker's dozen). Court interprets as 13.

### Implied Terms

**Courts imply terms** to fill gaps:
- **Duty of good faith and fair dealing**: Parties can't undermine contract's purpose
- **Best efforts**: If contract requires "best efforts," party must make vigorous attempt
- **Commercially reasonable**: UCC implies reasonableness where terms undefined

**Wood v. Lucy, Lady Duff-Gordon (1917)** (Cardozo): Designer gave exclusive marketing rights. Didn't expressly promise to market. Court implied duty to use reasonable efforts (else promise illusory).

## Breach and Remedies

**Breach**: Failure to perform contractual duty without legal excuse.

### Material vs Minor Breach

**Material breach**: Substantial failure; non-breaching party can:
- **Suspend performance**
- **Sue for damages**
- **Cancel contract** (if total breach)

**Minor breach**: Non-breaching party can sue for damages but must still perform.

**Test** (Restatement § 241):
- Extent to which injured party deprived of expected benefit
- Extent to which injured party can be compensated by damages
- Likelihood breaching party will cure
- Willful vs good faith breach

**Example**: Contract to build house with "Reading pipe" (specific brand). Builder used different brand of equal quality. Minor breach (Jacob & Youngs v. Kent, 1921)—homeowner must pay (minus damages for difference in value, if any).

### Anticipatory Repudiation

**Occurs when**: Before performance due, party clearly indicates won't perform.

**Non-breaching party can**:
- **Treat as breach immediately** and sue (don't have to wait)
- **Suspend own performance**
- **Demand adequate assurances** (UCC § 2-609)

**Hochster v. De La Tour (1853)**: Courier hired for June trip. In April, employer said trip canceled. Courier could sue immediately (didn't have to wait until June).

### Remedies: Expectation Damages

**Goal**: Put non-breaching party in position they'd be in if contract had been performed.

**Formula**: Expected value - (what received + costs avoided)

**Example**: Buyer contracts to buy machine for $10,000. Seller breaches. Buyer buys substitute for $12,000.
- **Expectation damages**: $2,000 (extra cost)

**Limitations**:

**1. Causation**: Damages must flow from breach.

**2. Foreseeability** (Hadley v. Baxendale, 1854):
- Damages recoverable only if:
  - Arise naturally from breach (general damages), OR
  - Within contemplation of parties at contracting (special damages)

**Example**: Mill's crankshaft broke. Contracted with carrier to transport to manufacturer as model for replacement. Carrier delayed. Mill lost profits.
- **Holding**: Lost profits not recoverable; carrier didn't know shaft was only one (so delay would stop entire mill).

**3. Certainty**: Damages must be proven with reasonable certainty (not speculative).
- **Lost profits**: Established business can recover; new business harder (uncertain).

**4. Mitigation**: Non-breaching party must take reasonable steps to minimize damages.
- **Example**: Landlord must try to re-rent apartment if tenant breaches (can't let sit vacant and collect full rent).

### Reliance Damages

**Alternative measure**: Restore party to position before contract (out-of-pocket losses).

**Used when**: Expectation damages too uncertain.

**Example**: Investor relied on entrepreneur's promise to form business. Spent $50,000 on prep. Entrepreneur breached. Business prospects uncertain. Investor recovers $50,000 reliance damages.

### Restitution

**Goal**: Prevent unjust enrichment; return value conferred.

**Used when**:
- No valid contract but one party benefited at other's expense
- Non-breaching party prefers restitution to contract price

**Example**: Contract to renovate house for $100,000. Owner repudiates after contractor spent $80,000 in labor/materials increasing home value by $90,000. Contractor can choose restitution ($90,000) over contract expectation ($100,000 - $80,000 cost = $20,000 profit).

### Specific Performance

**Equitable remedy**: Court orders breaching party to perform.

**Available when**:
- Legal damages inadequate
- Typically for unique goods (land, art, heirlooms)
- NOT for personal services (involuntary servitude concerns)

**Real estate**: Specific performance presumed available (each parcel unique).

**Goods**: UCC § 2-716 allows if goods unique or "other proper circumstances."

**Employment**: Courts won't order employee to work, but may enjoin employee from working for competitor (negative injunction if non-compete valid).

### Liquidated Damages

**Definition**: Parties agree in advance to damages amount if breach.

**Enforceable if**:
1. Damages difficult to estimate at contracting
2. Amount is reasonable forecast of actual damages (not penalty)

**Penalty**: Unenforceable (meant to punish, not compensate).

**Example**: Construction contract: $1,000/day for late completion. Enforceable if reasonable estimate of lost rent. But $1 million/day likely penalty.

## Defenses to Contract Enforcement

### Duress

**Definition**: Contract induced by improper threat leaving no reasonable alternative.

**Economic duress**: Threat to breach contract unless modification (if no alternative, modification voidable).

**Example**: Day before wedding, caterer demands double price. Couple has no alternative (no time to find replacement). Modification voidable for duress.

### Undue Influence

**Definition**: Unfair persuasion overcoming free will, typically in confidential relationship (attorney-client, caregiver-elderly).

**Example**: Caregiver convinces elderly person to "sell" house for $10,000. Voidable if caregiver abused trust.

### Misrepresentation and Fraud

**Fraudulent misrepresentation** (voidable):
1. False statement of material fact (not opinion)
2. Scienter (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard)
3. Intent to induce reliance
4. Justifiable reliance
5. Damages

**Non-fraudulent misrepresentation**: Even innocent misrepresentation of material fact can make contract voidable.

**Silence**: Generally no duty to disclose, EXCEPT:
- Fiduciary relationship
- Active concealment
- Half-truth
- Correcting past statement

**Example**: Seller knows house has termites. Doesn't disclose. Fraud. Buyer can rescind or sue for damages.

### Mistake

**Mutual mistake**: Both parties mistaken about basic assumption; contract voidable by adversely affected party.
- **Sherwood v. Walker (1887)**: Sold cow believed barren for $80. Actually pregnant (worth $1,000). Mutual mistake as to fertility; contract voidable.

**Unilateral mistake**: Generally not voidable UNLESS:
- Other party knew/should have known, OR
- Mistake makes enforcement unconscionable

**Mistake of value**: Not grounds for rescission (risk allocation).

### Impossibility, Impracticability, Frustration of Purpose

**Impossibility**: Performance objectively impossible (rare).
- **Example**: Contract to sell specific painting. Painting destroyed. Impossible.

**Impracticability** (UCC § 2-615, Restatement):
- Performance possible but extremely burdensome due to unforeseen event
- Event not assumed risk
- **Example**: Suez Canal closure drastically increases shipping cost (might excuse).

**Frustration of purpose**: Performance possible but contract's purpose destroyed.
- **Krell v. Henry (1903)**: Rented room to view King's coronation parade. Parade canceled. Purpose frustrated; discharged.

**COVID-19 cases**: Many contracts excused (government shutdown orders), but not all (increased cost alone typically insufficient).

### Unconscionability

**Definition**: Contract so one-sided as to be oppressive (UCC § 2-302, Restatement).

**Two types**:

**Procedural unconscionability**: Unfair bargaining process (adhesion contract, fine print, high-pressure tactics).

**Substantive unconscionability**: Unfair terms (grossly one-sided).

**Remedy**: Court can refuse enforcement, strike clause, or limit application.

**Example**: Rent-to-own agreement charging effectively 100% annual interest for TV to low-income consumer. Likely unconscionable (procedural + substantive).

## Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)

**Scope**: Transactions in goods (tangible, movable property). Does NOT apply to services, real estate, IP.

**Key UCC provisions**:

**Article 2 (Sales of Goods)**:
- **Liberal formation** (§ 2-204): Contract can be formed any manner showing agreement, even if terms uncertain.
- **Gap-fillers**: UCC supplies missing terms (price, delivery, etc.).
- **Battle of forms** (§ 2-207): Acceptance with additional terms doesn't fail (discussed above).
- **Modification** (§ 2-209): No consideration needed; just good faith.
- **Statute of Frauds** (§ 2-201): Writing required for goods ≥ $500.
- **Warranties** (§ 2-314, 2-315): Implied warranties of merchantability (fit for ordinary use) and fitness for particular purpose.
- **Remedies** (§ 2-711 to 2-725): Buyer/seller remedies, including cover (buy substitute), resale, specific performance.

**Merchants**: UCC imposes higher standards on merchants (professionals) than casual sellers.

## Promissory Estoppel (Detrimental Reliance)

**Purpose**: Enforce promises even without consideration if justice requires.

**Elements** (Restatement § 90):
1. Promise
2. Promisor should reasonably expect reliance
3. Promisee actually relies
4. Reliance is detrimental
5. Injustice can be avoided only by enforcement

**Example**: Employer promises pension. Employee retires in reliance. Employer reneges. No contract (past consideration). But promissory estoppel enforces promise.

**Ricketts v. Scothorn (1898)**: Grandfather gave granddaughter promissory note so she could quit job. She quit. He died; estate refused to pay. Promise enforceable via promissory estoppel.

**Remedy**: Usually reliance damages (not full expectation).

## Quasi-Contract (Unjust Enrichment)

**Purpose**: Prevent one party from retaining benefit unjustly at another's expense.

**NOT a real contract**: Implied-in-law remedy.

**Elements**:
1. Plaintiff conferred benefit on defendant
2. Defendant knew/appreciated benefit
3. Defendant retained benefit
4. Unjust for defendant to keep without paying

**Example**: Doctor treats unconscious accident victim. Victim owes reasonable value of services (quasi-contract).

**Not available if** valid contract covers subject matter.

## Property Law: Real vs Personal

**Real property**: Land and things attached (buildings, trees).
**Personal property**: Everything else (movable).

### Why Property Rights Matter

**Hernando de Soto** (The Mystery of Capital):
- Poor countries lack clear property titles
- Informal settlements: people occupy land without legal ownership
- Can't use as collateral for loans
- Trapped in subsistence; can't access capital to start businesses
- **Solution**: Formalize property rights (titling programs)

**Tragedy of the commons**: Without property rights, resources overused (everyone takes, no one maintains).
- **Example**: Overfishing in international waters (no owner, no incentive to conserve).

**Private property enables**:
- Investment (improve if you own)
- Trade (sell to higher-value user)
- Collateral (borrow against assets)

## Estates in Land

**Estate**: Possessory interest in land.

### Fee Simple Absolute

**Largest estate**: Infinite duration; fully transferable.

**Language**: "To A" or "To A and her heirs" (but "heirs" just indicates duration; A can sell).

**Rights**: Possess, use, transfer (sell, gift, devise by will).

### Life Estate

**Duration**: Measuring life.

**Language**: "To A for life"

**Life tenant's rights**: Possess, use, collect rents.

**Limitations**: Can't commit waste (damage property or deplete resources, harming future interest holder).

**Future interest**: Remainder (to third party) or reversion (to grantor).

**Example**: "To A for life, then to B." A has life estate; B has remainder in fee simple. When A dies, B owns outright.

### Future Interests

**Reversion**: Grantor's retained interest.
- **Example**: "To A for life." Grantor has reversion.

**Remainder**: Third party's future interest.
- **Vested**: Certain to become possessory (if survives).
  - **Example**: "To A for life, then to B." B has vested remainder.
- **Contingent**: Conditioned on uncertain event.
  - **Example**: "To A for life, then to B if B graduates college." B has contingent remainder.

**Executory interest**: Cuts short prior estate.
- **Example**: "To A, but if liquor sold on premises, to B." B has executory interest.

**Rule Against Perpetuities** (RAP): Contingent interests must vest or fail within 21 years after life in being at creation. (Complex; many states abolished or reformed.)

## Concurrent Ownership

**Tenancy in common**: Each owns undivided fractional share; can sell share; no right of survivorship.

**Joint tenancy**: Each owns whole with right of survivorship (when one dies, others automatically own).
- **Requires four unities**: Time, title, interest, possession.
- **Severance**: Unilateral transfer breaks joint tenancy (becomes tenancy in common).

**Tenancy by the entirety**: Married couple owns as one unit; right of survivorship; neither can unilaterally transfer (available in ~25 states).

**Community property** (9 states: AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI):
- Earnings/assets during marriage owned equally
- Each spouse can devise half by will (no automatic survivorship unless specified)

## Adverse Possession

**Acquire ownership** by possessing someone else's land for statutory period (typically 10-20 years).

**Requirements**:
1. **Actual**: Physical possession
2. **Open and notorious**: Visible (true owner could discover)
3. **Exclusive**: Not sharing with owner/public
4. **Hostile**: Without permission (adversarial)
5. **Continuous**: For full statutory period

**Rationale**:
- Quiets titles (resolves disputes)
- Punishes sleeping owners
- Rewards productive use

**Example**: A builds fence 10 feet into B's land, thinking it's boundary. Maintains for 15 years. A acquires 10-foot strip by adverse possession.

**Tacking**: Successive adverse possessors can add periods (if privity).

**Disabilities**: Statutory period tolled if true owner under disability (minor, insane, imprisoned) at time possession begins.

## Easements

**Definition**: Right to use another's land for specific purpose.

**Types**:

**Appurtenant easement**: Benefits particular land (dominant estate) by burdening another (servient estate).
- **Example**: A owns landlocked parcel. Easement across B's land to reach road. A's land is dominant; B's servient.
- **Runs with land**: Transfers automatically to future owners.

**Easement in gross**: Benefits individual, not land.
- **Example**: Utility company has easement to run power lines across property.

**Creation**:

1. **Express grant/reservation**: Written agreement
2. **Implication**: From prior use (subdivision; lot always used neighbor's driveway)
3. **Necessity**: Landlocked parcel (but terminates if necessity ends)
4. **Prescription**: Adverse use (like adverse possession but for use only, not ownership). Requires open, notorious, continuous, hostile use for statutory period.

**Scope**: Limited to purpose. Servient owner can use land as long as doesn't interfere.

**Termination**: Abandonment, merger (same person owns both), release, prescription (servient owner blocks use).

## Covenants and Equitable Servitudes

**Real covenant**: Promise regarding land use that runs with land (binds successors).

**Requirements to run**:
1. Writing
2. Intent to bind successors
3. Touch and concern land (affect use/value)
4. Privity (horizontal + vertical)

**Remedy**: Damages.

**Equitable servitude**: Promise enforceable in equity (not just law).

**Requirements**:
1. Writing (or implied from common plan)
2. Intent to bind
3. Touch and concern
4. Notice (actual, record, or inquiry)

**Remedy**: Injunction.

**Example**: Subdivision has covenant restricting lots to residential use. Homeowner tries to open store. Neighbors can get injunction (equitable servitude).

**Changed conditions**: If neighborhood character fundamentally changed, covenant may be unenforceable (equitable defense).

## Zoning

**Government regulation** of land use (residential, commercial, industrial).

**Authority**: Police power (health, safety, welfare).

**Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty (1926)**: Zoning constitutional (not taking).

**Variance**: Exception for hardship (e.g., odd-shaped lot can't comply).

**Non-conforming use**: Pre-existing use can continue (grandfathered) but typically can't expand.

**Spot zoning**: Rezoning small parcel inconsistent with plan (often invalid).

**Exclusionary zoning**: Zoning to exclude affordable housing (large-lot requirements, no apartments).
- **Mount Laurel doctrine** (NJ): Municipalities must allow fair share of affordable housing.

## Landlord-Tenant Law

**Types of tenancies**:

**Tenancy for years**: Fixed duration (e.g., 1-year lease). No notice required to terminate.

**Periodic tenancy**: Automatically renews (month-to-month). Requires notice to terminate (typically equal to period).

**Tenancy at will**: No fixed duration; either party can terminate anytime (may require notice).

**Tenancy at sufferance**: Holdover tenant (lease expired but still there). Landlord can evict or treat as renewal.

### Landlord's Duties

**Deliver possession**: Tenant entitled to occupy.

**Covenant of quiet enjoyment**: Landlord won't interfere with possession (actual or constructive eviction).

**Implied warranty of habitability** (residential): Premises fit for human habitation (heat, water, no vermin).
- **Remedy**: Tenant can withhold rent, repair-and-deduct, or terminate.
- **Modern trend**: Most states recognize (overruled old "caveat lessee").

**Common areas**: Landlord must maintain.

### Tenant's Duties

**Pay rent**: Failure allows landlord to evict (through legal process, not self-help).

**Not commit waste**: No damage beyond normal wear-and-tear.

**Not disturb neighbors**: Nuisance violations may justify eviction.

### Assignment vs Sublease

**Assignment**: Tenant transfers entire remaining term. Assignee in privity with landlord.
- **Tenant still liable** if assignee doesn't pay (original lease privity).

**Sublease**: Tenant transfers less than entire term (time or space). Subtenant not in privity with landlord.
- **Tenant liable** to landlord; subtenant liable to tenant.

**Lease may restrict**: Many leases require landlord consent to assign/sublease.

## Takings: Eminent Domain and Regulatory Takings

**5th Amendment**: "...nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation."

### Eminent Domain (Physical Taking)

**Government can condemn property** for public use if pays just compensation.

**Public use**: Broadly construed.

**Kelo v. City of New London (2005)**:
- **Facts**: City condemned homes for private development (economic revitalization)
- **Holding**: Economic development qualifies as "public use"
- **Dissent** (O'Connor): Eviscerates public use limitation; any taking for higher-tax-generating use would be OK
- **Backlash**: Many states passed laws restricting eminent domain for private development

**Just compensation**: Fair market value.

### Regulatory Takings

**If regulation goes too far**, constitutes taking requiring compensation.

**Tests**:

**Penn Central Transportation Co. v. New York City (1978)**: Balancing test:
1. Economic impact on owner
2. Interference with distinct investment-backed expectations
3. Character of governmental action

**Per se takings**:
- **Physical invasion**: Government authorizes permanent physical occupation (Loretto v. Teleprompter, 1982)
- **Total economic deprivation**: Regulation denies all economically beneficial use (Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, 1992)

**Example**: Zoning prohibits building on wetland lot. If lot worth $0 after regulation, per se taking (Lucas). But if retains some value, Penn Central balancing test.

**Exactions and conditions**: Government can condition permit on mitigation if:
- **Nexus**: Condition related to development's impact (Nollan v. California Coastal Comm'n, 1987)
- **Rough proportionality**: Condition's extent proportional to impact (Dolan v. City of Tigard, 1994)

## Key Terms

- **Offer**: Willingness to enter bargain on specified terms
- **Acceptance**: Assent to offer's terms
- **Consideration**: Bargained-for exchange
- **Statute of Frauds**: Certain contracts must be in writing
- **Capacity**: Legal ability to contract
- **Material breach**: Substantial failure to perform
- **Anticipatory repudiation**: Indication before performance due that party won't perform
- **Expectation damages**: Put non-breaching party in position if contract performed
- **Reliance damages**: Restore party to pre-contract position
- **Restitution**: Prevent unjust enrichment
- **Specific performance**: Equitable remedy ordering performance
- **Liquidated damages**: Pre-agreed damages amount
- **Duress**: Improper threat inducing contract
- **Fraud**: Intentional misrepresentation of material fact
- **Mistake**: Erroneous belief about fact
- **Unconscionability**: Oppressively one-sided contract
- **Promissory estoppel**: Enforce promise based on detrimental reliance
- **Quasi-contract**: Implied-in-law obligation to prevent unjust enrichment
- **Fee simple absolute**: Largest estate; infinite duration
- **Life estate**: Possessory interest for duration of life
- **Remainder**: Future interest in third party
- **Reversion**: Future interest in grantor
- **Joint tenancy**: Concurrent ownership with right of survivorship
- **Adverse possession**: Acquire ownership through continuous hostile possession
- **Easement**: Right to use another's land
- **Covenant**: Promise regarding land use that runs with land
- **Zoning**: Government land use regulation
- **Eminent domain**: Government power to take property for public use with compensation
- **Regulatory taking**: Regulation so burdensome it constitutes taking

## Summary

Contract law enables market economies by making promises enforceable. Valid contracts require offer, acceptance, consideration, and sometimes writing (Statute of Frauds). Courts interpret contracts starting with plain meaning, excluding prior oral statements that contradict writings (parol evidence rule). When breach occurs, expectation damages aim to put the non-breaching party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed. Specific performance is available for unique goods like real estate. Defenses include duress, fraud, mistake, and unconscionability. The UCC liberalizes contract formation for sales of goods and implies warranties. Promissory estoppel and quasi-contract provide remedies even without valid contracts.

Property law defines rights in land and goods. The fee simple absolute is the largest estate, while life estates and future interests divide ownership across time. Concurrent ownership takes forms like joint tenancy (with survivorship rights) and tenancy in common. Adverse possession allows occupiers to gain title through long-term, open, hostile use. Easements grant use rights in others' land. Covenants restrict land use and can bind successors. Zoning regulations control development, subject to constitutional limits.

Landlord-tenant law has evolved to impose duties like the implied warranty of habitability on landlords. The Takings Clause requires compensation when government physically occupies or severely restricts property use, as exemplified in controversial cases like Kelo v. City of New London.

Secure property rights, as Hernando de Soto argued, are essential for economic development—enabling owners to invest, trade, and access credit. Without clear titles and enforceable contracts, economies stagnate.
