# Constitutional Law

## What Constitutions Do

A constitution is the supreme law establishing a government's structure, powers, and limitations.

### Core Functions

**1. Create government structure**:
- Which institutions exist (executive, legislative, judicial)
- How officials are selected
- What procedures they must follow

**2. Allocate and limit power**:
- What each branch can/cannot do
- Division between national and subnational governments
- Individual rights that government cannot infringe

**3. Provide amendment process**:
- More difficult than ordinary legislation (entrenchment)
- Ensures stability while permitting evolution

**4. Express fundamental values**:
- Equality, liberty, democracy, rule of law
- Constitutes national identity

### Written vs Unwritten Constitutions

**Written** (most countries):
- **Single document**: US Constitution, German Basic Law
- **Codification**: Easier to identify what is/isn't constitutional
- **Rigid**: Formal amendment process

**Unwritten** (UK, New Zealand):
- **Multiple sources**: Statutes, court decisions, conventions
- **Flexible**: Parliament can amend by simple majority
- **Continuity**: Evolved over centuries

**US approach**: Written Constitution + amendments + judicial interpretation (creates "living" dimension).

## US Constitution: Structure and Content

Ratified 1788; in effect 1789. Oldest written national constitution still in use.

### Articles I-VII

**Article I: Legislative Power**
- Section 1: All legislative power vested in Congress (bicameral: House + Senate)
- Section 2-3: House and Senate composition, elections
- Section 7: How bills become laws (presentment, veto, override)
- Section 8: Enumerated powers (18 clauses)
  - Tax and spend for general welfare
  - Regulate interstate/foreign commerce
  - Establish post offices, patents/copyrights
  - Declare war, raise armies
  - Necessary and Proper Clause (implied powers)
- Section 9: Limits on Congress (no ex post facto laws, no bills of attainder)
- Section 10: Limits on states (no treaties, no impairment of contracts)

**Article II: Executive Power**
- Section 1: Executive power vested in President (4-year term, Electoral College)
- Section 2: Commander-in-chief, pardon power, treaty power (with Senate), appointment power
- Section 3: State of Union, convene Congress, take care that laws be faithfully executed
- Section 4: Impeachment for treason, bribery, high crimes and misdemeanors

**Article III: Judicial Power**
- Section 1: Judicial power vested in Supreme Court + inferior courts Congress creates
- Section 2: Jurisdiction (federal questions, diversity, cases affecting ambassadors)
- Section 3: Treason defined (two witnesses or confession required)

**Article IV: States' Relations**
- Full faith and credit (honor other states' judgments)
- Privileges and immunities (citizens of each state equal rights in other states)
- Admission of new states
- Republican form of government guaranteed

**Article V: Amendment Process**
- Proposal: 2/3 of both houses OR convention called by 2/3 of states
- Ratification: 3/4 of state legislatures OR 3/4 of state conventions

**Article VI: Supremacy Clause**
- Constitution, federal laws, treaties are "supreme law of the land"
- State judges bound thereby
- No religious test for office

**Article VII: Ratification**
- Required 9 of 13 states to ratify

### Bill of Rights (First 10 Amendments, 1791)

**1st Amendment**: Religion, speech, press, assembly, petition
**2nd Amendment**: Right to keep and bear arms
**3rd Amendment**: No quartering of soldiers
**4th Amendment**: No unreasonable searches and seizures
**5th Amendment**: Grand jury, double jeopardy, self-incrimination, due process, takings
**6th Amendment**: Criminal trial rights (speedy, public, jury, confront witnesses, counsel)
**7th Amendment**: Jury trial in civil cases over $20
**8th Amendment**: No excessive bail/fines, no cruel and unusual punishment
**9th Amendment**: Enumeration of rights doesn't deny others
**10th Amendment**: Powers not delegated to US reserved to states/people

### Subsequent Amendments (Highlights)

**11th (1795)**: States have sovereign immunity from suits by out-of-state citizens
**12th (1804)**: Separate electoral votes for President and VP
**13th (1865)**: Abolition of slavery
**14th (1868)**: Citizenship, privileges/immunities, due process, equal protection (applies Bill of Rights to states)
**15th (1870)**: Voting rights regardless of race
**16th (1913)**: Income tax authorized
**17th (1913)**: Direct election of Senators
**19th (1920)**: Women's suffrage
**22nd (1951)**: Presidential term limits (2 terms)
**24th (1964)**: Abolition of poll taxes
**26th (1971)**: Voting age reduced to 18

**Total**: 27 amendments. Only 17 since Bill of Rights (over 230+ years).

## Separation of Powers

**Purpose**: Prevent concentration of power; checks and balances.

### Three Branches

| Branch | Powers | Checks on Others |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| **Legislative** (Congress) | Make laws, tax, spend, declare war, confirm appointments, ratify treaties, impeach | Override veto, reject appointments/treaties, impeach President/judges, control budget |
| **Executive** (President) | Enforce laws, command military, make treaties, appoint officials, veto legislation | Veto bills, appoint judges, pardon criminals, executive orders |
| **Judicial** (Courts) | Interpret laws, decide cases, judicial review | Declare laws/actions unconstitutional, interpret statutes |

### Modern Challenges

**Administrative state**: Agencies (EPA, SEC, etc.) combine legislative (rulemaking), executive (enforcement), and judicial (adjudication) functions. Fits poorly with tripartite model.

**Executive aggrandizement**: Presidents claim broad powers (national security, executive privilege, signing statements). Congress often acquiesces.

**Examples**:
- **War powers**: Constitution says Congress declares war, but Presidents initiate military actions (Korea, Vietnam, Iraq, Libya) without declaration
- **Executive agreements**: Presidents make international agreements without Senate treaty ratification
- **Signing statements**: President declares which parts of law he'll enforce

**Judicial response**:
- **Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer (1952)**: President Truman couldn't seize steel mills during Korean War without congressional authorization
- **Hamdan v. Rumsfeld (2006)**: Military commissions for Guantanamo detainees violated Geneva Conventions and required congressional authorization
- **But**: Courts often defer to executive on foreign affairs/national security

## Federalism: Division Between Federal and State Power

**10th Amendment**: "Powers not delegated to the United States... are reserved to the States."

### Enumerated vs Reserved Powers

**Federal enumerated powers** (Article I, Section 8):
- Tax and spend
- Regulate interstate/foreign commerce
- Bankruptcy
- Intellectual property (patents, copyrights)
- Post offices
- Declare war, raise armies
- Naturalization
- Necessary and Proper Clause (implied powers to execute enumerated powers)

**State reserved powers** (everything else):
- Family law (marriage, divorce, custody)
- Property law
- Tort law
- Contract law
- Criminal law (mostly)
- Education
- Professional licensing
- Zoning and land use

### Commerce Clause Expansion

**Original understanding**: Congress can regulate trade between states.

**Key cases expanding federal power**:

**Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**:
- **Facts**: NY gave monopoly on steamboat navigation; Gibbons had federal license
- **Holding**: "Commerce" includes navigation; federal power is supreme
- **Significance**: Broad reading of "commerce"

**NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel (1937)**:
- **Facts**: Steel company refused to bargain with union; claimed manufacturing isn't commerce
- **Holding**: Manufacturing affects interstate commerce; Congress can regulate labor
- **Significance**: Ended Lochner era (courts striking down economic regulations)

**Wickard v. Filburn (1942)**:
- **Facts**: Farmer grew wheat exceeding quota for own consumption
- **Holding**: Even homegrown wheat affects interstate market (by not buying); Congress can regulate
- **Significance**: "Substantial effects" test—if aggregated activity affects interstate commerce, Congress can regulate

**Result**: By 1960s, very little beyond federal commerce power.

**Modern limits**:

**United States v. Lopez (1995)**:
- **Facts**: Gun-Free School Zones Act criminalized guns within 1,000 feet of school
- **Holding**: Not economic activity; too attenuated from interstate commerce
- **Significance**: First Commerce Clause limit since 1937

**United States v. Morrison (2000)**:
- **Facts**: Violence Against Women Act created federal civil remedy for gender-motivated violence
- **Holding**: Not economic activity; violence against women doesn't substantially affect interstate commerce
- **Significance**: Reaffirmed Lopez; Congress can't regulate non-economic activity with attenuated commerce connection

**NFIB v. Sebelius (2012)** (Affordable Care Act):
- **Facts**: Individual mandate required buying health insurance
- **Holding**: Mandate not justified by Commerce Clause (can't compel commerce) but valid as tax
- **Significance**: Further Commerce Clause limit; Congress can't force inactivity

### Spending Clause

**Article I, Section 8**: Congress can "tax and spend for the general welfare."

**Conditional spending**: Congress offers states money if they comply with conditions.
- **Example**: Federal highway funds conditioned on state drinking age of 21 (upheld in South Dakota v. Dole, 1987)

**Limits**:
- Conditions must relate to federal interest in program
- Cannot be coercive (NFIB: ACA Medicaid expansion too coercive)

## Judicial Review: Marbury v. Madison (1803)

**Context**: John Adams (Federalist) lost 1800 election to Thomas Jefferson (Democratic-Republican). In last days, Adams appointed many Federalist judges ("midnight judges"). Jefferson's Secretary of State (James Madison) refused to deliver some commissions, including to William Marbury.

**Legal question**: Marbury sued under Judiciary Act of 1789, which gave Supreme Court original jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus (orders to government officials).

**Chief Justice John Marshall's holding**:
1. Marbury has right to commission
2. Mandamus is proper remedy
3. BUT: Judiciary Act unconstitutionally expands Supreme Court's original jurisdiction beyond Article III
4. Constitution is supreme law; courts must strike down unconstitutional statutes
5. Therefore: Court lacks jurisdiction; Marbury loses

**Significance**:
- **Established judicial review**: Courts can declare laws unconstitutional
- **Counter-majoritarian difficulty**: Unelected judges override elected legislators
- **Political genius**: Marshall asserted power by declining to exercise it (avoided confrontation with Jefferson)

**Not in Constitution's text**: No explicit grant of judicial review power, though Federalist 78 (Hamilton) assumed it.

**Global spread**: Most constitutional democracies now have judicial review (Germany's Constitutional Court, South Africa's Constitutional Court, etc.).

## Due Process: 5th and 14th Amendments

**5th Amendment**: "No person shall... be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law" (applies to federal government).

**14th Amendment** (1868): "...nor shall any State deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law" (applies to states).

### Procedural Due Process

**Meaning**: Government must follow fair procedures before depriving life, liberty, or property.

**Requirements** (Mathews v. Eldridge, 1976):
1. Notice of deprivation
2. Opportunity to be heard
3. Neutral decision-maker

**Balancing test**:
- Individual's interest
- Risk of erroneous deprivation
- Government's interest (cost, administrative burden)

**Examples**:
- **Goldberg v. Kelly (1970)**: Welfare benefits require hearing before termination
- **Goss v. Lopez (1975)**: School suspension requires notice and informal hearing
- **Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (2004)**: US citizen detained as enemy combatant has right to challenge detention

### Substantive Due Process

**Meaning**: Certain rights are so fundamental that government cannot infringe them regardless of procedure.

**Controversial**: Critics say courts invent rights not in Constitution's text.

**Lochner era (1897-1937)**: Courts struck down economic regulations (minimum wage, maximum hours) as violating "liberty of contract."
- **Lochner v. New York (1905)**: NY max-hour law for bakers unconstitutional
- **Overruled by West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937)**: Minimum wage law upheld

**Modern substantive due process**: Focuses on personal/family autonomy, not economic rights.

**Fundamental rights requiring strict scrutiny**:

**Privacy and family**:
- **Griswold v. Connecticut (1965)**: Right to use contraception (married couples)
- **Eisenstadt v. Baird (1972)**: Extended to unmarried individuals
- **Roe v. Wade (1973)**: Right to abortion (overruled 2022, see below)
- **Lawrence v. Texas (2003)**: Right to same-sex sexual intimacy
- **Obergefell v. Hodges (2015)**: Right to same-sex marriage

**Parental rights**:
- **Meyer v. Nebraska (1923)**: Parents can control children's education
- **Pierce v. Society of Sisters (1925)**: Parents can send children to private school
- **Troxel v. Granville (2000)**: Parents have liberty interest in care/custody

**Right to refuse medical treatment**:
- **Cruzan v. Director, Missouri Dept. of Health (1990)**: Competent person can refuse life-sustaining treatment

**Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022)**:
- **Holding**: No constitutional right to abortion; Roe and Casey overruled
- **Reasoning**: Abortion not "deeply rooted in Nation's history and traditions"
- **Result**: States can ban abortion

**Incorporation**: 14th Amendment Due Process Clause "incorporates" most Bill of Rights protections against states.
- **Not incorporated**: 3rd Amendment (quartering), 5th Amendment (grand jury), 7th Amendment (civil jury), 8th Amendment (excessive fines)

## Equal Protection: 14th Amendment

**Text**: "No State shall... deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws."

**Meaning**: Government cannot treat similarly situated people differently without justification.

### Tiers of Scrutiny

**1. Strict Scrutiny** (suspect classifications or fundamental rights):
- **Standard**: Law must be narrowly tailored to achieve compelling government interest
- **Applies to**: Race, national origin, alienage (sometimes), fundamental rights
- **Nearly always fails**: "Strict in theory, fatal in fact"

**2. Intermediate Scrutiny** (quasi-suspect classifications):
- **Standard**: Law must be substantially related to important government interest
- **Applies to**: Sex, illegitimacy
- **Sometimes upheld**

**3. Rational Basis** (everything else):
- **Standard**: Law must be rationally related to legitimate government interest
- **Applies to**: Age, wealth, disability, sexual orientation (most contexts), economic regulation
- **Nearly always upheld**: Courts defer to legislatures

### Race Discrimination

**Landmark cases striking down discrimination**:

**Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**:
- **Facts**: Kansas segregated public schools by race
- **Holding**: "Separate but equal" violates Equal Protection; segregation inherently unequal
- **Overruled Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)**: "Separate but equal" constitutional
- **Significance**: Catalyzed civil rights movement

**Loving v. Virginia (1967)**:
- **Facts**: VA banned interracial marriage
- **Holding**: Anti-miscegenation laws violate Equal Protection and Due Process
- **Significance**: Established right to marry as fundamental

**Modern issues: Affirmative action**:

**Regents of Univ. of California v. Bakke (1978)**:
- **Facts**: UC Davis medical school reserved 16/100 seats for minorities
- **Holding**: Quotas unconstitutional, but race can be "plus factor" to achieve diversity
- **Significance**: Permitted limited affirmative action

**Grutter v. Bollinger (2003)**:
- **Facts**: U. Michigan Law School considered race in admissions
- **Holding**: Narrowly tailored to achieve diversity (compelling interest)
- **Dissent** (Scalia): Constitution is colorblind

**Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (2023)**:
- **Holding**: Harvard and UNC race-conscious admissions programs violate Equal Protection
- **Reasoning**: Not narrowly tailored; lack meaningful endpoints; use race stereotypes
- **Result**: Effective end of affirmative action in higher education

### Sex Discrimination

**Historical**: Women excluded from many professions, jury service, etc.

**Modern standard: Intermediate scrutiny** (Craig v. Boren, 1976).

**Landmark cases**:

**Reed v. Reed (1971)**:
- **Facts**: Idaho preferred males over females as estate administrators
- **Holding**: Arbitrary sex discrimination violates Equal Protection
- **Significance**: First case striking down sex classification

**Frontiero v. Richardson (1973)**:
- **Facts**: Military gave housing allowance automatically for servicemen's spouses, not servicewomen's
- **Holding**: Unconstitutional
- **Significance**: 4 justices said sex should be suspect class (like race); didn't get majority

**Craig v. Boren (1976)**:
- **Facts**: Oklahoma allowed 3.2% beer sales to women at 18, men at 21
- **Holding**: Unconstitutional; established intermediate scrutiny
- **Significance**: Set current standard

**United States v. Virginia (1996)**:
- **Facts**: Virginia Military Institute (public school) excluded women
- **Holding**: Exclusion violates Equal Protection; state must provide "exceedingly persuasive justification"
- **Significance**: Heightened intermediate scrutiny for sex

**Permissible sex classifications**:
- **Michael M. v. Superior Court (1981)**: Statutory rape law punishing only males upheld (preventing teen pregnancy)
- **Rostker v. Goldberg (1981)**: Male-only draft registration upheld (women excluded from combat at time)

### Other Classifications

**Sexual orientation**:
- **Romer v. Evans (1996)**: CO amendment prohibiting gay rights protections violates rational basis (animus insufficient)
- **Obergefell v. Hodges (2015)**: Right to same-sex marriage (due process + equal protection)
- **Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)**: Title VII prohibits firing employees for being gay/transgender (statutory, not constitutional)

**Transgender**: Emerging area; some courts apply intermediate scrutiny.

**Wealth**:
- **Not suspect class**: Rational basis applies
- **But**: Some contexts trigger strict scrutiny (e.g., criminal appeals—Griffin v. Illinois, 1956)

**Age**:
- **Massachusetts Board of Retirement v. Murgia (1976)**: Rational basis applies; mandatory retirement at 50 for police upheld

## First Amendment: Speech, Religion, Press, Assembly

**Text**: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances."

### Freedom of Speech

**Core principle**: Government generally cannot restrict speech based on viewpoint.

**Rationales**:
- **Marketplace of ideas** (Holmes): Truth emerges from competition
- **Democracy**: Informed citizens need access to information
- **Autonomy**: Self-expression is intrinsic good
- **Check on tyranny**: Dissent prevents abuses

#### Categories of Unprotected Speech

**1. Incitement**:
- **Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969)**: Government can punish speech advocating illegal action only if (1) intended to incite imminent lawless action and (2) likely to do so
- **Example**: "Let's burn down this building right now" at angry rally (unprotected) vs "Someday we should overthrow the government" (protected)

**2. True threats**:
- **Virginia v. Black (2003)**: Cross-burning with intent to intimidate unprotected
- **Counterman v. Colorado (2023)**: Recklessness (not just objective reasonable person standard) required for true threat

**3. Obscenity**:
- **Miller v. California (1973)**: Obscene if (1) appeals to prurient interest, (2) patently offensive, (3) lacks serious literary/artistic/political/scientific value
- **Hard to prosecute**: Porn generally protected; only most extreme material unprotected

**4. Defamation**:
- **NY Times v. Sullivan (1964)**: Public officials must prove actual malice (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard) to win
- **Gertz v. Welch (1974)**: Private figures need only prove negligence
- **Justification**: Robust debate requires breathing room; some false speech tolerated

**5. Fighting words**:
- **Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire (1942)**: Words that by their very utterance inflict injury or incite immediate breach of peace
- **Narrow**: Courts rarely uphold restrictions

**6. Child pornography**:
- **New York v. Ferber (1982)**: Depictions of minors in sexual conduct unprotected

#### Content-Based vs Content-Neutral Restrictions

**Content-based**: Restricts speech based on subject matter or viewpoint.
- **Standard**: Strict scrutiny (usually struck down)
- **Example**: Ban on political signs but not commercial signs

**Content-neutral**: Restricts time, place, manner regardless of message.
- **Standard**: Intermediate scrutiny (reasonable restrictions upheld)
- **Example**: No amplified sound after 10 PM
- **Requirements**: Narrowly tailored, serve significant government interest, leave open alternative channels

#### Government Speech and Subsidies

**Government speech**: Government can say what it wants (no First Amendment constraint).
- **Pleasant Grove City v. Summum (2009)**: City can choose which monuments to display in park

**Subsidies**: Government can decide what to fund, but cannot impose unconstitutional conditions.
- **Rust v. Sullivan (1991)**: Government can prohibit federally funded family planning clinics from discussing abortion
- **Agency for Int'l Dev. v. Alliance for Open Society (2013)**: Government can't require grant recipients to adopt policy positions

#### Campaign Finance

**Buckley v. Valeo (1976)**: Contributions can be limited (prevent corruption), but expenditures cannot (core speech).

**Citizens United v. FEC (2010)**:
- **Facts**: Federal law banned corporate/union independent expenditures for political speech
- **Holding**: Ban violates First Amendment; corporations have speech rights
- **Reasoning**: Speaker identity doesn't eliminate speech protection
- **Dissent** (Stevens): Corporations aren't people; risk of corruption
- **Significance**: Massive increase in political spending via Super PACs

### Freedom of Religion

Two clauses: Establishment Clause (no official religion) and Free Exercise Clause (can practice religion).

#### Establishment Clause

**Lemon v. Kurtzman (1971)**: Law violates Establishment Clause if:
1. Lacks secular purpose
2. Primary effect advances/inhibits religion
3. Creates excessive government entanglement with religion

**Modern trend**: Lemon disfavored; focus on "coercion" and "history and tradition."

**Kennedy v. Bremerton School District (2022)**:
- **Facts**: High school football coach led prayers on field after games
- **Holding**: School violated Free Exercise and Free Speech by prohibiting; no Establishment Clause violation
- **Reasoning**: Private prayer, not coerced; Lemon rejected

**Permissible**:
- **Legislative prayer**: Marsh v. Chambers (1983)
- **"In God We Trust" on currency**: Ceremonial deism
- **Holiday displays**: If secular elements included (Lynch v. Donnelly, 1984)

**Impermissible**:
- **School-sponsored prayer**: Engel v. Vitale (1962)
- **Graduation prayer**: Lee v. Weisman (1992)
- **Ten Commandments in courthouse** (if religious purpose): McCreary County v. ACLU (2005)

#### Free Exercise Clause

**Employment Division v. Smith (1990)**:
- **Facts**: OR denied unemployment benefits to Native Americans fired for using peyote in religious ceremony
- **Holding**: Neutral, generally applicable laws don't violate Free Exercise even if they burden religion
- **Significance**: Lowered protection (from strict scrutiny to rational basis)

**Congress responded**: Religious Freedom Restoration Act (1993) required strict scrutiny, but Supreme Court struck down as applied to states (City of Boerne v. Flores, 1997). Still applies to federal government.

**Exceptions requiring strict scrutiny**:
- **Not neutral or generally applicable**: If law targets religion
- **Hybrid rights**: Free exercise + another right (e.g., parental rights)

**Recent cases**:

**Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission (2018)**:
- **Facts**: Baker refused to make cake for same-sex wedding
- **Holding**: Commission showed hostility to religion; violation of Free Exercise
- **Narrow**: Decided on animus, not general question

**Fulton v. City of Philadelphia (2021)**:
- **Facts**: City refused to contract with Catholic agency that wouldn't place children with same-sex couples
- **Holding**: Policy not generally applicable (allowed exceptions); strict scrutiny applies; city lost
- **Avoided**: Broader question of whether to overrule Smith

## Fourth Amendment: Search and Seizure

**Text**: "The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause..."

### Warrant Requirement

**General rule**: Searches/seizures require warrant based on probable cause.

**Probable cause**: Fair probability that evidence of crime will be found.

**Warrant must specify**: Place to be searched, things to be seized (particularity requirement).

### Exceptions to Warrant Requirement

**1. Consent**: Voluntary consent eliminates need for warrant
**2. Search incident to arrest**: Can search person and immediate surroundings for weapons/evidence
**3. Automobile exception**: If probable cause, can search car without warrant (mobile)
**4. Plain view**: If lawfully present and evidence in plain view, can seize
**5. Exigent circumstances**: Emergency (e.g., suspect destroying evidence, hot pursuit)
**6. Stop and frisk**: Terry v. Ohio (1968)—if reasonable suspicion of crime and danger, can pat down for weapons
**7. Administrative searches**: Borders, airports, schools (reduced expectation of privacy)

### Exclusionary Rule

**Mapp v. Ohio (1961)**: Evidence obtained in violation of 4th Amendment inadmissible in criminal trial.

**Rationale**: Deter police misconduct.

**Exceptions**:
- **Good faith**: Officers relied on warrant later found invalid (US v. Leon, 1984)
- **Inevitable discovery**: Evidence would have been found anyway
- **Attenuation**: Connection between illegality and evidence too remote
- **Independent source**: Evidence also obtained lawfully

**Criticism**: Lets guilty go free; doesn't effectively deter; harms public safety.

### Modern Issues

**Technology**:
- **Kyllo v. US (2001)**: Thermal imaging of home is search (requires warrant)
- **US v. Jones (2012)**: GPS tracking device on car is search
- **Riley v. California (2014)**: Cell phone search requires warrant (not incident to arrest)
- **Carpenter v. US (2018)**: Historical cell-site location data requires warrant

**Third-party doctrine**: No expectation of privacy in info voluntarily given to third parties (banks, phone companies). Carpenter limited this.

## Second Amendment: Right to Bear Arms

**Text**: "A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed."

### Historical Debate

**Collective right view**: Protects state militias, not individual right.
**Individual right view**: Protects personal firearm ownership.

**District of Columbia v. Heller (2008)**:
- **Facts**: DC banned handgun possession at home
- **Holding**: Second Amendment protects individual right to possess firearms for lawful purposes (self-defense in home)
- **Reasoning**: "Right of the people" is individual; "militia" is prefatory clause explaining purpose
- **Limitations**: "Not unlimited"—concealed carry, sensitive places, dangerous weapons can be regulated
- **Dissent** (Stevens, Breyer): Militia-centric reading; regulation should be permitted

**McDonald v. City of Chicago (2010)**:
- **Holding**: Second Amendment incorporated against states via 14th Amendment

**NY State Rifle & Pistol Assoc. v. Bruen (2022)**:
- **Facts**: NY required "proper cause" for concealed carry permit
- **Holding**: Licensing schemes requiring showing of special need violate Second Amendment
- **Test**: Government must justify regulation by showing consistent with historical tradition
- **Significance**: Expanded gun rights; struck down many state laws

**Current debates**:
- **Assault weapon bans**: Likely unconstitutional after Bruen
- **Magazine capacity limits**: Unclear
- **Red flag laws**: Temporary removal from those deemed dangerous; constitutionality contested
- **Universal background checks**: Probably permissible

## Constitutional Interpretation: Originalism vs Living Constitution

### Originalism

**Core claim**: Constitution's meaning fixed at ratification; interpret based on original public meaning.

**Variants**:
- **Original intent**: What did Framers intend? (Criticized: whose intent? Madison's? Ratifiers'?)
- **Original public meaning**: How would reasonable person at time understand text? (Current preferred approach)

**Proponents**: Scalia, Thomas, Gorsuch, Barrett

**Justifications**:
- **Democratic legitimacy**: People ratified specific meaning; judges can't change without amendment
- **Constraint**: Limits judicial discretion; prevents judges from imposing own values
- **Predictability**: Stable meaning over time

**Problems**:
- **Vague provisions**: "Due process," "cruel and unusual"—what's original meaning?
- **Changed circumstances**: Founders didn't foresee internet, drones, etc.
- **Unjust results**: Original meaning sometimes conflicts with modern values (e.g., sex discrimination was OK in 1868)

### Living Constitution

**Core claim**: Constitution's meaning evolves with societal values and norms.

**Proponents**: Breyer, Kagan, Sotomayor (on current Court)

**Justifications**:
- **Durability**: Constitution must adapt to changing world to remain relevant
- **Moral progress**: Society's understanding of equality, liberty improves over time; Constitution should reflect that
- **Practicality**: Impossible to amend Constitution quickly; interpretation provides flexibility

**Methods**:
- **Precedent**: Respect past decisions even if original meaning differed
- **Structural inference**: Infer principles from Constitution's structure
- **Consequentialism**: Consider practical effects of rulings

**Problems**:
- **Judicial discretion**: Judges decide what "evolves"—risk of imposing own values
- **Counter-majoritarian**: Unelected judges override democratic choices
- **Unpredictability**: Meaning changes over time

### Hybrid Approaches

**Most justices blend**: Even originalists respect some precedents inconsistent with original meaning (e.g., paper money, administrative agencies).

**Common ground**: Text is starting point; disagreement over how much to supplement with history, precedent, consequences.

## Key Terms

- **Judicial review**: Power to declare laws unconstitutional
- **Separation of powers**: Division of government into legislative, executive, judicial branches
- **Federalism**: Division of power between national and state governments
- **Enumerated powers**: Powers explicitly listed in Constitution
- **Reserved powers**: Powers held by states (10th Amendment)
- **Supremacy Clause**: Federal law supreme over conflicting state law
- **Commerce Clause**: Congressional power to regulate interstate commerce
- **Necessary and Proper Clause**: Congressional power to enact laws needed to execute enumerated powers
- **Due process**: Fair procedures (procedural) or fundamental rights (substantive)
- **Equal protection**: Government can't treat similarly situated people differently without justification
- **Strict scrutiny**: Law must be narrowly tailored to compelling interest (highest standard)
- **Intermediate scrutiny**: Law must be substantially related to important interest
- **Rational basis**: Law must be rationally related to legitimate interest (lowest standard)
- **Incorporation**: 14th Amendment applies Bill of Rights to states
- **Suspect classification**: Category triggering strict scrutiny (race, national origin)
- **Fundamental right**: Right triggering strict scrutiny when infringed
- **Content-based restriction**: Speech regulation based on subject matter/viewpoint
- **Content-neutral restriction**: Speech regulation based on time/place/manner
- **Establishment Clause**: Government can't establish official religion
- **Free Exercise Clause**: Government can't prohibit religious practice (subject to limits)
- **Exclusionary rule**: Illegally obtained evidence inadmissible
- **Probable cause**: Reasonable belief crime committed or evidence present
- **Originalism**: Interpret Constitution based on original meaning
- **Living constitution**: Constitution's meaning evolves with society

## Summary

Constitutional law governs the structure, powers, and limits of government. The US Constitution creates three branches (legislative, executive, judicial) with checks and balances to prevent tyranny. Federalism divides power between national and state governments, with the Commerce Clause enabling expansive federal authority (though recent cases impose modest limits).

Judicial review, established in Marbury v. Madison, allows courts to strike down unconstitutional laws. This counter-majoritarian power remains controversial, especially when courts recognize unenumerated rights via substantive due process (e.g., privacy, contraception, abortion).

Equal Protection demands that government treat people equally absent sufficient justification. Strict scrutiny applies to race and fundamental rights; intermediate scrutiny to sex; rational basis to everything else. Landmark cases like Brown (school desegregation), Loving (interracial marriage), and Obergefell (same-sex marriage) expanded equality, while recent cases like Dobbs (abortion) and SFFA (affirmative action) narrowed rights.

The First Amendment protects speech (with exceptions for incitement, obscenity, defamation), religion (Establishment and Free Exercise Clauses), press, and assembly. The Fourth Amendment guards against unreasonable searches, enforced via the exclusionary rule. The Second Amendment protects individual gun ownership, though the scope of permissible regulations remains hotly contested.

Constitutional interpretation divides originalists (original public meaning controls) from living constitutionalists (meaning evolves). Both approaches struggle with vague text, changed circumstances, and the tension between democratic legitimacy and protecting rights.

Constitutional law shapes American life profoundly—determining who can vote, marry, speak, bear arms, and how power is distributed between governments and individuals.
