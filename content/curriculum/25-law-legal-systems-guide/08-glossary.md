# Glossary of Legal Terms

## A

**Acceptance**: Manifestation of assent to the terms of an offer, creating a binding contract.

**Actus reus**: "Guilty act" - the physical act or omission that constitutes the criminal offense.

**Ad hoc**: For this specific purpose; temporary (e.g., ad hoc tribunal for Yugoslavia war crimes).

**Administrative agency**: Government body created to implement and enforce specific laws through rulemaking, enforcement, and adjudication.

**Administrative law**: Rules governing how government agencies exercise delegated authority.

**Admiralty law**: Law governing maritime activities and disputes.

**Adversarial system**: Legal system where two opposing parties present their cases to a neutral decision-maker (judge/jury).

**Adverse possession**: Acquiring ownership of property by openly, notoriously, exclusively, hostilely, and continuously possessing it for the statutory period.

**Advisory opinion**: Non-binding legal opinion issued by a court at the request of a government entity.

**Affirmative action**: Policies favoring historically disadvantaged groups to remedy past discrimination.

**Affirmative defense**: Defense that doesn't dispute the plaintiff's allegations but introduces new facts that defeat the claim (e.g., statute of limitations, contributory negligence).

**Amicus curiae**: "Friend of the court" - non-party who submits a brief offering relevant information or argument.

**Anticipatory repudiation**: When a party clearly indicates before performance is due that they will not perform their contractual obligations.

**Antitrust**: Laws promoting competition and preventing monopolies, cartels, and anticompetitive conduct.

**APA** (Administrative Procedure Act): Federal statute (1946) governing agency rulemaking and adjudication procedures.

**Appellant**: Party appealing a lower court's decision.

**Appellee**: Party responding to an appeal (defending the lower court's decision).

**Arbitrary and capricious**: Standard for reviewing agency decisions; agency action must be based on relevant factors and rational connection between facts and decision.

**Arraignment**: Court proceeding where criminal defendant is informed of charges and enters a plea.

**Assault**: (1) Criminal law: attempt to commit battery or intentionally causing apprehension of imminent harmful contact. (2) Tort: intentional act causing reasonable apprehension of imminent harmful or offensive contact.

**Assignment**: Transfer of contract rights to another party.

**Assumption of risk**: Defense to negligence where plaintiff voluntarily encountered a known risk.

**Attenuation**: Break in causal chain between illegal police conduct and evidence obtained (exception to exclusionary rule).

**Auer deference**: Courts defer to agency's interpretation of its own regulations (doctrine's future uncertain post-Loper Bright).

## B

**Bail**: Money or property deposited with court to secure defendant's release pending trial.

**Battery**: Unlawful application of force to another person (harmful or offensive touching).

**Bench trial**: Trial before a judge without a jury.

**Beneficiary**: Person entitled to benefits from a trust, will, insurance policy, or contract.

**Beyond reasonable doubt**: Burden of proof in criminal cases; prosecutor must prove defendant's guilt so jurors have no reasonable doubt.

**Bilateral contract**: Contract where both parties exchange promises (promise for promise).

**Bill of attainder**: Legislative act that declares a person guilty of a crime without trial (prohibited by Constitution).

**Binding precedent**: Prior decision that must be followed by lower courts in the same jurisdiction.

**Bona fide**: "In good faith" - genuine, without fraud or deceit.

**Breach**: Failure to perform a contractual duty without legal excuse.

**Brief**: Written legal argument submitted to a court.

**Burden of proof**: Obligation to prove a claim or defense; standard varies (preponderance, clear and convincing, beyond reasonable doubt).

**Burglary**: Breaking and entering a structure with intent to commit a felony (modern statutes often eliminate "breaking" and "nighttime" requirements).

## C

**Cap-and-trade**: Market-based environmental regulation where total emissions are capped and tradable permits allocated.

**Capacity**: Legal ability to enter into binding contracts (minors, mentally incapacitated, and severely intoxicated persons lack capacity).

**Case law**: Law established through judicial decisions (precedent).

**Causation**: Connection between act and harm; requires factual causation (but-for) and proximate causation (foreseeability, legal cause).

**Cause of action**: Facts that give rise to a right to sue.

**Certiorari**: Discretionary writ by which Supreme Court agrees to review a lower court decision; "cert granted" means Court will hear case.

**Chevron deference**: Doctrine requiring courts to defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes (established in Chevron USA v. NRDC, 1984; **overruled** in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, 2024).

**Choice of law**: Rules determining which jurisdiction's law applies to a dispute with connections to multiple jurisdictions.

**Civil law**: (1) Body of law dealing with private rights (contracts, torts, property) as opposed to criminal law. (2) Legal tradition based on codified law rather than precedent (Continental Europe, Latin America).

**Class action**: Lawsuit brought by representative plaintiffs on behalf of a larger group with common claims.

**Clean hands doctrine**: Equitable principle that party seeking equitable relief must not have engaged in wrongful conduct related to the claim.

**Clear and convincing evidence**: Burden of proof higher than preponderance but lower than beyond reasonable doubt; used in some civil cases (fraud, termination of parental rights).

**Commerce Clause**: Article I, Section 8 provision granting Congress power to regulate interstate and foreign commerce.

**Common law**: (1) Judge-made law developed through precedent. (2) Legal tradition emphasizing case law over statutes (England, US, former British colonies).

**Community property**: Marital property regime where spouses equally own earnings and assets acquired during marriage (9 US states).

**Comparative negligence**: Tort doctrine reducing plaintiff's damages by percentage of fault attributable to plaintiff (vs contributory negligence which bars recovery).

**Complaint**: Initial pleading filed by plaintiff stating claims against defendant.

**Compulsory license**: Government authorization to use patented invention without owner's consent (with compensation), often for public health emergencies.

**Concurrence**: (1) In criminal law, mens rea must coincide with actus reus. (2) In judicial opinions, judge agrees with result but for different reasons.

**Concurrent jurisdiction**: When multiple courts have authority to hear a case (e.g., federal and state courts for diversity cases).

**Condition**: Provision in contract making performance contingent on occurrence (or non-occurrence) of an event.

**Conflict of laws**: Body of law determining which jurisdiction's law applies when case involves multiple jurisdictions.

**Consideration**: Bargained-for exchange necessary for contract formation; each party must give something of value.

**Constitutional law**: Law interpreting and applying constitutional provisions; addresses government structure, powers, and individual rights.

**Contempt of court**: Disobedience of court order; can be civil (coerce compliance) or criminal (punish disobedience).

**Content-based restriction**: Speech regulation targeting message/viewpoint; subject to strict scrutiny.

**Content-neutral restriction**: Speech regulation targeting time, place, or manner regardless of message; subject to intermediate scrutiny.

**Contract**: Legally enforceable agreement; requires offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, and legality.

**Contributory negligence**: Plaintiff's own negligence contributing to injury; common law rule barred recovery (most states now use comparative negligence).

**Conversion**: Tort involving wrongful exercise of control over another's personal property.

**Copyright**: Exclusive rights to original works of authorship fixed in tangible medium; duration of life plus 70 years (individuals) or 95 years from publication (corporate works).

**Corporation**: Legal entity separate from its owners, with limited liability and perpetual existence.

**Counterclaim**: Claim asserted by defendant against plaintiff in same action.

**Counteroffer**: Response to offer that changes its terms, rejecting the original offer and making a new one.

**Covenant**: (1) Promise in contract. (2) Promise regarding land use that runs with the land, binding successors.

**Criminal law**: Body of law defining crimes and prescribing punishments; enforced by government prosecution.

**Cross-examination**: Questioning of witness by opposing party.

**Cruel and unusual punishment**: Punishment violating Eighth Amendment; death penalty constitutional if not arbitrary and proportionate to crime.

**Customary international law**: International law derived from consistent state practice accepted as legally obligatory (opinio juris).

## D

**Damages**: Monetary compensation awarded for injury or loss; types include compensatory, punitive, nominal, and liquidated.

**De facto**: "In fact" - existing in reality, though perhaps not legally authorized.

**De jure**: "By law" - legally authorized or recognized.

**De minimis**: "Of minimal importance" - too trivial to merit legal concern.

**De novo**: "Anew" - reviewing decision without deference to lower court/agency.

**Declaratory judgment**: Court judgment declaring parties' rights without ordering enforcement.

**Defamation**: False statement harming another's reputation; includes libel (written) and slander (spoken).

**Default judgment**: Judgment entered against party who fails to respond to complaint.

**Defendant**: (1) Criminal law: person charged with crime. (2) Civil law: party against whom lawsuit is brought.

**Defense**: Reason why plaintiff should not prevail; includes affirmative defenses and negating elements of plaintiff's claim.

**Deposition**: Out-of-court testimony under oath, recorded for potential use at trial.

**Derivative work**: Work based on preexisting copyrighted work (adaptation, translation, sequel).

**Deterrence**: Punishment theory aimed at preventing future crime by making it costly.

**Dicta** (obiter dicta): Statements in judicial opinion not necessary to decision; not binding precedent.

**Dilution**: Weakening of famous trademark through blurring (association with different goods) or tarnishment (negative associations).

**Direct examination**: Questioning of one's own witness.

**Discovery**: Pre-trial process where parties exchange information and evidence (interrogatories, depositions, document requests).

**Discrimination**: Treating similarly situated people differently based on protected characteristics (race, sex, religion, etc.).

**Dissent**: Opinion by judge who disagrees with majority decision.

**Diversity jurisdiction**: Federal court jurisdiction over cases between citizens of different states where amount exceeds $75,000.

**DMCA** (Digital Millennium Copyright Act): 1998 statute addressing digital copyright issues; includes anti-circumvention provisions and safe harbors for online service providers.

**Docket**: Court's schedule of cases; also refers to case file.

**Domestic relations**: Area of law governing family matters (marriage, divorce, custody, support).

**Double jeopardy**: Fifth Amendment protection against being prosecuted twice for the same offense.

**Due process**: Constitutional requirement of fair procedures before depriving life, liberty, or property; includes procedural due process (fair procedures) and substantive due process (fundamental rights).

**Duress**: (1) Contracts: improper threat leaving no reasonable alternative, rendering contract voidable. (2) Criminal law: defense based on threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm.

**Duty**: Legal obligation; in negligence, obligation to exercise reasonable care.

## E

**Easement**: Right to use another's land for specific purpose (e.g., driveway access); can be appurtenant (benefits particular land) or in gross (benefits individual).

**EEZ** (Exclusive Economic Zone): Maritime zone extending 200 nautical miles from coast where coastal state has sovereign rights over resources.

**Embezzlement**: Fraudulent conversion of property by one in lawful possession.

**Eminent domain**: Government power to take private property for public use with just compensation (Fifth Amendment).

**En banc**: Full court (not just panel) hearing a case; used in appellate courts to resolve important issues or conflicts between panels.

**Enablement**: Patent requirement that specification teach person skilled in the art how to make and use invention.

**Entrapment**: Defense based on government inducement of crime by person not predisposed to commit it.

**Equal protection**: Fourteenth Amendment requirement that government not treat similarly situated people differently without sufficient justification; scrutiny level varies by classification.

**Equity**: (1) System of law providing remedies (injunctions, specific performance) where legal remedies (damages) inadequate. (2) Fairness.

**Erga omnes**: "Toward all" - obligations owed to international community as a whole.

**Error**: Mistake by trial court; reversible error requires new trial, harmless error does not.

**Establishment Clause**: First Amendment prohibition on government establishing official religion.

**Estate**: (1) Property law: possessory interest in land (fee simple, life estate). (2) Decedent's property.

**Estoppel**: Doctrine preventing party from asserting claim/defense inconsistent with prior position another relied upon.

**Ex parte**: "From one party" - proceeding where only one party appears (without notice to other party).

**Ex post facto law**: Retroactive criminal law (prohibited by Constitution).

**Exclusionary rule**: Fourth Amendment remedy making illegally obtained evidence inadmissible in criminal trial.

**Exclusive dealing**: Agreement requiring buyer to purchase exclusively from one seller; potentially anticompetitive under Clayton Act.

**Exculpatory clause**: Contract provision releasing party from liability for negligence; enforceable for ordinary negligence but not gross negligence/willful misconduct.

**Executed contract**: Fully performed contract.

**Executory contract**: Contract not yet fully performed.

**Expectation damages**: Contract remedy putting non-breaching party in position they would have been in if contract performed.

**Express warranty**: Explicit guarantee regarding product quality or performance.

## F

**Fair use**: Copyright defense permitting limited unlicensed use for purposes like criticism, commentary, news reporting, teaching; assessed by four factors (purpose, nature, amount, market effect).

**False imprisonment**: Intentional tort involving unlawful confinement or restraint.

**Federal question jurisdiction**: Federal court jurisdiction over cases arising under federal law (Constitution, statutes, treaties).

**Federalism**: Division of power between national government and states; Tenth Amendment reserves powers not delegated to federal government.

**Fee simple absolute**: Largest estate in land; infinite duration, fully transferable.

**Felony**: Serious crime punishable by more than one year imprisonment.

**Felony murder**: Unintentional killing during commission of dangerous felony; treated as murder.

**Fiduciary**: Person holding position of trust and confidence; must act in beneficiary's best interest (examples: trustee, corporate director, attorney, investment adviser).

**Forum non conveniens**: Doctrine allowing court to dismiss case if another forum is significantly more convenient.

**Forum shopping**: Plaintiff's choice of court expected to be most favorable.

**Fraud**: Intentional misrepresentation of material fact inducing reasonable reliance and causing damages.

**Free Exercise Clause**: First Amendment protection of religious practice (subject to neutral, generally applicable laws per Employment Division v. Smith).

**Frustration of purpose**: Contract doctrine excusing performance when unforeseen event destroys contract's purpose (performance possible but pointless).

**Fundamental right**: Right triggering strict scrutiny when infringed (voting, travel, privacy, marriage).

**Future interest**: Property interest that will (or may) become possessory in future (remainder, reversion, executory interest).

## G

**General intent**: Criminal intent to perform act (not necessarily to cause specific result).

**Generic**: (1) Trademark law: common name for product (not protectable). (2) Drugs: non-brand-name pharmaceuticals.

**Genocide**: Deliberate destruction of national, ethnic, racial, or religious group; international crime under Genocide Convention.

**Good faith**: Honesty in fact and observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing.

**Grand jury**: Body of citizens determining whether probable cause exists to indict for serious crimes; Fifth Amendment right in federal system.

**Guilty**: Criminal defendant's plea or verdict admitting/finding responsibility for charged offense.

## H

**Habeas corpus**: "You have the body" - writ requiring government to justify detention; constitutional right except in rebellion/invasion (Article I, Section 9).

**Harmless error**: Trial court mistake that didn't affect outcome; doesn't require reversal.

**Hearsay**: Out-of-court statement offered for truth of matter asserted; generally inadmissible (many exceptions).

**Holding**: Court's decision on legal question; binding precedent (vs dicta).

**Horizontal agreement**: Agreement between competitors (same distribution level); higher antitrust risk than vertical agreements.

**Hung jury**: Jury unable to reach unanimous verdict (or required supermajority); results in mistrial.

## I

**ICC** (International Criminal Court): Court established by Rome Statute (2002) to prosecute individuals for genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression.

**ICCPR** (International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights): 1966 treaty protecting civil and political rights (173 parties).

**ICJ** (International Court of Justice): UN court deciding disputes between states and issuing advisory opinions; located in The Hague.

**Illusory promise**: Apparent promise that doesn't actually commit promisor; not valid consideration.

**Immunity**: (1) Sovereign immunity: state's immunity from suit. (2) Diplomatic immunity: diplomat's immunity from host state prosecution. (3) Witness immunity: protection from prosecution in exchange for testimony.

**Impeachment**: (1) Process of removing official (President, judge) for high crimes and misdemeanors. (2) Challenging witness's credibility.

**Implied warranty**: Warranty imposed by law (not expressly stated); includes merchantability (fit for ordinary use) and fitness for particular purpose.

**Impossibility**: Contract defense when performance objectively impossible (destroyed subject matter, illegality).

**Impracticability**: Contract defense when performance possible but extremely burdensome due to unforeseen event.

**In camera**: "In chambers" - private judicial examination (not in open court).

**In personam jurisdiction**: Court's authority over person; requires minimum contacts with forum.

**In rem jurisdiction**: Court's authority over property within its territory.

**Incapacitation**: Punishment theory focusing on preventing crime by imprisonment.

**Inchoate crime**: Incomplete crime (attempt, conspiracy, solicitation).

**Indemnification**: Obligation to compensate another for loss or liability.

**Independent contractor**: Worker not under employer's control (vs employee); employer generally not liable for torts.

**Indictment**: Formal charge by grand jury.

**Injunction**: Equitable remedy ordering party to act or refrain from acting.

**Innocent until proven guilty**: Presumption of innocence in criminal trials.

**Inquisitorial system**: Legal system where judge actively investigates facts (civil law countries).

**Insanity**: Criminal defense based on mental disease/defect preventing understanding wrongfulness or controlling conduct; tests vary (M'Naghten, MPC, irresistible impulse).

**Insider trading**: Trading securities based on material non-public information in breach of duty.

**Intellectual property**: Legal rights protecting creations of mind (patents, copyrights, trademarks, trade secrets).

**Intent**: (1) Mental state accompanying act. (2) Purpose or objective.

**Intentional tort**: Tort requiring intent to cause consequence (battery, assault, false imprisonment, intentional infliction of emotional distress).

**Intermediate scrutiny**: Standard requiring law be substantially related to important government interest; applies to sex and illegitimacy classifications.

**International humanitarian law** (IHL): Law of armed conflict governing conduct during war (Geneva Conventions, customary law).

**International law**: Rules governing relations between states (and increasingly international organizations and individuals).

**Interrogatories**: Written questions one party sends to another during discovery; must be answered under oath.

**Intestate**: Dying without a valid will; estate distributed according to intestacy statutes.

**IPR** (Inter Partes Review): USPTO proceeding allowing third parties to challenge issued patent's validity on anticipation or obviousness grounds.

**IRAC**: Legal analysis framework: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion.

**ISDS** (Investor-State Dispute Settlement): Arbitration allowing foreign investors to sue host states for treaty violations.

## J

**Joint and several liability**: Multiple defendants each liable for entire judgment; plaintiff can collect from any defendant.

**Joint tenancy**: Concurrent ownership with right of survivorship; requires four unities (time, title, interest, possession).

**Judgment**: Final court determination of parties' rights.

**Judgment notwithstanding verdict** (JNOV): Judge overturns jury verdict as unsupported by evidence.

**Judicial review**: (1) Court's power to declare laws/actions unconstitutional (Marbury v. Madison). (2) Court review of agency action.

**Jurisdiction**: Court's authority to hear case and bind parties; requires subject matter jurisdiction and personal jurisdiction.

**Jurisprudence**: Philosophy of law; study of legal principles and systems.

**Jury**: Body of citizens determining facts and applying law to reach verdict; Sixth Amendment right in criminal cases (serious offenses), Seventh Amendment in civil cases (over $20).

**Jus cogens**: Peremptory norms of international law from which no derogation permitted (genocide, slavery, torture).

**Just compensation**: Fair market value required when government takes property via eminent domain.

**Justification defense**: Defense showing conduct not wrongful (self-defense, necessity, defense of others/property).

## K

**Knowledge**: Mental state involving awareness; higher culpability than recklessness, lower than purpose.

## L

**Laches**: Equitable defense based on unreasonable delay in asserting claim causing prejudice to defendant.

**Landlord**: Owner of property leased to tenant.

**Larceny**: Trespassory taking and carrying away of another's personal property with intent to permanently deprive.

**Law of the Sea**: International law governing maritime zones, navigation, resources; codified in UNCLOS.

**Leading question**: Question suggesting answer; generally prohibited on direct examination, allowed on cross-examination.

**Legal positivism**: Theory that law is whatever legitimate authorities enact, regardless of morality (separation of law and morality).

**Legal realism**: Theory that law is what courts actually do, not what rules say; emphasizes judicial discretion and indeterminacy.

**Legality**: Contract requirement that purpose be lawful.

**Liability**: Legal responsibility for harm or obligation.

**Libel**: Written defamation.

**Life estate**: Possessory interest in land measured by someone's lifetime.

**Likelihood of confusion**: Standard for trademark infringement; would reasonable consumer confuse source?

**Liquidated damages**: Damages amount agreed to in contract (enforceable if reasonable forecast, not penalty).

**Litigation**: Process of resolving disputes through courts.

**Living constitution**: Theory that Constitution's meaning evolves with societal values (vs originalism).

**Loper Bright**: 2024 Supreme Court case overruling Chevron deference; courts must exercise independent judgment interpreting statutes, not defer to agencies.

## M

**Mailbox rule**: Acceptance effective when sent (if reasonable method); revocation effective when received.

**Malice aforethought**: Mental state for murder (intent to kill, intent to inflict serious bodily harm, depraved heart, or felony murder).

**Mandatory minimum**: Statutorily required minimum sentence (judge has no discretion to impose less).

**Manslaughter**: Unlawful killing without malice aforethought; voluntary (heat of passion) or involuntary (criminal negligence).

**Market power**: Ability to raise prices above competitive level without losing significant business.

**Material**: (1) Contracts/torts: significant, important to decision. (2) Securities: reasonable investor would consider important.

**Mediation**: Alternative dispute resolution where neutral third party facilitates settlement.

**Mens rea**: "Guilty mind" - mental state required for crime (purposely, knowingly, recklessly, negligently).

**Merger**: (1) Combination of companies (may raise antitrust concerns). (2) Property law: when same person acquires both possessory and future interests, merger occurs. (3) Criminal law: lesser included offense merges into greater.

**Merit**: Substance of claim (vs procedural issues).

**Misdemeanor**: Crime less serious than felony; punishment up to one year.

**Misrepresentation**: False statement of fact; can be fraudulent (knowing), negligent, or innocent.

**Mistake**: Erroneous belief about fact; mutual mistake can make contract voidable; unilateral mistake generally not defense unless other party knew.

**Mistrial**: Trial terminated without verdict (hung jury, prejudicial error, juror misconduct).

**Mitigate**: Reduce damages; non-breaching party has duty to take reasonable steps to minimize loss.

**Model Penal Code** (MPC): Influential criminal law code drafted by American Law Institute; adopted (in whole or part) by many states.

**Monopolization**: Sherman Act § 2 offense; (1) monopoly power in relevant market + (2) willful acquisition/maintenance through exclusionary conduct.

**Moot**: No longer live controversy (case dismissed as moot if no practical relief possible).

**Motion**: Request for court order.

**Motion to dismiss**: Request to dismiss case for legal insufficiency (failure to state claim, lack of jurisdiction).

**Murder**: Unlawful killing with malice aforethought.

## N

**NAAQS** (National Ambient Air Quality Standards): EPA health-based air quality standards for criteria pollutants under Clean Air Act.

**Natural law**: Theory that law must conform to moral principles; unjust "laws" aren't really law.

**Necessary and Proper Clause**: Article I, Section 8 granting Congress power to enact laws necessary and proper to execute enumerated powers (implied powers).

**Necessity**: Criminal defense based on choosing lesser harm to avoid greater harm (elements: imminent threat, no alternative, harm avoided > harm caused).

**Negligence**: Tort involving (1) duty, (2) breach, (3) causation, (4) damages; failure to exercise reasonable care.

**Negligence per se**: Negligence established by violating statute (if plaintiff in protected class and harm of type statute intended to prevent).

**NEPA** (National Environmental Policy Act): Requires environmental impact statements for major federal actions significantly affecting environment.

**No contest** (nolo contendere): Criminal plea neither admitting nor denying guilt; treated as guilty for sentencing but can't be used in civil suit.

**Non-compete agreement**: Contract restricting employee from joining competitor; enforceability varies by state.

**Not guilty**: Criminal plea denying charges, or verdict finding prosecution failed to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt.

**Notice-and-comment rulemaking**: Standard APA process for regulations; agency publishes proposed rule, receives public comments, issues final rule.

**Novelty**: Patent requirement that invention be new (not anticipated by prior art).

**NPDES** (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System): Clean Water Act permit program for point source water pollution.

**Nuisance**: (1) Public nuisance: interference with public right (e.g., blocking highway). (2) Private nuisance: substantial, unreasonable interference with use/enjoyment of land.

## O

**Obiter dicta**: See dicta.

**Obviousness**: Patent requirement that invention not be obvious to person having ordinary skill in the art.

**Offer**: Manifestation of willingness to enter bargain on specified terms, creating power of acceptance.

**Opinio juris**: "Opinion of law" - belief that state practice is legally required (element of customary international law).

**Option contract**: Contract keeping offer open for specified time (offeree pays for this right; offeror can't revoke).

**Ordinance**: Municipal law/regulation.

**Originalism**: Constitutional interpretation based on original public meaning at ratification (vs living constitution).

**Overbreadth**: Doctrine invalidating laws regulating more speech than Constitution allows.

## P

**Pacta sunt servanda**: "Agreements must be kept" - fundamental treaty law principle.

**Pardon**: Executive clemency forgiving crime and removing punishment/consequences.

**Parody**: Imitation for comedic/critical purposes; defense to copyright infringement (if transformative) and trademark dilution.

**Parol evidence rule**: Contract interpretation rule excluding prior/contemporaneous oral statements that contradict integrated written contract.

**Patent**: Exclusive right to invention for limited time (20 years utility/plant, 15 years design).

**Patent troll**: Non-practicing entity asserting patents primarily to extract settlements (pejoratively; prefer "patent assertion entity").

**Per curiam**: "By the court" - unsigned opinion representing court as whole.

**Per se illegal**: Antitrust conduct conclusively presumed anticompetitive (horizontal price-fixing, market allocation, bid-rigging).

**Peremptory challenge**: Jury selection right to exclude juror without stating reason (limited number; can't exclude based on race/sex).

**Perjury**: Lying under oath; criminal offense.

**Personal jurisdiction**: Court's authority over defendant; requires minimum contacts with forum (International Shoe).

**Persuasive authority**: Legal authority not binding but influential (decisions from other jurisdictions, treatises, law review articles).

**Petit jury**: Trial jury (typically 6-12 members) deciding facts and reaching verdict.

**Petitioner**: Party initiating appeal or petition (e.g., for certiorari).

**Pigouvian tax**: Tax equal to external cost (e.g., carbon tax equal to social cost of carbon).

**Plaintiff**: Party bringing lawsuit.

**Plea bargaining**: Negotiation where defendant pleads guilty in exchange for concessions (reduced charges, sentencing recommendation).

**Pleadings**: Formal written statements of parties' claims and defenses (complaint, answer).

**Police power**: State authority to regulate health, safety, morals, general welfare.

**Precedent**: Prior judicial decision on point of law; binding on lower courts in jurisdiction (stare decisis).

**Preemption**: Federal law displaces conflicting state law (Supremacy Clause).

**Premeditation**: Planning before act (required for first-degree murder in many states).

**Preponderance of evidence**: Civil burden of proof; more likely than not (>50%).

**Presumption of innocence**: Criminal defendant presumed innocent until proven guilty.

**Prima facie**: "On its face" - sufficient evidence to establish fact unless rebutted.

**Principal**: (1) Criminal law: person who commits crime (vs accomplice). (2) Agency law: person represented by agent. (3) Debt: amount borrowed (vs interest).

**Prior art**: Previous patents, publications, public uses, or sales relevant to patent's novelty/obviousness.

**Prior restraint**: Government censorship before publication; generally unconstitutional (heavy presumption against).

**Privity**: Legal relationship between parties (e.g., parties to contract have privity).

**Probable cause**: Reasonable belief that crime was committed or evidence will be found; required for arrest, search warrant.

**Probate**: Court process of administering deceased's estate.

**Probation**: Supervised release instead of imprisonment; violation can result in incarceration.

**Procedural due process**: Requirement of fair procedures before depriving life, liberty, or property (notice, hearing, neutral decision-maker).

**Products liability**: Tort liability for defective products; theories include negligence, strict liability, breach of warranty.

**Promissory estoppel**: Enforcing promise without consideration based on detrimental reliance (Restatement § 90).

**Property**: Rights in things; includes real property (land) and personal property (movables).

**Proportionality**: (1) International humanitarian law: incidental civilian harm can't be excessive relative to military advantage. (2) Sentencing: punishment must be proportionate to crime (Eighth Amendment).

**Prosecutor**: Government attorney bringing criminal charges.

**Proximate cause**: Legal cause; foreseeable, not too remote connection between act and harm.

**Public domain**: Works not protected by intellectual property (expired copyright, no copyright, government works).

**Punitive damages**: Damages exceeding compensation, awarded to punish defendant and deter similar conduct.

## Q

**Qualified immunity**: Government officials immune from liability for constitutional violations unless violated clearly established law.

**Quasi-contract**: Obligation imposed by law to prevent unjust enrichment (not true contract).

**Quid pro quo**: "This for that" - exchange; consideration in contracts.

## R

**Rape**: Unlawful sexual intercourse without consent; modern statutes gender-neutral and focus on consent rather than force.

**Rational basis**: Lowest scrutiny level; law must be rationally related to legitimate government interest (nearly always upheld).

**Reasonable doubt**: Standard for criminal conviction; jury must have no reasonable doubt of guilt.

**Reasonable person**: Objective standard for negligence; how would ordinarily prudent person act in same circumstances?

**Recidivism**: Reoffending; returning to criminal behavior after conviction/release.

**Recklessness**: Conscious disregard of substantial and unjustifiable risk.

**Regulatory taking**: Regulation so burdensome it constitutes taking requiring compensation (Fifth Amendment).

**Rehabilitation**: Punishment theory focused on reforming offender to prevent recidivism.

**Reliance damages**: Contract remedy restoring party to pre-contract position (out-of-pocket losses).

**Remainder**: Future interest in third party following life estate or other limited estate.

**Remand**: Appellate court sends case back to lower court for further proceedings.

**Remedy**: Legal or equitable relief (damages, injunction, specific performance, restitution).

**Res ipsa loquitur**: "The thing speaks for itself" - negligence inferred from accident's occurrence (e.g., surgical instrument left in patient).

**Res judicata**: "Matter decided" - final judgment on merits bars relitigation of same claim between same parties.

**Rescission**: Canceling contract and restoring parties to pre-contract positions.

**Respondeat superior**: "Let the master answer" - employer liable for employee's torts within scope of employment.

**Respondent**: Party responding to appeal or petition.

**Responsibility to Protect** (R2P): International norm that if state fails to protect population from mass atrocities, international community has responsibility to intervene.

**Restitution**: (1) Remedy preventing unjust enrichment by returning benefit conferred. (2) Criminal: compensation to victim.

**Retribution**: Punishment theory based on moral desert; offender deserves punishment.

**Reversion**: Future interest in grantor following life estate or other limited estate granted to another.

**RICO** (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act): Federal statute prohibiting using racketeering income to acquire business, acquiring business through racketeering, or conducting business through racketeering.

**Robbery**: Larceny with force or intimidation.

**Rule against perpetuities**: Common law rule requiring contingent interests vest within 21 years after life in being at creation (many states abolished/reformed).

**Rule of law**: Governance by stable, known rules applied equally, not arbitrary official discretion.

**Rule of reason**: Antitrust standard weighing procompetitive justifications against anticompetitive effects.

**Rulemaking**: Agency process of creating regulations (typically notice-and-comment under APA).

## S

**Sanctions**: (1) International law: penalties for violations (economic embargoes, asset freezes, travel bans). (2) Court penalties for misconduct.

**Scienter**: Knowledge; in fraud/securities cases, knowing falsity or reckless disregard for truth.

**Search**: Government intrusion into area where person has reasonable expectation of privacy (Fourth Amendment).

**Search warrant**: Court authorization for search based on probable cause.

**Secondary meaning**: Descriptive mark acquires distinctiveness through consumer association with source; becomes protectable trademark.

**Seizure**: (1) Fourth Amendment: taking of property or person. (2) Property: creditor taking debtor's property.

**Self-defense**: Justification defense permitting reasonable force to repel imminent unlawful force (deadly force only if reasonable belief of death/serious bodily harm).

**Self-incrimination**: Fifth Amendment privilege against being compelled to testify against oneself in criminal case.

**Sentence**: Punishment imposed after criminal conviction.

**Sentencing guidelines**: Grid-based system determining sentence based on offense severity and criminal history (federal guidelines advisory post-Booker).

**Separation of powers**: Constitutional division of government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches with checks and balances.

**Service of process**: Delivering complaint and summons to defendant to notify of lawsuit.

**Settlement**: Parties' agreement resolving dispute without trial.

**Severability**: Contract/statute provision invalid in part can remain valid in other parts if severable.

**Sexual harassment**: Unwelcome sexual conduct; includes quid pro quo (job benefits conditioned on sexual favors) and hostile environment.

**Sherman Act**: 1890 federal antitrust statute; § 1 prohibits restraints of trade, § 2 prohibits monopolization.

**Slander**: Spoken defamation.

**Solicitation**: Inchoate crime of requesting/encouraging another to commit crime.

**Sovereign immunity**: Government immunity from suit (exceptions for waiver, constitutional violations, certain contexts).

**Specific intent**: Intent to accomplish specific result beyond act itself.

**Specific performance**: Equitable remedy ordering party to perform contract (available for unique goods like land).

**Standing**: Requirement that plaintiff have sufficient stake in controversy (injury in fact, causation, redressability).

**Stare decisis**: "Stand by things decided" - doctrine of respecting precedent.

**State action**: Requirement for constitutional claims; Constitution generally constrains government, not private parties.

**Statute**: Law enacted by legislature.

**Statute of Frauds**: Requires certain contracts be in writing (MY LEGS: Marriage, Year, Land, Executor, Goods, Surety).

**Statute of limitations**: Time limit for filing lawsuit (varies by claim type).

**Statutory construction**: Interpreting statutes; canons include plain meaning, legislative history, purpose.

**Statutory rape**: Sexual intercourse with person below age of consent (strict liability; reasonable mistake of age not defense).

**Stay**: Suspension of court proceedings or enforcement.

**Stipulation**: Parties' agreement on factual/procedural matter.

**Strict liability**: Liability without fault; applies to abnormally dangerous activities, defective products, statutory rape.

**Strict scrutiny**: Highest judicial review standard; law must be narrowly tailored to compelling government interest; applies to suspect classifications (race) and fundamental rights.

**Subject matter jurisdiction**: Court's authority to hear type of case (federal question, diversity).

**Sublease**: Tenant transfers less than entire remaining lease term (subtenant not in privity with landlord).

**Subpoena**: Court order compelling testimony or document production.

**Substantive due process**: Constitutional protection of fundamental rights from government infringement regardless of procedures used.

**Substantive law**: Law defining rights and duties (vs procedural law governing process).

**Summary judgment**: Judgment without trial when no genuine dispute of material fact and moving party entitled to judgment as matter of law.

**Summons**: Notice to defendant of lawsuit and requirement to respond.

**Supremacy Clause**: Article VI provision making Constitution, federal laws, and treaties supreme over conflicting state law.

**Suspect classification**: Category triggering strict scrutiny (race, national origin).

## T

**Takings Clause**: Fifth Amendment provision requiring just compensation when government takes private property for public use.

**Tenant**: Person leasing property from landlord.

**Tenancy in common**: Concurrent ownership where each owns undivided fractional share (no survivorship rights).

**Testimony**: Oral evidence given under oath.

**Tort**: Civil wrong causing harm (negligence, intentional torts, strict liability).

**Trade dress**: Product design/packaging protected as trademark if non-functional and distinctive.

**Trade secret**: Confidential business information providing competitive advantage (protected indefinitely if secrecy maintained).

**Trademark**: Brand identifier (word, symbol, design) distinguishing goods/services.

**Transferred intent**: Criminal law doctrine; intent to harm A transfers to actual victim B.

**Treaty**: Binding international agreement between states.

**Trespass**: (1) Real property: unauthorized entry onto another's land. (2) Personal property: unauthorized interference with another's personal property.

**Trial**: Formal proceeding where evidence presented and decision rendered.

**TRIPS** (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights): WTO agreement establishing minimum IP protections.

**Trust**: Fiduciary relationship where trustee holds property for beneficiary's benefit.

**Tying arrangement**: Seller conditions sale of one product (tying) on purchase of another (tied); potentially anticompetitive under Clayton Act.

## U

**UCC** (Uniform Commercial Code): Standardized commercial law adopted by all states (with variations); Article 2 governs sales of goods.

**UNCLOS** (UN Convention on the Law of the Sea): 1982 treaty establishing maritime zones, navigation rights, resource rights.

**Unconscionability**: Contract so one-sided as to be oppressive; includes procedural (unfair bargaining) and substantive (unfair terms) unconscionability.

**Undue influence**: Unfair persuasion overcoming free will, typically in confidential relationship; makes contract voidable.

**Unilateral contract**: Contract where offer accepted by performance (not promise); reward offers.

**Universal jurisdiction**: Any state can prosecute certain international crimes (piracy, genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity) regardless of location or nationality.

**Unjust enrichment**: Retaining benefit without paying where unjust; basis for quasi-contract.

**USMCA** (United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement): Free trade agreement replacing NAFTA (2020).

**Utility**: (1) Patent law: invention must be useful (low threshold). (2) Economics: satisfaction derived from consumption.

## V

**Vagueness**: Due process doctrine invalidating laws so unclear people can't know what's prohibited.

**Variance**: Exception from zoning requirement (granted for hardship).

**Venue**: Geographic location where case tried (within court system having jurisdiction).

**Verdict**: Jury's decision.

**Vertical agreement**: Agreement between different distribution levels (manufacturer-distributor); generally less anticompetitive than horizontal.

**Vested**: Right not subject to condition precedent (certain to become possessory if survive).

**Vicarious liability**: Liability for another's wrongful acts (respondeat superior, partnership).

**Void**: Null; without legal effect from inception.

**Voidable**: Valid until party with power chooses to avoid (contracts by minors, induced by fraud/duress/undue influence).

**Voir dire**: Jury selection process where attorneys question prospective jurors.

## W

**Waiver**: Voluntary relinquishment of known right.

**Warrant**: Court order authorizing action (arrest warrant, search warrant).

**Warranty**: Guarantee regarding product quality/performance; can be express or implied (merchantability, fitness).

**White-collar crime**: Non-violent financial crime (fraud, insider trading, embezzlement).

**Will**: Legal document directing distribution of estate after death.

**Willful**: Intentional, knowing, purposeful (vs accidental).

**Witness**: Person testifying in legal proceeding.

**Writ**: Court order (habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus).

**WTO** (World Trade Organization): International organization promoting trade liberalization and providing dispute settlement (164 members).

## Z

**Zoning**: Government regulation of land use (residential, commercial, industrial).

# Legal Latin Terms

**A fortiori**: With stronger reason; even more so.

**A priori**: From what comes before; based on theory rather than observation.

**Ab initio**: From the beginning.

**Actus reus**: Guilty act; physical element of crime.

**Ad hoc**: For this specific purpose; temporary.

**Amicus curiae**: Friend of the court; non-party submitting brief.

**Bona fide**: In good faith; genuine.

**Caveat emptor**: Let the buyer beware; buyer assumes risk.

**Corpus delicti**: Body of the crime; proof that crime occurred.

**De facto**: In fact; exists in reality.

**De jure**: By law; legally recognized.

**De minimis**: Of minimal importance; trivial.

**De novo**: Anew; from the beginning.

**Erga omnes**: Toward all; obligations owed to everyone.

**Ex parte**: From one party; proceeding without opposing party.

**Ex post facto**: After the fact; retroactive.

**Habeas corpus**: You have the body; writ requiring justification for detention.

**In camera**: In chambers; private judicial proceeding.

**In loco parentis**: In place of a parent.

**In personam**: Against the person; jurisdiction over individual.

**In rem**: Against the thing; jurisdiction over property.

**Inter alia**: Among other things.

**Ipso facto**: By the fact itself; inherently.

**Jus cogens**: Compelling law; peremptory norms.

**Malum in se**: Wrong in itself; inherently immoral (murder, rape).

**Malum prohibitum**: Wrong because prohibited; not inherently immoral (parking violations).

**Mens rea**: Guilty mind; mental element of crime.

**Nolo contendere**: I do not wish to contest; no contest plea.

**Nunc pro tunc**: Now for then; retroactive court order.

**Obiter dictum**: Said in passing; non-binding judicial commentary.

**Pacta sunt servanda**: Agreements must be kept.

**Per curiam**: By the court; unsigned opinion.

**Per se**: By itself; inherently.

**Prima facie**: On its face; at first appearance.

**Pro bono**: For the public good; free legal services.

**Pro rata**: Proportionally.

**Pro se**: For oneself; representing oneself.

**Quid pro quo**: This for that; exchange.

**Ratio decidendi**: Reason for decision; holding.

**Res ipsa loquitur**: The thing speaks for itself; negligence inferred.

**Res judicata**: Matter decided; bars relitigation.

**Respondeat superior**: Let the master answer; employer liability.

**Scienter**: Knowingly; with knowledge.

**Stare decisis**: Stand by things decided; follow precedent.

**Sua sponte**: Of one's own accord; court acts without party motion.

**Sub judice**: Under judgment; currently being decided.

**Sui generis**: Of its own kind; unique.

**Ultra vires**: Beyond the powers; exceeding authority.

**Voir dire**: Speak the truth; jury selection process.

# Summary

This glossary provides comprehensive definitions of legal terms spanning all major areas of law: constitutional law, contracts, property, torts, criminal law and procedure, administrative law, international law, and intellectual property. Terms are arranged alphabetically for quick reference.

The landmark cases table highlights pivotal Supreme Court decisions that shaped American law, from Marbury v. Madison establishing judicial review to recent cases like Dobbs ending the constitutional right to abortion and Loper Bright overruling Chevron deference.

Legal Latin phrases permeate judicial opinions and legal discourse; understanding terms like stare decisis, res judicata, and mens rea is essential for navigating legal texts.

The court hierarchy diagrams illustrate how cases flow through the federal and state systems, from trial courts establishing facts to appellate courts reviewing legal questions, culminating in supreme courts providing final interpretations. Together, these reference materials provide essential tools for understanding legal concepts, analyzing cases, and engaging with the law's technical vocabulary.
