# Intellectual Property

## What Is Intellectual Property?

**Definition**: Legal rights protecting creations of the mind (inventions, artistic works, symbols, names, designs).

**Distinguishes from tangible property**: Ideas, not physical objects.

**Four main types**:
1. **Patents**: Inventions (utility, design, plant)
2. **Copyright**: Creative works (books, music, films, software)
3. **Trademarks**: Brand identifiers (logos, names, slogans)
4. **Trade secrets**: Confidential business information

**Rationale**: Incentivize creation and innovation by granting temporary monopoly.

**Tension**: Exclusive rights vs public access. Too much protection stifles innovation; too little reduces incentives.

## Patents

**Definition**: Exclusive right to invention for limited time (20 years from filing).

**Rights**: Exclude others from making, using, selling, offering to sell, importing invention.

### What Is Patentable?

**35 USC § 101**: "Whoever invents or discovers any new and useful process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter, or any new and useful improvement thereof, may obtain a patent."

**Requirements**:

**1. Patentable subject matter**:
- **Processes, machines, manufactures, compositions of matter**
- **Not**: Laws of nature, natural phenomena, abstract ideas

**2. Utility**: Useful (low bar—practically any use suffices).

**3. Novelty** (§ 102): New (not anticipated by prior art).
- **Prior art**: Previous patents, publications, public use, sales
- **Grace period**: US allows 1-year grace period after inventor's own disclosure (most countries don't)

**4. Non-obviousness** (§ 103): Not obvious to person having ordinary skill in the art (PHOSITA).
- **Graham test**: Scope/content of prior art, differences, level of skill, secondary considerations (commercial success, long-felt need)

**5. Enablement and written description** (§ 112): Specification must teach how to make/use invention; written description must show possession.

**6. Claims**: Define invention's scope (what patent protects).

### Patentable Subject Matter Controversies

**Software patents**: Initially denied (mathematical algorithms); now often allowed (if tied to specific machine/transformation).
- **Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014)**: Abstract ideas implemented on generic computer not patentable. Narrowed software patents.

**Business methods**: "One-click ordering" (Amazon), tax strategies. Now scrutinized under Alice.

**Genes**: Naturally occurring DNA not patentable (Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, 2013—isolated DNA not patent-eligible). But synthetically created DNA (cDNA) patentable.

**Medical diagnostics**: Correlations between biomarkers and diseases often unpatentable (Mayo v. Prometheus, 2012—laws of nature).

### Types of Patents

**Utility patents** (most common): Inventions (machines, processes, compositions).
- **Term**: 20 years from filing
- **Examples**: Pharmaceutical drugs, mechanical devices, software

**Design patents**: Ornamental design of functional item.
- **Term**: 15 years from grant
- **Examples**: iPhone design, Coca-Cola bottle shape

**Plant patents**: Asexually reproduced plants (cuttings, grafting).
- **Term**: 20 years from filing
- **Example**: New rose variety

### Patent Application Process

**1. File application** with USPTO (US Patent and Trademark Office).
- **Provisional**: Quick, cheaper ($75-$300); 1-year priority; then must file non-provisional
- **Non-provisional**: Full application

**2. Examination**: Examiner searches prior art, assesses patentability.

**3. Office actions**: Examiner raises objections (anticipation, obviousness). Applicant responds, amends claims.

**4. Grant or rejection**: If allowable, patent issues. If rejected, can appeal to Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB), then Federal Circuit.

**Timeline**: Average 2-3 years (delays common; USPTO backlog).

**Cost**: $10,000-$30,000+ (including attorney fees).

### Patent Infringement

**Literal infringement**: Accused product/process meets every claim element.

**Doctrine of equivalents**: Insubstantial differences; performs substantially same function, in substantially same way, achieving substantially same result.

**Direct infringement** (§ 271(a)): Makes, uses, sells, offers to sell, or imports.

**Indirect infringement**:
- **Inducement** (§ 271(b)): Actively encourages infringement
- **Contributory** (§ 271(c)): Supplies component knowing it will be used to infringe (no substantial non-infringing use)

**Defenses**:
- **Invalidity**: Patent shouldn't have issued (anticipation, obviousness, § 101)
- **Non-infringement**: Product doesn't meet claim elements
- **Inequitable conduct**: Applicant defrauded USPTO (withheld material prior art)
- **Patent exhaustion**: First sale exhausts rights (can't sue downstream)
- **Experimental use**: Narrow exception for research (not commercial)

**Remedies**:
- **Injunction**: Stop infringement (eBay v. MercExchange, 2006—not automatic; traditional four-factor test)
- **Damages**: Lost profits or reasonable royalty
- **Willful infringement**: Up to treble damages
- **Attorney fees**: Exceptional cases

### Patent Litigation and Trolls

**Patent assertion entities (PAEs)** / "patent trolls": Don't make products; buy patents to sue.

**Business model**: Threaten litigation; settle for less than defense cost.

**Criticisms**:
- Tax on innovation
- Extort settlements from small businesses
- Vague software patents weaponized

**Defenses**:
- **America Invents Act (2011)**: Inter partes review (IPR) at PTAB—challenge validity cheaply/quickly
- **Fee shifting**: "Exceptional case" attorney fees (Octane Fitness, 2014—lowered bar)
- **Venue**: Restricted to defendant's state or where infringement occurred (TC Heartland, 2017—ended forum shopping to Eastern District of Texas)

**IPR (Inter Partes Review)**: PTAB proceeding to challenge issued patent's validity.
- **Petitioner**: Accused infringer (or anyone)
- **Grounds**: Anticipation, obviousness (based on prior art)
- **Standard**: Preponderance of evidence (easier than district court's clear and convincing)
- **Appeal**: To Federal Circuit
- **Success rate**: ~60% of challenged claims invalidated (at least partially)
- **Criticism**: "Death squads" killing valid patents; undermines property rights

### Pharmaceutical Patents and Evergreening

**High R&D costs**: Developing drug costs ~$1-2 billion, takes 10-15 years.

**Generic competition**: Once patent expires, generics enter (prices drop 80-90%).

**Evergreening**: Patenting minor variations to extend exclusivity.
- **Strategies**: New formulation (extended-release), new use, metabolite, combination
- **Criticism**: Delays generic entry; raises drug costs
- **Example**: AstraZeneca's Prilosec (omeprazole) → Nexium (esomeprazole, single isomer). Critics say trivial difference; company says meaningful improvement.

**Hatch-Waxman Act** (1984): Balances innovation and access.
- **Generics**: Can file Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) relying on brand's safety/efficacy data
- **Patent linkage**: Generic must certify brand patents invalid/not infringed (Paragraph IV)
- **30-month stay**: Brand can sue, delaying generic approval
- **180-day exclusivity**: First generic gets exclusivity (incentive to challenge patents)

**Biologics**: Complex molecules (antibodies, proteins) made in living cells. No true generics; "biosimilars" (similar but not identical).
- **BPCIA** (2010): Pathway for biosimilars (12 years exclusivity for biologics)

## Copyright

**Definition**: Exclusive rights to original works of authorship fixed in tangible medium.

**Term**: Life of author + 70 years (or 95 years from publication / 120 years from creation for corporate works).

### What Is Copyrightable?

**17 USC § 102**: "Original works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression."

**Categories**:
1. Literary works (books, articles, software)
2. Musical works
3. Dramatic works
4. Pantomimes and choreography
5. Pictorial, graphic, sculptural works
6. Motion pictures and audiovisual works
7. Sound recordings
8. Architectural works

**Requirements**:

**1. Originality**: Modicum of creativity (low bar).
- **Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone (1991)**: Phone book (alphabetical listings) not copyrightable (no creativity)

**2. Fixation**: Recorded in tangible form (written, recorded, saved).
- **Not**: Impromptu speech, unfixed performance

**Not copyrightable**:
- **Ideas**: Only expression of ideas (idea-expression dichotomy)
- **Facts**: Historical facts, data (but compilation/arrangement can be)
- **Government works**: Federal government works in public domain
- **Useful articles**: Functionality not copyrightable (only separable aesthetic features)
- **Short phrases, titles, names**: Too minimal for copyright (trademark may protect)

### Exclusive Rights (§ 106)

**1. Reproduction**: Make copies.

**2. Derivative works**: Adaptations (translations, movies from books, sequels).

**3. Distribution**: Sell, rent, lend copies.

**4. Public performance**: Perform publicly (plays, concerts, streaming).
- **Sound recordings exception**: No public performance right (except digital streaming)

**5. Public display**: Display publicly (art, photos).

**6. Digital audio transmission**: For sound recordings (limited; statutory licenses).

### Fair Use (§ 107)

**Defense**: Use of copyrighted work without permission for certain purposes.

**Four factors** (non-exhaustive; balancing test):

**1. Purpose and character of use**:
- **Transformative?**: Adds new meaning/message (favors fair use)
- **Commercial vs nonprofit educational**: Educational favors fair use (but not dispositive)

**2. Nature of copyrighted work**:
- **Published vs unpublished**: Published favors fair use
- **Factual vs creative**: Factual favors fair use (closer to ideas)

**3. Amount and substantiality**:
- **Quantity**: How much used (% of whole)
- **Quality**: Heart of the work?

**4. Effect on market**:
- **Substitution**: Does use harm market for original or derivatives?
- **Most important factor** (Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, 1994)

**Examples**:

**Fair use found**:
- **Parody**: 2 Live Crew's rap parody of "Oh, Pretty Woman" (Campbell, 1994—transformative)
- **Thumbnail images**: Google image search thumbnails (Perfect 10 v. Amazon, 2007—transformative; low-res)
- **Digital library**: Google Books scanning (Authors Guild v. Google, 2015—transformative; doesn't substitute)

**Fair use denied**:
- **Nation magazine excerpting Ford memoir** (Harper & Row v. Nation, 1985—unpublished; scooped serialization; harmed market)
- **Copying entire course packets**: Students paying for copies substitutes for textbook sales

**Fair use uncertain**:
- **AI training**: Scraping copyrighted works to train models. Ongoing litigation (several lawsuits against OpenAI, Stability AI, etc.).

### Copyright Infringement

**Elements**:
1. **Ownership**: Plaintiff owns valid copyright
2. **Copying**: Defendant copied (proven by access + substantial similarity)
3. **Improper appropriation**: Substantial similarity of protected expression (not just ideas)

**Substantial similarity**: Ordinary observer would find works similar in protected elements.

**Examples**:
- **Blurred Lines / Marvin Gaye**: Jury found Pharrell/Thicke infringed Gaye's "Got to Give It Up" (controversial; many musicians said verdict wrong)
- **Led Zeppelin / Spirit**: "Stairway to Heaven" didn't infringe "Taurus" (9th Cir. 2020)

**Defenses**:
- **Fair use** (most common)
- **Independent creation**: Created without access
- **Scenes à faire**: Stock elements (genres dictate)
- **Merger doctrine**: Idea and expression merged (limited ways to express)

**Remedies**:
- **Injunction**: Stop infringement
- **Actual damages + profits**: What plaintiff lost + what defendant gained
- **Statutory damages**: $750-$30,000 per work ($150,000 if willful)
- **Attorney fees**: If willful infringement or frivolous claim

### Digital Copyright: DMCA

**Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998)**: Implements WIPO treaties; addresses digital issues.

**Key provisions**:

**1. Anti-circumvention** (§ 1201): Prohibits circumventing technological protection measures (DRM).
- **Example**: Breaking encryption on DVDs, e-books
- **Exceptions**: Reverse engineering for interoperability, security research, accessibility

**2. Safe harbors** (§ 512): Internet service providers (ISPs) not liable for user infringement if:
- **Transitory communications**: Just transmitting
- **Caching**
- **Storage on system at user direction**: Hosting (YouTube, Facebook) if:
  - No knowledge of infringement
  - Remove expeditiously upon notice (notice-and-takedown)
  - Designate agent to receive notices
  - Terminate repeat infringers

**Notice-and-takedown**:
1. Copyright owner sends DMCA notice to platform
2. Platform removes content
3. User can send counter-notice (if believes use lawful)
4. Copyright owner has 10 days to sue; else platform restores content

**Abuse**: False DMCA notices (suppress criticism, competition). Penalties for knowing misrepresentation.

**Viacom v. YouTube (2010)**: YouTube entitled to safe harbor (no specific knowledge of infringing videos; removed promptly upon notice).

### Copyright Duration

**Current** (post-1978 works):
- **Individual author**: Life + 70 years
- **Corporate / work-for-hire**: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation (whichever earlier)

**Pre-1978 works**: Complex (depends on publication date, renewal).

**Public domain**: Works whose copyright expired (or never had copyright).
- **Examples**: Shakespeare, Beethoven, Mark Twain, Einstein's papers

**Mickey Mouse**: First appearance (Steamboat Willie, 1928) entered public domain Jan 1, 2024 (95 years after publication). Subsequent versions still protected.

**Copyright Term Extension Act (1998)** (Sonny Bono Act): Extended term by 20 years.
- **Criticism**: "Mickey Mouse Protection Act"; delays public domain; harms access
- **Challenge**: Eldred v. Ashcroft (2003)—Supreme Court upheld (Congress has broad power; not unconstitutional)

### Orphan Works

**Problem**: Works still under copyright but owner can't be located.
- **Libraries, archives, filmmakers** want to use but fear infringement suits
- **Estimates**: Millions of orphan works (old books, photos, films)

**Proposals**: Limit damages if good-faith effort to find owner; registration requirement (most countries don't require).

**Status**: No US legislation (lobbying by photographer groups opposed).

## Trademarks

**Definition**: Words, symbols, designs identifying source of goods/services.

**Purpose**: Prevent consumer confusion; protect brand reputation.

**Rights**: Prevent others from using confusingly similar marks on related goods/services.

**No time limit**: As long as used in commerce and maintained (renewals required).

### What Can Be Trademarked?

**Words**: Brand names (Apple, Nike, Coca-Cola).

**Logos**: Swoosh, golden arches.

**Slogans**: "Just Do It," "I'm Lovin' It."

**Trade dress**: Product design/packaging (if non-functional, distinctive).
- **Examples**: Coca-Cola bottle, iPhone design
- **Two Pesos v. Taco Cabana (1992)**: Restaurant décor can be trade dress

**Colors**: If distinctive.
- **Qualitex v. Jacobson (1995)**: Green-gold dry cleaning press pads trademarked

**Sounds**: NBC chimes, MGM lion roar.

**Smells**: Play-Doh scent (registered).

**Not trademarkable**:
- **Functional features**: Utilitarian aspects (protect via patent, not trademark)
- **Generic terms**: "Aspirin," "escalator," "thermos" (were trademarks; became generic)
- **Merely descriptive**: Without secondary meaning ("Cold and Creamy" for ice cream)
- **Deceptive**: Misleading
- **Scandalous/immoral**: (Mostly eliminated; Iancu v. Brunetti, 2019—First Amendment prohibits)

### Distinctiveness Spectrum

**Fanciful** (strongest): Made-up words (Kodak, Xerox, Exxon). Automatically distinctive.

**Arbitrary**: Real words, no relation to product (Apple for computers, Camel for cigarettes). Automatically distinctive.

**Suggestive**: Hints at quality/nature but requires imagination (Netflix, Greyhound buses). Automatically distinctive.

**Descriptive**: Describes product. Not protected unless secondary meaning (consumers associate with source).
- **Example**: "America Online" (descriptive of service, but acquired secondary meaning—consumers recognize AOL brand)

**Generic** (weakest): Common name for product (never protectable). "Aspirin," "app store."

**Genericization risk**: Strong marks can become generic if widely used (Google, Kleenex, Band-Aid at risk).

### Trademark Registration

**Common law rights**: Trademark rights arise from use (no registration required). But limited to geographic area where used.

**Federal registration** (Lanham Act): Register with USPTO.
- **Benefits**: Nationwide rights, presumption of validity, can sue in federal court, use ® symbol, basis for foreign registration, U.S. Customs enforcement
- **Process**: Apply → examination → publication (opposition period) → registration
- **Renewals**: Every 10 years (indefinitely)

**Intent-to-use**: Can apply before using (must file proof of use within 6 months, extendable).

### Trademark Infringement

**Standard**: Likelihood of confusion (between marks on related goods/services).

**Factors** (Polaroid factors, varies by circuit):
1. **Strength of plaintiff's mark**: Fanciful > arbitrary > suggestive > descriptive
2. **Similarity of marks**: Visual, phonetic, meaning
3. **Proximity of goods/services**: Related industries?
4. **Evidence of actual confusion**: Surveys, misdirected communications
5. **Defendant's intent**: Bad faith (intentional copying)?
6. **Quality of defendant's goods**: Inferior goods harm plaintiff's reputation?
7. **Sophistication of buyers**: Expert buyers less likely confused

**No confusion if**:
- **Different fields**: Apple Records (music) vs Apple Computers (before Apple Music)
- **Geographic separation**: Two local businesses, different states
- **Disclaimers**: "Not affiliated with..."

**Defenses**:
- **Fair use**: Descriptive use (describing own product, not as brand)
- **Nominative fair use**: Referring to trademark holder's product (e.g., "repair service for iPhones")
- **Parody**: If clear it's joke (no confusion)

**Remedies**:
- **Injunction**: Stop use
- **Damages**: Profits, actual damages
- **Destruction**: Infringing goods
- **Corrective advertising**: Dispel confusion

### Trademark Dilution

**Protection beyond confusion**: Famous marks protected from dilution (even if no confusion).

**Two types**:

**1. Blurring**: Weakens distinctiveness (association with different goods).
- **Example**: "Kodak pianos"—consumers would associate Kodak with multiple products, weakening camera association

**2. Tarnishment**: Harms reputation (negative associations).
- **Example**: "Barbie" in porn context (Mattel v. MCA Records, 2002—but parody defense prevailed)

**Requirements**:
- **Famous mark**: Household name
- **Actual dilution**: Moseley v. V Secret Catalogue (2003)—must prove actual harm (not just likelihood)
- **Trademark Dilution Revision Act (2006)**: "Likelihood of dilution" (easier standard)

**Defenses**: Fair use, parody, news reporting.

### Cybersquatting

**Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA)** (1999): Prohibits registering domain names in bad faith intending to profit from another's trademark.

**Example**: Registering "nike.com" to sell to Nike.

**Bad faith factors**: No legitimate interest in domain, intent to divert/tarnish, offered to sell for profit.

**Remedy**: Transfer domain, damages ($1,000-$100,000 per domain).

**UDRP** (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy): ICANN process for resolving domain disputes (faster/cheaper than ACPA).

## Trade Secrets

**Definition**: Confidential business information providing competitive advantage.

**Examples**: Coca-Cola formula, Google's search algorithm, customer lists, manufacturing processes.

**Protection**: State law (Uniform Trade Secrets Act—UTSA—adopted by most states) + federal (Defend Trade Secrets Act—DTSA, 2016).

### Requirements

**1. Information**: Technical or business (formulas, patterns, compilations, programs, methods, techniques).

**2. Secrecy**: Not generally known or readily ascertainable.

**3. Economic value**: Derives value from secrecy.

**4. Reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy**: NDAs, access restrictions, labeling, physical security.

### Misappropriation

**Acquisition**: Improper means (theft, bribery, espionage, breach of confidentiality).

**Disclosure/use**: Using/disclosing trade secret knowing acquired by improper means or breach of duty.

**Examples**:
- **Employee leaves, takes customer list**: Misappropriation (if list secret, reasonable efforts to protect)
- **Reverse engineering**: NOT misappropriation (permissible)
- **Independent discovery**: NOT misappropriation

**Defenses**:
- **Publicly available**: Not secret
- **Reverse engineered**: Lawful
- **Independent development**: Created without access

**Remedies**:
- **Injunction**: Prevent disclosure/use (limited duration—until no longer secret or lead time eliminated)
- **Damages**: Actual loss or unjust enrichment
- **Punitive damages**: If willful/malicious (UTSA allows; DTSA allows for exemplary damages up to 2x)
- **Attorney fees**: If willful/malicious or bad faith claim

### Trade Secrets vs Patents

**Trade secret advantages**:
- **No time limit**: Lasts as long as kept secret
- **No disclosure**: Don't reveal to public
- **No application cost/delay**

**Trade secret disadvantages**:
- **Lost if discovered**: Reverse engineering, independent discovery lawful
- **No exclusivity**: Others can use if discovered independently
- **Vulnerable to leaks**

**Strategic choice**: Patent if disclosure inevitable (product reveals invention); trade secret if can maintain secrecy (Coca-Cola formula).

### Non-Compete Agreements

**Related to trade secrets**: Employers use to prevent employees from joining competitors (and potentially disclosing secrets).

**Enforceability** (varies by state):
- **Generally enforceable if reasonable**: Scope (activity), duration, geography
- **California**: Generally unenforceable (except sale of business, dissolution of partnership)
- **Trend**: Increasing skepticism; some states ban for low-wage workers

**FTC proposed rule** (2023): Ban most non-competes (not finalized; legal challenges likely).

**Alternatives**: Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), non-solicitation (can't recruit clients/employees).

## IP in the Digital Age

### Software

**Patent**: If not abstract idea (post-Alice, harder).

**Copyright**: Code and structure (but not functionality; API may not be copyrightable—Google v. Oracle, 2021).

**Trade secret**: Source code, algorithms (if kept secret).

**Open source**: Licenses allowing use/modification (GPL, MIT, Apache). Copyrighted but broadly licensed.

### AI-Generated Content

**Copyrightability**: Only human-authored works copyrightable (Thaler v. Perlmutter, 2023—AI-generated art denied registration).
- **If human contributes creatively**: Copyrightable (human using AI as tool)
- **If purely AI-generated**: Not copyrightable

**Training data**: Scraping copyrighted works for AI training—fair use? Litigation ongoing.
- **Authors/artists sue**: OpenAI, Stability AI, Midjourney (claiming infringement)
- **Defenses**: Transformative use, doesn't output copies

**Inventorship**: Can AI be inventor on patent? USPTO says no (must be natural person). Federal Circuit affirmed (Thaler v. Vidal, 2022).

### 3D Printing

**Patents**: 3D printing patented objects infringes (even if for personal use).

**Copyright**: Printing copyrighted sculptures infringes. But useful articles not copyrightable (functionality).

**Enforcement challenge**: Distributed, home manufacturing hard to detect/police.

### Streaming and Digital Distribution

**Shift from ownership to access**: Spotify, Netflix, Kindle.

**Licensing**: Platforms license content (don't transfer ownership to users).

**First sale doctrine erosion**: Can't resell digital content (no physical copy to transfer; Capitol Records v. ReDigi, 2018—can't resell "used" digital music).

**DRM**: Digital rights management prevents copying/sharing.

**Piracy**: Remains challenge (BitTorrent, streaming sites). DMCA safe harbors protect platforms if remove infringing content.

## International IP

### Treaties

**Paris Convention** (1883): National treatment (foreign applicants treated same as domestic); priority (file in one country, have 12 months to file abroad with same priority date).

**Berne Convention** (1886): Copyright (automatic protection in all member countries; no formalities required; national treatment; minimum standards).

**TRIPS** (1995): WTO agreement requiring minimum IP protections (patents, copyrights, trademarks, trade secrets).
- **Patent term**: At least 20 years
- **Copyright term**: At least life + 50 years (or 50 years from publication for corporate)
- **Enforcement**: WTO dispute settlement

**WIPO Copyright Treaty / WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty** (1996): Digital rights (anti-circumvention; DMCA implements in US).

**Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)**: Single international patent application (but still examined separately in each country).

**Madrid Protocol**: International trademark registration (file in one country, extend to others).

### Enforcement Challenges

**Counterfeiting**: Fake luxury goods (Louis Vuitton bags, Rolex watches), pharmaceuticals.
- **China major source**: Estimates 60-80% of counterfeit goods from China
- **Enforcement**: U.S. Customs seizures, criminal prosecution

**Piracy**: Unauthorized copying/distribution (movies, music, software).
- **Napster, Kazaa, BitTorrent**: File-sharing networks
- **Responses**: DMCA takedowns, lawsuits (RIAA sued thousands), prosecution (Megaupload, Pirate Bay)

**Weak IP protection in some countries**: Patents/copyrights ignored; enforcement lax.

**Harmonization efforts**: TRIPS minimum standards; bilateral/regional agreements (USMCA, CPTPP).

### Compulsory Licenses

**Government authorizes use** of patented invention without owner's consent (but with compensation).

**Grounds** (vary by country):
- **Public health emergency**: Access to essential medicines (HIV antiretrovirals)
- **Anti-competitive practices**: Patent holder abuses monopoly
- **National security**: Government use

**Doha Declaration** (2001): Affirmed WTO members' right to issue compulsory licenses for public health.
- **HIV drugs in Africa**: Several countries issued licenses; prices dropped dramatically
- **Criticism**: Undermines incentives for R&D

**US**: Limited use (government can use patents with compensation; march-in rights for federally funded research—never exercised).

## Balancing Innovation Incentives vs Access

**Too much IP protection**:
- **High prices**: Patents allow monopoly pricing (drugs, software)
- **Patent thickets**: Overlapping patents block innovation (smartphones have thousands of patents)
- **Litigation costs**: Defensive patenting; trolls
- **Anticommons**: Too many rights holders; can't negotiate licenses

**Too little IP protection**:
- **Underinvestment**: If can't recoup R&D, won't invest (especially pharma)
- **No disclosure**: Trade secrets (vs patents requiring disclosure)
- **Free-riding**: Competitors copy without bearing development costs

**Policy debates**:
- **Patent term**: Should be shorter for fast-moving fields (software)?
- **Patentable subject matter**: Should software, business methods, genes be patentable?
- **Damages**: Should be limited (current damages sometimes exceed invention's value)?
- **Access to medicines**: Should compulsory licenses be broader?
- **Fair use**: Should be expanded (user-generated content, AI training)?

**Open innovation movements**:
- **Open source software**: Linux, Apache, Mozilla
- **Creative Commons**: Licenses allowing sharing/adaptation (with attribution)
- **Open access publishing**: Research freely available (vs paywalled journals)
- **Patent pools**: Cross-licensing to reduce thickets (MPEG, DVD)

## Key Terms

- **Patent**: Exclusive right to invention (20 years)
- **Utility patent**: Invention (process, machine, composition)
- **Design patent**: Ornamental design (15 years)
- **Prior art**: Previous disclosures (patents, publications, public use)
- **Obviousness**: Not patentable if obvious to PHOSITA
- **Enablement**: Patent must teach how to make/use
- **Claims**: Define patent's scope
- **Patent troll**: Non-practicing entity asserting patents for settlements
- **IPR** (Inter Partes Review): PTAB challenge to patent validity
- **Copyright**: Exclusive rights to creative works (life + 70 years)
- **Originality**: Modicum of creativity required
- **Fair use**: Defense allowing use without permission (four factors)
- **DMCA**: Digital Millennium Copyright Act (anti-circumvention, safe harbors)
- **Public domain**: Works with expired/no copyright
- **Trademark**: Brand identifier (words, symbols, designs)
- **Likelihood of confusion**: Trademark infringement standard
- **Dilution**: Famous mark protection (blurring, tarnishment)
- **Trade secret**: Confidential business information
- **Misappropriation**: Improper acquisition/use of trade secret
- **Non-compete**: Agreement restricting employee from joining competitor
- **TRIPS**: WTO agreement on minimum IP protections
- **Compulsory license**: Government-authorized use of patent without owner's consent

## Summary

Intellectual property law balances incentivizing innovation through exclusive rights against ensuring public access to knowledge and culture. Patents grant 20-year monopolies on useful, novel, non-obvious inventions, enabling inventors to recoup R&D investments. Controversies persist over software, business method, and gene patents, with recent Supreme Court decisions narrowing patentable subject matter. Patent trolls exploit the system by asserting weak patents for settlements, prompting reforms like inter partes review.

Copyright protects original creative works for life plus 70 years, granting exclusive rights to reproduce, adapt, distribute, perform, and display. Fair use permits limited unlicensed use for purposes like criticism, commentary, and education, assessed through a four-factor balancing test. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act addresses digital challenges through anti-circumvention rules and safe harbors for online platforms implementing notice-and-takedown systems.

Trademarks prevent consumer confusion by protecting brand identifiers indefinitely as long as maintained. Protection strength varies along a distinctiveness spectrum from fanciful (strongest) to generic (no protection). Dilution protects famous marks from blurring and tarnishment even absent confusion.

Trade secrets protect confidential business information through state and federal misappropriation laws, lasting indefinitely if secrecy maintained. Unlike patents, trade secrets require no disclosure but are lost if reverse-engineered or independently discovered.

The digital age raises novel IP issues: AI-generated content challenges human authorship requirements, 3D printing enables distributed infringement, and streaming services transform ownership models. International treaties like TRIPS harmonize minimum protections, though enforcement challenges persist with counterfeiting and piracy, particularly from jurisdictions with weak IP protection.

Ongoing debates center on balancing innovation incentives against access—pharmaceutical patent evergreening delays generic competition and raises drug prices, while compulsory licenses for essential medicines in health emergencies highlight tensions between property rights and public welfare. Open innovation movements offer alternative models, demonstrating that robust creation can occur outside traditional IP frameworks.
