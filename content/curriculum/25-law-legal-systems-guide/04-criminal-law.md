# Criminal Law & Procedure

## What Makes Conduct Criminal?

**Crime**: Act prohibited and punished by government.

**Distinguishes from civil wrongs**:
- **Prosecution**: State (not victim) prosecutes
- **Punishment**: Imprisonment, fines (not compensating victim)
- **Burden of proof**: Beyond reasonable doubt (not preponderance)
- **Stigma**: Criminal conviction carries moral condemnation

### Purposes of Criminal Punishment

**Retribution**: Offender deserves punishment (backward-looking; moral desert).
- **Kant**: Punish because justice requires it
- **Criticism**: Vengeance; doesn't reduce crime

**Deterrence**: Prevent future crime by making it costly.
- **General deterrence**: Deter others by example
- **Specific deterrence**: Deter this offender from reoffending
- **Criticism**: Assumes rational calculation (may not apply to passion crimes, mentally ill)

**Incapacitation**: Physically prevent crime by imprisonment.
- **Works while imprisoned** but doesn't change behavior
- **Criticism**: Expensive; collateral consequences for families/communities

**Rehabilitation**: Reform offender so they don't reoffend.
- **Education, treatment, job training**
- **Criticism**: Mixed success rates; "nothing works" pessimism (1970s)

**Expressive**: Denounce conduct; reinforce societal values.
- **Beyond deterrence**: Communicates wrongfulness
- **Criticism**: Symbolic; doesn't reduce crime

**Modern trend**: Hybrid approaches; sentencing depends on offense/offender characteristics.

## Elements of a Crime

**Actus reus** (guilty act) + **Mens rea** (guilty mind) + **Concurrence** + **Causation** (for result crimes)

### Actus Reus (Guilty Act)

**Voluntary act** or **omission** (where duty exists).

**Not criminal**: Involuntary acts (sleepwalking, seizure, reflex).

**Examples**:
- **Voluntary**: Pulling trigger, taking property
- **Involuntary**: Convulsion causing injury, hypnotic state

**Omissions**: Generally no duty to act (even if morally required).

**Duty to act** exists when:
1. **Statute**: Duty to file tax return, report child abuse
2. **Status relationship**: Parent-child, spouse
3. **Contract**: Lifeguard, caretaker
4. **Voluntary assumption**: Begin rescue, creating reliance
5. **Creation of peril**: Caused dangerous situation

**Example**: Parent fails to feed child; child dies. Actus reus satisfied (omission with duty).

**But**: Stranger sees drowning child, doesn't help. No criminal liability (no duty).

### Mens Rea (Guilty Mind)

**Mental state** accompanying act.

**Common law**: Specific intent vs general intent vs strict liability.

**Model Penal Code (MPC)**: Four levels (descending culpability):

**1. Purposely**: Conscious objective to cause result.
- **Example**: Shoots intending to kill.

**2. Knowingly**: Aware conduct will cause result (practical certainty).
- **Example**: Plants bomb in crowded building; knows people will die.

**3. Recklessly**: Consciously disregards substantial and unjustifiable risk.
- **Example**: Drunk driving; aware of risk but proceeds.

**4. Negligently**: Should be aware of substantial and unjustifiable risk (reasonable person would realize).
- **Example**: Leaves loaded gun where child finds it; didn't think about risk but should have.

**Strict liability**: No mens rea required.
- **Statutory rape**: Liability even if reasonably believed victim was adult
- **Regulatory offenses**: Selling adulterated food (public welfare)
- **Criticism**: Punishes without moral fault

**Transferred intent**: Intends harm to A but hits B; intent transfers.
- **Example**: Shoots at A, misses, kills B. Guilty of murdering B.

### Concurrence

**Mens rea must accompany actus reus** (not before or after).

**Example**: A decides to kill B. Next day, driving carefully, accidentally hits and kills B. No murder (no concurrence—intent preceded act but didn't accompany it).

### Causation (Result Crimes)

**Must prove**:
1. **Factual causation** (but-for): But for defendant's act, result wouldn't have occurred.
2. **Proximate causation** (legal cause): Defendant's act was foreseeable and not too remote.

**Intervening cause**: Event after defendant's act contributing to result.
- **Dependent**: Foreseeable response to defendant's act (doesn't break chain).
  - **Example**: Victim goes to hospital; doctor negligently treats; victim dies. Defendant liable (foreseeable).
- **Independent**: Unforeseeable event (may break chain).
  - **Example**: Defendant wounds victim; victim in ambulance hit by meteor and dies. Defendant not liable for death (meteor unforeseeable).

**Year-and-a-day rule** (mostly abolished): Death must occur within year and day of act.

## Categories of Crimes

### Felonies vs Misdemeanors

**Felony**: Serious crime; punishment over one year (state prison).
- **Examples**: Murder, rape, robbery, burglary, drug trafficking

**Misdemeanor**: Less serious; punishment up to one year (county jail).
- **Examples**: Petty theft, simple assault, DUI (first offense often)

**Infraction**: Minor violation; fine only (no jail).
- **Examples**: Speeding, jaywalking

**Consequences of felony conviction**:
- Disenfranchisement (many states)
- Ineligible for certain jobs (teaching, law, medicine)
- Deportation (non-citizens)
- Loss of gun rights
- Difficulty finding housing/employment

### Major Crimes Overview

#### Homicide

**Murder**: Unlawful killing with malice aforethought.

**Common law malice aforethought**:
1. Intent to kill
2. Intent to inflict serious bodily harm
3. Depraved heart (extreme recklessness)
4. Felony murder (death during dangerous felony)

**Degrees**:

**First-degree murder**: Premeditated and deliberate (or felony murder in some states).
- **Premeditation**: Thought beforehand (even brief moment)
- **Deliberation**: Cool reflection
- **Example**: Plans killing; buys gun; waits for victim.

**Second-degree murder**: Intent to kill (or depraved heart) but no premeditation.
- **Example**: Heat-of-the-moment killing.

**Depraved heart murder**: Extreme recklessness showing wanton disregard for life.
- **Example**: Fires gun into crowd; kills someone. No intent to kill specific person but extreme recklessness.

**Felony murder**: Death caused during commission of dangerous felony (burglary, arson, rape, robbery, kidnapping—BARRK).
- **Example**: Robber's accomplice killed by police. Robber guilty of murder (victim's identity irrelevant).
- **Limitations**:
  - Merger doctrine: Underlying felony can't be assault (would make all assaults murders)
  - Some states require death be foreseeable
  - Some states limit to deaths defendant caused (not co-felon killed by police)

**Voluntary manslaughter**: Intent to kill but with mitigation (heat of passion/adequate provocation).
- **Elements**:
  1. Provocation adequate to cause reasonable person to lose control
  2. Defendant actually provoked
  3. No cooling off period
  4. Defendant didn't actually cool off
- **Example**: Finds spouse in bed with paramour; immediately kills. Provocation reduces murder to manslaughter.

**Involuntary manslaughter**: Unintentional killing from criminal negligence or misdemeanor.
- **Example**: Drunk driver kills pedestrian. No intent to kill but gross negligence.

#### Rape and Sexual Assault

**Common law rape**: Unlawful carnal knowledge of woman by man, by force, without consent.

**Modern statutes**:
- **Gender-neutral**: Applies to all genders
- **Degrees**: Based on force, injury, age, victim incapacity
- **Consent focus**: Force no longer always required; lack of consent suffices

**Consent**:
- **Capacity**: Minors, intoxicated, mentally incapacitated can't consent
- **Affirmative consent**: Some states require active agreement (not just absence of "no")
- **Withdrawal**: Consent can be revoked

**Marital exemption**: Common law said husband can't rape wife (now abolished in all states).

**Statutory rape**: Sex with minor (strict liability; reasonable mistake of age not defense).

#### Theft Crimes

**Larceny**: Trespassory taking and carrying away of personal property of another with intent to permanently deprive.
- **Trespassory**: Without consent
- **Carrying away** (asportation): Even slight movement suffices
- **Intent to permanently deprive**: Not borrowing

**Embezzlement**: Fraudulent conversion of property by one in lawful possession.
- **Difference from larceny**: Possession initially lawful (e.g., employee, trustee)
- **Example**: Bank teller takes money from drawer.

**False pretenses**: Obtaining title to property by intentional false statement.
- **Difference from larceny**: Victim transfers title (not just possession)
- **Example**: Sells fake Rolex as genuine.

**Robbery**: Larceny + force or intimidation.
- **Example**: "Give me your wallet or I'll shoot."

**Burglary**: Breaking and entering dwelling of another at nighttime with intent to commit felony.
- **Modern statutes**: Often drop "nighttime" and "dwelling" requirements; any building suffices.
- **Intent**: Must have felonious intent at entry (not developed later).

**Receiving stolen property**: Knowingly receiving stolen goods with intent to deprive owner.

#### Assault and Battery

**Battery**: Unlawful application of force to another (harmful or offensive touching).
- **No injury required**: Offensive touching suffices

**Assault**: Attempt to commit battery OR intentionally causing apprehension of imminent harmful/offensive contact.
- **Example**: Swings fist but misses.

**Aggravated assault/battery**: With weapon, causing serious injury, or against protected class (police, child).

#### Drug Crimes

**Possession**: Actual or constructive (control even if not on person).
- **Constructive possession**: Drugs in car, home (must prove knowledge and control).

**Distribution/trafficking**: Selling or intending to sell (quantity often infers intent).

**Manufacturing**: Production of controlled substances.

**Conspiracy to distribute**: Agreement + overt act.

**Sentencing**: Federal mandatory minimums (e.g., 5 years for 5 grams crack, 500 grams powder cocaine—racially disparate impact; Fair Sentencing Act 2010 reduced disparity).

## Inchoate Crimes (Incomplete Crimes)

### Attempt

**Elements**:
1. **Specific intent** to commit target crime
2. **Substantial step** toward commission (beyond mere preparation)

**Substantial step**: MPC test—strongly corroborative of intent.
- **Preparation**: Not attempt (buying gun for future murder)
- **Substantial step**: Attempt (lying in wait, entering building)

**Legal impossibility**: Defense (attempted crime not actually illegal).
- **Example**: Believes receiving stolen goods is crime but goods not stolen. No attempt.

**Factual impossibility**: NOT defense (crime impossible on facts but would be crime if facts were as believed).
- **Example**: Shoots victim already dead. Still attempted murder (thought alive).

**Abandonment**: MPC recognizes voluntary and complete abandonment as defense. Common law generally doesn't.

### Conspiracy

**Elements**:
1. **Agreement** between two or more people
2. **Intent** to agree + intent to achieve objective
3. **Overt act** in furtherance (some jurisdictions)

**Agreement**: Need not be express; can be tacit understanding.

**Plurality**: Common law requires two guilty minds (if one is undercover cop, no conspiracy). MPC uses unilateral approach (defendant's intent suffices).

**Overt act**: Any act (even lawful) showing pursuance. Not required for serious felonies (MPC).

**Pinkerton rule**: Co-conspirator liable for crimes by others in furtherance of conspiracy (even if didn't personally commit).

**Withdrawal**: Affirmative act communicating withdrawal to co-conspirators. Terminates future liability but not liability for conspiracy itself.

**Wharton's rule**: If crime requires two people (bigamy, adultery), can't be conspiracy unless more people involved (e.g., adultery requires two people; not conspiracy unless third person involved).

### Solicitation

**Elements**:
1. **Enticing, encouraging, requesting** another to commit crime
2. **Intent** that crime be committed

**Complete upon asking**: No need for target to agree.

**Defense**: Renunciation if prevents crime (MPC).

**Merger**: If other agrees, solicitation merges into conspiracy.

## Defenses

### Justification Defenses (Conduct Not Wrongful)

#### Self-Defense

**Elements**:
1. **Reasonable belief** of imminent unlawful force
2. **Proportional response**: Force used reasonable to repel threat
3. **Non-aggressor**: Didn't start confrontation (unless withdrew and communicated withdrawal)

**Deadly force**: Allowed only if reasonable belief of imminent death or serious bodily harm.

**Duty to retreat**: Some states require retreat if safely possible (before using deadly force).
- **Castle doctrine**: No duty to retreat in one's home.
- **Stand-your-ground**: No duty to retreat anywhere (controversial; critics say encourages violence).

**Imperfect self-defense**: Honest but unreasonable belief. Not acquittal but mitigates murder to manslaughter.

**Battered woman syndrome**: Pattern of abuse may make belief of imminent threat reasonable even if objective circumstances unclear.

#### Defense of Others

**Can defend third party** if same justification as self-defense.

**Alter ego rule** (minority): Defender stands in shoes of person defended; if person defended not justified, defender not justified.

**Reasonable belief rule** (majority): If reasonably appears third party needs defense, justified even if mistaken.

#### Defense of Property

**Reasonable non-deadly force** to protect property.

**Deadly force NOT allowed** to protect property alone (some states allow if preventing arson, burglary of dwelling at night).

**Spring guns**: Deadly force devices generally prohibited (human life > property).

#### Necessity (Choice of Evils)

**Elements**:
1. **Imminent harm** threatened
2. **No legal alternative**
3. **Harm avoided > harm caused**
4. **Defendant not at fault** in creating situation

**Example**: Break into cabin to avoid freezing to death. Justified.

**Not applicable to homicide**: Can't kill innocent to save yourself.

**Example**: **Regina v. Dudley and Stephens (1884)**: Shipwrecked sailors killed and ate cabin boy to survive. Guilty of murder (necessity not defense to homicide).

#### Duress

**Elements**:
1. **Threat of imminent death or serious bodily harm**
2. **Reasonable belief** threat will be carried out
3. **No reasonable opportunity to escape**

**Not defense to homicide**: Can't kill innocent person to save yourself.

**Example**: Robber holds gun to A's head, orders A to rob bank. A robs bank. Duress defense (threat imminent, no alternative).

### Excuse Defenses (Conduct Wrongful But Not Blameworthy)

#### Insanity

**Rationale**: Punishing those who can't appreciate wrongfulness or control conduct is unjust.

**Tests**:

**1. M'Naghten Rule** (majority):
- Defendant didn't know:
  1. Nature and quality of act, OR
  2. Act was wrong
- **Cognitive test**: "Right from wrong" test
- **Example**: Delusion that victim is attacking; kills in self-defense. Didn't know act wrong.

**2. Irresistible Impulse**:
- Unable to control conduct (even if knew wrong)
- **Volitional test**
- **Criticism**: Hard to distinguish irresistible impulse from unresisted impulse

**3. Model Penal Code**:
- Lacks substantial capacity to:
  1. Appreciate criminality (wrongfulness) of conduct, OR
  2. Conform conduct to law
- **Combines cognitive + volitional**

**4. Durham "Product" Test** (NH, briefly DC):
- Crime was "product of" mental disease/defect
- **Broadest test**
- **Criticism**: Too broad; psychiatrists decide guilt

**Burden of proof**: Varies by state (defendant proves by preponderance, or prosecution disproves beyond reasonable doubt).

**Verdict**: Not guilty by reason of insanity (NGRI) → commitment to mental institution (not prison).

**Guilty but mentally ill** (some states): Verdict allowing conviction but treatment; criticized as meaningless.

**Abolition**: Four states (ID, KS, MT, UT) abolished insanity defense (allow evidence of mental illness only for mens rea).

#### Intoxication

**Voluntary intoxication**:
- **Not defense to general intent crimes**
- **Can negate specific intent** if so intoxicated couldn't form intent
- **Example**: Too drunk to form intent to kill; murder reduced to manslaughter or acquittal.

**Involuntary intoxication**: Drugged without knowledge; defense (treated like insanity).

#### Infancy

**Common law**:
- **Under 7**: No criminal capacity (irrebuttable presumption)
- **7-14**: Rebuttable presumption of no capacity
- **14+**: Adult capacity

**Modern**: Juvenile justice system (rehabilitation focus). Serious crimes may allow transfer to adult court.

#### Entrapment

**Elements**:
1. **Government inducement** to commit crime
2. **Defendant not predisposed** to commit it

**Predisposition**: Defendant already inclined (vs government created intent).

**Example**: Undercover officer repeatedly solicits crime from person with no history. Entrapment. BUT if defendant has prior convictions/showed eagerness, predisposed; no entrapment.

**Objective test** (MPC, minority): Focus on government conduct (outrageous inducement regardless of predisposition). Aimed at deterring police misconduct.

## Criminal Procedure: Constitutional Protections

### Fourth Amendment: Search and Seizure

(Covered in Constitutional Law section; key points repeated here)

**Protection**: Against unreasonable searches and seizures.

**Warrant requirement**: Generally need warrant based on probable cause.

**Exceptions**: Consent, search incident to arrest, automobile, plain view, exigent circumstances.

**Exclusionary rule**: Illegally obtained evidence inadmissible.

**Stop and frisk** (Terry v. Ohio): Reasonable suspicion of crime + danger → brief stop and pat-down for weapons.

### Fifth Amendment Protections

#### Self-Incrimination

**Privilege**: No person "shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself."

**Applies**:
- **Criminal proceedings**: Defendant can't be forced to testify
- **Any proceeding**: Can invoke if testimony might incriminate

**Miranda warnings** (Miranda v. Arizona, 1966):
- **Required** before custodial interrogation
- **Warnings**: Right to silence, statements may be used against you, right to attorney, attorney appointed if can't afford
- **Waiver**: Must be knowing, intelligent, voluntary
- **Invocation**: If invokes right to silence or attorney, questioning must stop

**Custody**: Reasonable person wouldn't feel free to leave.

**Interrogation**: Direct questioning or likely to elicit incriminating response.

**Exceptions to Miranda**:
- **Public safety**: Immediate threat (gun location)
- **Voluntary statements**: Not in response to interrogation
- **Impeachment**: Statements obtained in violation can impeach defendant's testimony (not used in prosecution's case-in-chief)

**Physical evidence**: 5th Amendment protects testimonial evidence only (not blood, DNA, handwriting).

#### Double Jeopardy

**Protection**: No person shall "be subject for the same offense to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb."

**Prohibits**:
1. **Retrial after acquittal**
2. **Retrial after conviction**
3. **Multiple punishments for same offense**

**Attachment of jeopardy**:
- **Jury trial**: When jury sworn
- **Bench trial**: When first witness sworn

**Exceptions** (retrial allowed):
- **Mistrial** for manifest necessity (hung jury, juror misconduct)
- **Defendant's motion** for mistrial (waives jeopardy)
- **Appeal by defendant**: If wins, retrial allowed (unless acquittal)

**Same offense**: Blockburger test—are elements different? If each crime requires proof of element the other doesn't, different offenses.
- **Example**: Robbery (theft + force) and murder. Different elements; can prosecute both.

**Separate sovereigns**: Federal and state governments can each prosecute for same conduct (not double jeopardy).

### Sixth Amendment: Right to Counsel and Fair Trial

#### Right to Counsel

**Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)**: Right to counsel in all felony cases.

**Argersinger v. Hamlin (1972)**: Right to counsel whenever imprisonment imposed (including misdemeanors).

**Scope**: Applies at all critical stages (arraignment, plea, trial, sentencing, first appeal).

**Effective assistance**: Counsel must be competent.
- **Strickland test**: Deficient performance + prejudice (reasonable probability of different result).

**Waiver**: Defendant can waive; must be knowing and intelligent (not recommended).

#### Other Trial Rights

**Speedy trial**: Prevent prolonged incarceration; preserve evidence; reduce anxiety.
- **Barker test**: Length of delay, reason, defendant's assertion of right, prejudice.

**Public trial**: Open courtroom (can be closed only for compelling reason).

**Impartial jury**: Drawn from fair cross-section; voir dire to exclude biased jurors.
- **Peremptory challenges**: Limited number; can strike without cause (but not based on race—Batson v. Kentucky).

**Confrontation**: Right to cross-examine witnesses (Crawford v. Washington: testimonial hearsay inadmissible unless witness unavailable and defendant had prior opportunity to cross-examine).

**Compulsory process**: Right to subpoena witnesses.

### Eighth Amendment: Cruel and Unusual Punishment

**Death penalty**: Constitutional (Gregg v. Georgia, 1976) if:
- Guided discretion (not mandatory or arbitrary)
- Proportionate to crime (not for rape, child rape, robbery)
- Not imposed on juveniles (Roper v. Simmons, 2005) or intellectually disabled (Atkins v. Virginia, 2002)

**Life without parole for juveniles**: Prohibited for non-homicide (Graham v. Florida, 2010). For homicide, individualized sentencing required (Miller v. Alabama, 2012).

**Proportionality**: Punishment grossly disproportionate to crime unconstitutional (rare).
- **Example**: Life for stealing golf clubs struck down (Solem v. Helm, 1983).
- **But**: Three-strikes laws often upheld (Ewing v. California, 2003).

## Plea Bargaining

**95%+ of convictions** result from guilty pleas (not trials).

**Process**:
1. **Prosecutor offers deal**: Plead guilty to lesser charge or recommend lighter sentence
2. **Defendant accepts**: Waives trial rights
3. **Court approval**: Judge must ensure plea knowing, voluntary, factual basis

**Types**:
- **Charge bargaining**: Plead to lesser charge (murder → manslaughter)
- **Sentence bargaining**: Plead guilty; prosecutor recommends sentence

**Benefits**:
- **Efficiency**: Courts couldn't handle all trials
- **Certainty**: Both sides avoid trial risk
- **Cooperation**: Defendants may testify against co-conspirators

**Criticisms**:
- **Coercion**: Massive sentence differential pressures innocent to plead (trial penalty)
- **Disparity**: Outcomes depend on negotiating skill, not justice
- **Circumvents jury**: Prosecutor decides guilt/punishment

**Boykin v. Alabama (1969)**: Plea must be voluntary and intelligent; judge must ensure defendant understands rights waived.

## Sentencing

### Determinate vs Indeterminate

**Determinate**: Fixed term (e.g., 10 years).

**Indeterminate**: Range (e.g., 5-15 years); parole board decides release within range.
- **Rehabilitation model**: Release when reformed
- **Criticism**: Disparity; parole boards arbitrary

### Sentencing Guidelines

**Federal Sentencing Guidelines** (1987): Grid based on offense level + criminal history.
- **Mandatory** (until US v. Booker, 2005—now advisory)
- **Reduced disparity** but criticized as too rigid

**State guidelines**: Many states adopted similar systems.

### Mandatory Minimums

**Statute requires minimum sentence** (judge has no discretion).

**Examples**:
- Federal drug minimums (5, 10, 20 years based on quantity)
- Three-strikes laws (25 years to life for third felony)

**Criticism**:
- **Inflexible**: Can't account for individual circumstances
- **Disparate impact**: Disproportionately affect minorities
- **Prosecutorial power**: Prosecutors decide what to charge (control sentence)

**Reform**: First Step Act (2018) reduced some federal mandatory minimums; allowed retroactive sentence reductions.

## Mass Incarceration

**US statistics**:
- **2.3 million incarcerated** (highest rate in world)
- **1 in 37 adults** under correctional supervision (prison, jail, parole, probation)
- **5x increase** since 1980

**Causes**:
1. **War on Drugs**: Tough enforcement; long sentences for drug offenses
2. **Mandatory minimums**: Reduced judicial discretion; increased lengths
3. **Three-strikes laws**: Life sentences for third felony (even non-violent)
4. **Truth-in-sentencing**: Require serving 85%+ of sentence (reduce parole)
5. **Pretrial detention**: Cash bail traps poor defendants; pressure to plead

**Racial disparities**:
- **Black Americans**: 5x incarceration rate of whites
- **Crack vs powder cocaine**: 100:1 sentencing disparity (crack, associated with Black users, punished far more harshly; reduced to 18:1 in 2010)
- **Policing**: Over-policing of minority neighborhoods

**Consequences**:
- **Collateral**: Disenfranchisement, employment barriers, family disruption, poverty
- **Recidivism**: 68% rearrested within 3 years (prisons don't rehabilitate)
- **Cost**: $80 billion/year

**Reform movements**:
- **Sentencing reform**: Reduce mandatory minimums, increase judicial discretion
- **Drug decriminalization**: Treat addiction as health issue
- **Bail reform**: Eliminate cash bail (pretrial detention based on risk, not wealth)
- **Police reform**: Reduce arrests for low-level offenses
- **Record expungement**: Clear records to reduce barriers

## White-Collar Crime

**Non-violent financial crimes** by businesses/professionals.

### Fraud

**Elements**:
1. **Misrepresentation** of material fact
2. **Knowledge** of falsity (scienter)
3. **Intent** to defraud
4. **Reliance** by victim
5. **Damages**

**Types**:
- **Securities fraud**: Misleading investors (Enron, Madoff)
- **Bank fraud**: False statements to obtain loans
- **Wire/mail fraud**: Use of wires or mail in fraudulent scheme (federal crime)
- **Healthcare fraud**: Billing for services not provided

**Honest services fraud** (18 USC § 1346): Deprive another of intangible right to honest services (public corruption, private-sector kickbacks).

### Insider Trading

**Trading securities** based on material non-public information in breach of duty.

**Theories**:
- **Classical**: Corporate insider trades on inside info (breach of fiduciary duty to shareholders)
- **Misappropriation**: Outsider (lawyer, journalist) trades on info misappropriated from source

**Material**: Reasonable investor would consider important.

**Example**: CEO knows earnings will miss projections. Sells stock before announcement. Insider trading.

**Tippee liability**: If insider tips outsider, tippee liable if knew tip was breach and insider benefited.

**Not insider trading**: Trading on superior analysis, public info, or mosaic theory (piecing together public info).

**SEC enforcement**: Civil penalties, disgorgement of profits.

**Criminal**: US v. O'Hagan (1997)—misappropriation theory valid; up to 20 years imprisonment.

### RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act)

**Prohibits**:
1. Using income from racketeering to acquire business
2. Acquiring business through racketeering
3. Conducting business through racketeering
4. Conspiring to do above

**Racketeering activity**: Pattern (2+ acts) of predicate crimes (murder, kidnapping, drug trafficking, fraud, etc.).

**Penalties**: Treble damages (civil), 20 years/forfeiture (criminal).

**Originally**: Target organized crime (Mafia).

**Expanded**: White-collar crime, corporate misconduct.

**Criticism**: Overbroad; ordinary fraud becomes RICO.

## Key Terms

- **Actus reus**: Guilty act (voluntary act or omission)
- **Mens rea**: Guilty mind (intent, knowledge, recklessness, negligence)
- **Concurrence**: Mens rea must accompany actus reus
- **Causation**: Act must cause result (factual + proximate)
- **Felony**: Serious crime; punishment over one year
- **Misdemeanor**: Less serious crime; punishment up to one year
- **Murder**: Unlawful killing with malice aforethought
- **Manslaughter**: Unlawful killing without malice (voluntary or involuntary)
- **Felony murder**: Death during dangerous felony
- **Larceny**: Trespassory taking of property with intent to deprive
- **Robbery**: Larceny + force/intimidation
- **Burglary**: Breaking and entering with intent to commit felony
- **Attempt**: Specific intent + substantial step toward crime
- **Conspiracy**: Agreement + intent + overt act (jurisdictions vary)
- **Solicitation**: Requesting another to commit crime
- **Self-defense**: Reasonable belief of imminent unlawful force; proportional response
- **Necessity**: Choose lesser harm to avoid greater harm
- **Duress**: Threat of imminent death/serious harm; no alternative
- **Insanity**: Lack capacity to appreciate wrongfulness or control conduct
- **Entrapment**: Government inducement of non-predisposed defendant
- **Miranda warnings**: Rights advisement before custodial interrogation
- **Double jeopardy**: Protection against retrial for same offense
- **Plea bargaining**: Negotiated guilty plea in exchange for concessions
- **Mandatory minimum**: Statute requiring minimum sentence
- **Mass incarceration**: Extremely high US imprisonment rate
- **White-collar crime**: Non-violent financial crime
- **Insider trading**: Trading on material non-public information

## Summary

Criminal law punishes conduct society deems seriously harmful, with purposes including retribution, deterrence, incapacitation, and rehabilitation. A crime requires actus reus (guilty act) and mens rea (guilty mind), with the Model Penal Code recognizing four mental states: purposely, knowingly, recklessly, and negligently.

Major crimes include homicide (murder and manslaughter, distinguished by intent and circumstances), sexual assault, theft crimes (larceny, robbery, burglary), and drug offenses. Inchoate crimes—attempt, conspiracy, and solicitation—punish incomplete crimes.

Defenses divide into justifications (self-defense, necessity) where conduct is not wrongful, and excuses (insanity, duress, intoxication) where the defendant is not blameworthy. The insanity defense remains controversial, with jurisdictions using different tests (M'Naghten, MPC, irresistible impulse).

Constitutional criminal procedure protects defendants through the Fourth Amendment (search and seizure), Fifth Amendment (self-incrimination, double jeopardy), Sixth Amendment (counsel, jury trial, confrontation), and Eighth Amendment (cruel and unusual punishment). Miranda warnings safeguard against coerced confessions. The exclusionary rule deters illegal searches by making evidence inadmissible.

In practice, over 95% of convictions result from plea bargaining rather than trials, raising concerns about coercion and circumventing juries. Sentencing has shifted toward determinate guidelines and mandatory minimums, contributing to mass incarceration. The United States imprisons 2.3 million people—the highest rate globally—with severe racial disparities. Reform movements seek to reduce sentences, eliminate cash bail, and treat drug addiction as a health issue.

White-collar crime—including fraud, insider trading, and RICO violations—targets financial misconduct, though enforcement and punishment typically remain lighter than for street crime, highlighting ongoing debates about equal justice under law.
