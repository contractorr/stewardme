# Regulatory & Administrative Law

## The Administrative State

**Administrative law**: Rules governing government agencies and their exercise of delegated power.

**Regulatory law**: Substantive rules agencies create and enforce.

### What Are Administrative Agencies?

**Definition**: Government bodies created by legislature to administer/implement specific laws.

**Examples**: SEC (securities), EPA (environment), FDA (food/drugs), FTC (consumer protection), FCC (communications), OSHA (workplace safety), CFPB (consumer finance).

**Three functions** (combine legislative, executive, judicial):
1. **Rulemaking**: Create regulations (legislative function)
2. **Enforcement**: Investigate, prosecute violations (executive function)
3. **Adjudication**: Resolve disputes (judicial function)

**Constitutionality concerns**: Agencies blend powers separated in Constitution. But upheld as practical necessity.

### Why Agencies Exist

**Expertise**: Complex technical issues (drug safety, pollution, financial markets) require specialized knowledge.

**Flexibility**: Agencies can respond faster than Congress; adjust rules as circumstances change.

**Efficiency**: Congress lacks time/resources to address every detail; delegates to agencies.

**Political insulation**: Independent agencies (multi-member commissions, staggered terms, for-cause removal) somewhat shielded from presidential control.

**Criticism (from both left and right)**:
- **Undemocratic**: Unelected bureaucrats make law
- **Capture**: Agencies serve regulated industry, not public (revolving door)
- **Overreach**: Exceed statutory authority
- **Inefficiency**: Red tape, slow decision-making

## Major Regulatory Agencies

### Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)

**Created**: 1934 (after 1929 crash, Great Depression)

**Mission**: Protect investors; maintain fair, orderly, efficient markets; facilitate capital formation.

**Key statutes**:
- **Securities Act of 1933**: New securities offerings must be registered (unless exempt); disclosure requirements
- **Securities Exchange Act of 1934**: Regulates secondary trading, brokers, exchanges; requires periodic reporting (10-K, 10-Q)
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002)**: Post-Enron reforms (CEO/CFO certifications, auditor independence, whistleblower protection)
- **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)**: Post-2008 financial crisis (Volcker Rule, derivatives regulation, CFPB creation)

**Structure**: 5 commissioners (bipartisan; staggered terms); Chair appointed by President.

**Enforcement**: Civil penalties, disgorgement, injunctions. Refers criminal cases to DOJ.

### Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)

**Created**: 1970 (Nixon administration)

**Mission**: Protect human health and environment.

**Key statutes**:
- **Clean Air Act** (CAA): Regulates air pollution (NAAQS for criteria pollutants; permits for new sources)
- **Clean Water Act** (CWA): Regulates water pollution (NPDES permits for point sources)
- **Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act** (CERCLA/Superfund): Hazardous waste cleanup; strict liability for polluters
- **Resource Conservation and Recovery Act** (RCRA): Cradle-to-grave tracking of hazardous waste
- **Toxic Substances Control Act** (TSCA): Regulates chemicals
- **National Environmental Policy Act** (NEPA): Requires Environmental Impact Statements (EIS) for major federal actions

**Enforcement**: Administrative penalties, civil/criminal prosecution (for knowing violations).

**Controversies**:
- **Cost-benefit analysis**: Should EPA weigh economic costs vs environmental benefits? (Executive orders require, but statutes vary)
- **Federalism**: Tension between federal standards and state implementation
- **Climate change**: EPA authority under CAA to regulate greenhouse gases (Massachusetts v. EPA, 2007—yes; later battles over Clean Power Plan)

### Food and Drug Administration (FDA)

**Created**: 1906 (Pure Food and Drug Act); reorganized 1930s

**Mission**: Protect public health by ensuring safety/efficacy of drugs, biological products, medical devices, food, cosmetics, tobacco.

**Key powers**:

**Drug approval**: Manufacturers must prove safety and efficacy through clinical trials (Phase I-III) before marketing.
- **Process**: Investigational New Drug (IND) application → clinical trials → New Drug Application (NDA) → FDA review/approval.
- **Timeline**: Often 10+ years, $1+ billion cost.

**Food safety**: Inspections, recalls, labeling requirements.

**Medical devices**: Risk-based classification (Class I-III); pre-market approval for high-risk.

**Tobacco**: Regulate marketing (not ban) to reduce youth use.

**Controversies**:
- **Drug lag**: Slow approval process delays access to treatments (vs rushing approval risks harm)
- **Off-label use**: Doctors can prescribe approved drugs for unapproved uses; FDA can't regulate practice of medicine
- **Right to try**: Terminal patients access experimental drugs (2018 law expanded access)

### Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

**Created**: 1914

**Mission**: Protect consumers from unfair/deceptive practices; promote competition.

**Two main functions**:

**1. Consumer protection**: Enforce against fraud, deception, unfairness.
- **Examples**: False advertising, data breaches, telemarketing scams, identity theft
- **FTC Act § 5**: "Unfair or deceptive acts or practices" unlawful

**2. Antitrust**: Enforce antitrust laws (shared with DOJ).
- **Sherman Act, Clayton Act, FTC Act**
- **Merger review**: Hart-Scott-Rodino Act requires pre-merger notification for large deals

**Enforcement**: Cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties, consumer redress.

**Structure**: 5 commissioners (bipartisan).

### Federal Communications Commission (FCC)

**Created**: 1934

**Mission**: Regulate interstate/international communications (radio, TV, wire, satellite, cable, broadband).

**Key functions**:

**Licensing**: Broadcast licenses (radio/TV stations).

**Spectrum allocation**: Assign radio frequency spectrum (auctions for wireless).

**Net neutrality**: Should ISPs treat all internet traffic equally? (FCC imposed 2015, repealed 2017, reinstated 2024).

**Universal service**: Ensure affordable access (subsidies for rural, low-income).

**Decency**: Regulate indecent content on broadcast (not cable/internet) during hours children likely watching.

**Controversies**:
- **Net neutrality**: ISPs vs content providers vs consumer advocates
- **Media ownership**: Limits to prevent concentration (e.g., newspaper-broadcast cross-ownership)

### Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)

**Created**: 1970

**Mission**: Ensure safe and healthful working conditions.

**Key powers**:

**Standards**: Set workplace safety standards (PPE, machine guarding, chemical exposure limits).

**Inspections**: Unannounced inspections of workplaces (with warrant or consent).

**Enforcement**: Citations, penalties for violations.

**Employer duties**:
- **General duty clause**: Provide workplace free from recognized hazards
- **Specific standards**: Comply with OSHA regulations

**Criticism**:
- **Overreach**: Too burdensome for small businesses
- **Underenforcement**: Too few inspectors (can't inspect all workplaces regularly)

### Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

**Created**: 2010 (Dodd-Frank Act)

**Mission**: Protect consumers in financial products/services (mortgages, credit cards, student loans, payday loans).

**Key powers**:

**Rulemaking**: Implement consumer financial laws.

**Supervision**: Examine financial institutions for compliance.

**Enforcement**: Sue for violations; impose penalties.

**Examples**: Restrictions on payday lenders, mortgage underwriting standards, credit card fee disclosures.

**Controversy**:
- **Structure**: Single director removable only for cause; funded by Federal Reserve (not congressional appropriations). Challenges to constitutionality (Seila Law v. CFPB, 2020—single-director for-cause removal unconstitutional; fixed by making removable at will).

## How Regulations Are Made: Rulemaking

**Administrative Procedure Act (APA)** (1946): Governs agency rulemaking and adjudication.

### Notice-and-Comment Rulemaking (Informal Rulemaking)

**Standard process** for most regulations:

**1. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)**:
- Published in Federal Register
- Describes proposed rule, legal authority, rationale
- Invites public comment (typically 30-60 days)

**2. Public comment period**:
- Anyone can submit comments (industry, advocacy groups, individuals)
- Agency must consider comments

**3. Final rule**:
- Agency publishes final rule in Federal Register
- Responds to major comments
- Rule effective typically 30 days after publication

**4. Judicial review**:
- Parties can challenge rule in court (arbitrary and capricious standard)

**Example**: EPA proposes emission standards for power plants. Publishes NPRM. Industry submits comments (too costly). Environmental groups submit comments (too weak). EPA considers, issues final rule. Industry sues; court reviews.

### Formal Rulemaking (Rare)

**Statute requires**: Trial-like hearing with testimony, cross-examination.

**Slow, expensive**: Rarely used (statutes avoid triggering).

### Exemptions from Notice-and-Comment

**Interpretive rules**: Explain existing regulations (don't create new obligations).

**Policy statements**: Announce agency priorities (not binding).

**Procedural rules**: Internal agency operations.

**Good cause**: Emergency or notice/comment impracticable (rare; courts scrutinize).

## Judicial Review of Agency Action

**APA § 706**: Reviewing court shall set aside agency action if:
1. **Arbitrary, capricious** (abuse of discretion)
2. **Contrary to law**
3. **Unconstitutional**
4. **Procedural defect**
5. **Unsupported by substantial evidence** (formal proceedings)
6. **Unwarranted by facts** (de novo review in certain contexts)

### Arbitrary and Capricious Standard

**Most common**: Applies to informal rulemaking, policy decisions.

**Test** (Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Ass'n v. State Farm, 1983):
- Did agency examine relevant data?
- Did agency articulate satisfactory explanation?
- Did agency consider important aspect of problem?
- Is explanation contrary to evidence?

**Deferential but not toothless**: Agency must show reasoned decision-making.

**Example**: NHTSA rescinded passive restraint (airbag) requirement. Court struck down: failed to consider airbags as alternative to automatic seatbelts.

### Chevron Deference (Now Overruled)

**Chevron USA v. NRDC (1984)**: Two-step test for reviewing agency's interpretation of statute:
1. **Step 1**: Is statute clear? If yes, follow clear meaning.
2. **Step 2**: If ambiguous, is agency's interpretation reasonable? If yes, defer.

**Rationale**: Congress delegated gap-filling to agency (expertise, political accountability).

**Criticism**:
- Transfers power to executive from courts/Congress
- Agencies change interpretations with each administration (instability)

**Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024)**: Supreme Court **overruled Chevron**.
- **Holding**: Courts must exercise independent judgment in deciding statutory meaning (no deference).
- **Reasoning**: APA § 706 requires courts to "decide all relevant questions of law"; deference inconsistent.
- **Impact**: Agencies lose powerful tool; more regulations struck down; increased litigation.

### Other Deference Doctrines (Post-Loper Bright)

**Skidmore deference**: Agency interpretation entitled to respect based on "power to persuade" (expertise, consistency, formality). Not binding. (Skidmore v. Swift, 1944).

**Auer deference**: Agency's interpretation of own regulations. (Uncertain post-Loper Bright; may be limited or eliminated).

## Cost-Benefit Analysis in Regulation

**Should agencies weigh costs against benefits?**

**Executive orders** (Reagan, Clinton, Obama, Trump, Biden): Require regulatory impact analysis for major rules (economically significant).

**OMB (Office of Management and Budget)**: Reviews proposed regulations; can delay/reject if costs exceed benefits.

**Statutory variation**:
- **Some statutes require**: TSCA (must consider costs)
- **Some prohibit**: Clean Air Act (NAAQS set without regard to costs—Whitman v. American Trucking, 2001)
- **Some silent**: Ambiguous

**Arguments for**:
- **Efficiency**: Maximize social welfare; avoid wasteful regulations
- **Transparency**: Forces agencies to articulate tradeoffs
- **Accountability**: Political oversight (OMB/President)

**Arguments against**:
- **Undervalues lives**: Monetizing human life/health unseemly (though agencies use VSL—value of statistical life, ~$10 million)
- **Industry bias**: Costs easier to quantify than benefits (environmental, health); tilts toward deregulation
- **Paralysis**: Slows rulemaking

**Discount rate debate**: Should future benefits (lives saved in 50 years) be discounted to present value? Higher rate makes long-term environmental benefits (climate change) seem negligible.

## Types of Regulation

### Command-and-Control Regulation

**Government mandates** specific conduct (technology, practices).

**Examples**:
- **Emission standards**: Power plants must install scrubbers
- **Speed limits**: Can't exceed 65 mph
- **Food safety**: Restaurants must maintain certain temperatures

**Pros**:
- Clear, enforceable
- Certainty of outcome

**Cons**:
- Inflexible (one-size-fits-all)
- Ignores cost differences across regulated entities
- Stifles innovation (compliance, not improvement)

### Market-Based Regulation (Economic Incentives)

**Harness market forces** to achieve goals at lower cost.

**Types**:

**1. Cap-and-trade (emissions trading)**:
- **Cap**: Total allowable pollution set
- **Permits**: Allocated/auctioned to firms; each permit allows X tons of pollution
- **Trade**: Firms can buy/sell permits
- **Example**: SO₂ (acid rain) trading program (1990 CAA); highly successful (exceeded targets, lower than projected cost)
- **Efficiency**: Firms with low abatement costs reduce more; firms with high costs buy permits (minimizes total cost)

**2. Pigouvian taxes**: Tax equal to external cost (pollution, carbon).
- **Carbon tax**: Tax per ton CO₂; incentivizes reduction
- **Example**: British Columbia carbon tax (revenue-neutral; reduce income taxes)

**3. Subsidies**: Pay for desired behavior.
- **Renewable energy tax credits**
- **Criticism**: Expensive; may subsidize activity that would occur anyway

**Comparison**:

| Feature | Command-and-Control | Cap-and-Trade | Tax |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|-----|
| Certainty of outcome | High | High (total cap) | Low (depends on response) |
| Cost efficiency | Low | High | High |
| Flexibility | Low | High | High |
| Political feasibility | Often easier (no "tax") | Moderate | Difficult (tax label) |

## Antitrust Law

**Goal**: Promote competition; prevent monopolies, cartels, anticompetitive conduct.

**Benefits of competition**: Lower prices, higher quality, innovation, consumer choice.

### Sherman Act (1890)

**Section 1**: "Every contract, combination... or conspiracy, in restraint of trade" unlawful.
- **Requires agreement** (not unilateral conduct)
- **Examples**: Price-fixing, market allocation, bid-rigging

**Section 2**: "Every person who shall monopolize, or attempt to monopolize" unlawful.
- **Monopolization**: (1) Monopoly power in relevant market + (2) willful acquisition/maintenance (exclusionary conduct)
- **Monopoly power**: Ability to control prices or exclude competition
- **Exclusionary conduct**: Anticompetitive (not competition on the merits)

### Clayton Act (1914)

**Addresses gaps** in Sherman Act.

**Section 2**: Price discrimination (Robinson-Patman Act amendment).

**Section 3**: Exclusive dealing, tying (if substantially lessen competition).

**Section 7**: Mergers/acquisitions that substantially lessen competition.

**Section 8**: Interlocking directorates (same person on boards of competing firms).

### FTC Act (1914)

**Section 5**: "Unfair methods of competition" unlawful.
- **Broader than Sherman Act**: Covers incipient violations, practices not yet anticompetitive

### Per Se vs Rule of Reason

**Per se illegal**: Conduct so harmful, no inquiry into effects needed (conclusive presumption).
- **Examples**: Horizontal price-fixing, market allocation, bid-rigging
- **Rationale**: No plausible procompetitive justification; easy to identify

**Rule of reason**: Weigh procompetitive justifications vs anticompetitive effects.
- **Applies to**: Most conduct (vertical restraints, mergers, monopolization)
- **Analysis**: Define market, assess market power, evaluate conduct's effects, consider justifications

**Quick look**: Intermediate—conduct likely anticompetitive but not per se; abbreviated rule of reason.

### Key Antitrust Concepts

**Horizontal vs vertical**:
- **Horizontal**: Between competitors (same level of distribution). Higher risk.
- **Vertical**: Between different levels (manufacturer-distributor). Often procompetitive.

**Relevant market**: Product market (substitutes) + geographic market (where sold).
- **Example**: "Soft drinks in US" or "premium streaming video services in North America"

**Market power**: Ability to raise prices above competitive level without losing business.

**Market concentration**: HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index)—sum of squared market shares.
- **Unconcentrated**: HHI < 1,500
- **Moderately concentrated**: 1,500-2,500
- **Highly concentrated**: > 2,500

### Merger Review

**Hart-Scott-Rodino Act** (1976): Pre-merger notification to FTC/DOJ for large transactions (thresholds adjusted annually; ~$100 million+).

**Waiting period**: 30 days (often extended); agencies review competitiveness.

**Challenge**: If substantially lessens competition.
- **Horizontal merger** (competitors): Presumptively anticompetitive if significantly increases concentration.
- **Vertical merger** (different levels): Generally OK unless forecloses rivals.
- **Conglomerate merger**: Usually OK.

**Defenses**:
- **Efficiencies**: Merger generates cost savings benefiting consumers
- **Failing firm**: Target would exit anyway; merger preserves assets

**Remedies**:
- **Block**: Injunction preventing merger
- **Divestitures**: Sell overlapping business units (consent decree)

**Recent enforcement**: FTC/DOJ challenging more mergers (tech, healthcare, labor markets).

### Tech Antitrust

**Big Tech scrutiny**: Google, Amazon, Facebook (Meta), Apple.

**Allegations**:
- **Monopolization**: Abuse of dominant position (Google search, Amazon marketplace, Apple App Store)
- **Self-preferencing**: Favor own products in search results, app stores
- **Exclusionary contracts**: Google paying Apple to be default search
- **Killer acquisitions**: Buying nascent competitors (Facebook-Instagram, Facebook-WhatsApp)

**US v. Google (2023-ongoing)**: DOJ alleges Google monopolized search via exclusive deals. Trial ongoing.

**Epic v. Apple (2021)**: App Store restrictions anticompetitive. Mixed verdict.

**Proposed remedies**:
- **Structural**: Break up companies
- **Behavioral**: Prohibit self-preferencing, require interoperability
- **Legislation**: Multiple bills (not yet passed)

## Securities Regulation

**Goals**: Investor protection, market integrity, capital formation.

### Securities Act of 1933 (Issuance)

**Requires**: New securities offerings registered with SEC (unless exempt).

**Registration statement** (Form S-1): Detailed disclosure (business, risks, financials, use of proceeds).

**Prospectus**: Disclosure document given to investors.

**Liability** (§ 11): Issuers, underwriters, directors, officers liable for material misstatements/omissions (defendants can assert due diligence defense).

**Exemptions** (no registration required):
- **Private placements** (Reg D): Sales to sophisticated investors (accredited investors)
- **Small offerings** (Reg A, Reg CF): Crowdfunding
- **Intrastate**: Sales only within one state

### Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (Trading)

**Applies to**: Publicly traded companies, brokers, exchanges.

**Periodic reporting**: Public companies must file:
- **10-K**: Annual report (audited financials)
- **10-Q**: Quarterly report
- **8-K**: Current report (material events)
- **Proxy statements**: For shareholder votes

**Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5**: Prohibit fraud in connection with securities trading.
- **Elements**: Material misstatement/omission, scienter (intent), reliance, causation, damages
- **Private right of action**: Investors can sue
- **Broad application**: Covers insiders, outsiders, any security

**Section 16(b)**: Short-swing profits (insiders profit from trades within 6 months) disgorged.

**Section 13(d)**: Beneficial owners of 5%+ must disclose (within 10 days).

### Insider Trading (Rule 10b-5)

**Theories**:

**Classical theory**: Corporate insider trades on material non-public info in breach of fiduciary duty to shareholders.

**Misappropriation theory**: Outsider trades on info misappropriated from source in breach of duty (US v. O'Hagan, 1997).
- **Example**: Lawyer learns of client's acquisition plan; trades in target's stock. Breaches duty to client (law firm).

**Tippee liability**: Tippee (person receiving tip) liable if:
1. Tipper breached duty
2. Tipper received personal benefit (direct or indirect—gift to family, reputational benefit)
3. Tippee knew or should have known of breach

**Dirks v. SEC (1983)**: Tippee liable only if tipper benefited. (Whistleblowing tips to expose fraud OK if no personal benefit.)

**Material**: Reasonable investor would consider important in making decision (TSC Industries).

**Non-public**: Not generally available (but once disclosed publicly, can trade after reasonable time for dissemination).

**Defenses**:
- **Rule 10b5-1 plan**: Pre-scheduled trading plan (adopted when no inside info)
- **No scienter**: Didn't know info material or non-public (hard to prove)

**Penalties**: SEC civil penalties, disgorgement, trading bans; criminal (up to 20 years, $5 million fine).

## Financial Regulation

### Banking Regulation

**Goals**: Safety and soundness (prevent bank failures); consumer protection; financial stability.

**Regulators**:
- **Federal Reserve**: Monetary policy, bank holding companies, systemic risk
- **OCC** (Office of Comptroller of Currency): National banks
- **FDIC**: Deposit insurance, failed bank resolution
- **CFPB**: Consumer financial products

**Key statutes/events**:

**Glass-Steagall Act (1933)**: Separated commercial banking (deposits/loans) from investment banking (securities underwriting).
- **Rationale**: Conflicts of interest, risk-taking caused Great Depression bank failures
- **Repealed (1999)**: Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act allowed affiliations

**Dodd-Frank Act (2010)**: Post-2008 financial crisis reforms.
- **Volcker Rule**: Banks can't engage in proprietary trading (trading for own profit, not clients)
- **Resolution authority**: FDIC can wind down failing systemically important institutions
- **Capital requirements**: Higher capital cushions (Basel III)
- **Stress tests**: Large banks tested for resilience to economic shocks
- **Derivatives**: Centralized clearing, margin requirements
- **CFPB**: New consumer protection agency

**Basel Accords**: International capital standards (Basel I, II, III).
- **Capital ratios**: Banks must hold capital (equity) as percentage of risk-weighted assets
- **Tier 1 capital**: Common equity, retained earnings (highest quality)

**Too big to fail**: Largest banks pose systemic risk; government bailouts incentivize excessive risk-taking.
- **Dodd-Frank response**: Orderly liquidation authority, living wills, higher capital requirements
- **Criticism**: Largest banks still pose risk; complexity makes resolution difficult

### Fiduciary Duties

**Fiduciary**: Person holding assets/power for another; must act in beneficiary's best interest.

**Duty of loyalty**: No self-dealing; disclose conflicts.

**Duty of care**: Act with skill, prudence, diligence.

**Contexts**:
- **Corporate directors/officers**: To shareholders (though business judgment rule gives deference)
- **Investment advisers**: To clients (SEC-registered advisers are fiduciaries)
- **Broker-dealers**: Traditionally, lower standard (suitability, not best interest); Reg BI (2019) requires acting in best interest (but not full fiduciary)
- **Trustees**: To beneficiaries (ERISA for pension plans)

## Environmental Regulation

### Clean Air Act (CAA)

**National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)**: EPA sets standards for criteria pollutants (PM, ozone, CO, NO₂, SO₂, lead) based on health.

**State Implementation Plans (SIPs)**: States develop plans to meet NAAQS; EPA approves.

**New Source Performance Standards (NSPS)**: Technology-based standards for new sources.

**Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD)**: Limits pollution increases in clean air areas.

**Hazardous Air Pollutants (HAPs)**: Maximum Achievable Control Technology (MACT) for 187 listed toxics.

**Mobile sources**: Emission standards for vehicles.

**Title IV (Acid Rain)**: SO₂ cap-and-trade (successful).

**Greenhouse gases**: Massachusetts v. EPA (2007)—EPA has authority to regulate GHGs if endangerment finding made. Obama EPA issued Clean Power Plan (CO₂ from power plants); Trump repealed; Biden issued new rules.

**West Virginia v. EPA (2022)**: Major questions doctrine limits EPA authority; generation shifting (forcing coal to gas/renewables) exceeds statutory authority.

### Clean Water Act (CWA)

**NPDES permits**: Point sources (pipes, ditches) discharging pollutants into navigable waters need permits (set technology-based and water quality-based limits).

**Dredge and fill**: Section 404 permits for filling wetlands (Army Corps of Engineers).

**WOTUS** (Waters of the US): Jurisdictional disputes over what qualifies as "navigable waters."
- **Sackett v. EPA (2023)**: Narrowed WOTUS to traditional navigable waters, relatively permanent waters, wetlands with continuous surface connection.

**Nonpoint source pollution** (runoff from farms, streets): Not directly regulated; states develop plans.

### NEPA (National Environmental Policy Act)

**Requires**: Federal agencies prepare Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) for "major federal actions significantly affecting the environment."

**Purpose**: Informed decision-making (not mandate particular outcome; procedural).

**Process**:
1. **Categorical exclusion**: Routine actions exempt
2. **Environmental Assessment** (EA): Preliminary analysis; if no significant impact, Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)
3. **EIS**: Detailed analysis (alternatives, impacts, mitigation)

**Public comment**: Required; agencies must respond.

**Judicial review**: Courts ensure procedural compliance (did agency take "hard look"?).

**Criticism**:
- **Delays**: EIS process slow (years)
- **Ineffective**: Rarely changes decisions (agencies rationalize preferences)

## Regulatory Capture and Public Choice Theory

### Regulatory Capture

**Definition**: Agency serves regulated industry's interests (not public's).

**Mechanisms**:
- **Revolving door**: Regulators move to industry (and vice versa); incentivizes favorable treatment
- **Industry expertise**: Regulated firms have information asymmetry; shape rules
- **Lobbying**: Industry spends vastly more than public interest groups
- **Concentrated benefits, diffuse costs**: Industry gains much from favorable regulation; each citizen loses little (rational ignorance)

**Examples alleged**:
- **FAA**: Criticized for deferring to Boeing (737 MAX crashes)
- **SEC**: Accused of weak enforcement against Wall Street
- **FDA**: "Cozy" relationship with pharma

**Evidence mixed**: Some agencies captured; others resist (EPA often opposes industry).

### Public Choice Theory

**Applies economics to politics**: Legislators, bureaucrats, voters act in self-interest (not public interest).

**Implications**:
- **Legislators**: Maximize re-election (cater to interest groups providing campaign funds)
- **Bureaucrats**: Maximize budget, power, job security
- **Voters**: Rationally ignorant (cost of information > benefit of informed vote)

**Prediction**: Regulation serves well-organized interest groups (industry, unions) at expense of diffuse public.

**Example**: Sugar tariffs benefit small number of sugar producers; cost spread across millions of consumers (each pays little more for candy; producers lobby intensely).

**Counter**: Assumes pure self-interest; ignores ideology, public spirit, reputation.

## Key Regulatory Concepts

**Negative externalities**: Costs imposed on third parties (pollution, noise). Justifies regulation (internalize costs).

**Information asymmetry**: One party knows more (sellers vs buyers in securities). Justifies disclosure requirements.

**Public goods**: Non-excludable, non-rivalrous (clean air, national defense). Underprovided by market; justifies government provision/regulation.

**Moral hazard**: Insurance/protection reduces incentives to avoid risk (deposit insurance → risky lending). Justifies regulation (capital requirements, supervision).

**Race to the bottom**: Jurisdictions compete by lowering standards (environmental, labor) to attract business. Justifies federal minimum standards.

**Precautionary principle**: Regulate potential harms even with scientific uncertainty. (Criticism: paralyzes innovation; everything poses hypothetical risk.)

## Key Terms

- **Administrative agency**: Government body implementing/enforcing laws
- **Rulemaking**: Agency process of creating regulations
- **Notice-and-comment**: Standard rulemaking process (NPRM, comment, final rule)
- **APA** (Administrative Procedure Act): Governs agency procedures
- **Arbitrary and capricious**: Standard for reviewing agency decisions
- **Chevron deference**: Courts defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes (overruled 2024)
- **Command-and-control**: Government mandates specific conduct
- **Cap-and-trade**: Market-based regulation (emission permits tradable)
- **Pigouvian tax**: Tax equal to external cost
- **Antitrust**: Laws promoting competition
- **Sherman Act**: Prohibits restraints of trade, monopolization
- **Per se illegal**: Conduct presumed anticompetitive (no further inquiry)
- **Rule of reason**: Weigh procompetitive vs anticompetitive effects
- **Horizontal agreement**: Between competitors
- **Vertical agreement**: Between different distribution levels
- **Market power**: Ability to raise prices above competitive level
- **Merger**: Combination of firms (may lessen competition)
- **Insider trading**: Trading on material non-public information in breach of duty
- **Fiduciary**: Person acting for another; must act in their best interest
- **NAAQS**: National Ambient Air Quality Standards (health-based)
- **NPDES**: National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (permits for water pollution)
- **EIS** (Environmental Impact Statement): Detailed environmental analysis (NEPA)
- **Regulatory capture**: Agency serves regulated industry (not public)

## Summary

The administrative state consists of agencies combining legislative (rulemaking), executive (enforcement), and judicial (adjudication) functions. Major agencies include the SEC (securities), EPA (environment), FDA (food/drugs), FTC (consumer protection/antitrust), FCC (communications), OSHA (workplace safety), and CFPB (consumer finance).

Agencies create regulations through notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act, publishing proposed rules, receiving public input, and issuing final rules subject to judicial review. Courts review agency actions for arbitrary and capricious decision-making. The Supreme Court's 2024 Loper Bright decision overruled Chevron deference, requiring courts to independently interpret statutes rather than deferring to agencies.

Cost-benefit analysis in regulation weighs economic costs against benefits, mandated by executive orders but with mixed statutory requirements. Regulation takes forms including command-and-control (mandating conduct) and market-based approaches (cap-and-trade, Pigouvian taxes) that harness economic incentives for greater efficiency.

Antitrust law, enforced through the Sherman Act, Clayton Act, and FTC Act, prevents monopolization, cartels, and anticompetitive conduct. Courts apply per se illegality to horizontal price-fixing and the rule of reason to most other conduct. Merger review screens acquisitions for anticompetitive effects, with recent intensified enforcement against Big Tech.

Securities regulation requires disclosure for new offerings (1933 Act) and ongoing reporting for public companies (1934 Act). Insider trading prohibits using material non-public information in breach of duty, enforced through substantial civil and criminal penalties. Financial regulation aims to prevent bank failures and systemic crises, with Dodd-Frank imposing heightened capital requirements, stress tests, and the Volcker Rule after the 2008 crisis.

Environmental regulation includes the Clean Air Act's health-based air quality standards, the Clean Water Act's discharge permits, and NEPA's environmental impact statements for federal actions. Debates continue over regulatory stringency, economic costs, and agency authority, particularly regarding climate change.

Regulatory capture—when agencies serve industry interests—and public choice theory's insight that concentrated interest groups often prevail over diffuse publics raise ongoing concerns about whether regulation serves the public good or entrenches incumbent advantages.
