# Institutions & Organizations

## Overview

Institutions are the rules; organizations are the players. Institutions shape incentives; organizations respond to them. Understanding how institutions emerge, function, and constrain organizations is central to explaining why some societies prosper and others stagnate.

This distinction is critical: The United States Constitution is an institution — it establishes rules for governance. Congress, the Supreme Court, and the Executive branch are organizations operating within those rules. FIFA establishes rules for soccer; Barcelona and Manchester United are organizations competing under those rules. Markets are institutions; firms are organizations. Property rights are institutions; landlords and tenants are organizations.

Institutions solve coordination and commitment problems. Without property rights, you won't invest in improvements (no commitment). Without contracts, you can't coordinate complex production. Without money, exchange requires double coincidence of wants. Institutions enable cooperation among strangers, making modern societies possible.

## Formal vs Informal Institutions

### Formal Institutions

Codified rules enforced by official bodies:
- **Constitutions**: Fundamental structure, rights, and powers
- **Legal systems**: Property rights, contract enforcement, criminal law, torts
- **Regulatory frameworks**: Environmental, financial, labor, health and safety regulations
- **Organizational rules**: Corporate bylaws, university policies, association rules

**Characteristics**:
- Written and publicly available
- Enforced by designated authorities (courts, police, regulators)
- Violation triggers formal sanctions (fines, imprisonment, dissolution)
- Can be changed through specified procedures (legislation, amendment, court rulings)

**Examples**:
- **Property rights**: US law specifies ownership, transfer, and dispute resolution for land, patents, copyrights
- **Corporate law**: Delaware General Corporation Law governs most US corporations, specifying fiduciary duties, shareholder rights, merger procedures
- **Labor law**: Fair Labor Standards Act establishes minimum wage, overtime, child labor prohibitions
- **Environmental law**: Clean Air Act sets emissions standards enforced by EPA

**Variation**:
Legal traditions differ globally:
- **Common law** (UK, US, former British colonies): Judge-made law through precedent, adversarial system
- **Civil law** (Continental Europe, Latin America): Codified statutes, inquisitorial system
- **Religious law** (Iran, Saudi Arabia): Sharia law derived from religious texts

Research shows common law systems correlate with stronger property rights, more developed financial markets, and higher economic growth — but causality is debated (legal origins or other factors?).

### Informal Institutions

Unwritten norms enforced by social mechanisms:
- **Trust**: Generalized trust in strangers vs particularized trust in kin/co-ethnics
- **Norms of reciprocity**: Gift-giving, favors, mutual aid, tit-for-tat
- **Social sanctions**: Gossip, ostracism, reputation damage, shaming
- **Cultural values**: Work ethic, attitudes toward authority, corruption tolerance, honor codes

**Characteristics**:
- Not written, transmitted through socialization
- Enforced through reputation, social pressure, internalized guilt/shame
- Violation triggers informal sanctions (exclusion, reputation loss, social disapproval)
- Change slowly through cultural evolution, not legislative fiat

**Examples**:
- **Business norms**: Handshake deals in diamond industry (trust without contracts)
- **Academic norms**: Peer review, citation practices, authorship conventions (enforced by reputation, not law)
- **Tipping**: 15-20% in US restaurants (enforced by social pressure, not law; varies by country)
- **Queuing**: Orderly lines in UK (strong norm), chaotic crowds elsewhere

**Measurement**:
World Values Survey measures trust: "Generally speaking, would you say that most people can be trusted?"
- **High trust**: Scandinavia (~65% yes), Netherlands (~60%)
- **Medium trust**: US (~35%), UK (~30%)
- **Low trust**: Brazil (~7%), Colombia (~5%)

Trust correlates with economic development, effective government, and social cooperation. High-trust societies can sustain complex institutions; low-trust societies struggle with collective action.

### Interaction: Formal and Informal

When formal and informal institutions conflict, informal often wins:

**Example: Anti-corruption laws**
Many countries have strong anti-corruption laws but high corruption. Why? Informal norms accept gift-giving, nepotism, and patronage as normal. Formal law says corruption is illegal; informal culture says it's how things work. Informal wins.

**Example: Prohibition (US, 1920-1933)**
18th Amendment banned alcohol. Formal law criminalized drinking. But informal norms (social drinking, celebratory toasts) persisted. Result: massive bootlegging, organized crime, widespread law-breaking. Formal law failed because informal institutions didn't support it.

**Example: Post-Soviet Russia**
Russia inherited formal democratic institutions (constitution, elections, multiparty system) but lacked informal norms of rule-following, transparency, and trust. Without supporting informal institutions, formal democracy became "managed democracy" — elections held but outcomes predetermined, opposition permitted but harassed.

When formal and informal align, institutions are stable and effective:
- **Singapore**: Formal anti-corruption laws + informal norms against corruption (+ harsh enforcement) = low corruption
- **Scandinavian welfare states**: Formal high taxes + informal norms of tax compliance + trust in government = sustainable redistribution

## North's Institutional Framework

Douglass North (Nobel Prize, 1993): institutions are "the rules of the game" that structure incentives.

**Key insights**:

1. **Uncertainty reduction**: Institutions provide stable structure to human interaction. Without property rights, you don't know if you'll keep what you produce. Without contracts, you can't plan complex transactions. Institutions reduce uncertainty, enabling long-term investment and cooperation.

2. **Transaction costs**: All economic activity involves transaction costs — searching for partners, negotiating terms, writing contracts, monitoring performance, enforcing agreements. Institutions lower transaction costs. Clear property rights reduce negotiation costs. Trusted intermediaries (banks, escrow) reduce monitoring costs. Courts reduce enforcement costs.

3. **Path dependence**: Institutions are path-dependent — early choices constrain later options. Sunk costs, learning effects, coordination effects, and adaptive expectations create lock-in. You can't just transplant Denmark's institutions to Somalia — the historical path matters.

4. **Incremental change**: Institutional change is typically incremental, not revolutionary. New institutions layer onto old ones. Revolutionary change is rare (post-war reconstruction, revolutions, state collapse).

**Hierarchy of institutions** (Oliver Williamson):

| Level | Type | Change Rate | Examples | Mechanism |
|-------|------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **1: Embeddedness** | Culture, religion, informal norms | Centuries to millennia | Trust, honor, social norms | Evolutionary, generational |
| **2: Institutional environment** | Formal rules | Decades to centuries | Constitutions, property rights, rule of law | Political change, revolution |
| **3: Governance** | Contracts, organizations | Years to decades | Corporate structure, contracts | Negotiation, reorganization |
| **4: Resource allocation** | Prices, quantities | Continuous | Market transactions, production | Supply and demand |

Higher levels constrain lower levels but change more slowly. Culture shapes which constitutional arrangements are feasible. Constitutions constrain organizational forms. Organizations operate within markets.

**Implication**: You can't change culture quickly, but you can change laws. You can't transplant institutions from Level 2 without considering Level 1. Development isn't just about getting policies (Level 4) right — it requires building institutions (Levels 2-3) consistent with culture (Level 1).

## Transaction Cost Economics

### Why Do Firms Exist? (Coase, 1937)

If markets are efficient, why not contract everything through the market? Why have firms at all?

Ronald Coase (Nobel Prize, 1991): Firms exist because markets have **transaction costs**. Market transactions require:
1. **Search costs**: Finding trading partners, discovering prices
2. **Negotiation costs**: Bargaining over terms, writing contracts
3. **Monitoring costs**: Ensuring contract compliance
4. **Enforcement costs**: Resolving disputes, punishing breaches

When these costs exceed the costs of internal coordination, firms internalize the activity. The boundary of the firm is determined by comparative transaction costs.

**Example**: Why does Apple design chips in-house but outsource manufacturing to TSMC?
- **Design**: Highly specific to Apple's products, requires deep coordination with other components, difficult to specify in contracts → internalize
- **Manufacturing**: Standardizable, TSMC has scale economies, contract can specify quality → outsource

**Example**: Why do companies have employees rather than contracting with independent workers for each task?
- **Employment relationship**: Lower transaction costs than renegotiating contracts continually. The employment contract says "do what I tell you within bounds" rather than specifying every task.

### Williamson's Framework

Oliver Williamson (Nobel Prize, 2009) identified three dimensions determining governance choice:

| Factor | Description | Low (Market) | High (Hierarchy/Firm) |
|--------|-------------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Asset specificity** | Extent assets are specialized to relationship | Generic assets usable elsewhere | Specialized investments with no alternative use |
| **Frequency** | How often transaction occurs | One-off or rare | Repeated regularly |
| **Uncertainty** | Ability to specify contract terms | Outcomes easily specified | Outcomes hard to predict/specify |

**Governance structures**:
1. **Market**: Discrete transactions, arms-length contracting. Example: Buying gasoline
2. **Hybrid**: Long-term contracts, relationships, franchising. Example: Automotive suppliers with long-term contracts
3. **Hierarchy**: Vertical integration, employment relationships. Example: In-house manufacturing

**When to use hierarchy?**
When assets are specific, transactions frequent, and uncertainty high, hierarchical governance (bringing activity in-house) beats markets.

**Classic example: Fisher Body and General Motors**
1919: GM contracted with Fisher Body for car bodies. As GM volume grew, GM wanted Fisher to locate plants near GM assembly lines (asset specificity). Fisher refused (hold-up risk — once Fisher invested in GM-specific plant, GM could demand lower prices). Result: GM vertically integrated by acquiring Fisher Body (1926).

**Modern example: Platform companies**
Uber uses hybrid governance:
- **Market**: Riders and drivers contract for individual trips (low asset specificity)
- **Hierarchy**: Algorithm controls matching, pricing, driver ratings (high uncertainty in quality → monitoring needed)

### Principal-Agent Problems

Whenever one party (principal) delegates to another (agent), information asymmetry creates problems:

| Problem | Definition | Example | Market Failure |
|---------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Moral hazard** | Agent takes unobservable actions against principal's interest | Employee shirks; insured person takes risks | Effort unobservable |
| **Adverse selection** | Agent has private information before contracting | Used car seller knows defects; sick people buy insurance | Quality unobservable |
| **Hold-up** | One party exploits other's sunk investment | Supplier raises prices after buyer builds specialized factory | Ex-post opportunism |

**Solutions**:

1. **Monitoring**: Supervision, auditing, performance reviews, surveillance
   - Example: Managers monitor workers; auditors monitor corporate accounts
   - Limitation: Costly, incomplete (can't monitor everything)

2. **Incentives**: Align agent interests with principal interests
   - Example: Stock options for CEOs; commissions for salespeople; profit-sharing
   - Limitation: Risk-averse agents dislike performance-based pay; gaming (teaching to the test)

3. **Screening**: Acquire information before contracting
   - Example: Probation periods, interviews, background checks, credit scores
   - Limitation: Costly, imperfect signals

4. **Signaling**: Agent credibly reveals quality
   - Example: Education credentials, warranties, certifications
   - Limitation: Costly signaling may be wasteful (degree signals ability without creating it)

5. **Bonding**: Agent posts collateral or accepts penalties
   - Example: Performance bonds, earnest money, non-compete clauses
   - Limitation: Binds both parties, reduces flexibility

### Application: Corporate Governance

**Principal-agent problem**: Shareholders (principal) delegate to managers (agent) to run the company.

**Agency costs**:
- **Empire-building**: Managers pursue growth beyond profit-maximizing size (prestige, power)
- **Perquisites**: Excessive compensation, corporate jets, luxury offices
- **Risk aversion**: Managers avoid beneficial but risky projects to protect their positions
- **Short-termism**: Managers focus on short-term stock price (tied to bonuses) at expense of long-term value

**Solutions**:
1. **Board oversight**: Independent directors monitor management
   - Limitation: Directors often captured by management, lack information/incentives

2. **Executive compensation**: Stock options, restricted stock align manager-shareholder interests
   - Limitation: Can encourage excessive risk-taking, short-term focus, earnings manipulation

3. **Shareholder activism**: Large investors (pension funds, hedge funds) pressure management
   - Limitation: Free-rider problem (small shareholders don't monitor); activists may have short-term focus

4. **Market for corporate control**: Hostile takeovers discipline poorly performing management
   - Limitation: Takeover defenses (poison pills, staggered boards); bidders may overpay

5. **Debt financing**: Debt forces discipline (must pay interest or face bankruptcy)
   - Limitation: Bankruptcy costs; debt overhang problem

No perfect solution exists. All corporate governance involves tradeoffs between managerial discretion (needed for business judgment) and shareholder control (preventing agency costs).

## Bureaucracy

### Weber's Ideal Type

Max Weber identified bureaucracy as the dominant organizational form of modernity:

| Feature | Description | Benefit | Pathology |
|---------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Hierarchy** | Clear chain of command, pyramid structure | Accountability, coordination | Rigidity, slow decisions |
| **Formal rules** | Written procedures and protocols | Consistency, fairness | Red tape, goal displacement |
| **Specialization** | Division of labor by function | Expertise, efficiency | Silos, narrow focus |
| **Impersonality** | Rules apply regardless of person | Equal treatment | Dehumanization, inflexibility |
| **Merit selection** | Hiring/promotion by qualification | Competence | Credentialism, paper qualifications |
| **Career orientation** | Lifetime employment, promotion through ranks | Experience, loyalty | Resistance to change, inbreeding |

Weber saw bureaucracy as technically superior to other organizational forms (traditional administration based on personal loyalty; charismatic leadership based on extraordinary individuals). Bureaucracy is predictable, calculable, efficient — but it creates an "iron cage" of rationalization, trapping people in dehumanizing systems.

**Modern examples**:
- **US federal bureaucracy**: 2.1 million civilian employees (2023) across 15 Cabinet departments, 100+ agencies
- **University administration**: Faculty, departments, deans, provost, president — hierarchical, specialized, rule-bound
- **Military**: Rank hierarchy, formal protocols, specialization (infantry, logistics, intelligence)

### Pathologies of Bureaucracy

| Pathology | Description | Example | Cause |
|-----------|-------------|---------|-------|
| **Goal displacement** | Means become ends; rules matter more than mission | Following procedure even when it harms mission | Rule-following becomes focus |
| **Red tape** | Excessive rules impede action | FDA approval taking 10+ years for life-saving drugs | Accountability drives paperwork |
| **Trained incapacity** | Expertise creates blind spots | Specialists missing cross-cutting issues | Specialization narrows perspective |
| **Bureaucratic inertia** | Resistance to change | Maintaining obsolete programs | Sunk costs, routines, interests |
| **Parkinson's law** | Work expands to fill time available; staff grows regardless of workload | Growing administration without growing output | Self-interest of bureaucrats |
| **Peter principle** | People promoted to level of incompetence | Excellent engineer becomes mediocre manager | Different skills at different levels |

**Robert Merton's analysis**:
Bureaucratic rules aim for efficiency and reliability. But strict rule-following becomes ritualistic — the rule itself becomes sacred regardless of outcomes. Officials develop "bureaucratic personality" — rigidity, timidity, risk aversion.

**Example: VA Hospital scandal (2014)**
Veterans Affairs employees falsified wait-time data to meet bureaucratic targets while veterans died waiting for appointments. The metric (wait times) became the goal, displacing the actual mission (veteran care).

### Iron Law of Oligarchy (Michels)

Robert Michels (1911): All organizations, even democratic ones, inevitably develop oligarchic leadership. "Who says organization says oligarchy."

**Why?**
1. **Technical necessity**: Organizations require coordination, specialized knowledge, leadership
2. **Psychological**: Masses are apathetic; leaders develop attachment to power
3. **Structural**: Leaders control information, communications, and agenda-setting

**Evidence**: Labor unions formed to represent workers but leadership becomes entrenched, distant from rank-and-file. Revolutionary parties establish bureaucracies that resist democracy. NGOs develop professional staff that dominates volunteers.

**Mechanisms**:
- **Information asymmetry**: Leaders know more than members
- **Agenda control**: Leaders determine what gets discussed
- **Resource control**: Leaders control funding, jobs, patronage
- **Credentialing**: Leaders require qualifications that exclude ordinary members

**Implications**: Direct democracy is unstable; representative democracy tends toward oligarchy. The question isn't whether organizations will be oligarchic but what kind of oligarchy — accountable or unaccountable, rotating or permanent, self-interested or mission-driven.

## Path Dependence and Lock-In

**Path dependence**: Early choices constrain later options, even if better alternatives exist. History matters; you can't costlessly switch from one equilibrium to another.

**Sources of path dependence**:
1. **Sunk costs**: Investments in old technology/institutions that would be lost if switching
2. **Learning effects**: Experience with old technology improves it relative to new alternatives
3. **Coordination effects**: Many users of one standard makes switching costly for any individual
4. **Adaptive expectations**: Others expect you to use the dominant standard

**QWERTY keyboard**:
Designed in 1870s to prevent typewriter jams by separating frequently-used letter pairs. Typewriters don't jam anymore, but QWERTY persists because:
- Billions of keyboards exist (sunk costs)
- Touch-typists trained on QWERTY (learning effects)
- Software, keyboards assume QWERTY (coordination)
- Employers expect QWERTY skills (expectations)

Alternative Dvorak layout may be more efficient, but switching costs exceed individual benefits. Network effects create lock-in.

**Institutional path dependence examples**:

**US healthcare**: Employer-sponsored insurance emerged from WWII wage controls (wage ceiling but not benefit ceiling → employers competed with health insurance). Now deeply entrenched despite inefficiency:
- Tax code subsidizes employer insurance
- Insurance industry built around it
- Medicare/Medicaid layered on top
- Unions negotiate for health benefits
Result: Path-dependent system no one would design from scratch

**British driving on the left**: Historical convention (knights kept right hand free for sword). Enormous switching costs (road signs, cars, driver training). Countries that switched (Sweden 1967) faced chaos. Britain stays left-handed.

**Legal traditions**: Former British colonies use common law; former French/Spanish colonies use civil law. Colonial institutions established 100-200 years ago still structure governance. Countries can't easily switch legal traditions — judges, lawyers, legal education, business practices all adapted to existing system.

**VHS vs Betamax**: Betamax had superior quality, but VHS won through network effects (more movies available in VHS → more people bought VHS players → more movies released in VHS). Early lead became self-reinforcing.

**Increasing returns** drive lock-in: The more people use a standard, the more valuable it becomes, making switching less likely regardless of alternatives' quality.

**W. Brian Arthur**: Conventional economics assumes diminishing returns (equilibrium unique and efficient). But increasing returns create multiple equilibria (path-dependent), positive feedback (early advantages compound), and lock-in (inefficient outcomes can persist).

## Why Nations Fail (Acemoglu & Robinson)

The most influential institutional argument for economic development.

### Inclusive Institutions

- **Secure property rights** for broad population (not just elites)
- **Rule of law** applied equally (courts aren't tools of rulers)
- **Pluralistic politics** with checks on power (executive, legislative, judicial balance)
- **Contract enforcement** enabling complex transactions (courts enforce agreements)
- **Public services** (education, infrastructure) broadly provided (not just to elites)
- **Entry** relatively open (low barriers to starting businesses, entering professions)
- **Creative destruction** permitted (inefficient firms fail; innovation disrupts incumbents)

**Examples**: US, UK, Scandinavia, Japan, South Korea, Botswana

**Mechanisms promoting growth**:
1. **Investment**: Secure property → people invest (won't be expropriated)
2. **Innovation**: Rewards to innovation → entrepreneurs take risks
3. **Talent allocation**: Open entry → talent flows to productive uses
4. **Accountability**: Political pluralism → governments provide public goods
5. **Adaptation**: Creative destruction → resources flow from less to more productive uses

### Extractive Institutions

- **Insecure property rights** — elites can expropriate
- **Weak rule of law** — power determines outcomes, not rules
- **Concentrated power** — no checks and balances
- **Restricted entry** — elites control economic opportunities (monopolies, licenses, barriers)
- **Patronage** — public resources distributed to supporters, not based on merit
- **Resistance to creative destruction** — incumbents block innovation that threatens them

**Examples**: North Korea, Zimbabwe, Venezuela, pre-modern absolutist states

**Mechanisms causing stagnation**:
1. **Low investment**: Risk of expropriation → don't invest
2. **Little innovation**: Rewards flow to regime supporters, not innovators
3. **Misallocation**: Talent flows to rent-seeking (politics, military), not production
4. **Public goods underprovided**: Rulers extract, don't invest in education/infrastructure
5. **Resistance to change**: Incumbents block technologies/institutions threatening their power

### Evidence

**Natural experiments**: North vs South Korea (1945 division), East vs West Germany (1945-1990), Nogales AZ vs Nogales Sonora — same geography, culture, climate; radically different institutions → radically different outcomes.

**Korean example**:
- 1945: Korea divided at 38th parallel
- **North**: Communist dictatorship, command economy, dynastic succession (Kim family)
- **South**: Authoritarian initially but gradual democratization, market economy, pluralism
- 2024: North GDP per capita ~$1,300, South ~$35,000
- Difference: Almost entirely institutional

**German example**:
- 1945: Germany divided by Allied occupation
- **East**: Soviet-imposed communism, command economy, one-party state
- **West**: Democracy, market economy, rule of law
- 1989: East collapsed, reunification followed
- Difference: Institutional, not cultural or geographical

**Nogales example**:
- **Nogales, Arizona** (US side): GDP per capita ~$30,000, good schools, low corruption
- **Nogales, Sonora** (Mexico side): GDP per capita ~$10,000, worse schools, higher corruption
- Same people (many families span border), same geography, same climate
- Difference: US institutions (rule of law, property rights, public goods) vs Mexican institutions (weaker rule of law, more corruption, less public goods)

**Colonial origins** (Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson, 2001):
European colonizers established different institutions depending on settler mortality:
- **Low mortality** (temperate climate, low disease): Colonizers settled → built inclusive institutions (property rights, rule of law) to protect themselves → US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand
- **High mortality** (tropical, high disease): Colonizers extracted resources → built extractive institutions (forced labor, expropriation, authoritarianism) → much of Africa, Latin America

These institutions persisted after independence. Settler mortality in 1700s predicts GDP today. Instrumented variable regression shows causation: institutions → growth.

**Reversal of fortune**: Before colonization, densely populated regions (Aztec, Inca, Mughal empires) were richer. After colonization, these regions became poorer — extractive institutions extracted from dense populations. Sparsely populated regions (North America, Australia) saw settler institutions → growth. Colonization reversed the geography of wealth through institutions.

## Institutional Complementarities

Institutions cluster. **Varieties of capitalism** (Hall & Soskice):

| Feature | Liberal Market (US, UK) | Coordinated Market (Germany, Japan) |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Labor relations** | Flexible, at-will employment | Coordinated, works councils, lifetime employment |
| **Corporate governance** | Shareholder primacy | Stakeholder model (employees, suppliers, banks) |
| **Education/training** | General skills (universities) | Firm-specific, apprenticeships |
| **Inter-firm relations** | Competitive, arm's-length | Cooperative, long-term networks |
| **Finance** | Equity markets, dispersed shareholders | Bank financing, concentrated ownership |
| **Innovation** | Radical (startups, disruption) | Incremental (process, quality improvements) |
| **Welfare state** | Minimal, means-tested | Generous, universal |

These institutions are **complementary** — each reinforces the others:
- Shareholder primacy → short-term focus → general skills (employees mobile) → competitive labor markets → equity finance
- Stakeholder model → long-term relationships → firm-specific skills (employees stay) → coordinated labor → bank finance

**Implication**: You can't transplant one institution from one cluster to another without changing complementary institutions. German-style works councils in US without changing corporate governance, finance, and training systems won't work.

**Comparative advantage**:
- **Liberal markets**: Better at radical innovation (biotech, software, finance) requiring rapid resource reallocation
- **Coordinated markets**: Better at incremental innovation (manufacturing, engineering) requiring long-term commitment and coordination

Neither is universally superior — each excels in different domains.

**Institutional change**: Complementarities create stability (changing one institution requires changing many) but also crisis potential (when one institution changes, pressure builds on complementary institutions, potentially causing cascading change).

## Key Terms

- **Transaction Costs**: Costs of searching, negotiating, monitoring, and enforcing agreements (Coase, Williamson)
- **Principal-Agent Problem**: Conflict of interest between delegator (principal) and delegate (agent)
- **Moral Hazard**: Hidden action — agent takes unobservable risks
- **Adverse Selection**: Hidden information — one party knows more before contracting
- **Asset Specificity**: Extent to which assets are specialized to a particular relationship
- **Hold-Up Problem**: One party exploits other's sunk investment through ex-post opportunism
- **Path Dependence**: Early choices constrain future options through sunk costs, learning, coordination, expectations
- **Lock-In**: Trapped in suboptimal standard/institution due to switching costs and network effects
- **Inclusive Institutions**: Broad property rights, rule of law, pluralistic politics, creative destruction (Acemoglu & Robinson)
- **Extractive Institutions**: Concentrated power, expropriation, restricted entry, blocked innovation
- **Iron Law of Oligarchy**: Organizations inevitably develop elite leadership (Michels)
- **Varieties of Capitalism**: Institutional clusters differ between liberal market economies and coordinated market economies (Hall & Soskice)
- **Institutional Complementarities**: Institutions reinforce each other; changing one requires changing many
- **Embeddedness**: Institutions embedded in culture, norms, and history (Williamson's Level 1)
- **Creative Destruction**: Process where innovation displaces incumbents, reallocating resources to more productive uses (Schumpeter)

## Summary

Institutions (rules) and organizations (players) together determine economic and social outcomes. Formal institutions (laws, regulations) and informal ones (norms, trust) interact — when they conflict, informal often wins. Effective institutions require alignment of formal and informal.

Transaction cost economics (Coase, Williamson) explains why firms exist and how they organize. When asset specificity, frequency, and uncertainty are high, hierarchical governance beats markets. Principal-agent problems arise whenever delegation occurs, requiring monitoring, incentives, screening, signaling, and bonding — no perfect solution exists.

Bureaucracy (Weber) is the dominant organizational form of modernity — hierarchical, rule-bound, specialized, impersonal, merit-based, career-oriented. It's efficient but creates pathologies: goal displacement, red tape, trained incapacity, inertia. Michels' iron law of oligarchy shows that all organizations, even democratic ones, tend toward elite rule through information, agenda, and resource control.

Path dependence means early institutional choices have lasting consequences through sunk costs, learning effects, coordination effects, and adaptive expectations. QWERTY keyboards, employer-sponsored health insurance, legal traditions, and driving sides all reflect historical accidents now locked in through network effects.

Acemoglu and Robinson's framework distinguishes inclusive from extractive institutions as the key determinant of national prosperity. Natural experiments (Korea, Germany, Nogales) and colonial history support the argument: institutions explain divergence better than geography, culture, or resources. Inclusive institutions enable investment, innovation, talent allocation, accountability, and creative destruction. Extractive institutions produce expropriation, misallocation, stagnation.

Institutions cluster in complementary bundles. Varieties of capitalism shows liberal market economies (US, UK) and coordinated market economies (Germany, Japan) have different but internally consistent institutional configurations. You can't transplant one institution without changing complementary ones. Institutional change faces inertia from complementarities but also potential for cascading change during crises.
