# Glossary

## A

**Adaptive Expectations**: People form beliefs expecting others to use dominant standard, creating self-fulfilling prophecy and lock-in effects (e.g., everyone expects QWERTY keyboards, so learning QWERTY becomes rational).

**Adverse Selection**: Hidden information before contracting where one party knows quality/type that others don't. Classic example: used car market (sellers know defects; buyers don't), insurance (sick people more likely to buy), labor market (workers know their ability better than employers).

**Agency**: Individuals' capacity to act freely and make choices despite structural constraints. Contrasts with determinism — humans as autonomous agents shaping their lives vs. products of social forces.

**AGIL Schema** (Parsons): Every social system must fulfill four functions: Adaptation (resource acquisition via economy), Goal attainment (collective goals via polity), Integration (coordination via law/community), Latency/pattern maintenance (culture transmission via family/education/religion).

**Alienation** (Marx): Workers' estrangement from: (1) product of labor (don't own output), (2) process of labor (don't control work), (3) species-being (humans are creative; capitalism reduces to cogs), (4) other workers (competition, not solidarity). Modern example: Amazon warehouse workers following algorithms.

**Algorithmic Governance**: Platforms using algorithms to regulate billions of users' behavior. Facebook content moderation, YouTube recommendations, Uber pricing, Amazon marketplace rules. Raises concerns about transparency, accountability, bias, and concentration of private power over public matters.

**Anchoring**: Cognitive bias where initial value serves as reference point, affecting subsequent judgments. Used in nudges (suggest donation amounts $50/$100/$200 vs $25/$50/$100 produces higher donations). First number "anchors" judgment.

**Anomie** (Durkheim): Normlessness; breakdown of social bonds and moral guidance. Occurs during rapid social change (industrialization, urbanization, economic crises) when traditional norms collapse before new ones emerge. Durkheim showed suicide rates spike during anomic periods (both economic booms and busts).

**Arrow's Impossibility Theorem**: No ranked voting system with ≥3 options satisfies all fairness criteria simultaneously: (1) Unanimity, (2) Independence of irrelevant alternatives, (3) Non-dictatorship, (4) Transitivity, (5) Unrestricted domain. Fundamental limit on democracy, not design flaw to be fixed. All voting systems involve tradeoffs.

**Asset Specificity**: Extent to which assets are specialized to particular relationship and have no alternative use. High asset specificity creates hold-up problem (can't redeploy to other uses if partner exploits). Examples: factory tooled for specific customer, training specific to one employer. Drives vertical integration (Williamson).

**Assortative Mating**: Like marries like — people partner with those of similar education, income, values. Concentrates advantages across generations (two college-educated high-earners produce highly advantaged children). Contributes to inequality persistence and low mobility.

**Authority Types** (Weber): (1) Traditional (custom, inherited status — monarchy, patriarchy), (2) Charismatic (personal magnetism — prophets, revolutionary leaders), (3) Rational-legal (rules and procedures — modern states, bureaucracies). Modern societies rest on rational-legal authority but charismatic leaders periodically emerge.

## B

**Bayesian Incentive Compatibility (BIC)**: Mechanism design property where truth-telling is best response given beliefs about others' types. Weaker than dominant strategy IC but more flexible. Many mechanisms achieve BIC but not DSIC.

**Bonding Social Capital**: Ties within groups (family, ethnic community, co-religionists). Provides support, solidarity, and trust within group but can create exclusion and parochialism. Contrast with bridging capital.

**Borda Count**: Voting system where candidates receive points for rankings (n-1 points for 1st place, n-2 for 2nd, etc.). Considers full ranking; rewards consensus candidates. But vulnerable to strategic manipulation and violates independence of irrelevant alternatives.

**Bounded Rationality** (Simon): Rationality limited by information, cognitive capacity, and time. People don't optimize globally; they "satisfice" (choose good-enough options). Contrast with perfect rationality assumed in classical economics. Behavioral economics explores implications.

**Bourgeoisie** (Marx): Capitalist class that owns means of production (factories, land, capital). Employs proletariat for wages; extracts surplus value. Historical role: overthrew feudalism through bourgeois revolutions; will be overthrown by proletariat (Marx predicted).

**Bridging Social Capital**: Ties across groups. Provides access to diverse information, opportunities, influence. Granovetter's "strength of weak ties" — acquaintances (weak ties) provide access to different networks than close friends (strong ties) who know same people/information.

**Bureaucracy** (Weber): Organizational form with: (1) Hierarchy (pyramid, chain of command), (2) Formal rules (written procedures), (3) Specialization (division of labor), (4) Impersonality (rules apply regardless of person), (5) Merit selection (qualifications, not connections), (6) Career orientation (lifetime employment, promotion through ranks). Efficient but creates "iron cage" of rationalization.

**Bureaucratic Pathologies**: Goal displacement (means become ends), red tape (excessive rules), trained incapacity (specialization creates blind spots), inertia (resistance to change), Parkinson's law (work expands to fill time; staff grows regardless of workload), Peter principle (promoted to incompetence level).

## C

**Charismatic Authority** (Weber): Legitimacy based on leader's extraordinary personal qualities. Prophets, revolutionary leaders, founders. Problem: "Routinization of charisma" — how to institutionalize movement after charismatic founder departs. Christianity after Jesus; American republic after Washington; Soviet Union after Lenin.

**Chasm** (Moore): Gap between early adopters (want cutting-edge technology) and early majority (want practical, proven solutions) in diffusion of innovations. Many innovations fail to cross this chasm. Requires adapting product/marketing from enthusiasts to pragmatists.

**Class** (Weber): Economic position determined by market situation — what you own (property), what you can sell (credentials, skills), what control you have (organizational authority). Continuous variable, not binary. Contrast with Marx's binary bourgeoisie/proletariat.

**Class Consciousness** (Marx): Working class recognition of shared interests and opposition to capitalist class. Necessary for revolution. Opposite of false consciousness (workers identifying with owners' interests).

**Cobra Effect**: Incentive causing opposite of intended outcome. British India offered bounty for dead cobras → people bred cobras for bounty → when bounty canceled, breeders released cobras → more cobras than before. Lesson: Monetary incentives can backfire when people game the system.

**Coercion** (Olson): Solution to collective action problem through mandatory participation. Taxation enforced with fines/imprisonment. Military draft enforced with criminal penalties. Solves free-rider problem but requires state capacity and legitimacy.

**Collective Action Problem** (Olson): Individual rationality leading to collectively suboptimal outcomes. Rational individuals won't voluntarily act in group's interest unless group is small or coercion/selective incentives exist. Classic examples: voting, union dues, public radio donations, climate action. Each person reasons: "My contribution negligible; costs certain; rational to free-ride."

**Common Law**: Legal tradition (UK, US, former British colonies) where judge-made law through precedent dominates. Adversarial system (opposing lawyers). Contrast with civil law (codified statutes, inquisitorial system). Common law correlates with stronger property rights and financial development.

**Commitment Device**: Mechanism to bind future self to beneficial behavior. "Save More Tomorrow" (commit to save from future raises), Ulysses contracts (self-imposed penalties for failing goals). Exploits present bias (we know future self will be tempted but current self can bind them).

**Condorcet Winner**: Candidate who beats all others in head-to-head contests. Condorcet paradox: May not exist (rock-paper-scissors preferences). Condorcet voting systems elect Condorcet winner if exists, but complex to count and rarely used despite theoretical appeal.

**Conformist Bias**: Cultural transmission bias toward adopting most common behavior. "When in Rome, do as Romans do." Efficient (majority likely right in local context) but can lock in harmful practices (foot binding persisted 1,000 years through conformist pressure).

**Content Bias**: Some ideas intrinsically more memorable, emotionally compelling, or useful. Negativity bias (bad news spreads faster), threat detection (danger information), social information (gossip), counterintuitive concepts (minimally unusual = memorable). Shapes cultural evolution.

**Coordination Game**: Multiple equilibria; everyone benefits from coordinating on same choice but which choice doesn't matter intrinsically. Driving side (left vs right — doesn't matter which but must coordinate). Technology standards (Betamax vs VHS). First-mover advantage creates lock-in.

**Corruption**: Using public office for private gain. Types: Petty (small bribes for routine services), Grand (high-level theft of millions/billions), Systemic (corruption embedded in institutional norms), Legal (influence through lawful means like lobbying/revolving door).

**Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI)**: Transparency International measure of perceived corruption (0-100; higher = less corrupt). Denmark/Finland/NZ ~87-90 (very clean). US ~69. Russia ~26. Somalia ~11-12 (extreme corruption). Correlates with rule of law, development, democratic quality.

**Countervailing Power**: Galbraith's concept that concentrated power on one side creates incentives for organization on other side. Corporate power → labor unions. Industry lobbies → consumer advocates. Prevents total domination; creates balance.

**Creative Destruction** (Schumpeter): Process where innovation displaces incumbents, reallocating resources to more productive uses. Old firms/industries fail; new ones emerge. Drives long-term growth but creates short-term disruption and resistance. Inclusive institutions permit creative destruction; extractive institutions block it to protect incumbents.

**Credentialism** (Collins): Degrees serving as gatekeeping devices more than skill developers. Many jobs requiring degrees don't need degree-taught skills — degree signals class membership, conformity, ability to navigate bureaucracy. Evidence: Credential inflation (jobs requiring degrees that didn't before), requirements unrelated to performance.

**Critical Juncture**: Crisis moment when institutional change becomes possible because normal constraints are relaxed. Examples: Great Depression → New Deal; WWII → postwar order; 2008 crisis → Dodd-Frank. Windows of opportunity open briefly; if reform doesn't happen quickly, opportunity closes.

**Cultural Capital** (Bourdieu): Knowledge, skills, education, tastes signaling class position. Three forms: Embodied (dispositions, habits, accent), Objectified (cultural goods like books, art), Institutionalized (credentials — degrees, certifications). Converts into economic capital (credentials → jobs → income) and social capital (elite schools → elite networks).

**Cultural Evolution**: Change in norms, beliefs, technologies through transmission and selection. Unlike biological evolution: Lamarckian (acquired traits transmitted), fast (years/decades vs millennia), through social learning (not genes). Shaped by transmission biases (prestige, conformist, content, success).

## D

**DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)**: Blockchain-based governance with rules encoded in smart contracts. Members vote on proposals; execution automated. Promise: Transparent, corruption-resistant, global. Reality: Low participation, plutocracy (votes proportional to tokens), smart contract vulnerabilities, legal uncertainty.

**Deferred Acceptance** (Gale-Shapley): Algorithm for matching problems (students-schools, residents-hospitals). Produces stable matching (no unmatched pair prefers each other to assignment). Student-proposing version is student-optimal. Strategy-proof for proposing side. Used in medical residency matching, school choice, kidney exchange.

**Democratic Deficit**: Institutions making decisions affecting people who have no democratic input. EU example: European Commission proposes legislation but isn't directly elected. Global institutions (IMF, WTO) impose policies on countries with limited voice. Platforms (Facebook, Google) govern billions without electoral accountability.

**Diffusion of Innovations** (Rogers): How new technologies/ideas spread. S-curve adoption: Innovators (2.5%) → Early adopters (13.5%) → Early majority (34%) → Late majority (34%) → Laggards (16%). Faster diffusion when innovation has: Relative advantage, Compatibility, Low complexity, Trialability, Observability.

**Disenchantment** (Weber): Rationalization process replacing magic, tradition, and mystery with calculable reason. Modern world loses enchantment as instrumental rationality dominates. "Disenchantment of the world" — no room for meaning beyond efficiency and control.

**Dominant Strategy**: Best response regardless of others' actions. In dominant strategy incentive compatible (DSIC) mechanisms, truth-telling dominates. Vickrey auction: bidding true value is dominant strategy (overbidding and underbidding can hurt, can't help).

**Dominant Strategy Incentive Compatible (DSIC)**: Mechanism design property where truth-telling is best regardless of others' strategies. Strongest form of incentive compatibility. Vickrey auction is DSIC; first-price auction isn't (should shade bid below value).

**Dramaturgical Analysis** (Goffman): Social life as performance. Front stage (public behavior for audiences) vs backstage (private, drop the act). Impression management (strategic self-presentation). Example: Waiter deferential to customers (front) but complains about them in kitchen (back).

**Dual Inheritance Theory** (Boyd & Richerson): Humans inherit through two channels: Genetic (DNA, slow) and Cultural (learned, fast). Gene-culture coevolution: Cultural practices alter genetic selection; genes shape cultural transmission biases. Examples: Lactose tolerance (dairy farming selected for lactase persistence), honor cultures.

**Duverger's Law**: Electoral system shapes party system. Plurality/first-past-the-post voting → two parties (voters abandon third parties to avoid "wasting" votes). Proportional representation → multiparty systems (small parties viable).

## E

**Early Adopters**: Second group to adopt innovations (13.5% of population). Opinion leaders, educated, respected. Reduce uncertainty for others by testing innovations. Sought for advice. Financial resources to absorb losses.

**Economic Capital** (Bourdieu): Financial assets, income, property. Can buy other capitals (economic → cultural through education; economic → social through exclusive memberships). Most liquid and convertible form of capital.

**Embeddedness** (Polanyi, Granovetter): Economic activity embedded in social relationships and institutions. Pre-modern economies embedded in kinship, community, tradition. Modern capitalism appears disembedded but still embedded in legal systems, norms, networks.

**Empire-Building**: Managers pursuing growth beyond profit-maximizing size for prestige, power, compensation. Agency problem: Shareholders want profits; managers want size. Evidence: Conglomerates in 1960s-80s (bigger but not more profitable), mergers destroying value.

**Epistemic Community**: Network of experts sharing causal beliefs and normative values. Influences policy by defining problems, proposing solutions, legitimating choices. Examples: Climate scientists, development economists, public health experts.

**Expanding Circle** (Singer): Moral concern progressively expanding from kin → tribe → nation → all humans → animals → potentially future beings/AI. Historical trajectory: Slavery abolished, suffrage expanded, civil rights, LGBTQ rights, animal welfare. Driven by economic development, literacy, urbanization, media, moral argument.

**Extractive Institutions** (Acemoglu & Robinson): Institutions concentrating power and expropriating wealth. Insecure property rights, weak rule of law, restricted entry, blocked creative destruction. Extract resources for elite rather than enabling broad prosperity. Examples: North Korea, Zimbabwe, colonial economies. Lead to stagnation.

**Externality**: Cost or benefit affecting parties not involved in transaction. Negative (pollution, congestion, disease transmission). Positive (vaccination, education, R&D spillovers). Markets fail to account for externalities; institutions needed to internalize them (Pigouvian taxes, subsidies, regulation).

## F

**False Consciousness** (Marx): Workers failing to recognize true class interests, instead identifying with capitalist class interests. Ideology (ruling class ideas become ruling ideas) creates false consciousness. Prevents class solidarity and revolution.

**Fatherhood Bonus**: Men's wages increase ~6% per child. Contrast with motherhood penalty (~4% decrease). Mechanisms: Employers assume fathers more committed/mature; fathers work longer hours; positive stereotypes.

**Feedback**: Nudge showing consequences of behavior. Energy usage compared to neighbors reduced consumption 2-3%. Social comparison + motivation. Works best when: Frequent, Timely, Actionable.

**Fidelity**: In cultural transmission, accuracy of copying. High fidelity (vertical transmission, institutionalized) vs low fidelity (horizontal transmission, peer-to-peer). Trade-off: High fidelity preserves knowledge but slows adaptation.

**Folk Theorem**: In infinitely repeated games with patient players, any outcome between mutual defection and mutual cooperation sustainable as equilibrium through appropriate strategies. Implication: Cooperation possible in repeated interactions even when not in one-shot.

**Framing** (Social Movements): How movements define problems (diagnostic), solutions (prognostic), and urgency (motivational). Successful framing aligns with audience values. Examples: Civil Rights framed segregation as immoral/unconstitutional; Climate framed as existential threat/jobs opportunity.

**Free Rider**: Benefits from collective goods without contributing. Classic examples: Listening to public radio without donating, benefiting from clean air without reducing emissions, enjoying union-negotiated wages without paying dues. Rational individually but collectively disastrous. Olson's collective action problem.

**Functionalism**: Theoretical perspective viewing society as system of interdependent parts serving functions. Institutions exist because they solve adaptive problems. Durkheim, Parsons, Merton. Critique: Conservative bias (assumes existing institutions functional), ignores conflict and change.

## G

**Gale-Shapley Algorithm**: See Deferred Acceptance.

**Gene-Culture Coevolution**: Cultural practices alter genetic selection pressures; genes shape cultural transmission. Examples: Lactose tolerance (dairy farming selected for lactase persistence), sickle-cell anemia (malaria resistance in agricultural regions), high-altitude adaptations (Tibetan plateau).

**Gibbard-Satterthwaite Theorem**: Any non-dictatorial voting system with ≥3 candidates susceptible to strategic voting (voting insincerely to achieve better outcome). Implication: Can't design voting rule that's both democratic and strategy-proof. Voters must think strategically ("Who can win?" not just "Who do I prefer?").

**Gini Coefficient**: Measure of inequality (0 = perfect equality, everyone has same; 1 = maximum inequality, one person has everything). Calculated from Lorenz curve. US income Gini ~0.39, wealth Gini ~0.85. Scandinavia ~0.25-0.28. South Africa ~0.63 (highest globally).

**Glass Ceiling**: Invisible barrier preventing women/minorities from reaching top positions. Women are 44% of entry-level workers but only 28% of senior managers, 8.8% of Fortune 500 CEOs. At each promotion level, women drop out at higher rates.

**Glass Cliff**: Women more likely appointed to leadership during crises. Companies in trouble appoint women; if fail, blame woman; if succeed, credit leadership despite unfavorable conditions. Example: UK political parties selecting female leaders after electoral losses.

**Goldthorpe Class Schema**: Empirical class classification widely used in European sociology. I-II (Service class: professionals, managers), III (Routine non-manual), IV (Petty bourgeoisie: self-employed), V-VI (Skilled manual), VII (Unskilled manual). Predicts life chances: health, mobility, political attitudes.

**Goodhart's Law**: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Examples: Test scores → teaching to test; Citation counts → citation gaming; Arrest quotas → arrests for trivial offenses; Hospital wait times → patients kept in ambulances.

**Great Gatsby Curve** (Corak): Higher inequality correlates with lower social mobility. Countries with higher Gini have higher intergenerational elasticity (income advantage transmitted across generations). Mechanisms: Educational investment, neighborhood effects, social capital, cultural capital, assortative mating.

## H

**Habitus** (Bourdieu): Internalized dispositions shaped by social position — how you walk, talk, eat, dress, think, feel. Durable (persists across situations), transposable (generates practices across domains), generative (produces new practices adapted to situations). Upper-class habitus valued in professional contexts; working-class habitus devalued.

**Hayek's Knowledge Problem**: Central planners lack distributed, tacit knowledge held by millions of individuals. Markets aggregate knowledge through prices. Designed institutions may be less efficient than evolved ones. Implication for institutional design: Humility about designer knowledge; value of emergent order.

**Historical Materialism** (Marx): Economic base (means and relations of production) determines ideological superstructure (law, politics, religion, culture). How society produces goods determines class structure → political institutions → prevailing ideas. Material conditions shape consciousness, not vice versa.

**Hold-Up Problem**: One party exploits other's sunk investment through ex-post opportunism. After relationship-specific investment made, partner can demand better terms because alternatives are costly. Example: Supplier builds factory for specific buyer; buyer then demands lower prices. Solution: Vertical integration (bring activity in-house).

**Honor Culture**: Cultural pattern emphasizing aggressive defense of reputation. Emerges in herding societies (livestock easily stolen; weak formal law; must credibly signal willingness to retaliate). Persists after ecological cause disappears. US South example: Scottish-Irish herders brought honor culture; persists despite urbanization.

**Horizontal Transmission**: Cultural learning from peers. Fast spread, variable fidelity. Examples: Fashion, slang, technology adoption, social media trends. Contrast with vertical (parent-child) and oblique (elder-to-younger).

## I

**Incentive Compatibility**: Mechanism design property where truthful reporting is best strategy. Dominant strategy IC (truth best regardless of others). Bayesian IC (truth best given beliefs about others). When mechanisms aren't IC, agents lie and game system; resources wasted on strategizing.

**Inclusive Institutions** (Acemoglu & Robinson): Institutions protecting rights, enforcing contracts, distributing power broadly. Secure property rights for many (not just elite), rule of law, pluralistic politics, open entry, creative destruction permitted. Enable investment, innovation, talent allocation. Examples: US, UK, Scandinavia, Japan, South Korea, Botswana.

**Increasing Returns**: The more people use something, the more valuable it becomes. Creates network effects, positive feedback, multiple equilibria, path dependence, lock-in. Examples: QWERTY keyboard, VHS, Windows, Facebook. Contrast with diminishing returns (traditional economics assumption).

**Individual Rationality (IR)**: Mechanism design constraint that agents prefer participating over not participating. Ex-ante IR (before knowing type), Interim IR (after knowing type, before mechanism), Ex-post IR (after mechanism, in every outcome). Ex-post strongest/hardest to satisfy.

**Innovators**: First group to adopt innovations (2.5%). Risk-tolerant, affluent, cosmopolitan, connected beyond local community. Comfortable with uncertainty. Technically savvy. Financially secure to absorb losses.

**Institution** (North): "Rules of the game in a society" — humanly devised constraints structuring interaction. Reduce uncertainty by providing stable framework. Include formal (constitutions, laws, regulations) and informal (norms, customs, trust). Determine incentive structure; shape whether societies prosper or fail.

**Institutional Complementarities**: Institutions reinforce each other as systems. Can't transplant one without others. Varieties of capitalism: Liberal market (US/UK) and coordinated market (Germany/Japan) have different but internally consistent bundles. Changing one institution creates pressure on complementary ones.

**Institutional Sclerosis** (Olson): Over time, special interest groups accumulate, each capturing small benefits while imposing diffuse costs. Regulations accumulate; rules rarely removed; growth slows as resources go to rent-seeking. Evidence: Post-WWII, Germany/Japan (interest groups destroyed) grew faster than Britain/US (groups intact).

**Institutional Transplantation**: Copying institutions across countries. Often fails due to: Missing complementarities, informal institutions differ, capacity constraints, political economy resistance. Successful examples (Japan's Meiji, Singapore, South Korea, Botswana) involved selective borrowing and local adaptation.

**Intergenerational Elasticity (IGE)**: Percentage of parental income advantage transmitted to children. IGE=0 (perfect mobility; parent income doesn't predict child's). IGE=1 (no mobility; perfectly transmitted). Denmark ~0.15, US ~0.47, China ~0.60. Measure of equality of opportunity.

**Intergenerational Mobility**: Movement between positions across generations. Your position relative to parents'. Contrasts with intragenerational (within lifetime). Absolute (material improvement) vs relative (rank change). Great Gatsby Curve: Higher inequality → lower mobility.

**Intersectionality** (Crenshaw): Overlapping identities creating unique experiences not captured by single-axis analysis. Black woman's experience isn't simply Black experience + woman experience. Race, gender, class, sexuality, disability intersect. Example: Black women earn 70% of white men (not 82% gender gap or 80% racial gap).

**Iron Cage** (Weber): Bureaucratic rationalization traps people in dehumanizing systems. Rules and efficiency dominate; meaning and freedom lost. Modernity's dialectic: Rationalization necessary for progress but creates cage confining human spirit.

**Iron Law of Oligarchy** (Michels): All organizations, even democratic ones, inevitably develop oligarchic leadership. "Who says organization says oligarchy." Mechanisms: Technical necessity (coordination requires leadership), psychological (masses apathetic; leaders attached to power), structural (leaders control information, agenda, resources).

**Isomorphism** (DiMaggio & Powell): Organizations becoming similar through: Coercive (laws/regulations require it), Mimetic (copying successful others under uncertainty), Normative (professional norms spread through training). Result: Organizational fields converge on similar forms.

## J

**Jevons Paradox**: Increasing efficiency of resource use can increase total consumption. More efficient engines → cheaper to drive → more driving → total fuel consumption increases despite efficiency gains. Examples: LED lighting, fuel efficiency, data storage. Efficiency doesn't guarantee conservation.

## K

**Knowledge Problem**: See Hayek's Knowledge Problem.

## L

**Laggards**: Last group to adopt innovations (16%). Traditional, suspicious of change, limited resources, isolated from social networks. Adopt only when no alternative or strong pressure. Often don't adopt at all.

**Late Majority**: Fourth group to adopt innovations (34%). Skeptical, wait until most have adopted. Economic necessity or peer pressure drives adoption. Risk-averse. "Majority" has demonstrated it works.

**Latent Function** (Merton): Unintended, unrecognized consequences of institutions. Schools educate (manifest) but also provide childcare, facilitate dating, transmit class culture (latent). Crime clarifies moral boundaries (latent). Poverty provides cheap labor, motivates work (latent).

**Legal Capacity**: State's ability to enforce laws through courts, contracts, property rights. Weak legal capacity → contracts not enforced, property insecure, disputes violent. Strong: Netherlands, New Zealand. Weak: Venezuela, Zimbabwe.

**Legitimacy**: Belief that authority rightfully exercises power. Weber's authority types differ in legitimation basis: Traditional (custom), Charismatic (personal qualities), Rational-legal (proper procedures). Institutions require legitimacy to function; coercion alone insufficient.

**Libertarian Paternalism** (Thaler & Sunstein): Design choice environments steering toward better decisions while preserving freedom. "Nudging" through defaults, salience, social proof, simplification, commitment devices. Preserve opt-out. Controversial: Who decides "better"?

**Lock-In**: Trapped in suboptimal standard due to switching costs and network effects. QWERTY keyboard, employer-sponsored health insurance, legal traditions, VHS, Windows. Path dependence creates inertia even when better alternatives exist.

**Logrolling**: Vote trading. "I'll support your bridge if you support my military base." Can be efficient (intensity of preference) or wasteful (pork-barrel spending). Enables coalitions but enables projects exceeding benefits.

## M

**Manifest Function** (Merton): Intended, recognized consequences of institutions. Schools educate. Hospitals heal. Police maintain order. Contrast with latent (unintended) functions.

**Market Design** (Roth): Applying mechanism design to problems where prices don't clear markets. Kidneys (illegal to sell), medical residencies (unpriced), school choice (shouldn't depend on ability to pay). Use matching algorithms, queuing, priority rules instead of prices.

**Market Failure**: Markets fail to achieve efficient allocation when: Public goods (non-excludable, non-rival), Externalities (costs/benefits to third parties), Information asymmetry (adverse selection, moral hazard), Natural monopoly (declining average cost), Incomplete markets (risk can't be insured). Institutions can correct failures.

**Mechanical Solidarity** (Durkheim): Cohesion through similarity. Pre-modern societies: Everyone does same things, shares same beliefs. Division of labor minimal. Collective consciousness strong. Contrast with organic solidarity (cohesion through interdependence).

**Mechanism Design**: Designing rules to achieve desired outcomes. "Reverse game theory" — given desired outcome, what rules produce it? Applications: Auctions (spectrum), matching (residencies, kidneys, schools), voting, platforms. Revelation principle simplifies analysis.

**Median Voter Theorem**: In two-candidate elections with single-dimensional policy space, both candidates converge to median voter's position. Explains why Democrats and Republicans seem similar. Limitations: Assumes single dimension, informed voters, ignores primaries.

**Meme** (Dawkins): Cultural analogue to gene — unit of cultural information replicating through imitation. Ideas, tunes, catchphrases, fashions spread like viruses. Subject to variation, selection, transmission. Limitations: Unlike genes, memes lack clear boundaries, fidelity unclear, transmission mechanism imprecise.

**Meritocracy**: System where positions allocated by talent and effort, not birth or connections. Ideal: Equal opportunity, rewards skill. Reality: Reproduces inequality when wealthy can invest more in children's human capital. "Meritocratic" selection advantages already-privileged.

**Moral Hazard**: Hidden action after contracting where agent takes unobservable risks. Insurance makes people take more risks (have insurance → drive carelessly). Bailouts make banks take excessive risks (too big to fail). Monitoring and incentives address but don't eliminate.

**Motherhood Penalty**: Women's wages decrease ~4% per child. Mechanisms: Employers assume mothers less committed; mothers reduce hours; career interruptions. Contrast with fatherhood bonus (~6% increase).

## N

**Nash Equilibrium**: Strategy profile where no player can improve by unilateral deviation (given others' strategies). Stable outcome. Prisoner's dilemma: Mutual defection is Nash equilibrium (but not optimal). Many games have multiple equilibria; some have none (mixed strategy equilibria exist).

**Nested Enterprises** (Ostrom): Multi-level governance for larger systems. Local, regional, national tiers. Addresses problems at appropriate scale. Example: Watershed management with local irrigation, regional river management, national environmental policy.

**Network Effects**: Product/service becomes more valuable as more people use it. Telephones, fax machines, social networks, platforms. Creates increasing returns, positive feedback, winner-take-all dynamics. Drives lock-in and path dependence.

**New Institutional Economics (NIE)**: Bridge between economics and sociology. Coase, North, Williamson, Ostrom. Key insights: Transaction costs determine organizational boundaries, property rights drive development, institutions are path-dependent, commons can be self-governed.

**Nudge**: Choice architecture steering toward better decisions while preserving freedom. Default (opt-out organ donation), salience (calorie counts), social proof ("most guests reuse towels"), simplification (pre-filled forms), commitment devices (Save More Tomorrow), feedback (energy comparison). Small effects but preserves autonomy.

## O

**Oblique Transmission**: Cultural learning from non-parent elders (teachers, mentors, community elders). Medium speed, medium fidelity. Formal education, apprenticeships, professional training. Quality control through institutional gatekeeping.

**Occupational Segregation**: Concentration of demographic groups in particular occupations. Women in nursing, teaching, social work. Men in engineering, construction, tech. Within same field, segregation by level (women nurses, men doctors). Contributes to wage gaps.

**Oligarchy**: Government by small elite group. Michels' iron law: All organizations become oligarchic. The question isn't whether oligarchy but what kind: Accountable vs unaccountable, rotating vs permanent, self-interested vs mission-driven.

**Organic Solidarity** (Durkheim): Cohesion through interdependence and specialization. Modern societies: Everyone does different things; depends on others. Division of labor extensive. Collective consciousness weak. New form of solidarity through mutual dependence.

**Ostrom's Design Principles**: 8 principles for successful commons governance: (1) Clearly defined boundaries, (2) Rules match local conditions, (3) Collective choice, (4) Monitoring, (5) Graduated sanctions, (6) Conflict resolution, (7) Right to organize, (8) Nested enterprises. Examples: Swiss alpine meadows, Maine lobster fisheries, irrigation systems.

## P

**Palma Ratio**: Income share of top 10% divided by bottom 40%. More sensitive to extremes than Gini. Named for José Gabriel Palma who observed middle 50% typically gets ~50% across countries. Finland ~0.87, US ~1.81, Brazil ~3.04.

**Parkinson's Law**: Work expands to fill time available; bureaucratic staff grows regardless of workload. Observed in British naval bureaucracy (ships declined but admiralty staff increased). Self-interest of bureaucrats drives expansion.

**Participation Constraint**: See Individual Rationality.

**Path Dependence**: Early choices constrain future options through sunk costs, learning effects, coordination effects, adaptive expectations. QWERTY keyboard, legal traditions, healthcare systems. History matters; can't costlessly switch equilibria. Increasing returns drive lock-in.

**Patronage**: Distributing government jobs to political supporters rather than merit. "Spoils system" in US pre-1883. President Garfield assassinated by disappointed office-seeker. Pendleton Act (1883) created civil service merit system. Modern reversal: Increasing political appointees.

**Peter Principle**: People promoted to level of incompetence. Excellent engineer becomes mediocre manager (different skills required at different levels). Promotions based on past performance, not future role requirements.

**Political Opportunity Structure** (Tarrow, McAdam): Movements succeed when: Elite divisions, institutional access, declining repression, influential allies create favorable conditions. Example: Civil Rights Movement timing (1950s-60s) benefited from Northern Democratic need for Black votes, sympathetic Supreme Court, declining Southern dominance, Cold War embarrassment.

**Polycentricity** (Ostrom): Multiple overlapping governance jurisdictions rather than single hierarchy. Advantages: Experimentation, competition, redundancy, local knowledge, flexibility. Examples: Swiss cantons, US federalism, international organizations, internet governance. Criticisms: Coordination problems, inequality, externalities, capture.

**Post-Materialist Values** (Inglehart): As basic material needs met, people prioritize "post-materialist" concerns: Environmentalism, human rights, participation, quality of life. Explains Green parties, LGBTQ rights, environmentalism in wealthy democracies. Cohort replacement drives change (raised in prosperity vs scarcity).

**Power** (Weber): Ability to achieve outcomes despite resistance. Three dimensions: Class (economic), status (social prestige), power (political authority). Relational (exists in relationships, not individuals). CEOs have power over workers; workers collectively have power over CEOs.

**Prestige Bias**: Copying high-status individuals' behaviors in cultural transmission. Efficient heuristic (prestigious people filtered successful behaviors) but spreads irrelevant behaviors (Warren Buffett's diet has nothing to do with investment success).

**Principal-Agent Problem**: Conflict of interest when one party (principal) delegates to another (agent). Information asymmetry creates: Moral hazard (hidden action), adverse selection (hidden information), hold-up (ex-post opportunism). Solutions: Monitoring, incentives, screening, signaling, bonding. No perfect solution.

**Prisoner's Dilemma**: Game where mutual defection is equilibrium despite mutual cooperation being better. Dominant strategy: Defect (regardless of what other does, defection better). But both defecting worse than both cooperating. Classic social dilemma. Escape through: Repetition, communication, reputation, institutions, altruism.

**Proletariat** (Marx): Working class selling labor for wages. Don't own means of production. Exploited by bourgeoisie (surplus value extraction). Marx predicted: Increasing immiseration → class consciousness → revolution. Didn't happen; middle class expanded instead.

**Property Rights**: Legal rights to use, transfer, and exclude others from assets. Secure property rights → investment (won't be expropriated). Inclusive institutions protect broad property rights. Extractive institutions concentrate rights in elite hands. Acemoglu/Johnson/Robinson: Property rights are key determinant of development.

**Protestant Ethic** (Weber): Calvinist theology (predestination, worldly asceticism) fostered attitudes conducive to capitalism. Believe God predetermined salvation; worldly success signals likely salvation → pressure to work hard, live frugally, reinvest profits. Culture shapes economy (not monocausal but interactive).

**Public Choice Theory** (Buchanan, Tullock): Applying economic self-interest models to political behavior. Politicians, bureaucrats, voters are self-interested agents, not benevolent servants. Government failure parallels market failure: Rent-seeking, rational ignorance, concentrated benefits with diffuse costs, bureaucratic budget-maximizing.

**Public Good**: Non-excludable (can't prevent non-payers from benefiting) and non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce others'). Examples: National defense, clean air, knowledge, lighthouse. Markets underprovide (can't capture benefits). Solution: Government provision funded by taxation.

## Q

No major terms begin with Q in this guide.

## R

**Rational-Legal Authority** (Weber): Legitimacy based on formal rules and proper procedures, not tradition or personal qualities. Modern states, bureaucracies, corporations. Impersonal — rules apply regardless of who you are. Dominant form in modernity.

**Rational Ignorance**: Voters rationally stay uninformed because information costs (time, effort to learn) exceed benefits (influence is near-zero). Each vote barely matters in large elections; why invest in learning? Politicians exploit ignorance with misleading claims. Public choice theory concept.

**Rationalization** (Weber): Historical process replacing tradition, emotion, charisma with instrumental reason. Calculability, efficiency, control, predictability dominate. "Disenchantment of the world" — mystery and meaning replaced by technical rationality. Creates iron cage.

**Regulatory Capture** (Stigler): Regulated industries co-opting regulators. Mechanisms: Information asymmetry (industry knows more), revolving door (regulators seek industry jobs), concentrated benefits vs diffuse costs, expertise (regulation requires industry knowledge), resources (industry funds lobbying). Examples: FAA-Boeing, SEC-Wall Street, FDA-pharma.

**Rent-Seeking**: Expending resources to capture transfers rather than create value. Lobbying for tariffs, subsidies, regulations protecting incumbents. Socially wasteful (resources fight over pie slices rather than enlarge pie). Tullock's paradox: Why isn't more spent on rent-seeking given potential gains?

**Repatrimonialization** (Fukuyama): Converting impersonal public institutions into personal fiefdoms. Patronage, nepotism, clientelism replace merit-based bureaucracy. Constant pressure to repatrimonialize even in modern states. Examples: Increasing political appointees, revolving door, family members as advisors.

**Repugnant Markets** (Sandel): Things "money can't buy" due to ethical constraints: Organs, babies, votes, citizenship. Can't use prices to allocate but resources scarce. Mechanism design creates non-price allocation: Matching algorithms (kidneys), queuing (waiting lists), priority rules.

**Resource Mobilization Theory** (McCarthy & Zald): Social movements succeed through organizational capacity (money, members, media, leadership, alliances), not grievances alone. Grievances always present; what varies is resources. Civil Rights Movement had churches, NAACP, SCLC, students, Northern allies.

**Revelation Principle** (Myerson): Any outcome achievable by any mechanism can also be achieved by direct revelation mechanism where agents truthfully report private information. Simplifies mechanism design enormously — just check truth-telling equilibrium.

**Revenue Equivalence Theorem**: Under standard assumptions (risk-neutral bidders, independent private values, symmetric), all auction formats yield same expected revenue. Implication: Choose format based on other criteria (simplicity, speed, robustness to collusion), not revenue.

## S

**Satisficing** (Simon): Choosing "good enough" options rather than optimal. Bounded rationality prevents global optimization; people stop searching when find acceptable option. Contrast with maximizing (traditional economics). Behavioral economics foundation.

**Selective Incentives** (Olson): Benefits available only to contributors, solving free-rider problem. Union example: Healthcare, training, legal representation only for dues-paying members. Converts public good (wage increases to all) into club good (benefits to members only).

**Shadow of the Future**: Future interactions matter, enabling cooperation in repeated games. If game ends, terminal period has no future → defection. If interactions continue (or high probability thereof), cooperation possible through tit-for-tat and similar strategies.

**Signaling** (Spence): Agent credibly reveals quality. Education signals ability (smart people get degrees) more than creating it. Employers can't observe ability directly; degrees provide imperfect signal. Implication: Some education spending wasteful (costly signaling not skill development). But individuals rationally invest to signal.

**Sludge** (Sunstein): Friction deliberately designed to prevent choices. Canceling subscriptions is hard, claiming benefits requires complex forms. "Dark nudging" — using choice architecture against people's interests. Opposite of helpful nudges.

**Social Capital**: Networks, connections, reciprocity norms facilitating cooperation. Bonding (within groups — support, solidarity) vs bridging (across groups — information, opportunities). Granovetter's weak ties, Putnam's civic engagement, Burt's structural holes. Converts into economic capital (networks → jobs) and cultural capital (elite schools → elite culture).

**Social Facts** (Durkheim): External phenomena constraining individual behavior. Laws, norms, language, money, institutions exist before you, persist after you, exert force on you. Can't "opt out" of inflation, social stigma. Have causal power independent of individual psychology.

**Social Mobility**: Movement between positions in stratification system. Intergenerational (relative to parents) vs intragenerational (within lifetime). Absolute (material improvement) vs relative (rank change). Great Gatsby Curve: Higher inequality → lower mobility.

**Social Proof**: Nudge showing what others do. "Most guests reuse towels" increases reuse 10-30%. Conformity + information about appropriate behavior. Works through: Social comparison, descriptive norms.

**Social Stratification**: Systematic ranking of groups in hierarchy of wealth, power, prestige. Universal but varies in form (caste vs class), degree (more/less equal), rigidity (mobility possible?), legitimacy (accepted or contested).

**Stable Matching**: No unmatched pair prefers each other to assigned partners. Gale-Shapley algorithm produces stable matchings. Unstable matchings unravel (pairs defect to form better matches outside system).

**Stag Hunt**: Coordination game where high payoff from mutual cooperation but safe payoff from defection. Hunt stag together (high payoff) or individually hunt hares (safe, lower). Best to cooperate if confident partner will; best to defect if uncertain. Multiple equilibria.

**State Capacity**: Government's ability to implement policies, collect taxes, provide services, enforce rules. Dimensions: Extractive (tax collection), administrative (policy implementation), coercive (violence monopoly), legal (contract enforcement). Strong: Denmark, Singapore. Weak: Somalia, Afghanistan.

**Status** (Weber): Social prestige/honor. Status groups share lifestyles, consumption patterns. Exclude others through social closure (country clubs, elite schools, residential segregation, marriage). Status correlates with class but independent — professors have high status, moderate wealth.

**Strategy-Proof**: Can't benefit from lying about preferences. Gale-Shapley deferred acceptance is strategy-proof for proposing side. Vickrey auction is strategy-proof (truth-telling dominant). Gibbard-Satterthwaite: All non-dictatorial voting rules not strategy-proof.

**Streisand Effect**: Attempting to suppress information causes wider spread. Named for Barbra Streisand's attempt to suppress house photos (lawsuit drew massive attention). Censorship often backfires.

**Structural Holes** (Burt): Gaps between disconnected networks. People bridging holes have informational and control advantages. Can broker between groups, arbitrage information, see opportunities others miss.

**Structural Racism**: Institutional policies/practices producing racial inequality regardless of individual intent. Redlining, school funding via property taxes, criminal justice disparities, hiring discrimination. Structure, not just attitudes.

**Structure vs Agency**: Does structure (positions, institutions, norms) determine behavior? Or do agents (individuals with free will) create/change structures? Synthesis (Giddens, Bourdieu): Structures shape agents who reproduce and transform structures.

**Success Bias**: Copying successful individuals' behaviors regardless of whether behaviors caused success. Correlation ≠ causation. Bill Gates dropped out of Harvard; doesn't mean dropout advice generalizes. Survivorship bias (don't see failures who did same thing).

**Symbolic Capital** (Bourdieu): Prestige, recognition, legitimacy, honor. Accumulated through other capitals + recognition. Legitimizes other capitals. Awards, titles, reputation, "brand name." Harvard degree is symbolic capital (recognition of cultural capital).

**Symbolic Interactionism**: Society constructed through meaningful face-to-face interactions. People act based on meanings they assign; meanings emerge through interaction. Mead, Blumer, Goffman. Focus on micro-level meaning-making. Critique: Neglects macro structures, power.

## T

**Tipping Point**: Threshold beyond which change becomes self-sustaining. Adoption spreads on own momentum without promotion. Granovetter's threshold model: If thresholds vary smoothly (0%, 1%, 2%...), cascade occurs. If gap in distribution, cascade stops.

**Tit-for-Tat** (Axelrod): Cooperate first, then mirror opponent's last move. Properties: Nice (never defects first), retaliatory (punishes defection immediately), forgiving (returns to cooperation after punishment), clear (simple, predictable). Winner of repeated prisoner's dilemma tournaments.

**Total Institution** (Goffman): Places controlling all aspects of life. Prisons, mental hospitals, military, monasteries. Strip away previous identities; impose new ones through: Isolation, surveillance, mortification, programming. Goffman's *Asylums*: Mental hospitals created behaviors they claimed to treat.

**Tragedy of the Commons** (Hardin): Shared resource overexploited because costs socialized. Each herder benefits from additional cow; costs (overgrazing) shared by all. As users increase, individual cost decreases, making exploitation more attractive. Resource collapses. Examples: Overfishing, deforestation, groundwater depletion, antibiotic resistance.

**Transaction Costs** (Coase, Williamson): Costs of searching, negotiating, monitoring, enforcing agreements. Firms exist because markets have transaction costs. When transaction costs exceed internal coordination costs, hierarchical governance (firms) beats markets. Institutions lower transaction costs (property rights, contracts, courts).

**Transitivity**: If A > B and B > C, then A > C. Social preferences should be coherent. Arrow's impossibility: Can't guarantee transitivity with other fairness conditions. Condorcet paradox: Majority preferences can be intransitive (rock-paper-scissors).

**Two-Sided Platform**: Market connecting two sides with cross-side network effects. Uber (drivers-riders), Airbnb (hosts-guests), Amazon (sellers-buyers). Design elements: Matching algorithms, pricing (often subsidize one side), trust mechanisms (ratings), governance rules, network effects.

## U

**Unintended Consequences**: Interventions produce unexpected results by changing incentives. Cobra effect, Goodhart's law, Jevons paradox, Streisand effect. Every institutional design creates new incentives agents respond to, often in unpredictable ways. Humility about design required.

## V

**Varieties of Capitalism** (Hall & Soskice): Institutional clusters: Liberal market economies (US, UK: flexible labor, shareholder primacy, equity finance, general skills, radical innovation) vs coordinated market economies (Germany, Japan: coordinated labor, stakeholder model, bank finance, specific skills, incremental innovation). Complementary bundles; can't transplant one element alone.

**Vertical Transmission**: Cultural learning parent-to-child. Slow, high fidelity. Examples: Language, religion, food preferences, political orientation. Tested across generations, adapted to local conditions. Conservative (preserves culture) but slow to adapt to change.

**Verstehen** (Weber): Interpretive understanding of meaningful action. Unlike natural sciences, social science must understand meaning from actors' perspectives. Why vote this way? Need to understand their beliefs, values, interpretation. Doesn't abandon causal explanation but explanation must account for meaning.

**Vetocracy** (Fukuyama): System with so many veto points that action is blocked. US example: President, House, Senate, courts, filibuster, committee chairs, interest groups, federalism, bureaucracy all can block. Checks and balances → gridlock. 18th century design doesn't fit 21st century problems.

**Vickrey Auction**: Second-price sealed bid. Highest bid wins but pays second-highest price. Truth-telling is dominant strategy (overbidding and underbidding can hurt, can't help). Efficient allocation, strategy-proof. Used in eBay, AdWords, stamp auctions.

**Virtuous/Vicious Cycle**: Self-reinforcing patterns. Virtuous: Tax capacity → services → development → more tax revenue. Trust → cooperation → prosperity → more trust. Vicious: Low capacity → poor services → low development → low taxes. Distrust → defection → poverty → more distrust.

## W

**Washington Consensus**: 1990s neoliberal reforms (privatization, deregulation, trade liberalization, fiscal discipline) IMF/World Bank imposed on developing countries. Often failed because: Assumed institutional capacity that didn't exist, ignored political economy (reforms created losers), didn't adapt to context, created crony capitalism where institutions weak.

**Wealth Gap**: Wealth inequality much higher than income inequality. Income is flow (annual); wealth is stock (accumulated). Wealth Gini US ~0.85 vs income Gini ~0.39. Mechanisms: Wealth accumulates across lifetimes, generates more wealth (returns), transmits across generations (inheritance), tax policy often favors wealth.

**Winner's Curse**: Winner of auction likely overpaid. Occurs in common-value auctions where value uncertain and same for everyone (oil field, company acquisition). Winner is person with most optimistic estimate; likely overestimated. Solution: Bid below expected value; English auction reveals information reducing curse.

**World Values Survey**: Survey covering 100+ countries, 90% of world population since 1981. Maps countries on two dimensions: Survival ↔ self-expression, Traditional ↔ secular-rational. Economic development predicts values but cultural heritage creates enduring clusters (Protestant, Catholic, Confucian, Islamic).

## X, Y, Z

**Zero-Sum**: One person's gain is another's loss. Redistribution, positional goods, status competition. Contrast with positive-sum (trade, cooperation, innovation create value for all). Politics often treated as zero-sum even when not. Rent-seeking is negative-sum (resources wasted fighting over pie).

## Institutional Frameworks Comparison

| Framework | Key Question | Core Concepts | Main Thinkers | Policy Implications |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **New Institutional Economics** | Why do institutions matter? | Transaction costs, property rights, path dependence, contracts | North, Coase, Williamson, Ostrom | Secure property rights, reduce transaction costs, respect path dependence |
| **Political Order** | How do states develop and decay? | State capacity, vetocracy, repatrimonialization, elite competition | Fukuyama, Tilly, Skocpol | Build administrative capacity, prevent patronage, maintain checks and balances |
| **Why Nations Fail** | Why are some nations rich and others poor? | Inclusive vs extractive institutions, creative destruction, colonial origins | Acemoglu & Robinson | Establish inclusive institutions, permit creative destruction, constrain elites |
| **Governing the Commons** | How are shared resources managed? | 8 design principles, polycentricity, community governance | Ostrom, Hardin, Olson | Clear boundaries, local participation, graduated sanctions, nested governance |
| **Varieties of Capitalism** | Why do institutional clusters differ across countries? | Complementarities, liberal vs coordinated markets, comparative advantage | Hall & Soskice, Streeck | Recognize complementarities, don't transplant isolated institutions, leverage comparative advantage |
| **Collective Action** | Why don't large groups cooperate voluntarily? | Free-riding, selective incentives, public goods, prisoner's dilemma | Olson, Ostrom, Hardin | Selective incentives, coercion for large groups, facilitate small-group cooperation |
| **Public Choice** | How do self-interested actors behave in politics? | Rent-seeking, rational ignorance, median voter, bureaucratic incentives | Buchanan, Tullock, Niskanen | Constitutional constraints, transparency, competition, realistic expectations |
| **Mechanism Design** | How to design rules achieving desired outcomes? | Incentive compatibility, revelation principle, auctions, matching | Vickrey, Roth, Myerson, Wilson & Milgrom | Design incentive-compatible mechanisms, use auctions/matching where appropriate |
