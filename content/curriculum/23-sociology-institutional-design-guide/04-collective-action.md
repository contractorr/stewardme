# Collective Action & Social Dilemmas

## Overview

Many of humanity's greatest challenges — climate change, overfishing, antibiotic resistance, public goods provision, pollution, pandemic response — are collective action problems: situations where individually rational behavior leads to collectively irrational outcomes. Understanding why cooperation fails, and how institutions can make it succeed, is central to institutional design.

The core paradox: What's rational for each individual is irrational for the group. Each fisherman benefits from catching more fish; collectively, overfishing depletes the stock. Each driver benefits from driving; collectively, everyone sits in traffic. Each country benefits from emitting carbon; collectively, climate destabilizes. Each person benefits from not getting vaccinated (avoiding risks); collectively, herd immunity fails.

Solving collective action problems is the fundamental challenge of social organization. Families, firms, nations, and the global community all must overcome the tension between individual and collective rationality. Institutions — rules that change incentives — are the primary mechanism.

## The Collective Action Problem

Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965): Rational individuals will **not** voluntarily act in their group's interest unless the group is small or coercion/selective incentives exist.

**Why?** Each individual reasons: "My contribution makes negligible difference to the outcome, but the cost to me is real. Whether others contribute or not, I'm better off free-riding."

This logic applies whenever:
1. **Non-excludability**: Can't prevent non-contributors from benefiting
2. **Large groups**: Individual contribution is negligible
3. **Rational calculation**: Individuals weigh personal costs vs personal benefits (ignoring collective)

**Classic examples**:

**Union membership**: Why pay dues if you benefit from wage increases negotiated by the union anyway? If everyone reasons this way, the union collapses — but each individual is acting rationally given others' likely behavior.

**Voting**: Your single vote almost never changes an outcome (probability ~1 in 10 million in a presidential election). Costs of voting (time, effort, information) are certain; benefits are near-zero. Yet millions vote — paradox of voting.

**Climate action**: Your personal emissions reduction costs you (harder transportation, higher expenses, lifestyle changes) but makes negligible difference to global climate. China and India emit more regardless of your choice. Rational to defect (emit freely), but if everyone defects, catastrophe.

**Public radio donation**: You can listen regardless of whether you donate. Costs of donating are real (money); benefits whether you donate or not (programming continues). Rational to free-ride, but if everyone does, no funding.

**Tax compliance**: If tax enforcement is weak, you benefit from public services (roads, schools, defense) whether or not you pay. Why pay if others pay and enforcement is unlikely? But if everyone evades, government collapses.

### Olson's Solutions

**Small groups**: Your contribution is noticeable; free-riding is visible. In a 5-person team project, shirking is obvious. In a class of 500, anonymity enables free-riding. Size matters: small groups can sustain cooperation through monitoring and social pressure.

**Selective incentives**: Benefits available only to contributors. Union example: healthcare, training, legal representation available only to dues-paying members. Couldn't be provided to non-members even if union wanted to. Converts public good (wage increases benefiting all) into club good (selective benefits to members only).

**Coercion**: Mandatory participation. Taxation isn't voluntary — it's enforced with fines and imprisonment. Military draft isn't voluntary — it's enforced with criminal penalties. Mandatory vaccination laws use coercion to achieve herd immunity. Coercion solves free-rider problem but requires state capacity.

### Applications

**Interest group politics**:
- **Narrow interests** (sugar producers, defense contractors): Small groups with large per-capita stakes → organize easily, lobby effectively
- **Diffuse interests** (consumers, taxpayers): Large groups with small per-capita stakes → struggle to organize, get exploited

Result: Concentrated benefits, diffuse costs. Sugar subsidies cost taxpayers billions but save each household ~$10/year — too small to organize against. Sugar producers gain millions each — worth lobbying for.

**Environmental movements**:
Olson predicts environmental groups shouldn't exist (diffuse benefits, large group). But they do because:
- Selective incentives (magazines, discounts, prestige of membership)
- Moral motivations (people care about more than narrow self-interest)
- Entrepreneurs mobilize (leaders who bear organizational costs)

**Public goods underprovision**:
Markets underprovide public goods (national defense, basic research, public health) because firms can't capture benefits (non-excludability). Rational firms under-invest. Solution: government provision funded by taxation (coercion).

## Tragedy of the Commons

Garrett Hardin (1968): When a shared resource has no ownership, each user has incentive to overexploit it. Each additional cow on the commons benefits the herder fully while costs (overgrazing) are shared by all.

**Mathematical structure**:
- Individual benefit from exploitation: B (accrues entirely to exploiter)
- Individual cost from degradation: C/N (shared equally among N users)
- Rational to exploit if B > C/N
- But total exploitation = N × B, total cost = C
- If N × B > C, resource degrades

As N increases (more users), C/N decreases (costs diffuse), making exploitation more attractive to each individual. Result: resource collapses even though everyone would be better off with restraint.

**Classic examples**:

**Overfishing**: Atlantic cod collapse (1990s). Each fishing boat rationally maximizes catch. Collectively, stock collapses. Once dominant fishery off Newfoundland reduced by 99%. Moratorium declared 1992. Stock hasn't recovered 30 years later. Economic devastation for fishing communities. Perfect example of commons tragedy.

**Deforestation**: Amazon rainforest, Southeast Asia. Each farmer/company benefits from clearing land for agriculture/logging. Costs (climate change, biodiversity loss, ecosystem disruption) are global and delayed. Result: accelerating deforestation despite knowing long-term consequences.

**Groundwater depletion**: Ogallala Aquifer (US Great Plains) declining because each farmer pumps independently. Rational for each: pumping costs less than surface water, enables profitable irrigation. Collectively: aquifer declining faster than recharge rate, threatening future agriculture.

**Antibiotic resistance**: Each doctor benefits from prescribing antibiotics (satisfies patient, prevents litigation, easy solution). Costs (resistance) are shared globally and delayed. Result: overuse creating resistant bacteria, threatening medicine's ability to treat infections.

**Traffic congestion**: Each driver benefits from driving (convenience, speed vs transit). Costs (congestion) are shared by all drivers. Result: everyone sits in traffic. Adding lanes makes it worse (induced demand).

**Ocean plastic**: Each entity benefits from cheap plastic and externalizing disposal costs. Costs (ocean pollution, marine ecosystem damage) are shared globally. Result: Great Pacific Garbage Patch.

### Hardin's Solutions

**Privatization**: Assign property rights. Owner internalizes costs and benefits, sustainably manages resource. Example: Private forests managed for long-term timber yields. Private ranches maintain wildlife populations.

**Critique**: Many resources can't be privatized (ocean, atmosphere, biodiversity). Privatization can create inequality (access based on ability to pay, not need). Monitoring and enforcement costs.

**Government regulation**: State imposes rules limiting exploitation — quotas, permits, seasonal restrictions. Example: Fishery regulations, emissions caps, water use limits.

**Critique**: Requires state capacity. Regulatory capture (industry shapes rules). Information problems (optimal quota?). Enforcement challenges.

Elinor Ostrom showed a third way: **community self-governance**.

## Ostrom's Design Principles

Elinor Ostrom (Nobel Prize, 2009) studied communities that successfully managed commons for centuries without privatization or government control. She identified 8 design principles predicting success:

| # | Principle | Description | Example | Why It Matters |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------|----------------|
| **1** | **Clearly defined boundaries** | Who's in, who's out, what's the resource | Maine lobster territories | Excludes outsiders, enables monitoring |
| **2** | **Rules match local conditions** | Not one-size-fits-all | Irrigation rules vary by water availability | Efficiency, local knowledge |
| **3** | **Collective choice** | Those affected by rules help make them | Swiss alpine meadows - users set grazing limits | Legitimacy, compliance |
| **4** | **Monitoring** | Rule compliance is observed | Community members patrol fishing grounds | Detects violations, deters cheating |
| **5** | **Graduated sanctions** | Mild penalties first, escalating for repeat offenders | Warning → fine → suspension → expulsion | Forgives mistakes, punishes persistence |
| **6** | **Conflict resolution** | Low-cost, accessible dispute mechanisms | Village elders mediate disputes | Resolves conflicts without destroying cooperation |
| **7** | **Right to organize** | External authorities respect community governance | Government doesn't override local rules | Autonomy enables effective governance |
| **8** | **Nested enterprises** | Multi-level governance for larger systems | Watershed management with local/regional/national tiers | Addresses problems at appropriate scale |

**Examples of successful commons management**:

**Swiss alpine meadows**: Villages collectively managed pastures for centuries. Clear boundaries (village membership), rules match conditions (grazing limits based on land quality), collective choice (village assembly decides), monitoring (community members observe), graduated sanctions (fines escalate for violations), conflict resolution (village council), right to organize (Swiss federalism protects local autonomy), nested (village → canton → federal).

**Maine lobster fisheries**: Informal territorial systems. Fishermen defend territories against outsiders (boundaries), rules vary by harbor (local conditions), fishermen establish norms (collective choice), mutual monitoring (small community), social pressure → violence for violations (graduated sanctions), reputation and mediation (conflict resolution), state recognizes territories (right to organize).

**Irrigation systems**: Nepal, Spain, Philippines. Users build and maintain canals (boundaries clear — who gets water), allocation rules match water availability (seasonal variation, drought rules), users decide allocation (collective choice), water guards monitor (paid by users), sanctions for violators (fines, water cut-off), elders mediate (conflict resolution), government respects systems (right to organize), nested tiers for large systems.

**Wikipedia**: Digital commons. Clear boundaries (anyone can read, account required to edit), rules evolved through community discussion (collective choice), monitoring via recent changes feed and watchlists (monitoring), graduated sanctions (warnings → temporary blocks → permanent bans), conflict resolution (talk pages, mediation, arbitration committee), right to organize (Wikimedia Foundation protects community autonomy), nested (local language Wikipedias + global foundation).

**Open-source software**: Linux, Apache, thousands of projects. Contributors (boundaries), norms and licenses (rules), community-driven development (collective choice), code review and version control (monitoring), social pressure and expulsion (sanctions), project governance structures (conflict resolution), GPL and similar licenses (right to organize), hierarchical project structure (nested).

### Why Ostrom Matters

Overturned Hardin's dichotomy (privatization or government). Showed communities can self-govern commons effectively — often more effectively than markets or states.

**Conditions favoring self-governance**:
- Resource is sufficiently valuable that organization costs are justified
- Community is relatively small and stable (repeated interactions build trust)
- Users depend on resource (incentive to preserve it)
- Monitoring is feasible (resource isn't too large or mobile)
- External authorities don't undermine local rules

**Limitations**:
- Global commons (atmosphere, oceans) harder to self-govern (boundaries unclear, community diffuse)
- Mobile resources (fish stocks, wildlife) challenge monitoring
- Heterogeneous users with conflicting interests complicate collective choice
- Rapid change (technology, population, market integration) disrupts traditional systems

Nonetheless, Ostrom's work revolutionized understanding of collective action. Polycentric governance — multiple overlapping authorities rather than single hierarchy — often outperforms top-down control.

## Game Theory Foundations

### Prisoner's Dilemma

Two suspects arrested. Police offer each a deal: testify against the other.

| | Player B: Cooperate (silent) | Player B: Defect (testify) |
|--|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Player A: Cooperate** | 3, 3 (both get light sentence) | 0, 5 (A gets maximum, B goes free) |
| **Player A: Defect** | 5, 0 (A goes free, B gets maximum) | 1, 1 (both get medium sentence) |

**Dominant strategy**: Defect (regardless of what B does, A is better off defecting).
- If B cooperates, A gets 5 by defecting vs 3 by cooperating → defect
- If B defects, A gets 1 by defecting vs 0 by cooperating → defect

But mutual defection (1,1) is worse for both than mutual cooperation (3,3). This is the dilemma: individual rationality leads to collective irrationality.

**Real-world prisoner's dilemmas**:
- **Arms races**: Each country is safer with more weapons given the other's armament. Both arm; both less safe than if neither armed.
- **Doping in sports**: Each athlete is better off doping given others' choices. All dope; sport degraded, health harmed.
- **Price competition**: Each firm better off lowering price. All lower prices; profits collapse.
- **Environmental compliance**: Each firm better off polluting. All pollute; environment degraded.

**Escaping the dilemma**:
- **Repetition**: In repeated prisoner's dilemma, cooperation can emerge (shadow of the future)
- **Communication**: Allows coordination, though cheap talk isn't automatically credible
- **Reputation**: Future partners care about past behavior
- **Institutions**: Rules change payoffs (fines for defection, rewards for cooperation)
- **Altruism**: Some people care about others' payoffs, not just their own

### Other Social Dilemma Games

| Game | Structure | Key Feature | Real-World Example | Equilibrium |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Stag Hunt** | High payoff from cooperation if both cooperate; safe payoff from defection | Coordination problem, risk | Technology standards, bank runs | Multiple equilibria |
| **Chicken** | Worst outcome if both defect; prefer other to yield | Brinkmanship | Nuclear standoffs, budget negotiations | Mixed strategy |
| **Assurance** | Cooperate only if confident others will | Trust | Climate agreements, Kyoto Protocol | Cooperation if trust |
| **Public Goods** | Contribute to shared benefit or free-ride | Free-riding | Tax compliance, vaccination, public radio | Under-contribution |

**Stag Hunt**: Two hunters can cooperate to catch a stag (high payoff) or individually catch hares (safe, lower payoff). If your partner defects, you get nothing from stag-hunting. Best to hunt stag if confident partner will; best to hunt hare if uncertain.

Real-world: Economic development requires coordinated investment in education, infrastructure, institutions. Each investor wants assurance others invest. Multiple equilibria: high-cooperation development or low-cooperation stagnation.

**Chicken**: Two drivers race toward each other. First to swerve is "chicken" (loses prestige). If neither swerves, both crash (worst outcome). Prefer other to yield, but yielding beats crashing.

Real-world: Cuban Missile Crisis. Neither US nor USSR wanted nuclear war (mutual defection = crash). Each preferred other to back down. Kennedy and Khrushchev played brinkmanship. Resolution required signaling, communication, face-saving (Khrushchev withdrew missiles, Kennedy secretly withdrew US missiles from Turkey).

**Public Goods Game**: N players choose contributions to public good. Total contributions multiplied and divided equally. Individual gets (1/N) × multiplier × total contributions, but pays full cost of own contribution. If multiplier < N, rational to contribute zero. But if all contribute, all benefit.

Real-world: Classroom participation, team projects, Wikipedia editing, open-source contribution, climate action, pandemic response.

### Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma

Robert Axelrod's tournaments (1984): Scholars submitted strategies for repeated prisoner's dilemma. Strategies played against each other in round-robin tournament.

**Winner**: Tit-for-tat (Anatol Rapoport)
- Cooperate on first move
- Then mirror opponent's last move
- If opponent cooperated, cooperate
- If opponent defected, defect

**Properties that made it successful**:
- **Nice**: Never defects first (doesn't exploit cooperative opponents)
- **Retaliatory**: Punishes defection immediately (doesn't get exploited)
- **Forgiving**: Returns to cooperation after one punishment (doesn't hold grudges)
- **Clear**: Strategy is simple and predictable (opponents can learn it)

**Conditions for cooperation in repeated games**:
1. **Shadow of the future**: Interactions continue indefinitely (or high probability of continuation). If interactions end, terminal period has no future, inducing defection, which unravels backwards.
2. **Ability to identify**: Players recognize each other (can't cooperate with strangers you'll never see again)
3. **Ability to remember**: Past behavior is tracked (reputation matters)
4. **Ability to punish**: Defection can be sanctioned (punishment must be credible)

**Folk theorem**: In infinitely repeated games with patient players, any outcome between mutual defection and mutual cooperation can be sustained as equilibrium through appropriate strategies.

**Why cooperation is easier in some settings**:
- **Small communities** (everyone knows everyone): Identification and memory easy
- **Stable populations** (low turnover): Shadow of future long
- **Frequent interactions**: More opportunities to punish
- **Visible behavior**: Monitoring easy

**Why cooperation is harder in other settings**:
- **Large anonymous populations** (cities, internet): Can't identify or remember
- **One-shot interactions** (tourists, online purchases): No shadow of future
- **Infrequent interactions**: Can defect and disappear before punishment
- **Hidden behavior**: Can't monitor to detect defection

Institutions solve these problems:
- **Reputation systems** (eBay ratings, Yelp reviews): Enable memory in large populations
- **Identification** (verified accounts, KYC): Enable recognition
- **Enforcement** (courts, police): Enable punishment when private sanctioning fails
- **Repeat interactions** (long-term contracts, memberships): Create shadow of future

## Voting and Arrow's Impossibility

Kenneth Arrow (1951) proved that no voting system with ≥3 options can simultaneously satisfy all of these reasonable conditions:

1. **Unanimity/Pareto efficiency**: If everyone prefers A to B, A should rank above B
2. **Independence of irrelevant alternatives** (IIA): Ranking of A vs B shouldn't depend on C's presence
3. **Non-dictatorship**: No single voter determines the outcome
4. **Transitivity**: If A > B and B > C, then A > C (social preferences are coherent)
5. **Unrestricted domain**: Any pattern of individual preferences is allowed

**Implication**: All voting systems with ≥3 candidates are imperfect. No aggregation of preferences perfectly represents the "will of the people." This is a fundamental limit on democracy, not a design flaw to be fixed.

**Proof sketch** (simplified):
Arrow showed that satisfying 1, 2, 3, 4 together is impossible. Any voting rule meeting four conditions violates the fifth. Most voting systems violate IIA (adding/removing a candidate changes rankings of others).

**Example of IIA violation**:
- **Scenario 1**: 40% prefer A > B > C, 35% prefer B > C > A, 25% prefer C > B > A
- **Plurality**: A wins (40% vs 35% vs 25%)
- **Scenario 2**: C drops out. Now 40% prefer A > B, 60% prefer B > A
- **Plurality**: B wins
- C's presence/absence changed A vs B outcome — violates IIA

**Gibbard-Satterthwaite theorem** (1973, 1975): Any non-dictatorial voting system with ≥3 candidates is susceptible to strategic voting (voting insincerely to achieve a better outcome).

**Example**:
- Preferences: 35% prefer A > B > C, 33% prefer B > C > A, 32% prefer C > B > A
- **Sincere voting**: A wins with 35% (plurality)
- **Strategic voting**: The 32% who prefer C > B > A vote for B instead (realizing C can't win). B wins with 65%.
- Insincere voting produces better outcome for strategic voters.

**Implications**:
- Voting systems are manipulable — outcomes depend on strategic behavior, not just sincere preferences
- "Will of the people" is ambiguous — different aggregation methods produce different results from same preferences
- Institutional design matters — choosing voting rules has real consequences

**Different voting systems, different outcomes**:

| Preferences | Plurality (most votes) | Ranked-choice (IRV) | Condorcet (head-to-head) | Borda count (points for rank) |
|------------|----------|------------|----------|--------|
| 35% A>B>C, 33% B>C>A, 32% C>B>A | A wins | B wins | B wins | B wins |

Same preferences, different winners depending on voting rule. No "correct" answer.

## Public Choice Theory

James Buchanan (Nobel Prize, 1986) and Gordon Tullock applied economic reasoning to political behavior.

**Core insight**: Politicians, bureaucrats, and voters are self-interested agents, not benevolent public servants. Government failure is as real as market failure.

**Key concepts**:

**Rent-seeking**: Expending resources to capture transfers rather than create value. Lobbying costs society because resources go to redistribution, not production.

Example: Steel tariffs. Domestic steel producers lobby for tariffs on imports. Producers gain $X (higher prices). Consumers lose $X+Y (higher prices + lobbying costs + inefficiency). Lobbying costs Y are pure waste — resources spent fighting over pie slices rather than enlarging pie.

Tullock's paradox: Why isn't more spent on rent-seeking? Potential gains from capture often exceed lobbying expenditure. Answer: Uncertainty of success, free-riding among beneficiaries, norms against blatant rent-seeking.

**Concentrated benefits, diffuse costs**: Small groups with large per-capita stakes organize easily; large groups with small per-capita stakes don't.

Example: Agricultural subsidies. US sugar program costs consumers $3-4 billion/year. Per household: ~$30/year — too small to notice or organize against. Sugar producers gain millions each — worth hiring lobbyists. Result: subsidies persist despite net social loss.

**Rational ignorance**: Learning about policy costs time and effort. Each vote barely matters. So voters rationally stay uninformed. Politicians exploit ignorance with misleading claims.

Evidence: Surveys show voters ignorant of basic facts (which party controls Congress, size of budget deficit, foreign aid as % of budget). Voters overestimate salience of rare events (terrorism, crime) and underestimate common risks (diabetes, traffic accidents).

**Median voter theorem**: In two-candidate elections with single-dimensional policy space and voters voting for nearest candidate, both candidates converge to median voter's position.

Implication: Both parties move to center, ignoring extremes. Explains why Democrats and Republicans often seem similar on many issues.

Limitation: Assumes single dimension (left-right). Real politics is multi-dimensional. Assumes voters informed (contradicts rational ignorance). Ignores primaries (where extremes matter).

**Logrolling**: Vote trading. "I'll support your bridge if you support my military base."

Can be efficient (allows intensity of preference to be expressed) or wasteful (enables pork-barrel spending exceeding benefits).

Example: Omnibus bills load up with unrelated provisions to assemble majority coalition. Many provisions wouldn't pass independently but get bundled.

**Bureaucratic budget maximizing** (William Niskanen): Bureaucrats seek larger budgets regardless of need because budget size determines status, salary, power.

Evidence: Agencies resist budget cuts, expand mandates, create justifications for growth. Defense Department develops new threat assessments to justify spending.

Critique: Assumes bureaucrats control information (principal-agent problem). Reality: Legislative oversight, media scrutiny, competing agencies constrain budget maximization.

### Implications for Institutional Design

Public choice theory suggests pessimism about government solutions to collective action problems. If politicians, bureaucrats, and voters are self-interested, why expect government to serve public interest?

Responses:
1. **Constitutional constraints**: Limit government power to prevent abuse (checks and balances, federalism, rights)
2. **Competition**: Electoral competition, bureaucratic competition, federalism create accountability
3. **Transparency**: Sunshine laws, freedom of information, whistleblower protection reduce rent-seeking
4. **Credible commitment**: Institutions that bind government (independent central banks, judiciary, international treaties)

Public choice doesn't imply libertarianism or anarchism. It implies realistic institutional design acknowledging self-interest rather than assuming benevolence.

## Real-World Applications

### Climate Change as Collective Action

The ultimate tragedy of the commons and prisoner's dilemma:
- **Benefits of emissions**: Local and immediate (economic growth, consumption)
- **Costs of emissions**: Global and delayed (climate damage decades later)
- **Each country's incentive**: Emit freely while others reduce (free-ride)
- **Collective outcome if all defect**: Climate catastrophe

**Challenges**:
- No global sovereign to enforce agreements (unlike national laws)
- Distributional conflicts (who should reduce? Developed countries caused problem but developing countries emit more now)
- Time inconsistency (benefits now, costs later → hyperbolic discounting favors present)
- Uncertainty (exact damages uncertain, creates excuse for inaction)
- Monitoring and verification (can countries cheat on commitments?)

**Paris Agreement approach** (2015):
- Voluntary national commitments (NDCs — Nationally Determined Contributions)
- No enforcement mechanism (can't punish non-compliance)
- Reliance on: Repeated game dynamics, reputation, peer pressure, transparency, domestic political pressure

**Criticisms**:
- Commitments insufficient (NDCs lead to 2.5-3°C warming, well above 1.5°C target)
- No enforcement (countries can defect without penalty)
- Monitoring challenges (emissions data self-reported)
- Free-rider problem unsolved (countries have incentive to defect)

**Alternative approaches**:
- **Carbon border adjustments**: Tariffs on imports from non-complying countries (creates selective incentive to comply)
- **Climate clubs** (William Nordhaus): Coalition of willing countries imposing trade sanctions on non-members
- **Technological solutions**: Make clean energy cheaper than fossil fuels (removes cooperation problem)
- **Geoengineering**: Unilateral intervention by one country (raises governance questions)

### Wikipedia as Governed Commons

Surprising success of collaborative content creation:
- **Tragedy of the commons prediction**: Free-riding, vandalism, decline
- **Actual outcome**: 60+ million articles, 300+ languages, primary information source

**How Ostrom's principles apply**:

1. **Boundaries**: Anyone can read, editing requires account, administrator privileges earned
2. **Rules match conditions**: Different language Wikipedias have different norms, article-specific guidelines
3. **Collective choice**: Community discusses and votes on policy changes
4. **Monitoring**: Recent changes feed, watchlists, bots detect vandalism
5. **Graduated sanctions**: Warnings → temporary blocks (hours/days) → longer blocks → permanent bans
6. **Conflict resolution**: Talk pages for discussion, mediation for disputes, arbitration committee for serious cases
7. **Right to organize**: Wikimedia Foundation protects community autonomy from external interference
8. **Nested**: Local (article), topic (WikiProject), language (Wikipedia), global (Foundation)

**Mechanisms sustaining cooperation**:
- **Intrinsic motivation**: Many contributors motivated by knowledge-sharing, not material rewards
- **Reputation**: Recognition through edit count, barnstars, administrator status
- **Reciprocity**: Contributors receive help when they help others
- **Low costs**: Editing is easy, barriers to entry low
- **Reversibility**: Vandalism easily undone (reduces damage from defection)
- **Technology**: Bots automate monitoring, software makes collaboration easy

**Challenges**:
- **Contributor decline**: Active editors declining since 2007 peak
- **Gender gap**: ~90% of editors are male
- **Systemic bias**: Coverage biased toward topics of interest to contributors (Western, tech-focused)
- **Edit wars**: Controversial topics see repeated reversions
- **Vandalism**: Constant vigilance required

Despite challenges, Wikipedia demonstrates that digital commons can be governed successfully through community norms, monitoring, and graduated sanctions.

## Key Terms

- **Collective Action Problem**: Individual rationality leading to collectively suboptimal outcomes (Olson)
- **Free Rider**: Benefits from others' contributions without contributing
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: Shared resource overexploited because costs are socialized (Hardin)
- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: Game where mutual defection is equilibrium despite mutual cooperation being better
- **Dominant Strategy**: Best response regardless of others' choices
- **Nash Equilibrium**: Strategy profile where no player can improve by unilateral deviation
- **Tit-for-Tat**: Cooperate first, then mirror opponent's last move (Axelrod)
- **Shadow of the Future**: Future interactions matter, enabling cooperation in repeated games
- **Folk Theorem**: In infinitely repeated games, many outcomes sustainable as equilibria
- **Arrow's Impossibility Theorem**: No voting system can satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously
- **Gibbard-Satterthwaite**: All non-dictatorial voting rules susceptible to strategic voting
- **Rent-Seeking**: Expending resources to capture transfers, not create value (Tullock)
- **Public Choice**: Applying economic self-interest models to political behavior (Buchanan)
- **Rational Ignorance**: Voters stay uninformed because information costs exceed benefits
- **Median Voter Theorem**: In two-candidate elections, both converge to median voter position
- **Logrolling**: Vote trading enabling coalitions but potentially enabling wasteful pork
- **Ostrom's Principles**: 8 design principles for successful commons governance
- **Selective Incentives**: Benefits available only to contributors (solving free-rider problem)
- **Concentrated Benefits, Diffuse Costs**: Small groups with high per-capita stakes organize easily; large groups don't

## Summary

Collective action problems arise when individual rationality leads to collective irrationality — the fundamental challenge of social organization. Olson showed that large groups fail to provide public goods without coercion or selective incentives because free-riding is rational. Hardin's tragedy of the commons describes overexploitation of shared resources when costs are socialized.

Game theory formalizes these dilemmas. The Prisoner's Dilemma captures the core structure: defection dominates but mutual cooperation is better. In repeated games, cooperation can emerge through tit-for-tat strategies if the shadow of the future is long enough. Arrow's impossibility theorem and Gibbard-Satterthwaite establish fundamental limits on collective decision-making through voting.

Public choice theory (Buchanan, Tullock) applies self-interest models to politics, revealing government failures parallel to market failures: rent-seeking, rational ignorance, concentrated benefits with diffuse costs, bureaucratic budget-maximizing. Institutional design must account for self-interest, not assume benevolence.

Ostrom demonstrated that communities can self-govern commons through clear boundaries, participatory rule-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, autonomy, and nested governance — a third path beyond privatization and government control. Successful examples include Swiss alpine meadows, Maine lobster fisheries, irrigation systems, Wikipedia, and open-source software.

Real-world applications reveal both the challenge and possibility of collective action. Climate change exemplifies the difficulty: global scope, delayed costs, free-rider incentives, no enforcement mechanism. The Paris Agreement relies on voluntary commitments and reputation rather than coercion. Wikipedia exemplifies success: community governance, monitoring through technology, intrinsic motivation, and Ostrom's principles sustain cooperation among millions.

Understanding collective action problems and institutional solutions is essential for addressing humanity's greatest challenges — from climate and pandemics to public goods provision and commons management. Institutions change incentives, making cooperation individually rational when it would otherwise fail.
