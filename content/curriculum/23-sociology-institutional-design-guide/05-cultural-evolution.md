# Cultural Evolution & Social Change

## Overview

Culture evolves. Norms, beliefs, technologies, and practices change over time through processes analogous to — but distinct from — biological evolution. Understanding cultural evolution explains how societies transform, why some innovations spread and others don't, and how moral progress occurs.

Unlike biological evolution, which operates through genetic transmission across generations spanning decades, cultural evolution occurs through social learning and can produce dramatic changes within a single generation. A person born in 1900 witnessed the invention of radio, television, computers, the internet, and smartphones. Cultural change outpaces genetic change by orders of magnitude.

This chapter examines the mechanisms of cultural transmission, the biases shaping what spreads, modernization's effects on values, how social movements drive change, how innovations diffuse, and the trajectory of moral progress. Understanding cultural evolution is understanding how humanity transforms itself.

## Cultural Evolution Theory

### Dual Inheritance Theory (Boyd & Richerson)

Humans inherit information through two channels:
1. **Genetic**: DNA, biological evolution (slow, millions of years)
2. **Cultural**: Learned behaviors, beliefs, technologies (fast, years to decades)

These channels interact — **gene-culture coevolution**. Cultural practices alter selection pressures on genes, and genetic dispositions shape which cultural variants spread. The relationship is bidirectional and dynamic.

**Key examples**:

**Lactose tolerance**: Most mammals lose the ability to digest lactose (milk sugar) after weaning. But in populations that domesticated cattle and practiced dairy farming (Northern Europe, East Africa), mutations allowing adult lactose digestion spread rapidly — becoming nearly universal in ~10,000 years. Culture (dairy farming) changed genes (lactase persistence). This happened independently in multiple populations: European (LCT gene variant), East African (different regulatory mutations).

Timeline: Cattle domestication ~10,000 years ago → dairy farming creates selective pressure → lactase persistence allele spreads from rare to 90%+ in Northern Europeans. People who could digest milk had nutritional advantages (extra calories, calcium, protein) → more offspring → gene spreads.

**Honor cultures**: Herding societies developed "culture of honor" (aggressive defense of reputation) because herds are easily stolen and formal legal systems are weak in pastoral settings. You must credibly signal willingness to retaliate violently or risk theft. These norms persist centuries after the ecological cause disappeared.

US South: Settled by Scottish-Irish herders who brought honor culture. Experimental evidence (Nisbett & Cohen): Southern men respond to insults with elevated cortisol and aggression; Northern men don't. This persists despite urbanization and legal systems. Culture becomes self-perpetuating through socialization.

**Agriculture and cooperation**: Farming requires delayed gratification (plant now, harvest months later), coordination (irrigation), and property rights. Agricultural societies developed cultural norms supporting these behaviors. Over millennia, this may have selected for genetic variants supporting cooperation and future orientation.

### Memetics (Dawkins)

Richard Dawkins coined "meme" (1976) as a cultural analogue to the gene — a unit of cultural information that replicates through imitation. Ideas, tunes, catchphrases, fashions, and technologies spread like viruses, subject to variation, selection, and transmission.

**Meme characteristics**:
- **Replication**: Copied from mind to mind through imitation, teaching, communication
- **Variation**: Transformed during transmission (like genetic mutation)
- **Selection**: Some variants spread more successfully (like natural selection)
- **Longevity**: Persist in memory
- **Fecundity**: Copied to many recipients
- **Fidelity**: Accuracy of copying

**Examples of successful memes**:
- **Religious ideas**: Afterlife belief spreads because it's psychologically comforting and motivates costly religious commitment
- **Conspiracy theories**: Spread because they're emotionally arousing, explain complex events simply, create in-group solidarity
- **Viral content**: Cat videos, rage bait, inspirational quotes succeed because they trigger strong emotional responses
- **Urban legends**: "Hook hand killer," "kidney theft" spread because they're scary, memorable, have moral lessons

**Limitations**: Unlike genes, memes lack clear boundaries (where does one idea end and another begin?), aren't discretely copied (transmission introduces variation), and don't have clear replication mechanisms. The metaphor is useful but imprecise. Cultural transmission is Lamarckian (acquired traits transmitted) not Darwinian (only inherited traits transmitted).

## Modes of Cultural Transmission

Cultural information flows through distinct channels with different properties:

| Mode | Direction | Speed | Fidelity | Example | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------|-----------|-------|----------|---------|------------|---------------|
| **Vertical** | Parent → child | Slow | High | Language, religion, food preferences, political orientation | Tested across generations, adapted to local conditions | Conservative, slow adaptation |
| **Horizontal** | Peer → peer | Fast | Variable | Fashion, slang, technology, social media trends | Rapid spread, responsive to environment | Error-prone, fads vs lasting change |
| **Oblique** | Elder/teacher → younger | Medium | Medium | Formal education, apprenticeships, professional training | Quality control, expertise transmission | Institutional inertia, gatekeeping |

### Transmission Biases

Not all cultural variants spread equally. Transmission biases determine what gets copied:

**Prestige bias**: Copy the behavior of high-status individuals. If CEOs read certain books, those books become popular regardless of quality. If athletes wear certain brands, those brands gain status. If influencers promote products, sales increase.

**Mechanism**: Prestige is a heuristic for success. Copying prestigious individuals is an efficient shortcut to successful behavior — they've already filtered options. But prestige doesn't guarantee causal connection between behavior and success (successful person may have succeeded despite, not because of, the copied behavior).

**Example**: Warren Buffett's folksy wisdom (drink Coke, eat burgers) gets imitated despite having nothing to do with his investment success. Steve Jobs' black turtleneck became iconic; countless entrepreneurs copied the style without copying his product vision.

**Conformist bias**: Adopt the most common behavior. When in Rome, do as Romans do. Follow the crowd. Do what everyone else does. Majority-rules copying.

**Mechanism**: Conformity reduces social friction, coordinates behavior, and exploits wisdom of crowds (if most people do X, X is probably adaptive in this context). But conformity can lock in harmful practices.

**Example**: Fashion trends — wearing what others wear signals group membership. Language convergence — accents shift toward local norm. Foot binding in Imperial China persisted for ~1,000 years through conformist pressure despite causing disability. Mothers bound daughters' feet because unbinding meant unmarriageability.

**Content bias**: Some ideas are intrinsically easier to remember, more emotionally compelling, or more useful. Disgust-eliciting stories spread faster than neutral ones. Fear-inducing information captures attention. Practical knowledge that improves survival spreads.

**Evolved cognitive biases shape content preferences**:
- **Negativity bias**: Bad news spreads faster than good ("if it bleeds, it leads")
- **Threat detection**: Information about dangers spreads rapidly (predators, poisons, diseases)
- **Social information**: Gossip about others' behavior spreads because it's reproductively relevant
- **Counterintuitive concepts**: Minimally counterintuitive ideas (talking animals, invisible agents) memorable because they violate expectations slightly

**Urban legends exploit content biases**: "Alligators in sewers" (disgusting + surprising), "kidney theft" (threatening + plausible), "poisoned Halloween candy" (harm to children triggers strong emotion).

**Success bias**: Copy behaviors of successful individuals (regardless of whether the behaviors caused success). The problem: Correlation ≠ causation. Successful people's visible behaviors may be irrelevant to their success, but we copy them anyway.

**Survivorship bias**: We see successful people's behaviors but not unsuccessful people who did the same thing. We see Bill Gates dropping out of Harvard and starting Microsoft, so dropout advice spreads — but we don't see the thousands who dropped out and failed.

### Multiple Biases Interact

Real-world cultural transmission combines biases. A behavior spreads if:
- Many people do it (conformist)
- Prestigious people do it (prestige)
- It's emotionally compelling (content)
- Successful people do it (success)

**Example: Smoking decline**
- 1950s: Smoking rate ~45% (conformist bias supports smoking)
- 1960s: Surgeon General report links smoking to cancer (content bias — fear)
- Prestigious actors/athletes continue smoking (prestige bias maintains it)
- 1980s-2000s: Smoking bans, anti-smoking campaigns, celebrity endorsements of quitting (conformist bias shifts, prestige bias reverses)
- 2020s: Smoking rate ~12% (conformist, prestige, content all against smoking)

## Modernization Theory

### Inglehart's Theory of Value Change

Ronald Inglehart: As societies develop economically, values shift predictably:

**Survival values** → **Self-expression values**:
When people face existential insecurity (poverty, violence, disease), they prioritize safety and economic security. As societies develop and existential threats recede, priorities shift toward autonomy, creativity, tolerance, environmental concern, and quality of life.

**Traditional values** → **Secular-rational values**:
Traditional societies emphasize religion, deference to authority, national pride, and traditional family structures. As societies develop, emphasis shifts toward skepticism, individualism, rational inquiry, and acceptance of diverse lifestyles.

**Mechanism**: **Existential security hypothesis**: Values reflect the conditions of one's formative years. People raised in scarcity develop survival values that persist throughout life. Generational replacement drives value change as younger cohorts raised in prosperity replace older cohorts raised in scarcity.

**Evidence**: World Values Survey (1981-present) covers 100+ countries, 90% of world population, 400,000+ respondents. Clear correlation between GDP per capita and values. Time-series shows gradual shift toward self-expression and secular-rational values as countries develop.

**Post-materialist values**: Inglehart's earlier concept (1970s) — as basic material needs are met, people prioritize "post-materialist" concerns: environmentalism, human rights, participation, quality of life. This explains rise of Green parties, LGBTQ rights movements, and environmentalism in wealthy democracies.

### World Values Survey Map

Countries cluster on two dimensions:
- **X-axis**: Survival ↔ Self-expression
- **Y-axis**: Traditional ↔ Secular-rational

| Cluster | Position | Countries | Characteristics |
|---------|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Protestant Europe** | High secular, high self-expression | Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Netherlands | Low religiosity, high tolerance, strong welfare states |
| **English-speaking** | Medium secular, high self-expression | US, UK, Australia, Canada | Individualistic, free-market oriented, religious for development level |
| **Catholic Europe** | Medium secular, medium self-expression | France, Spain, Italy, Poland | Moderate religiosity, family-oriented |
| **Confucian** | High secular, medium self-expression | Japan, South Korea, Taiwan | Secular but hierarchical, education emphasis |
| **Latin America** | Low secular, medium self-expression | Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Chile | Catholic heritage, democratic transitions |
| **African-Islamic** | Low secular, low self-expression | Nigeria, Egypt, Pakistan, Morocco | High religiosity, traditional family values |
| **Ex-Communist** | High secular, low self-expression | Russia, Ukraine, Bulgaria | Communist legacy: secular but survival-focused |

**Key finding**: Economic development is the strongest predictor of value change, but cultural heritage (Protestant, Catholic, Confucian, Islamic) creates enduring value clusters. Path dependence: Culture shapes the trajectory of modernization.

**US anomaly**: United States is far more religious than its level of development would predict. Why? Explanations: Voluntary religious participation (vs state churches in Europe), immigrant renewal, religious pluralism creating competition, existential insecurity (weaker welfare state).

## Social Movements

How do groups mobilize to change society?

### Resource Mobilization Theory (McCarthy & Zald)

Social movements succeed not from grievance alone (grievances are always present) but from **organizational capacity**: money, members, media access, leadership, alliances.

**Critique of earlier theories**: "Relative deprivation" theories argued that movements emerge when people feel unjustly deprived. But grievances are constant — poverty, inequality, injustice exist everywhere always. So why do movements emerge when and where they do?

**Resource mobilization answer**: Movement emergence depends on resources:
- **Material resources**: Money for organizing, office space, communications
- **Human resources**: Committed activists, professional organizers, volunteers
- **Social resources**: Networks, organizations, community ties
- **Cultural resources**: Shared narratives, collective identity, framing
- **Moral resources**: Legitimacy, celebrity endorsements, public sympathy

**Civil Rights Movement resources**:
- **Churches**: Meeting spaces, communication networks, moral authority
- **NAACP**: Legal expertise, organizational infrastructure
- **SCLC**: Leadership, national profile (Martin Luther King Jr.)
- **Students**: SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee) provided energetic activists
- **Northern allies**: Financial support, media access, political pressure

### Political Opportunity Structure (Tarrow, McAdam)

Movements succeed when political conditions are favorable:
- **Elite divisions**: Splits within power structure create space for movements
- **Institutional access**: Mechanisms for making claims (courts, legislatures, media)
- **Declining repression**: State capacity or willingness to repress declines
- **Influential allies**: Sympathetic elites provide resources and legitimacy

**Example: Civil Rights Movement timing**
Why 1950s-60s, not 1920s-30s?
- **Elite divisions**: Northern Democrats needed Black votes; Southern Democrats resisted (split)
- **Institutional access**: Warren Court sympathetic (Brown v Board 1954)
- **Declining repression**: Federal government willing to confront Southern states
- **Allies**: Northern liberals, labor unions, churches supported movement
- **International pressure**: Cold War made Jim Crow embarrassing (Soviet propaganda used it)

**Example: Arab Spring (2011)**
Multiple countries experienced simultaneous uprisings. Why?
- **Elite divisions**: Military unsure whether to support autocrats
- **Communication technology**: Social media enabled coordination despite repression
- **Demonstration effects**: Tunisia's success inspired others
- **Economic grievances**: High unemployment, food prices created dissatisfaction
- **But**: Most failed (Egypt returned to military rule, Syria civil war, Libya collapsed) because deeper institutional factors (state capacity, civil society, military role) differed

### Framing (Snow & Benford)

Movements must frame issues to resonate:

**Diagnostic framing**: What's the problem? Identify injustice, assign blame, create sense of urgency.
- Civil Rights: "Segregation is immoral and unconstitutional"
- Climate: "Carbon emissions cause catastrophic warming"
- MeToo: "Sexual harassment is pervasive and normalized"

**Prognostic framing**: What's the solution? Propose concrete changes, strategies, tactics.
- Civil Rights: Nonviolent protest + legislation (Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act)
- Climate: Carbon tax, clean energy transition, international agreements
- MeToo: Believe survivors, workplace policies, criminal prosecution

**Motivational framing**: Why act now? Create urgency, moral imperative, collective identity.
- Civil Rights: Dignity, justice, moral arc of universe
- Climate: Existential threat, intergenerational responsibility
- MeToo: Solidarity, empowerment, "Time's up"

**Frame alignment**: Movements succeed when frames align with target audience's values and experiences. Climate movement struggled when framed as environmental (abstract, distant) but gained traction when framed as jobs (concrete, immediate: "Green New Deal").

**Counter-framing**: Opponents frame movements differently. Civil Rights opponents framed it as "state's rights," "outside agitators," "communism." Climate opponents frame it as "job-killing regulations," "economic harm," "China benefits."

### Movement Tactics

| Tactic | Description | Example | Effectiveness |
|--------|-------------|---------|---------------|
| **Disruption** | Interrupt business-as-usual | Sit-ins, strikes, blockades | High visibility but risks backlash |
| **Visibility** | Public demonstrations | Marches, rallies, protests | Signals strength but requires sustained mobilization |
| **Legal** | Courts, legislation | Lawsuits, lobbying, electoral | Slow but durable if successful |
| **Cultural** | Change norms and discourse | Art, media, education | Gradual but fundamental |
| **Violence** | Property damage, armed resistance | Riots, terrorism, insurgency | Risky; often backfires; delegitimizes |

Successful movements typically combine tactics. Civil Rights: Nonviolent disruption (sit-ins, freedom rides) + legal strategy (court cases) + cultural change (media coverage shifting norms) + electoral (voter registration).

## Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers)

Everett Rogers' model (1962) of how new technologies and ideas spread through populations:

### Adopter Categories

| Category | Share | Characteristics | Psychographic | Decision Process |
|----------|-------|-----------------|---------------|------------------|
| **Innovators** | 2.5% | Risk-tolerant, connected, affluent, cosmopolitan | Venturesome | Minimal |
| **Early adopters** | 13.5% | Opinion leaders, educated, respected | Respectable | Deliberate |
| **Early majority** | 34% | Deliberate, follow leaders | Deliberate | Careful |
| **Late majority** | 34% | Skeptical, adopt from necessity/peer pressure | Skeptical | Cautious |
| **Laggards** | 16% | Traditional, resistant, isolated | Traditional | Very slow |

**Characteristics vary**:
- **Innovators**: Technically savvy, high risk tolerance, comfortable with uncertainty, financially secure, social networks extend beyond local community
- **Early adopters**: Respected locally, sought for advice, reduce uncertainty for others by testing innovations, financial resources to absorb losses
- **Early majority**: Rarely lead but adopt before average, deliberate, need proof it works
- **Late majority**: Skeptical, wait until most have adopted, economic necessity or peer pressure drives adoption
- **Laggards**: Traditional, suspicious of change, limited resources, isolated from social networks

### The S-Curve

Adoption follows a sigmoid (S-curve) over time:
1. **Slow start**: Only innovators adopt (2.5%)
2. **Takeoff**: Early adopters join, rate accelerates (reaches 10-20%)
3. **Rapid growth**: Early and late majority adopt (20-80%)
4. **Saturation**: Only laggards remain, rate slows (80-100%)

**The chasm** (Geoffrey Moore): Gap between early adopters (who want cutting-edge technology) and early majority (who want practical solutions). Many innovations fail to cross this chasm.

**Example: Smartphone adoption (US)**
- 2007: iPhone released — innovators adopt (2%)
- 2010: Multiple models available — early adopters (15%)
- 2012-2015: Rapid growth — majority adopts (50% → 75%)
- 2020: Saturation — 85% ownership, mainly laggards remaining

### Factors Affecting Adoption Rate

**Innovation characteristics** (Rogers):
1. **Relative advantage**: Better than existing solution? (Email vs postal mail: instant, free)
2. **Compatibility**: Fits existing values/practices? (Organ donation in cultures vs incompatible with some religious beliefs)
3. **Complexity**: Easy to understand/use? (iPod simple, early computers complex)
4. **Trialability**: Can test before full commitment? (Free samples, trial periods)
5. **Observability**: Can others see the benefits? (Solar panels visible on roofs, meditation benefits internal)

Higher relative advantage, compatibility, trialability, and observability → faster diffusion. Lower complexity → faster diffusion.

**Network effects**: Some innovations become more valuable as more people adopt (telephones, fax machines, social networks, messaging apps). This creates increasing returns: early slow adoption, then explosive growth after critical mass.

**Example: Fax machines**: One fax machine is useless. Two can communicate. As more adopt, network becomes more valuable. Adoption accelerated until internet/email provided better alternative; faxes declined.

### Tipping Points

Malcolm Gladwell popularized the concept; it originates in epidemiology and sociology. A tipping point is reached when adoption becomes self-sustaining — the innovation spreads on its own momentum without promotional efforts.

**Mark Granovetter's threshold model**: Each person has a threshold — they adopt when enough others have adopted. If thresholds vary smoothly (0%, 1%, 2%... 99%), a cascade occurs. If there's a gap in the threshold distribution, the cascade stops.

**Example**: Imagine a population deciding whether to join a protest.
- Person A has 0% threshold (will protest alone)
- Person B has 1% threshold (will protest if 1% already protesting)
- Person C has 2% threshold...
- Person Z has 99% threshold

Result: Cascade. A starts, B joins (now 2%), C joins, etc. → revolution.

But if no one has 0% threshold (everyone requires some others first), no cascade. Or if there's a gap (thresholds jump from 20% to 40%), cascade stops at 20%.

**Real-world tipping points**:
- **Crime reduction**: As crime falls, law-abiding residents return → neighborhoods stabilize → more return (positive feedback)
- **Urban revitalization**: As hip residents move to cheap neighborhood → cafes/galleries open → more hip residents move in
- **Social norms**: As smoking declines → fewer places allow it → fewer people smoke → social norm shifts
- **Technology adoption**: As network grows → more valuable → more adopt → grows faster

## Moral Progress: The Expanding Circle

Peter Singer's "expanding circle" thesis: Moral concern has progressively expanded from kin → tribe → nation → all humans → animals → potentially AI/future beings.

### Historical Trajectory

**Slavery**: Nearly universal across societies and history → abolitionist movements (18th-19th centuries) → largely abolished by early 20th century. Still ~40 million in modern slavery, but morally condemned universally.

Timeline:
- Ancient world: Slavery normal, ~30% of some populations enslaved
- 1500s: Trans-Atlantic slave trade begins
- 1700s: Quakers oppose slavery, abolitionist movement emerges
- 1800s: Gradual abolition (Britain 1833, US 1865, Brazil 1888)
- 1900s: Formal slavery mostly abolished, but forms persist (forced labor, trafficking)

**Women's suffrage**: Women barred from voting in virtually all societies → suffragette movements (19th-20th centuries) → universal suffrage in most democracies by late 20th century.

Timeline:
- Pre-1800s: No women's suffrage anywhere
- 1893: New Zealand first country (national level)
- 1920: US (19th Amendment)
- 1945-1975: Most democracies grant suffrage
- 1984: Liechtenstein (last Western democracy)
- 2015: Saudi Arabia (last absolute monarchy to grant limited voting rights)

**Racial equality**: Legal segregation, explicit racial hierarchies → civil rights movements (mid-20th century) → legal equality in most democracies (though discrimination persists).

Timeline:
- Pre-1950s: Apartheid (South Africa), Jim Crow (US), colonial systems
- 1950s-1960s: Civil Rights Movement (US), decolonization (Africa/Asia)
- 1994: Apartheid ends (South Africa)
- 2020s: Legal equality widespread; de facto inequality persists

**LGBTQ rights**: Criminalized and pathologized → gay rights movement (1970s-present) → marriage equality in many countries (2000s-2010s).

Timeline:
- Pre-1960s: Homosexuality criminalized in most countries
- 1969: Stonewall riots spark gay rights movement
- 1970s-1990s: Decriminalization spreads
- 2001: Netherlands first country to legalize same-sex marriage
- 2015: US (Obergefell v Hodges)
- 2020s: Legal in 30+ countries; still criminalized in ~70 countries

**Animal welfare**: Animals as property with no moral status → animal rights movement (1970s-present) → factory farming increasingly controversial, animal cruelty laws strengthen.

Timeline:
- Pre-1800s: Minimal concern for animal suffering
- 1822: UK first animal cruelty law
- 1975: Peter Singer's *Animal Liberation*
- 1980s-present: Animal welfare laws strengthen, factory farming criticized
- 2020s: Cell-cultured meat emerges, veganism grows

### Mechanisms of Moral Expansion

**Economic development**: As survival pressures recede, people can afford to consider moral concerns beyond immediate family/tribe. Inglehart's post-materialist values.

**Literacy and education**: Reading exposes people to other perspectives, builds empathy, enables moral reasoning.

**Urbanization**: Cities bring diverse people into contact, breaking down parochial loyalties and forcing tolerance.

**Media**: Mass media (newspapers, radio, TV) made distant suffering visible. Social media amplifies this (viral videos of injustice).

**Moral argument**: Philosophers, religious leaders, activists make arguments that persuade. Abolitionism drew on Enlightenment ideas (equality, human dignity), religious concepts (souls, God's children), and economic arguments (free labor more efficient).

**Norm entrepreneurs**: Individuals who challenge existing norms and create new ones. Martin Luther King Jr., Emmeline Pankhurst, Harvey Milk, Malala Yousafzai.

**Empathy expansion**: As transportation and communication connect people, empathy extends beyond immediate community. Peter Singer: "The circle of altruism has expanded from the family and the tribe to the nation and the race, and we are beginning to recognize that our obligations extend to all human beings."

**Demonstrating hypocrisy**: Movements expose contradictions in dominant values. Abolitionism: "How can a Christian nation enslave fellow humans?" Civil Rights: "How can a democracy deny citizens voting rights?" These arguments work because they invoke shared values.

### Steven Pinker's Argument

*The Better Angels of Our Nature* (2011): Violence has declined dramatically over centuries — despite perceptions, we live in the most peaceful era in human history.

**Evidence**:
- **Homicide rates**: Medieval Europe ~20-100 per 100,000; modern Europe ~1 per 100,000
- **War deaths**: 20th century wars horrific but killed smaller percentage of population than earlier conflicts
- **Genocide**: Rare now; common historically (entire cities sacked)
- **Torture**: Routine punishment historically; banned by most countries now
- **Capital punishment**: Declining (140 countries abolished or rarely use it)
- **Slavery**: Nearly universal historically; illegal everywhere now (though not eliminated)
- **Violence against children**: Corporal punishment declining
- **Violence against women**: Domestic violence increasingly criminalized
- **Attitudes**: Acceptance of violence declining (surveys show increasing intolerance)

**Mechanisms** (Pinker's "better angels"):
1. **Leviathan**: State monopoly on violence reduces feuding
2. **Commerce**: Trade makes others more valuable alive than dead
3. **Feminization**: Women's empowerment reduces cultural glorification of violence
4. **Cosmopolitanism**: Literacy, urbanization, travel expand empathy
5. **Reason**: Enlightenment ideals of equality, dignity spread

**Critiques**:
- Pinker may cherry-pick data (what about 20th century wars?)
- Violence hasn't disappeared (terrorism, police brutality, domestic abuse persist)
- Structural violence (poverty, inequality) not measured
- Future violence (nuclear war, climate conflict) remains possible

**Response**: Even accounting for WWI/WWII, deaths as percentage of population lower than many pre-modern conflicts (Mongol invasions killed ~40 million, ~10% of world population). Trend is clear even if progress isn't linear.

## Technology and Social Change

Technology doesn't just affect material conditions — it reshapes social structures, values, and behaviors.

### Historical Examples

| Technology | Introduction | Social Changes | Unintended Consequences | Long-term Impact |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Printing press** | 1440 | Reformation (Bible accessible), scientific revolution (disseminating knowledge), literacy (education spreads), nationalism (standardized languages) | Propaganda, misinformation, copyright disputes, challenged Church authority | Fundamentally democratized knowledge |
| **Telegraph** | 1844 | News speed (hours to minutes), market integration (price coordination), time zones (standardization needed), journalism (wire services) | Information overload, anxiety from constant news | Began instantaneous global communication |
| **Telephone** | 1876 | Personal communication, business coordination, urban sprawl (enabled living far from work) | Reduced letter-writing, phone addiction precursor | Made synchronous distance communication normal |
| **Automobile** | 1908 | Suburbs (commuting possible), individualism (personal mobility), women's mobility (independence), national highways | Pollution, sprawl, traffic deaths (35,000/yr in US), oil dependence | Restructured cities and daily life |
| **Television** | 1950s | Mass culture (shared national experience), political advertising (image over substance), civil rights (broadcasts of violence shifted opinion) | Couch potatoes, attention fragmentation, parasocial relationships | Created visual mass culture |
| **Birth control pill** | 1960 | Women's liberation (reproductive control), delayed marriage, sexual revolution, career opportunities | Some argue family breakdown; others say female empowerment | Fundamentally changed gender dynamics |
| **Internet** | 1990s | Information access (Wikipedia, search), global connection (email, chat), e-commerce, remote work | Surveillance, addiction, echo chambers, misinformation | Comparable to printing press in impact |
| **Social media** | 2000s-2010s | Democratized publishing, social movements (Arab Spring), celebrity culture, influencers | Mental health (anxiety, depression, especially teens), polarization, misinformation, outrage culture | Still unfolding; potentially society-reshaping |
| **Smartphones** | 2007 | Constant connectivity, mobile internet, photography ubiquity, GPS | Distraction, reduced attention spans, "phubbing," surveillance | Extended internet access to everywhere |

### Unintended Consequences

Institutional design must anticipate unintended consequences, though this is difficult.

**Cobra effect**: British India offered bounty for dead cobras (to reduce cobra population). People bred cobras for bounty income. When bounty canceled, breeders released cobras. More cobras than before.

**Lesson**: Monetary incentives can backfire when people game the system. Similar: Rat bounties in Hanoi, pest bounties creating pest farms.

**Goodhart's Law**: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Examples:
- **Test scores** as education target → teachers teach to test, not deeper learning
- **Citation counts** as academic target → citation cartels, self-citation, salami slicing publications
- **Arrest quotas** for police → arrests for trivial offenses to hit numbers
- **Hospital wait times** as target → patients kept in ambulances to avoid starting "wait time clock"
- **GDP growth** as target → activities boosting GDP (construction, resource extraction) prioritized over quality of life

**Jevons Paradox**: Increasing efficiency of resource use can *increase* total consumption. More efficient engines → cheaper to drive → more driving → total fuel consumption increases despite efficiency gains.

Examples:
- **LED lighting**: More efficient bulbs → cheaper lighting → people leave lights on longer, install more lights
- **Fuel efficiency**: Better MPG → driving costs less per mile → people drive more miles
- **Data storage**: Cheaper storage → people store more (photos, videos, files)

**Streisand effect**: Attempting to suppress information causes it to spread more widely. Named for Barbra Streisand's attempt to suppress photos of her house (lawsuit drew massive attention to photos).

**Moral hazard**: Protection from consequences encourages risky behavior. Flood insurance → people build in flood plains. Bailouts → financial firms take excessive risks ("too big to fail").

## Key Terms

- **Gene-Culture Coevolution**: Cultural practices alter genetic selection pressures; genes shape cultural transmission biases
- **Prestige Bias**: Copying high-status individuals' behaviors (efficient heuristic but spreads irrelevant behaviors)
- **Conformist Bias**: Adopting the most common behavior (coordinates groups but locks in harmful practices)
- **Content Bias**: Some ideas intrinsically more memorable/compelling (negativity bias, threat detection)
- **Vertical/Horizontal/Oblique Transmission**: Parent-child / peer / elder-to-younger cultural learning
- **Diffusion of Innovations**: How new ideas spread through populations (S-curve, innovators to laggards)
- **Tipping Point**: Threshold beyond which adoption becomes self-sustaining cascade
- **Expanding Circle**: Progressive widening of moral concern from kin → tribe → humanity → beyond (Singer)
- **Goodhart's Law**: Measure becomes target → ceases to be good measure (incentive distortion)
- **Jevons Paradox**: Efficiency gains increase total consumption (rebound effect)
- **Cobra Effect**: Incentive causing opposite of intended outcome (unintended consequences of rewards)
- **Framing**: How movements define problems (diagnostic), solutions (prognostic), urgency (motivational)
- **Resource Mobilization**: Movement success depends on organizational capacity, not just grievances
- **Political Opportunity Structure**: Movements succeed when elite divisions, access, declining repression create openings
- **Post-Materialist Values**: Shift from survival to self-expression values as development proceeds (Inglehart)

## Summary

Cultural evolution operates through transmission (vertical, horizontal, oblique) shaped by biases (prestige, conformist, content, success). Gene-culture coevolution links biological and cultural change — lactose tolerance and honor cultures demonstrate the feedback. Cultural evolution is Lamarckian (acquired traits transmit) unlike genetic evolution.

Modernization theory (Inglehart) predicts value shifts from survival to self-expression and traditional to secular-rational as economies develop. World Values Survey confirms these patterns while showing cultural heritage (Protestant, Catholic, Confucian, Islamic) creates enduring clusters. Generational replacement drives change as cohorts raised in prosperity replace those raised in scarcity.

Social movements succeed through resource mobilization (organizational capacity), political opportunity structure (favorable conditions), and effective framing (diagnostic, prognostic, motivational). Civil Rights Movement exemplifies combining disruption, legal strategy, cultural change, and electoral politics.

Innovations diffuse through populations in S-curves (Rogers): innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards. The chasm between early adopters and majority is where many innovations die. Relative advantage, compatibility, simplicity, trialability, and observability determine adoption rate. Network effects create tipping points where adoption becomes self-sustaining.

Moral progress — the expanding circle — has been dramatic: slavery abolished, suffrage expanded, legal discrimination reduced, violence declined (Pinker). Mechanisms include economic development, literacy, urbanization, media making suffering visible, moral arguments, norm entrepreneurs, and empathy expansion. Progress isn't inevitable or linear, but trend is clear.

Technology drives social change but produces unintended consequences (Goodhart's law, Jevons paradox, cobra effect, Streisand effect). Printing press enabled Reformation but also propaganda. Automobiles enabled suburbs but caused sprawl. Social media enabled movements but caused polarization. Understanding cultural evolution means understanding how societies transform themselves — intentionally through movements and institutions, unintentionally through technology and cultural transmission.
