# Social Structures & Stratification

## Overview

Every society organizes people into hierarchies. Social stratification — the systematic ranking of individuals and groups — determines life chances: who gets education, health care, wealth, power, and respect. Understanding stratification means understanding why inequality persists and how it reproduces across generations.

Stratification is universal but varies in form (caste vs class), intensity (Gini coefficients from 0.25 to 0.63), and legitimation (meritocracy vs divine right). This chapter examines how inequality is structured, measured, reproduced, and potentially challenged.

## Dimensions of Stratification

Weber identified three independent dimensions:

| Dimension | Basis | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **Class** | Economic position (wealth, income) | Billionaire vs minimum wage worker |
| **Status** | Social prestige/honor | Professor vs used car salesman |
| **Power** | Ability to impose will despite resistance | Senator vs ordinary citizen |

These correlate but don't perfectly overlap. A university professor has high status but moderate wealth. A drug lord has wealth and power but low status. A retired Supreme Court justice retains high status without active power.

Weber's insight: stratification is multidimensional. Marx reduced everything to class (economic position). Weber showed that status and power operate independently. A newly rich tech entrepreneur has wealth (class) but may lack status (cultural capital). An impoverished aristocrat has status (noble title) but no wealth.

**Status groups** maintain boundaries through:
- **Endogamy**: Marrying within the group
- **Commensality**: Eating together (excluding outsiders)
- **Monopolistic practices**: Restricting occupations to members
- **Distinctive lifestyles**: Consumption patterns signaling status

Example: Medieval guilds controlled who could practice crafts, establishing status distinctions among artisans. Modern professions (law, medicine) use licensing to create status hierarchies.

## Class Analysis

### Marx: Two Classes
- **Bourgeoisie**: Own means of production (factories, land, capital)
- **Proletariat**: Sell labor for wages
- **Class consciousness**: Workers must recognize shared interests to act collectively
- **False consciousness**: Workers who identify with owners' interests against their own

Marx's class analysis is relational, not gradational. Class position derives from relationship to means of production, not income level. A small business owner who works alongside employees is petty bourgeoisie — owning capital but also laboring. A highly paid executive who doesn't own significant capital is still proletariat (selling labor).

**Class fractions** complicate Marx's binary:
- **Lumpenproletariat**: Unemployed, criminals, marginal population. Marx dismissed them as politically unreliable.
- **Petty bourgeoisie**: Small business owners. Squeezed between big capital and organized labor.
- **Managerial class**: Controls labor process without owning means of production.

Marx predicted increasing polarization — middle classes would disappear as capitalism concentrated wealth. Instead, 20th century saw growth of managerial/professional middle class. But 21st century trends (rising inequality, declining labor share) suggest polarization resuming.

### Weber: Multiple Dimensions
Class is about market position, not just ownership. Skilled workers, managers, and professionals occupy different class positions. Weber saw class as continuous, not binary.

**Market capacities** include:
- Ownership of property and capital
- Skills and credentials
- Organizational position
- Network connections

A software engineer's market capacity derives from scarce skills. A manager's from organizational position. A landlord's from property ownership. These create distinct class positions with different interests.

### Modern Class Schemes

**Erik Olin Wright** (neo-Marxist): Class defined by three types of exploitation:
- Ownership of capital
- Control of organizational assets (management)
- Possession of scarce skills/credentials

Wright's typology produces 12 class positions based on combinations of these three dimensions. This captures complexity while retaining Marxist focus on exploitation.

Example: A manager who owns no capital but controls workers experiences "organizational exploitation" — extracting surplus from subordinates. A credentialed professional without supervisory role experiences "skill exploitation" — monopoly rents from scarce expertise.

**Goldthorpe Class Schema** (widely used in research):

| Class | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| I-II | Service class (professionals, managers) | Doctors, executives |
| III | Routine non-manual | Clerical, sales |
| IV | Petty bourgeoisie | Small business owners |
| V-VI | Skilled manual | Electricians, machinists |
| VII | Semi/unskilled manual | Factory workers, cleaners |

Goldthorpe's scheme is based on employment relations (employers, self-employed, employees) and labor market position. It predicts life outcomes better than income alone because it captures stability, autonomy, and career prospects.

Research using Goldthorpe schema finds:
- Strong class effects on educational attainment
- Class-based voting patterns (declining but persistent)
- Class differences in health, mortality, family structure
- Intergenerational class immobility

## Social Mobility

### Types
- **Intergenerational**: Your position relative to your parents
- **Intragenerational**: Movement within your own lifetime (career)
- **Absolute**: Are you better off than your parents in material terms?
- **Relative**: Has your *rank* changed relative to others?

**Absolute mobility** can occur without **relative mobility**. If everyone's income doubles, absolute mobility is 100% (everyone better off) but relative mobility is 0% (rankings unchanged).

Post-WWII generation experienced high absolute mobility (rising living standards) but modest relative mobility (class positions reproduced). Modern generation faces declining absolute mobility (many won't out-earn parents) and modest relative mobility.

**Structural mobility** results from occupational structure changes. Industrialization moved people from farms to factories. Post-industrialization moved people from factories to services. This creates mobility opportunities without changing underlying inequality.

**Circulation mobility** is zero-sum movement between positions. For you to move up, someone else must move down (or at least not move up). Perfect meritocracy would produce circulation mobility based on ability, but structural factors limit it.

### The Great Gatsby Curve

Miles Corak (2012) documented a striking relationship: countries with **higher inequality** have **lower social mobility**. Plotted on a graph, this shows that the American Dream is more real in Scandinavia than in America.

| Country | Gini (inequality) | Intergenerational Elasticity (lower = more mobile) |
|---------|------|-----|
| Denmark | 0.28 | 0.15 |
| Norway | 0.27 | 0.17 |
| Finland | 0.27 | 0.18 |
| Canada | 0.32 | 0.19 |
| Australia | 0.35 | 0.26 |
| Germany | 0.31 | 0.32 |
| France | 0.33 | 0.41 |
| USA | 0.39 | 0.47 |
| UK | 0.35 | 0.50 |
| Italy | 0.36 | 0.48 |
| Brazil | 0.53 | 0.58 |
| China | 0.47 | 0.60 |

Intergenerational elasticity of 0.47 means 47% of income advantage is transmitted to the next generation in the US. A child of parents earning double the average will earn 47% above average themselves (controlling for regression to mean).

**Mechanisms linking inequality to immobility**:
1. **Educational investment**: Rich parents can afford better schools, tutoring, college
2. **Neighborhood effects**: Concentrated poverty creates poor schools, limited role models
3. **Network effects**: Elite social networks provide access to opportunities
4. **Marriage patterns**: Assortative mating (educated marry educated) concentrates advantage
5. **Inherited wealth**: Rich parents transfer assets directly

Raj Chetty's research using IRS data shows massive variation within the US: a child born in the bottom quintile in San Jose has a 12.9% chance of reaching the top quintile; in Charlotte, just 4.4%. Seattle (12.2%), Salt Lake City (10.8%), and Pittsburgh (10.5%) have high mobility. Memphis (4.8%), Atlanta (4.5%), and Indianapolis (5.2%) have low mobility.

What explains variation? Chetty finds five factors:
1. **Segregation**: Less residential segregation → more mobility
2. **Inequality**: Less income inequality → more mobility
3. **Schools**: Better K-12 schools → more mobility
4. **Social capital**: More civic engagement → more mobility
5. **Family structure**: More two-parent families → more mobility

These findings suggest mobility is an outcome of community-level institutions, not just individual effort. Your ZIP code at birth substantially determines your life chances.

## Inequality Metrics

### Gini Coefficient
Ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one person has everything). Calculated from Lorenz curve — plotting cumulative income share against cumulative population share.

| Country/Region | Gini (Income) | Gini (Wealth) |
|----------------|------|------|
| Scandinavia | 0.25-0.28 | 0.65-0.75 |
| Western Europe | 0.29-0.35 | 0.70-0.80 |
| United States | 0.39 | 0.85 |
| China | 0.47 | 0.70 |
| Brazil | 0.48 | 0.84 |
| South Africa | 0.63 | 0.95 |

**Interpreting Gini**: 0.25 (Denmark) means if two people are randomly selected, expected income difference is 25% of mean income. 0.63 (South Africa) means expected difference is 63% of mean income.

**Limitations of Gini**:
- Different distributions can produce same Gini (sensitivity to middle, not extremes)
- Doesn't reveal *where* inequality exists (top vs bottom)
- Can decrease even as poverty increases (if rich also get poorer)

### Wealth vs Income
Income inequality understates wealth inequality. US income Gini ≈ 0.39; wealth Gini ≈ 0.85.

US wealth distribution (2023 approximate):
- Top 1%: ~31% of all wealth
- Top 10%: ~69% of all wealth
- Bottom 50%: ~2.5% of all wealth

**Why wealth inequality exceeds income inequality**:
- Wealth accumulates over time (compound returns)
- Inheritance transmits wealth across generations
- Capital income taxed lower than labor income
- Wealthy can invest in high-return assets (private equity, hedge funds)
- Poor must hold liquid assets (low returns) or debt (negative wealth)

Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014): When return on capital (r) exceeds economic growth rate (g), inequality increases. Postwar period had g > r due to rapid growth and capital destruction (war). Since 1980, r > g again, driving rising inequality.

Piketty's data spans centuries and dozens of countries. Wealth inequality is U-shaped — high in 19th century, declined mid-20th (wars, taxes, growth), rising again since 1980. Top 1% wealth share in US: 45% (1929) → 22% (1975) → 35% (2020).

### Palma Ratio
Share of top 10% divided by bottom 40%. More sensitive to extremes than Gini.

US Palma ratio: ~1.8 (top 10% earns 1.8 times bottom 40%)
Denmark Palma ratio: ~0.9

**P90/P10 ratio**: Income at 90th percentile divided by income at 10th percentile. Measures dispersion.

US P90/P10 ratio: ~5.6 (someone at 90th percentile earns 5.6 times someone at 10th)
Denmark P90/P10 ratio: ~2.8

### Top Income Shares
Piketty-Saez methodology tracks top 1%, 0.1%, 0.01% income shares using tax data.

US top 1% income share:
- 1928: 23.9% (pre-Depression peak)
- 1945: 11.3% (wartime compression)
- 1976: 8.9% (postwar low)
- 2007: 23.5% (pre-recession peak, matching 1928)
- 2019: 20.5%

Rising top shares driven by:
- CEO pay explosion (1965: CEO pay 20x worker; 2020: 350x worker)
- Finance sector growth (bankers, hedge funds)
- Winner-take-all markets (tech, media, consulting)
- Capital income concentration (dividends, capital gains)
- Declining top tax rates (1950s: 91% top rate; now: 37%)

## Race, Ethnicity, and Stratification

Racial inequality in the US is deeply structural:

**Wealth gap**: Median white family wealth ~$188K; Black family ~$24K; Hispanic family ~$36K (Federal Reserve, 2019). A 8:1 ratio that has barely changed in decades.

Even controlling for income, education, and family structure, racial wealth gaps persist. Why?

**Mechanisms**:
- **Historical**: Slavery, Jim Crow, redlining, exclusion from GI Bill benefits, exclusion from FHA loans, discriminatory Social Security
- **Institutional**: Disparities in school funding, criminal justice (sentencing disparities), hiring discrimination (callback studies show ~50% lower rates for Black-sounding names), medical care
- **Wealth transmission**: Homeownership (the primary wealth vehicle for most Americans) was systematically denied to Black families through redlining (1934-1968)

**Redlining** (Home Owners' Loan Corporation, 1930s-1960s): Federal agency created maps rating neighborhoods for mortgage risk. "Hazardous" areas (marked red) — predominantly Black — were denied loans. This prevented Black families from buying homes during postwar housing boom when white families built wealth.

Rothstein (*The Color of Law*, 2017): Government created segregation through explicit policy, not just private discrimination. Public housing was segregated by law. FHA refused to insure mortgages in integrated neighborhoods. Highway construction destroyed Black neighborhoods.

**Cumulative effects**: A white family buying a $15,000 home in 1950 (with FHA loan) now has an asset worth $400,000. A Black family denied that loan has zero housing wealth. That $400,000 difference transmits to children (down payment, inheritance, college funds).

**Incarceration**: 1 in 3 Black men will be incarcerated in their lifetime (vs 1 in 17 white men). Incarceration reduces lifetime earnings by 40%, disrupts families, disenfranchises, and creates criminal records limiting employment.

Michelle Alexander (*The New Jim Crow*, 2010): Mass incarceration functions as racial caste system. War on Drugs disproportionately targets Black communities despite similar drug use rates across races. Once labeled felon, legally discriminated against in employment, housing, benefits, voting.

**Educational disparities**: School funding tied to property taxes creates enormous inequalities. Poor Black districts spend ~$11,000/student; rich white districts spend ~$20,000/student. Even controlling for spending, segregation reduces achievement (peer effects, teacher quality, resources).

**Labor market discrimination**: Audit studies send identical resumes with white-sounding vs Black-sounding names. "Emily" and "Greg" receive 50% more callbacks than "Lakisha" and "Jamal." Discrimination persists at every level (hiring, promotion, pay).

Devah Pager's study: White applicants with criminal records received more callbacks than Black applicants without criminal records. Being Black is a bigger penalty than having a felony conviction.

## Gender Stratification

**Wage gap**: Women earn ~82 cents per dollar earned by men (US, full-time workers, 2023). Controlling for occupation, experience, and hours narrows this to ~92-95 cents, but the "unexplained" gap persists.

**Decomposing the gap**:
- ~38%: Occupational segregation (women concentrated in lower-paying sectors)
- ~32%: Work experience differences (career interruptions for childcare)
- ~18%: Education/field of study (women in humanities, men in STEM)
- ~12%: Unexplained (discrimination, negotiation, promotion bias)

**Contributing factors**:
- **Occupational segregation**: Women concentrated in care work (nursing, teaching, social work), clerical roles. Men in high-paying fields (engineering, finance, management). Within occupations, women in lower-paying specialties (pediatrics vs cardiology).
- **Motherhood penalty**: ~4% wage decrease per child for women; fatherhood *bonus* for men. Employers assume mothers less committed. Mothers face discrimination in hiring, promotion.
- **Negotiation differences**: Women ask for raises less often, request less when they ask. Partly because assertive women face backlash ("aggressive"), while assertive men rewarded.
- **Part-time work**: Women more likely to work part-time (childcare), which pays less per hour and limits advancement.
- **Glass ceiling**: Women are 8.8% of Fortune 500 CEOs (2023), 29% of senior management, 27% of Congress. Pipeline explanations don't fully account for gap.
- **Glass cliff**: Women more likely appointed to leadership during crises (when failure likely), then blamed when turmoil continues.

Claudia Goldin (Nobel Prize, 2023): Historical research shows gender pay gap was small in early 20th century (women/men did similar work), widened mid-century (occupational segregation), narrowing now (education convergence) but stalled due to career/family tradeoffs.

Goldin's key insight: The gap isn't mainly discrimination but temporal flexibility demands. Jobs requiring long, inflexible hours pay disproportionately more. Lawyers working 80 hours/week earn far more than twice those working 40 hours (nonlinear returns). Women disproportionately choose flexibility-friendly jobs/hours due to childcare, reducing lifetime earnings.

**Solutions**: Make high-paying jobs more flexible (remote work, project-based rather than time-based pay), subsidize childcare, paternity leave policies shifting care work to men.

**Intersectionality** (next section): Black women face both gender and race penalties. Their earnings are 63% of white men's, reflecting compounded discrimination.

## Bourdieu's Forms of Capital

Pierre Bourdieu argued that class reproduction operates through multiple forms of capital:

| Capital Type | Definition | Example | Conversion |
|-------------|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Economic** | Financial assets, income, property | Wealth, investments, salary | Can buy other capitals |
| **Cultural** | Knowledge, skills, education, taste | Degrees, knowing which fork, accent | Signals class, enables jobs |
| **Social** | Networks, connections, membership | Alumni networks, country club | Access to opportunities |
| **Symbolic** | Prestige, recognition, legitimacy | Awards, titles, reputation | Enables economic capital |

These capitals are **convertible**: economic capital buys cultural capital (private schools), which generates social capital (elite networks), which produces symbolic capital (prestige), which enables economic capital (high-paying jobs).

**Example**: An upper-class family uses economic capital (tuition) to send child to elite private school. Child acquires cultural capital (refined tastes, knowledge, credentials) and social capital (friendships with future elites). These convert to symbolic capital (Harvard degree) and back to economic capital (Wall Street job).

**Cultural capital** has three forms:

1. **Embodied**: Internalized knowledge, skills, dispositions. Takes time to acquire, can't be transferred instantly. Examples: speaking multiple languages, appreciating classical music, table manners, body language.

2. **Objectified**: Cultural goods requiring cultural capital to appreciate. Examples: art collections, rare books, classical instruments. Owning a Picasso requires economic capital; appreciating it requires cultural capital.

3. **Institutionalized**: Credentials certifying cultural capital. Examples: degrees, certifications, professional licenses. Harvard degree signals cultural capital in portable, standardized form.

**Habitus**: Internalized dispositions shaped by social position — how you walk, talk, eat, dress, and think. Upper-class habitus signals competence in upper-class settings; working-class habitus signals outsider status. This is how class reproduces itself without explicit rules.

Habitus generates "reasonable" practices without conscious calculation. Working-class students self-eliminate from elite colleges ("not for people like me") without explicit barriers. This isn't free choice — it's habitus reproducing class structure.

Bourdieu's ethnographic work in French schools showed teachers unconsciously favor students with upper-class habitus — rewarding "natural" intelligence and style that actually reflect class-based socialization. Working-class students working equally hard receive lower grades because they lack cultural capital teachers implicitly reward.

**Social capital** (Bourdieu's definition): Durable networks of relationships providing access to resources. The collective-owned capital of a network that benefits members.

Differs from Putnam's "social capital" (civic engagement, trust). Bourdieu emphasizes exclusivity and power. Elite social capital (Harvard alumni networks, country clubs) actively excludes outsiders to preserve members' advantages.

**Symbolic capital**: The form other capitals take when recognized as legitimate. Wealth becomes symbolic capital when seen as deserved ("earned through talent"). Cultural refinement becomes symbolic capital when seen as natural superiority rather than class privilege.

Symbolic capital legitimizes inequality. Elites possess not just more resources but superior qualities (intelligence, sophistication, leadership). This makes inequality seem natural and just.

## Intersectionality

Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989) argued that race, gender, class, and other identities don't operate independently — they intersect to create unique experiences of disadvantage (or privilege).

A Black woman's experience isn't simply "Black experience + woman experience" — it's a distinct position with unique challenges not captured by analyzing race or gender alone.

**Example**: Employment discrimination lawsuits. Courts found no race discrimination (Black men were hired) and no sex discrimination (white women were hired), but Black women were systematically excluded — falling through both legal categories.

Black women face:
- **Double jeopardy** (Beale, 1970): Simultaneous racism and sexism
- **Multiple consciousness** (King, 1988): Awareness of multiple oppressions
- **Controlling images** (Collins, 1990): Stereotypes (mammy, jezebel, welfare queen) that justify oppression

**Intersectional wage gaps** (US, 2023):
- White men: $1.00 (baseline)
- White women: $0.79
- Black men: $0.71
- Black women: $0.63
- Hispanic men: $0.66
- Hispanic women: $0.57
- Asian men: $1.15
- Asian women: $0.85

Black women earn 63% of white men's wages. This isn't 79% (gender penalty) times 71% (race penalty) = 56%. The intersection creates unique disadvantages.

**Privilege and disadvantage**: Intersectionality also reveals privilege. A white woman experiences sexism but benefits from racial privilege. A Black man experiences racism but benefits from male privilege. A wealthy Black woman faces racism but has class privileges.

Peggy McIntosh's "white privilege" concept (1988): White people carry "invisible knapsacks" of unearned advantages. Not being followed in stores, seeing people of your race in positive media portrayals, being judged as individual rather than representative of race.

**Critique of single-axis analysis**: Feminism historically focused on white, middle-class women's experiences, ignoring how race and class shape women's oppression differently. Working-class women's struggles differ from professional women's. Black women's experiences differ from white women's.

**Kimberlé Crenshaw's traffic analogy**: Discrimination like traffic intersection. Black women stand at intersection of race and gender "roads." They can be hit by traffic from either direction (or both). Single-axis analysis examines each road separately, missing the unique dangers of the intersection.

**Extensions**: Intersectionality now incorporates sexuality, disability, religion, nationality, age. A disabled, queer, Latina woman faces compounded marginalizations that can't be understood by adding separate categories.

**Empirical applications**: Intersectional research examines how combinations of identities affect outcomes. Black women have higher maternal mortality than white women or Black men. LGBTQ people of color face higher suicide rates than white LGBTQ or straight people of color.

## Education and Stratification

Education is both a cause and consequence of inequality:

**Human capital theory**: Education increases productivity → higher wages. Investment in education pays returns. Empirically, each additional year of education correlates with ~10% higher earnings (US).

Evidence: Cross-country studies show education strongly predicts economic growth. Microeconomic studies show educated workers more productive. Returns to education persist after controlling for ability (comparing siblings, twins).

**Credentialism** (Collins): Degrees serve as gatekeeping devices. Many jobs requiring degrees don't actually require the skills degrees teach — the degree signals class membership and conformity.

Randall Collins (*The Credential Society*, 1979): Educational credentials proliferate not because jobs become more complex but because credential inflation serves status competition. Employers demand degrees to screen applicants; workers get degrees to compete; jobs requiring high school diplomas now require BAs.

Evidence: Job requirements increased faster than job complexity. Administrative assistants once required high school; now require BA. Not because tasks changed but because credential supply increased.

**Signaling** (Spence): Education signals ability rather than creating it. Smart people get degrees; the degree signals pre-existing traits to employers.

Michael Spence (Nobel Prize, 2001): If education were purely human capital, everyone would benefit from free education. But if education is signaling, making it free might not increase wages (more degrees dilutes signal). Empirically, wage premiums for degrees persist even when skills don't change, supporting signaling.

Sheepskin effect: Finishing degree yields wage jump beyond additional year of education. A person with 3 years of college earns much less than BA holder, despite similar knowledge. The credential matters more than learning.

**Reproduction** (Bourdieu): Elite schools transmit cultural capital and social networks that reproduce class positions across generations. Harvard produces leaders partly by selecting future leaders and connecting them.

Samuel Bowles and Herbert Gintis (*Schooling in Capitalist America*, 1976): Schools reproduce class structure by training different classes for different roles. Working-class schools emphasize obedience and rote learning (preparing factory workers). Elite schools emphasize creativity and leadership (preparing managers).

Annette Lareau (*Unequal Childhoods*, 2003): Class differences in parenting reproduce inequality. Middle-class "concerted cultivation" (organized activities, reasoning, negotiation) develops skills rewarded in schools and white-collar jobs. Working-class "accomplishment of natural growth" (free play, directives rather than reasoning) develops different skills.

**Elite university admissions**: Legacy admissions (children of alumni) receive huge advantages. Harvard admits 33% of legacy applicants vs 6% overall. Recruited athletes (disproportionately affluent, white) account for 10% of Ivy admissions. These preferences concentrate advantages.

**Test score gaps**: SAT scores correlate 0.5 with family income. Students from families earning $200K+ score ~250 points higher than students from families earning <$20K. This reflects access to test prep, private tutoring, better schools, stress of poverty.

**Tracking**: Within schools, tracking channels students into different academic levels. Ostensibly based on ability, but track placement correlates with race and class. Teachers hold higher expectations for higher-track students, creating self-fulfilling prophecies.

Adam Gamoran's research: Track placement in 8th grade predicts college completion better than test scores. Students with identical scores but different tracks have different outcomes — tracking itself shapes achievement.

## Key Terms

- **Social Stratification**: Systematic ranking of groups in hierarchy of wealth, power, prestige
- **Social Mobility**: Movement between positions in stratification system
- **Intergenerational Elasticity**: Degree to which parents' income predicts children's income
- **Great Gatsby Curve**: Higher inequality correlates with lower mobility
- **Gini Coefficient**: Measure of inequality (0 = equal, 1 = maximally unequal)
- **Palma Ratio**: Top 10% income share divided by bottom 40%
- **Cultural Capital**: Knowledge, skills, education, and tastes that signal class position (Bourdieu)
- **Habitus**: Internalized dispositions shaped by social position (Bourdieu). Durable but not permanent.
- **Social Capital**: Networks and connections providing access to resources (Bourdieu/Coleman/Putnam)
- **Symbolic Capital**: Prestige and recognition legitimizing power (Bourdieu)
- **Intersectionality**: Overlapping identities creating unique experiences of disadvantage (Crenshaw)
- **Credentialism**: Using degrees as gatekeeping rather than skill verification (Collins)
- **Signaling**: Education indicating pre-existing ability rather than creating it (Spence)
- **Structural Racism**: Institutional policies/practices producing racial inequality regardless of individual intent
- **Redlining**: Government-sponsored racial segregation through mortgage lending discrimination
- **Glass Ceiling**: Invisible barriers preventing women/minorities from top positions
- **Glass Cliff**: Women more likely appointed to leadership during crises
- **Motherhood Penalty**: Wage decrease women experience per child
- **Occupational Segregation**: Concentration of demographic groups in specific occupations
- **Wealth Gap**: Disparity in assets (larger than income gap)
- **Absolute Mobility**: Being better off than parents in material terms
- **Relative Mobility**: Changing rank in distribution
- **Structural Mobility**: Mobility due to occupational structure changes
- **Concerted Cultivation**: Middle-class parenting emphasizing organized activities and reasoning (Lareau)

## Summary

Social stratification is universal but varies in form and intensity. Weber's three dimensions — class, status, power — provide the framework. Modern class analysis ranges from neo-Marxist (Wright) to empirical (Goldthorpe). Class positions predict life outcomes through multiple mechanisms: income, stability, autonomy, networks.

Social mobility varies dramatically across countries and within them. The Great Gatsby Curve shows inequality and immobility reinforce each other. Countries with high inequality (US, UK, Italy) have low mobility; countries with low inequality (Denmark, Norway, Finland) have high mobility. Within the US, mobility varies by geography — San Jose and Seattle have high mobility; Charlotte and Atlanta have low mobility.

Race and gender stratification operate through both historical legacies and ongoing institutional mechanisms. The racial wealth gap (8:1 white/Black) reflects centuries of slavery, Jim Crow, and redlining, plus continuing discrimination in labor markets, housing, criminal justice, and education. Gender wage gaps (82 cents on dollar) reflect occupational segregation, motherhood penalties, and time flexibility demands in high-paying careers.

Bourdieu's capitals framework reveals how economic, cultural, social, and symbolic resources convert into each other, reproducing class across generations through habitus. Elite families transmit advantages not just through money but through cultural knowledge, social networks, and prestigious credentials. Habitus shapes aspirations and behaviors in ways that reproduce inequality without explicit barriers.

Intersectionality shows that multiple identity dimensions interact rather than simply adding up. Black women's experiences differ qualitatively from either Black men's or white women's. Analyzing race or gender separately misses unique oppressions (and privileges) at intersections.

Education plays an ambiguous role — potentially equalizing through human capital development and meritocratic selection, but also reproducing inequality through credentialism, signaling, and elite network formation. Elite universities concentrate advantages through legacy preferences, wealth-based access (test prep, private counselors), and social capital formation.

Understanding stratification reveals how inequality is structured, measured, legitimized, and reproduced — essential knowledge for analyzing institutions' distributive consequences and designing more equitable systems.
