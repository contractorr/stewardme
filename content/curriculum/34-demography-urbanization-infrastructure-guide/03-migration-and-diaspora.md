# Migration & Diaspora

## Types of Migration

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic** | Seeking better work/wages | Mexico→US, Philippines→Gulf states, Poland→UK |
| **Refugee/asylum** | Fleeing persecution, war, violence | Syria→Turkey/Europe, Rohingya→Bangladesh, Ukraine→Europe |
| **Climate/environmental** | Displaced by environmental change | Pacific Islanders (sea level), Sahel (drought) |
| **Family reunification** | Joining family members abroad | Largest category in US legal immigration |
| **Skilled/professional** | Brain drain/gain; recruited talent | India/China→US (tech), Africa→UK (healthcare) |
| **Seasonal/circular** | Temporary, recurring | Agricultural workers, Gulf state contract workers |
| **Internal** | Within country; usually rural→urban | Chinese hukou system, Indian urbanization |
| **Forced/trafficked** | Coerced; human trafficking, forced labor | ~50M in modern slavery (ILO estimate) |

## Historical Mass Migrations

| Migration | Period | Scale | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bantu Expansion | ~3000 BC – 500 AD | Millions over millennia | Spread agriculture/iron/language across Sub-Saharan Africa |
| Indo-European migrations | ~4000–1000 BC | Unknown | Spread language family from steppes to Europe, Iran, India |
| Germanic/Völkerwanderung | 300–600 AD | Millions | Transformed post-Roman Europe |
| Arab expansion | 630–750 AD | Hundreds of thousands | Spread Islam from Arabia to Spain/Central Asia |
| Mongol conquests | 1200s | Millions (displaced + killed) | Reshaped Central/East Asia |
| Atlantic slave trade | 1500–1870 | ~12.5M transported | Devastated Africa; built Americas |
| European emigration | 1815–1914 | ~60M | Populated Americas, Australia; largest voluntary migration |
| Partition of India | 1947 | ~15M displaced, ~1-2M killed | Largest single migration event in history |
| Chinese internal migration | 1980–present | ~300M rural→urban | Largest migration in human history |
| Syrian refugee crisis | 2011–present | ~13M displaced (6.8M external) | Largest refugee crisis since WWII (until Ukraine) |
| Ukrainian displacement | 2022–present | ~6M external refugees + ~7M internal | Fastest-growing displacement crisis |

## Push-Pull Framework

**Push factors (origin):** Poverty, unemployment, political instability, war, persecution, natural disasters, lack of services, environmental degradation

**Pull factors (destination):** Higher wages, safety, rule of law, education opportunities, family networks, demand for labor, better climate

**Intervening factors:** Distance, border controls, visa policies, migrant networks, transportation costs, language barriers, smuggling networks

## Remittances

### Scale
- Global remittances: ~$656B (2022) to low/middle-income countries
- Exceeds foreign direct investment and foreign aid for many countries
- Top recipients: India ($111B), Mexico ($61B), China ($51B), Philippines ($38B), Egypt ($32B)

### Impact
- **Positive:** Poverty reduction, consumption smoothing, human capital investment (education/health), foreign exchange
- **Negative:** "Dutch disease" (currency appreciation), dependency, brain drain, household inequality
- Remittance costs: ~6% average (target: <3% by SDG 10); varies by corridor

### Remittance-dependent economies
- Tonga: ~44% of GDP from remittances
- Tajikistan: ~32%
- Nepal: ~23%
- Honduras: ~26%
- El Salvador: ~24%

## Immigration Policy Models

### Points-Based (Skills-Focused)
- **Canada, Australia, New Zealand**
- Score based on: age, education, language, work experience, job offer
- Prioritizes economic contribution
- Canada admits ~400K permanent residents/year; ~60% economic class

### Family-Based
- **US (historically dominant)**
- ~65% of US green cards through family sponsorship
- Criticized for not prioritizing skills; defended as humanitarian/integration-promoting
- Long backlogs (India/China: 10-20+ year waits for some categories)

### Guest Worker
- **Gulf States (kafala system), Singapore, historical Germany (Gastarbeiter)**
- Temporary; tied to employer; limited rights
- Kafala: ~23M foreign workers in GCC; often exploitative conditions; passport confiscation

### Asylum and Refugee
- **1951 Refugee Convention:** Can't return people to persecution (non-refoulement)
- UNHCR: ~110M forcibly displaced (2023); ~36M refugees
- Top hosting countries: Turkey (~3.6M), Iran (~3.4M), Colombia (~2.5M), Germany (~2.1M), Pakistan (~1.7M)
- Resettlement: <1% of refugees resettled annually; vast majority remain in neighboring countries

## Brain Drain vs Brain Gain

### Brain Drain Concern
- ~1 in 9 Africans with tertiary education lives in OECD country
- 25-50% of science/engineering PhDs in US are foreign-born
- Healthcare: WHO identified 55 countries with critical health workforce shortage; many lose doctors to wealthy nations

### Counterarguments
- Remittances offset (see above)
- Diaspora networks facilitate trade, investment, knowledge transfer
- Return migration brings skills back
- "Brain circulation" replaces "brain drain" in some cases (China, India, Taiwan)

## Urbanization as Internal Migration

- **Largest migration in history:** China's rural-urban migration; ~300M moved since 1980
- **Hukou system:** Household registration ties social services to birthplace; migrants to cities often lack access to education, healthcare, housing
- **India:** ~40% urban (2024); projected ~50% by 2040; informal sector absorbs most migrants
- **Africa:** Fastest urbanizing continent; Lagos from ~300K (1960) → ~15M+ (2024); largely informal/unplanned

## Diaspora Networks and Their Influence

### Economic Influence
- **Jewish diaspora:** Historical role in finance, trade; Israel-diaspora economic links
- **Chinese diaspora (~50M):** Southeast Asian economies; investment flows to China; tech entrepreneurship in Silicon Valley
- **Indian diaspora (~18M):** $111B remittances; tech leadership (Google, Microsoft, IBM CEOs); political influence (UK, US)
- **Lebanese diaspora (~4-14M vs ~5M in Lebanon):** Commerce networks across West Africa, Latin America

### Political Influence
- Diaspora lobbying (AIPAC, Armenian-American lobby, Cuban-American lobby)
- Dual citizenship → transnational political participation
- Conflict financing (Tamil Tigers funded by diaspora; Irish-American funding of IRA)
- Homeland politics shaped by diaspora expectations

## Contemporary Migration Challenges

### Europe's Migration Dilemma
- Aging population → needs immigration for workforce; EC estimates EU needs 6M+ immigrants/year to maintain working-age population ratio
- Cultural anxieties → rise of anti-immigration politics (AfD, RN, Fratelli d'Italia, Sweden Democrats, PVV in Netherlands)
- Mediterranean crossings: ~2,500+ deaths/year (IOM); smuggling networks charge $3-10K per person; Libya as transit hub with detention camp abuses documented
- Integration outcomes vary hugely: Germany invested heavily in language courses/job training for 2015 cohort; 50%+ employed within 5 years. France: suburban banlieues with multigenerational marginalization; 2005 riots; persistent employment gaps. Sweden: generous welfare but high unemployment among foreign-born (~15% vs ~4% native)
- No EU consensus on burden-sharing (Dublin Regulation failures); Eastern European countries refused relocation quotas; New EU Pact on Migration (2024) attempts mandatory solidarity but contentious
- Demographic paradox: Europe's most anti-immigration countries (Hungary, Poland) are also aging fastest and losing population to emigration to Western Europe

### US-Mexico Border
- ~2M+ encounters/year (2022-2023); mix of economic migrants, asylum seekers, unaccompanied minors
- Nationalities shifting: No longer primarily Mexican; now Venezuelan, Haitian, Central American, Cuban, Chinese, Indian
- Root causes: Central American poverty, violence, climate (dry corridor); Venezuelan collapse; global awareness of border dynamics
- Policy oscillation: enforcement-focused vs reform vs humanitarian; no comprehensive immigration reform since 1986 (IRCA)
- ~11M unauthorized immigrants in US (~3.3% of population); deeply embedded in economy (agriculture, construction, services)
- Economic integration: CBO estimates unauthorized immigrants contribute ~$7T to GDP over decade; pay ~$13B/year in Social Security taxes they can't claim
- Border enforcement spending: ~$25B/year (CBP+ICE); wall/barrier coverage ~700 miles of 1,954-mile border
- Employer demand is fundamental driver: US economy structurally dependent on immigrant labor at both high and low skill levels; enforcement focused on supply (border) rather than demand (employers)

### Climate Migration (Emerging)
- World Bank projects ~216M internal climate migrants by 2050 (under pessimistic scenario)
- Hotspots: Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Latin America
- Sea level rise threatens: Bangladesh (~20M at risk), Pacific Islands (existential), coastal megacities
- No international legal framework for "climate refugees" (not covered by 1951 Convention)
- Already happening: Sahel drought → pastoralist displacement → conflict (Lake Chad shrinkage); Central American "dry corridor" driving northward migration
- Tuvalu, Kiribati negotiating sovereignty-in-exile arrangements; New Zealand offered climate visas to Pacific Islanders

### Gulf States Migration System
- GCC countries (Saudi, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman): ~23M foreign workers; extreme dependency
- UAE: ~88% of population is non-citizen; Qatar ~90% non-citizen
- Kafala (sponsorship) system: Worker tied to employer; employer holds passport (technically illegal but widespread); limited labor rights; exit permit requirements
- Sectoral dependence: Construction, domestic work, services, hospitality almost entirely migrant-staffed
- Qatar World Cup: Spotlight on labor conditions; estimated 6,500 migrant deaths during construction period (Guardian estimate, disputed); reforms announced but enforcement weak
- Remittance corridor: India→Gulf, Philippines→Gulf, Pakistan→Gulf among world's largest
- No path to citizenship or permanent residency; intergenerational non-belonging (born in UAE ≠ Emirati)

### Venezuela Diaspora Crisis
- ~7.7M Venezuelans left since 2014 (largest displacement in Western Hemisphere history)
- Economic collapse (GDP fell ~75%); hyperinflation; political repression
- Main destinations: Colombia (~2.9M), Peru (~1.5M), Chile, Ecuador, Brazil, US
- Strain on receiving countries; backlash growing; some countries reversed open-door policies
- Unlike Syria: not fleeing war but economic/institutional collapse — tests refugee definition limits

### Afghanistan
- Decades of displacement: Soviet invasion (1979), civil war, Taliban, US war, Taliban return (2021)
- ~6.5M Afghan refugees worldwide (largest refugee population for decades before Syria)
- Pakistan/Iran host majority; decreasing tolerance (Pakistan deportation drives 2023-24)
- Brain drain severe: educated class fled 2021 Taliban takeover; women's education banned

## Economic Impacts of Immigration

### Fiscal Impact

| Finding | Source |
|---|---|
| Immigrants to US generate net fiscal positive over lifetime (~$173K per immigrant, NPV) | NAS 2017 |
| First-generation immigrants: net fiscal cost (education, services) | NAS 2017 |
| Second generation: large net fiscal contributors (highest of any group) | NAS 2017 |
| High-skilled immigrants: strongly net positive immediately | CBO, multiple |
| Low-skilled immigrants: net fiscal cost in short-run; mixed long-run | CBO |
| Refugees: Initial cost (~$60-80K resettlement); break-even at ~8-11 years; net positive by year 20 | NBER |

**Key nuance:** Fiscal impact depends heavily on: skill level, age at arrival, receiving country's welfare state design, whether children are counted as "immigrant" costs.

### Labor Market Effects

**Consensus findings (Card, Peri, others):**
- Immigration increases total GDP (more workers = more output)
- Per-capita GDP effect: small positive for natives
- **Wage effects on natives:** Small and concentrated
  - High-skilled immigration: raises wages for complementary workers; small negative for competing high-skilled natives
  - Low-skilled immigration: -0 to -3% wage effect on native workers without high school diploma (Borjas estimate higher ~4-8%; Card estimate ~0%)
  - **Mariel Boatlift natural experiment (Card 1990):** 125K Cubans arrived in Miami (7% labor force increase); no detectable wage or employment effect on native workers — foundational paper (but contested by Borjas reanalysis)
- Immigrants often complement rather than substitute for native workers (different skills, occupations, language abilities)
- Fiscal gains, consumer price reductions (cheaper services, food), innovation effects often omitted from narrow wage-impact studies

### Innovation and Entrepreneurship
- 45% of Fortune 500 companies founded by immigrants or their children
- Immigrants 80% more likely to found a company than native-born Americans
- ~25% of US patent filings include at least one immigrant inventor
- H-1B workers concentrated in STEM; disproportionate contributors to R&D
- Startup visa programs: Canada, UK, Australia, Chile (Start-Up Chile) actively compete for immigrant founders

### Specific Sector Dependencies (US)
| Sector | Immigrant Share of Workforce |
|---|---|
| Agriculture | ~73% (mostly unauthorized) |
| Construction | ~30% |
| Hospitality/food service | ~23% |
| Healthcare (physicians) | ~29% of all physicians; ~40% in rural areas |
| Tech (software) | ~25-30% (much higher in Silicon Valley) |
| Meatpacking | ~50%+ |

### Macroeconomic Effects
- Immigration major factor preventing US/Canada/Australia from population decline
- Without immigration, US population would begin declining ~2035; Social Security insolvency accelerates
- Labor force flexibility: immigrants fill gaps in both high and low-skill markets; geographic mobility higher than natives
- Inflationary pressure vs deflationary pressure: debated; housing demand increase vs labor supply increase
