## Demographic Transition Model (Detailed)

### Stage 1: Pre-Industrial
- Birth rate: 40-50 per 1,000
- Death rate: 40-50 per 1,000 (fluctuating with famine, plague, war)
- Growth: Near zero (high birth, high death cancel out)
- No modern country in Stage 1
- Historical example: Medieval Europe; pre-colonial Africa

### Stage 2: Early Transition
- Death rate falls (improved sanitation, nutrition, basic medicine)
- Birth rate remains high (cultural lag; children still economic asset)
- **Rapid population growth** (population explosion)
- Examples: Sub-Saharan Africa (parts); historical Europe 1750-1850

**Why death rate falls first:**
- Public health interventions (clean water, sewage) imported quickly
- Vaccines, antibiotics arrive before cultural change in fertility norms
- Food production improvements (Green Revolution in developing world)

### Stage 3: Late Transition
- Birth rate falls (urbanization, education especially female, contraception, child labor decline, retirement security)
- Death rate continues low
- Growth rate slows
- Examples: India (TFR 2.0 in 2023), Bangladesh, Indonesia, Brazil

**Why birth rate eventually falls:**
- Children become economic cost not asset (urban context)
- Women's education and empowerment (single strongest predictor of fertility decline)
- Contraception availability
- Lower child mortality → less need for "insurance births"
- Opportunity cost of childrearing rises

### Stage 4: Post-Industrial
- Birth and death rates both low
- Population stable or very slow growth
- Aging population
- Examples: US (TFR ~1.6), France, UK, Australia

### Stage 5: Population Decline
- Birth rate below replacement (TFR < 2.1)
- Death rate may exceed birth rate
- Population shrinks (without immigration)
- Aging crisis: high dependency ratio

## Fertility

### Total Fertility Rate (TFR)

| Country/Region | TFR (2023) | Trend |
|---|---|---|
| Niger | 6.7 | Highest in world; slowly declining |
| Nigeria | 5.1 | Declining but huge absolute numbers |
| India | 2.0 | Below replacement; dropped from 5.9 in 1960 |
| Indonesia | 2.1 | At replacement |
| US | 1.6 | Below replacement since 1971 |
| France | 1.8 | Highest in Western Europe (pro-natalist policies) |
| China | 1.0 | Crashed from 6.1 (1960); one-child policy + cost of living |
| South Korea | 0.72 | Lowest in world; demographic crisis |
| Japan | 1.2 | Declining for decades |

**Replacement level:** ~2.1 in developed countries (accounts for child mortality); ~2.3 in developing countries.

### Why Fertility Is Falling Everywhere
1. **Urbanization:** Children = economic cost in cities (vs farm labor)
2. **Female education:** Each year of female schooling reduces fertility by ~0.3 children
3. **Contraception:** Modern methods available to ~80% of women in developing world
4. **Cultural change:** Individualism, career focus, delayed marriage
5. **Economic factors:** Housing costs, childcare costs, economic insecurity
6. **Child mortality decline:** Fewer "replacement" births needed

### Below-Replacement Fertility: Can It Be Reversed?
- **Pro-natalist policies tried:** Cash bonuses (Russia, Singapore), parental leave (Nordics), childcare subsidies (France), housing preferences (Hungary)
- **Results:** Modest at best. France: +0.1-0.2 TFR. Most policies shift timing not total births.
- **No country has sustainably reversed fertility decline once below ~1.5** (the "low-fertility trap")

## Mortality and Life Expectancy

### Historical Life Expectancy

| Period | Life Expectancy at Birth | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Paleolithic | ~25-30 | High infant mortality; if survived childhood, often lived to 50-60 |
| Ancient Rome | ~25-35 | Same pattern; high infant/child mortality dominates average |
| Medieval Europe | ~30-35 | Plague episodes lowered further |
| 1800 (global) | ~30 | Pre-modern medicine |
| 1900 (global) | ~32 | Sanitation improvements beginning |
| 1950 (global) | ~47 | Antibiotics, vaccines |
| 2000 (global) | ~67 | Continued improvements |
| 2023 (global) | ~73.4 | COVID-19 temporarily reduced in many countries |

**Most life expectancy gains came from reducing infant/child mortality**, not extending old-age lifespan. A Roman who survived to 60 might expect to reach 70; modern humans who reach 60 can expect ~83.

### Leading Causes of Death Shift
- 1900: Pneumonia, TB, diarrhea (infectious)
- 2023: Heart disease, cancer, stroke (chronic/non-communicable)
- This is the **epidemiological transition**

## Age Structure

### Population Pyramids
- **Expansive (pyramid):** Wide base (young), narrow top; rapid growth; Stage 2-3 (Nigeria)
- **Stationary (column):** Even distribution; low growth; Stage 4 (US)
- **Constrictive (inverted pyramid):** Narrow base (few young), wide top (many old); shrinking; Stage 5 (Japan, South Korea)

### Dependency Ratio
- **Dependency ratio** = (Population <15 + Population 65+) / Working-age population (15-64)
- Low ratio = "demographic dividend" (many workers, few dependents)
- High ratio = strain on social systems (either too young or too old)

### The Demographic Dividend

**Mechanism:** Birth rate falls → fewer children to support → working-age share rises → dependency ratio drops → per-capita income growth potential. NOT automatic — requires jobs, education, governance to convert demographic structure into economic growth.

**Three conditions for dividend realization:**
1. **Labor supply:** Working-age population must find productive employment (not just grow)
2. **Human capital:** Education and health investment must precede the demographic window
3. **Governance/institutions:** Property rights, macroeconomic stability, openness to trade

**Quantification:** Bloom & Williamson (1998) estimate 1/3 of East Asian miracle attributable to demographics. Dividend window typically lasts 40-60 years.

#### Country Case Studies: Demographic Dividend

**East Asian Tigers (Success Case)**
- South Korea: TFR dropped from 6.0 (1960) → 1.6 (1990); dependency ratio fell from 83% → 44%
- Education spending surged simultaneously; literacy 22% (1945) → 98% (1990)
- GDP per capita: $158 (1960) → $6,500 (1990) → $33,000 (2023)
- Manufacturing employment absorbed demographic bulge; export-led growth
- Window closing: aging now fastest in OECD; dependency ratio rising again

**India (In Progress)**
- Currently in demographic dividend window; 65% of population under 35
- Median age 28 (vs 38 in US, 49 in Japan); ~1M new labor market entrants/month
- Challenge: Only ~10% of working-age population in formal employment; massive informal sector
- State variation enormous: Kerala (TFR 1.5, aging) vs Bihar (TFR 3.0, youth bulge) — "India is not one country demographically"
- Risk: If job creation fails, dividend becomes "demographic disaster" — youth unemployment → instability
- Education quality gap: 50% of 10-year-olds can't read age-appropriate text (ASER data)

**Sub-Saharan Africa (Potential but Uncertain)**
- Will enter dividend period ~2030-2050 IF education/employment keep pace
- Working-age population projected to double by 2050 (+1 billion workers)
- Current youth unemployment 10-30% across continent; informal economy dominates (~85% of employment)
- If absorbed productively: potential to replicate Asian growth miracle at massive scale
- If not: youth bulge → political instability (Goldstone correlation: societies with >40% aged 15-29 significantly more conflict-prone)
- Bright spots: Rwanda, Ethiopia (pre-conflict), Kenya showing education gains
- Key constraint: manufacturing sector small and not growing; services-led growth path unclear

**Brazil (Partially Realized)**
- Dividend window 1970s-2020s; TFR dropped from 6.2 (1960) → 1.6 (2023)
- Economic gains real but incomplete; inequality persisted; deindustrialization limited manufacturing jobs
- Now entering aging phase without having fully industrialized — "getting old before getting rich"

**Thailand (Cautionary Tale)**
- Fast fertility decline: TFR 6.1 (1960) → 1.3 (2023); one of fastest transitions
- Benefited from dividend in 1980s-2000s (manufacturing boom)
- Now aging rapidly; middle-income trap; insufficient pension/healthcare infrastructure
- Illustrates: dividend is time-limited; must build institutions during the window

## Population Decline: Case Studies

### Japan
- Population peaked 2008 (~128M); now ~123M; projected ~88M by 2065
- TFR: 1.2; ~800K births vs ~1.6M deaths per year
- ~29% aged 65+ (highest in world)
- Consequences: labor shortages, rural depopulation, school closures, pension strain
- Response: Robotics/automation, slowly loosening immigration, pro-natalist incentives (modest effect)

### South Korea
- TFR 0.72 (2023) — lowest ever recorded by any country; dropped below 1.0 in 2018 and keeps falling
- Causes: extreme housing costs (Seoul apartment prices ~25x median income), education pressure ("SKY" university obsession; ~$20K/year private tutoring per child), gender inequality in home duties (women expected to quit careers), longest work hours in OECD, cultural shift toward individualism
- Government spending $200B+ on pro-natalist policies since 2006; TFR still falling
- Policies tried: cash bonuses ($10K+ per birth), subsidized housing, free childcare — none working
- "Demographic emergency" declared; population projected to halve by 2100 (52M → 24M)
- Military implications: conscription pool shrinking; service length already reduced
- Economic model (export manufacturing) requires workforce that won't exist; automation is partial answer

### China
- Population began declining 2022 (first time since Great Famine, 1959-1961)
- One-child policy (1979-2015) → two-child (2016) → three-child (2021); none working
- TFR ~1.0; ~40% decline in births 2016-2023
- Working-age population already shrinking; aged dependency ratio rising fast
- Economic implications: lower growth trajectory, pension system stress, potential military manpower constraint

### Europe
- Many countries below replacement for 40+ years; population maintained by immigration
- Italy: TFR 1.2; population projected to fall from 59M to 47M by 2070; entire southern towns emptying; ~7,000 villages abandoned or near-abandoned; "1 euro house" schemes to attract buyers
- Germany: Would have shrunk by ~8M without immigration since 1972; took 1M+ Syrian refugees (2015-16) partly for demographic reasons; still short ~400K workers/year
- Spain: TFR 1.2; youth unemployment historically 25-40%; young people can't afford children even if they want them; emigration of educated youth to Northern Europe
- Hungary/Poland: Pro-natalist; Hungary's Orban government offers lifetime income tax exemption for mothers of 4+ children; some temporary fertility bump but TFR still below replacement; combined with anti-immigration stance creates contradiction
- Russia: Population peaked 1993 (~148M); now ~144M; losing ~500K/year; alcohol-related male mortality historically extreme (male life expectancy was 57 in mid-1990s, now ~68); Putin's "maternity capital" program ($8K per child) had modest effect

## Population Momentum

Even after fertility drops to replacement, population continues growing for decades because:
1. Large cohorts of young people haven't had children yet
2. Improved survival means more people alive at reproductive age
3. Population "momentum" can add 30-40% more growth after reaching replacement

**Example:** India hit replacement TFR (~2.1) in 2021; population will still grow to ~1.7 billion by ~2060 before stabilizing.

## Malthus vs Cornucopia: Where Are We?

### Malthusian Concerns (Still Relevant)
- Water scarcity in MENA, South Asia
- Topsoil depletion
- Biodiversity loss
- Climate change limiting agricultural productivity
- Phosphorus depletion (finite; essential for fertilizer)

### Cornucopian Responses
- Green Revolution fed billions (Borlaug's wheat → India self-sufficient)
- Desalination costs falling
- Precision agriculture, GMOs, vertical farming
- Energy transition may solve climate constraint
- Population growth self-limiting (demographic transition)

### Synthesis
- Neither pure Malthus nor pure Simon is right
- Local Malthusian crises absolutely possible (water wars, regional famine)
- Global catastrophe unlikely with good governance and technology investment
- Binding constraint is probably **governance and distribution**, not absolute resource limits

## Country Deep Dives: Demographic Profiles

### Nigeria
- Population ~225M (2024); projected to surpass US by ~2050 (~375M); could reach 400M+ by 2100
- TFR 5.1; median age 18; ~70% under 30
- Urbanization rapid but unplanned; Lagos growing ~3%/year (projected 30M+ by 2050)
- Youth unemployment ~33% (real rate likely higher); informal economy ~65% of GDP
- Demographic momentum enormous: even if TFR dropped to replacement tomorrow, population would still grow for 50+ years
- Key risk: Africa's most populous country with massive youth bulge, oil dependency, weak institutions, ethnic/religious tensions — demographic pressure amplifies all

### Bangladesh
- Demographic success story: TFR dropped from 6.9 (1970) → 1.9 (2023) WITHOUT reaching high income
- Key driver: NGO-led family planning (BRAC, Grameen); female garment factory employment; micro-credit
- Population dense: ~170M in area of Iowa; 1,265 people/km² (most dense major country)
- Climate vulnerability extreme: 10% of land area <1m elevation; cyclone/flood exposure; 20M+ at risk from sea level rise
- Entering demographic dividend window; garment sector ($40B exports) absorbing labor but low wages

### Iran
- One of fastest fertility declines in history: TFR 6.5 (1985) → 1.7 (2023)
- Post-revolution government initially encouraged large families (Iran-Iraq War); reversed in late 1980s
- Free contraception, family planning clinics in every village, female university enrollment surged (>60% of students now women)
- Now facing same aging challenges as developed nations but at lower income level
- Government reversed course again (banned vasectomies, restricted contraception ~2021); TFR not responding

### Ethiopia
- ~126M population (2nd in Africa); TFR 4.0, falling; median age 19
- Rapid growth: population was 20M in 1950; projected 200M+ by 2050
- Agricultural economy transforming; manufacturing push (Chinese-built industrial parks)
- Internal ethnic tensions and Tigray conflict (2020-2022) disrupted development trajectory
- Addis Ababa as emerging African hub city; Chinese infrastructure investment heavy
