## Urban Scaling Laws (West & Bettencourt)

### The Mathematics of Cities

When city population doubles:

| Category | Change | Exponent | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Infrastructure | +85% (not +100%) | ~0.85 | Road length, cable, pipe, gas stations |
| Socioeconomic output | +115% | ~1.15 | GDP, patents, wages, restaurants |
| Social pathologies | +115% | ~1.15 | Crime, disease spread, traffic congestion |
| Individual needs | +100% | ~1.0 | Water use, housing, employment |

**Implications:**
- Cities are inherently more efficient per capita (sublinear infrastructure scaling)
- Cities are inherently more productive per capita (superlinear output scaling)
- But also inherently concentrate problems (crime, inequality, congestion)
- These relationships hold across countries, cultures, and time periods

### Zipf's Law for City Sizes
- Rank × Size ≈ Constant (roughly)
- Second-largest city ≈ half the size of largest; third ≈ one-third; etc.
- Holds remarkably well for most countries
- **Primate city** exceptions: countries where largest city far exceeds Zipf prediction (Bangkok, Buenos Aires, London)

33 megacities globally (2024); projected 43 by 2030. Most growth in Asia and Africa.

### Megacity Challenges
- Traffic: Lagos, Mumbai, Jakarta among worst; 1-4 hours daily commute
- Housing: Informal settlements house ~1 billion globally; Dharavi (Mumbai), favelas (Rio)
- Water: Cape Town "Day Zero" crisis (2018); Mexico City sinking as aquifer depleted
- Air pollution: Delhi AQI regularly >500 (hazardous); ~4.2M premature deaths/year from outdoor air pollution
- Waste: ~2 billion tonnes municipal solid waste/year globally; many megacities lack adequate treatment
- Heat islands: Urban areas 1-3°C warmer than surroundings; deadly during heat waves

### City Case Studies

#### Lagos, Nigeria
- Population: ~15-21M (estimates vary wildly; no reliable census)
- Growth: ~3.2%/year; adding ~600K people/year; projected 30M+ by 2050
- Geography: Built on lagoons and swamps; much of city <2m above sea level; flooding chronic
- Transport: No functional mass transit (BRT carries ~200K/day vs need for millions); average commute 3+ hours; road network overwhelmed
- Economy: ~30% of Nigeria's GDP; thriving tech scene ("Yaba Valley"); massive informal economy
- Housing: ~70% live in informal settlements; Makoko ("floating slum") on Lagos Lagoon houses ~100K in stilted structures
- Governance: Lagos State government relatively capable by Nigerian standards; still overwhelmed by growth
- Eko Atlantic: Massive land reclamation project; $6B; intended as new financial district; critics call it "city for the rich while slums expand"

#### Singapore
- Population: ~5.9M in 733 km² (one of world's densest nations)
- Model planned city-state: 80% live in government-built HDB flats; ethnic integration mandated (racial quotas per building)
- Transport: World-class MRT system; congestion pricing since 1975; car ownership requires $100K+ certificate
- Water: "NEWater" — recycled wastewater meets 40% of demand; desalination; rainwater collection; imports from Malaysia (geopolitical tension point)
- Housing: 90% homeownership rate (government-subsidized); public housing is middle-class, not poverty marker
- Limitations: Authoritarian governance; low birth rate (TFR 1.0); extreme dependence on imported food, energy, water

#### Mexico City
- Population: ~22M metro; altitude 2,240m; built on drained lakebed (Aztec Tenochtitlan)
- Sinking: 50 cm/year in some areas due to aquifer extraction; buildings visibly tilting; infrastructure cracks
- Water paradox: Built on a lake but water-scarce; 40% lost to leaks; some colonias get water delivery trucks only 1-2x/week while wealthy areas have 24/7 supply
- Air quality: Was world's most polluted city (1990s); massive improvement through vehicle restrictions ("Hoy No Circula"), cleaner fuel, catalytic converters; still problematic but much better
- Earthquake risk: 1985 earthquake killed ~10,000; soft lakebed amplifies seismic waves; building codes improved but enforcement uneven
- Mobility: Metro system (12 lines, 3.5M daily riders) + Metrobús BRT + massive car traffic; 2-4 hour commutes common from periphery

#### Tokyo
- Population: ~37M (metro); largest urban agglomeration in history
- Why it works at scale: Rail-centric development; JR East + 70+ private rail lines carry ~40M trips/day; rail stations are economic hubs (department stores, offices built around stations)
- Density + livability: Clean, safe, efficient; smallest apartments in developed world but public space is high-quality
- Earthquake engineering: Entire city built to withstand magnitude 7+; building codes world's strictest; early warning system gives seconds-minutes of notice
- Declining: Population peaked ~2020; now slowly shrinking; rest of Japan emptying toward Tokyo (but even Tokyo projected to decline)
- Cost: Surprisingly affordable vs London/NYC; government never restricted housing supply the way Western cities did; zoning is national (not local NIMBYism)

#### Dhaka, Bangladesh
- Population: ~22M; density ~23,000/km² in city core (one of densest on Earth)
- Growth: Driven by rural migration (climate displacement, economic opportunity); adds ~400K/year
- Flooding: Sits on floodplain; inundated annually; climate change worsening; many slum residents live on flood-prone land
- Garment industry: City's economic engine; ~4M garment workers (mostly women); factories often in residential areas; Rana Plaza collapse (2013) killed 1,134 → global supply chain reckoning
- Infrastructure deficit: Water supply intermittent; sewerage covers ~20% of city; traffic gridlock (average speed <7 km/h); air pollution severe
- Rickshaw capital: ~400K cycle rickshaws; more than any city; both transport solution and congestion contributor

#### Nairobi, Kenya
- Population: ~5M; East Africa's commercial hub; tech startup ecosystem ("Silicon Savannah")
- Kibera: One of Africa's largest slums; ~200K-500K residents (estimates vary); no formal sewerage; limited water; yet vibrant economy, schools, healthcare within
- M-Pesa: Mobile money revolution started here; 96% of households use it; leapfrogged banking infrastructure
- Infrastructure: New expressway (Chinese-built, tolled); SGR railway to Mombasa; BRT planned
- Inequality visible: Wealthy suburbs (Karen, Muthaiga) adjacent to informal settlements; gated communities next to slums

## Urban Planning Schools

### Garden City Movement (Ebenezer Howard, 1898)
- Self-contained communities surrounded by greenbelts
- Balance of town and country; population capped (~32,000)
- Influenced: Letchworth, Welwyn (UK); Canberra, new towns movement
- Legacy: greenbelts, planned communities; but often too rigid

### Modernist Planning (Le Corbusier, ~1920s-60s)
- "Radiant City": towers in parks; separated zones (living, working, recreation, transport)
- Car-oriented; wide highways; demolish old neighborhoods
- Influenced: Brasília, Chandigarh, US urban renewal, public housing towers
- **Critique:** Destroyed street life, community; high-rise public housing often became dangerous/isolated (Pruitt-Igoe demolished 1972)

### Jane Jacobs (~1960s)
- **"The Death and Life of Great American Cities" (1961)** — most influential urban planning book
- Cities are living systems, not machines; complexity is strength
- **Four conditions for vitality:** Mixed uses, short blocks, buildings of varied age/condition, density
- Fought Robert Moses' highway plans through Greenwich Village
- Legacy: mixed-use zoning, walkability, community-driven planning, "eyes on the street"

### New Urbanism (~1980s-present)
- Walkable neighborhoods, mixed-use, transit-oriented development (TOD)
- Traditional neighborhood design: grid streets, front porches, ground-floor retail
- Examples: Seaside (FL), Celebration (FL), Poundbury (UK, Prince Charles)
- Critique: Often expensive; can be aesthetically prescriptive; sometimes "New Urbanist for the rich"

### Smart Cities (2010s+)
- Technology-enabled urban management: sensors, IoT, data analytics
- Singapore: Most advanced; real-time traffic, building energy management, "Digital Twin" of entire city-state
- Songdo (South Korea): $40B purpose-built smart city; LEED-certified; pneumatic waste collection; 40% green space; criticized as sterile/soulless; occupancy below targets
- Masdar (UAE): Originally planned as zero-carbon; scaled back dramatically; personal rapid transit system partially abandoned; useful as R&D campus but not replicable model
- Sidewalk Labs / Quayside (Toronto): Google/Alphabet proposal for sensor-laden neighborhood; cancelled 2020 after privacy backlash; data governance was core dispute
- **Concerns:** Surveillance (China's "safe city" platforms exported to 100+ countries), privacy, digital divide, technosolutionism (tech can't fix governance failures), vendor lock-in (cities dependent on proprietary platforms)
- **What actually works:** Traffic signal optimization (10-15% congestion reduction), smart water leak detection, predictive infrastructure maintenance, real-time transit information — incremental improvements, not utopian reimagination

### Urban Transit Systems

| System | Capacity (passengers/hour/direction) | Cost per km | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metro/subway | 40,000-80,000 | $100-300M | Dense cities; >30K riders/day on corridor |
| Light rail | 10,000-20,000 | $30-100M | Medium density; suburban connectors |
| BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) | 10,000-45,000 | $5-30M | Cost-effective; Bogotá TransMilenio model |
| Commuter rail | 20,000-50,000 | $20-80M | Suburb-to-city; existing rail corridors |
| Cable car/gondola | 2,000-6,000 | $5-15M | Steep terrain; Medellín MetroCable pioneered |

- BRT: Often called "rail quality at bus prices"; Curitiba (Brazil) pioneered (1974); Bogotá TransMilenio carries 2.4M/day; Jakarta, Istanbul, Guangzhou have extensive networks; key: dedicated lanes, pre-paid boarding, level platforms
- Fare integration: Cities with unified fare systems (London Oyster, Tokyo IC cards, Seoul T-money) have 20-30% higher transit ridership vs fragmented systems
- Congestion pricing: London (2003, -30% traffic in zone), Stockholm (2006), Singapore (1975), NYC (2024); revenue funds transit; politically difficult but effective

## Slums and Informal Settlements

### Scale
- ~1 billion people live in slums/informal settlements (~1 in 8 humans)
- Sub-Saharan Africa: ~56% of urban population in slums
- South Asia: ~31%
- Definition (UN-Habitat): Lacking ≥1 of: durable housing, sufficient living space, access to water, access to sanitation, security of tenure

### Characteristics
- Self-built; incrementally improved over decades
- Often on hazardous land (floodplains, steep slopes, waste dumps)
- Dense social networks; informal economy; community organization
- Lack: formal tenure (eviction risk), sanitation, paved roads, electricity (sometimes tapped illegally)

### Key Slum Case Studies

**Dharavi, Mumbai**
- ~600,000-1M people in 2.4 km² (~300,000/km²); Asia's largest slum
- Economic output: ~$1B/year; recycling hub (processes 80% of Mumbai's plastic waste); leather, pottery, garments
- Not a place of despair: Dense social networks, intergenerational businesses, schools, healthcare
- Redevelopment: $3B Adani-backed project (2024) to rehouse residents in towers; residents fear loss of livelihoods, community, ground-floor workshop space that towers don't provide
- Illustrates: Slums are economic ecosystems, not just housing problems

**Favelas, Rio de Janeiro**
- ~1.4M people across ~1,000 favelas (~22% of city population)
- Originated 1897 (demobilized soldiers); grew with rural-urban migration; built on steep hillsides
- "Pacification" (UPP program, 2008-2016): Police occupation of gang-controlled favelas; initial success then declined; violence returned
- Services: Electricity mostly regularized (was tapped illegally); water/sewerage still patchy; community-built infrastructure ingenious but fragile
- Cultural production: Funk, samba, capoeira; tourism (controversial); some favelas gentrifying

**Orangi, Karachi (Success Story)**
- Population ~2.4M; one of world's largest informal settlements
- Orangi Pilot Project (OPP, 1980s): Community self-built sewerage system; residents financed and constructed neighborhood-level sewers; government connected to trunk lines
- Result: 97% of Orangi residents now connected to sewerage; model replicated across Pakistan and globally
- Key lesson: Community-driven infrastructure can be cheaper (1/5th of government cost) and more effective

### Approaches
- **Slum clearance** (historical, largely failed): Displacement without alternatives; often pushes poor further to periphery; demolished communities rebuild elsewhere
- **Sites and services** (World Bank, 1970s-80s): Provide infrastructure, let people build; mixed results; often located too far from employment
- **In-situ upgrading** (current best practice): Improve existing settlements — water, sewage, roads, tenure; cheaper and less disruptive than relocation; Medellín model (cable cars, escalators connecting hillside barrios to city center) widely celebrated
- **Community-led development:** SDI (Shack Dwellers International) in 33 countries; community savings groups; participatory enumeration (residents map their own communities); SPARC/Mahila Milan in India
- **Incremental housing:** Provide core structure (foundation, roof, wet wall); residents complete over time; Elemental (Alejandro Aravena) "half a good house" model in Chile
- **Tenure security:** Often most impactful single intervention; formal title → investment in home improvement, access to credit, political voice; but titling alone insufficient without infrastructure

## Suburbanization and Sprawl

### US Suburbanization (Post-WWII)
- GI Bill → home loans → suburban housing boom
- Federal Highway Act (1956) → interstate system → car dependence
- White flight + redlining → racial segregation
- Result: low-density, car-dependent, single-use zoning sprawl

### Sprawl Costs
- Infrastructure: Suburban infrastructure costs 2-3x per capita vs compact development
- Transport: Americans drive ~21,000 km/year (vs ~10,000 in Europe)
- Health: Sedentary lifestyle, air pollution, mental health (isolation)
- Environment: Land consumption, habitat destruction, higher emissions per capita
- Fiscal: Many suburban developments don't generate enough tax revenue to maintain their infrastructure long-term (Strong Towns analysis)

### Counter-Trends
- Millennial/Gen-Z preference for urban living (somewhat)
- Transit-oriented development
- Missing middle housing (duplexes, fourplexes, townhomes)
- YIMBY movement: Pro-housing advocacy; zoning reform
- Remote work: Could enable either more sprawl or smaller-city growth

## Urban Governance

### Fragmentation Problem
- Most metropolitan areas governed by dozens-hundreds of jurisdictions
- NYC: 5 boroughs, unified; Greater London: 33 boroughs under GLA
- Greater Jakarta: 6 cities, 3 provinces, no unified authority
- Fragmentation → coordination failures (transport, housing, environment)

### Revenue
- Property tax: Primary urban revenue in many countries (US, UK)
- Land value capture: Tax increase in land value from public investment (Hong Kong model)
- User fees: Congestion pricing (London, Singapore, Stockholm, NYC)
- Central government transfers: Dominant in many developing countries

### Urban-Rural Divide
- Political polarization: Urban areas trend progressive; rural conservative (near-universal pattern globally)
- Economic divergence: Cities generating larger share of GDP; rural areas declining
- Service provision: Rural healthcare, education, broadband gaps
- Cultural tension: Cosmopolitan vs traditional values
