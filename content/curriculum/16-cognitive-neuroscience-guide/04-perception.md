# Perception

## Overview

Perception is the process of organizing and interpreting sensory information to create meaningful experience. The brain doesn't passively receive information—it actively constructs perceptions through complex neural processing. This chapter covers visual perception (the most studied system), auditory perception, and principles common to all sensory systems.

## Key Concepts

### General Principles of Sensory Processing

**Transduction:**
- Conversion of physical energy into neural signals
- Receptor cells specialized for different energy types

**Sensory Coding:**
- **Labeled line code** - Different neurons for different qualities
- **Rate code** - Intensity encoded by firing rate
- **Temporal code** - Information in timing patterns
- **Population code** - Pattern across many neurons

**Hierarchical Processing:**
```
Sensory receptors
       ↓
Primary sensory areas (simple features)
       ↓
Secondary areas (complex features)
       ↓
Association areas (integration, meaning)
```

**Parallel Processing:**
- Multiple pathways process different aspects simultaneously
- Example: "What" vs "Where" pathways

**Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up:**
- **Bottom-up** - Stimulus-driven, data from senses
- **Top-down** - Knowledge/expectation-driven, influences perception

### Visual Perception

#### The Visual Pathway

```
Light → Cornea → Pupil → Lens → Retina
                                  │
                    Photoreceptors (rods/cones)
                                  │
                    Bipolar cells
                                  │
                    Retinal ganglion cells
                                  │
                    Optic nerve
                                  │
                    Optic chiasm (partial crossing)
                                  │
                    LGN (thalamus)
                                  │
                    Primary visual cortex (V1)
                                  │
                    Higher visual areas (V2, V4, MT, etc.)
```

#### Retinal Processing

**Photoreceptors:**

| Type | Sensitivity | Distribution | Function |
|------|-------------|--------------|----------|
| **Rods** | Low light, no color | Periphery | Night vision |
| **Cones** | Bright light, color | Fovea (center) | Day vision, acuity |

**Three cone types:** S (blue), M (green), L (red) → basis of color vision

**Receptive Fields:**
- Area of visual field affecting a neuron
- Center-surround organization in retina
- Cells respond to contrast, not absolute light

#### Primary Visual Cortex (V1)

**Location:** Occipital lobe (calcarine sulcus)

**Retinotopic Organization:**
- Neighboring retinal points → neighboring cortical points
- Fovea over-represented (cortical magnification)

**Feature Detection:**

| Cell Type | Responds To | Discoverers |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Simple cells** | Oriented edges at specific locations | Hubel & Wiesel |
| **Complex cells** | Oriented edges, position-invariant | Hubel & Wiesel |
| **Hypercomplex** | Edges of specific length | |

**Columnar Organization:**
- Neurons in vertical columns share properties
- **Orientation columns** - Preferred edge orientation
- **Ocular dominance columns** - Left vs. right eye input

#### Higher Visual Processing

**Ventral Stream ("What" pathway):**
- V1 → V2 → V4 → Inferior Temporal (IT) cortex
- Object recognition, form, color
- Damage → visual agnosia

**Dorsal Stream ("Where/How" pathway):**
- V1 → V2 → MT → Parietal cortex
- Motion, spatial location, action guidance
- Damage → optic ataxia, akinetopsia

**Key Areas:**

| Area | Function |
|------|----------|
| **V4** | Color processing, form |
| **MT/V5** | Motion perception |
| **Fusiform Face Area (FFA)** | Face recognition |
| **Parahippocampal Place Area (PPA)** | Scene/place recognition |
| **Lateral Occipital Complex (LOC)** | Object recognition |

#### Perceptual Organization

**Gestalt Principles:**
- **Proximity** - Near elements grouped together
- **Similarity** - Similar elements grouped
- **Continuity** - Smooth, continuous patterns preferred
- **Closure** - Incomplete figures completed
- **Figure-ground** - Separating object from background
- **Common fate** - Elements moving together grouped

**Object Recognition Theories:**
- **Template matching** - Compare to stored templates (limited)
- **Feature analysis** - Detect component features
- **Recognition-by-components** - Objects as arrangements of "geons" (Biederman)
- **Viewpoint-dependent** - Multiple stored views

### Auditory Perception

#### The Auditory Pathway

```
Sound waves → Outer ear (pinna) → Ear canal
                                      ↓
                              Tympanic membrane (eardrum)
                                      ↓
                              Middle ear (ossicles)
                                      ↓
                              Oval window
                                      ↓
                              Cochlea (inner ear)
                                      ↓
                              Hair cells (transduction)
                                      ↓
                              Auditory nerve
                                      ↓
                              Cochlear nucleus (brainstem)
                                      ↓
                              Superior olive → Inferior colliculus
                                      ↓
                              MGN (thalamus)
                                      ↓
                              Primary auditory cortex (A1)
```

#### Frequency Coding

**Place code:**
- Different frequencies → different locations on basilar membrane
- High frequencies: base of cochlea
- Low frequencies: apex of cochlea

**Tonotopic organization:**
- Preserved through auditory pathway
- A1 organized by frequency

**Temporal code:**
- Phase-locking to sound wave
- Works for lower frequencies (<4kHz)

#### Sound Localization

**Interaural Time Difference (ITD):**
- Sound reaches closer ear first
- Effective for low frequencies

**Interaural Level Difference (ILD):**
- Sound louder at closer ear (head shadow)
- Effective for high frequencies

**Superior olive:** First site of binaural integration

### Other Senses

#### Somatosensation

**Modalities:** Touch, pressure, temperature, pain, proprioception

**Receptors:**
| Receptor | Sensation |
|----------|-----------|
| Merkel discs | Pressure, texture |
| Meissner corpuscles | Light touch, flutter |
| Pacinian corpuscles | Vibration, deep pressure |
| Ruffini endings | Stretch |
| Free nerve endings | Pain, temperature |

**Pathway:** Receptors → Spinal cord → Thalamus (VPL) → S1 (postcentral gyrus)

**Somatotopic organization:** Body surface mapped onto cortex (homunculus)

#### Pain

**Types:**
- **Nociceptive** - Response to tissue damage
- **Neuropathic** - Nerve damage
- **Inflammatory** - Immune response

**Gate Control Theory (Melzack & Wall):**
- Non-painful input can close "gates" to painful input
- Explains why rubbing reduces pain

**Central modulation:**
- Descending pathways from brainstem can suppress pain
- Endogenous opioids, placebo effects

### Multisensory Integration

**Superior Colliculus:**
- Integrates visual, auditory, somatosensory
- Orienting responses

**Principles:**
- **Spatial rule** - Enhanced response when stimuli co-located
- **Temporal rule** - Enhanced when simultaneous
- **Inverse effectiveness** - Greatest enhancement for weak stimuli

**Examples:**
- **McGurk effect** - Visual lip movements alter heard speech
- **Rubber hand illusion** - Vision dominates proprioception
- **Ventriloquism** - Vision captures sound localization

## Real-World Examples

- **Prosopagnosia:** FFA damage → cannot recognize faces despite intact vision
- **Blindsight:** V1 damage → unconscious visual processing via subcortical routes
- **Synesthesia:** Cross-activation between senses (seeing sounds, tasting shapes)
- **Cochlear implants:** Bypass damaged hair cells, directly stimulate auditory nerve
- **Phantom limb pain:** Continued pain perception from missing limb

## Summary

Perception is an active, constructive process. The brain transforms raw sensory input through hierarchical processing, extracting increasingly complex features. Visual processing divides into "what" (ventral) and "where" (dorsal) streams. Auditory processing encodes frequency through place and temporal codes. All senses share principles: topographic organization, feature extraction, hierarchical processing, and top-down modulation. Perception isn't a faithful copy of reality—it's the brain's best inference about what's out there.
