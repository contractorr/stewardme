# Brain Anatomy and Organization

## Overview

The human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons organized into distinct structures and systems. Understanding brain anatomy is essential for cognitive neuroscience—knowing where things are helps us understand what they do. This chapter covers major brain structures, the cerebral cortex's organization, and key anatomical terms.

## Key Concepts

### Anatomical Directions

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Anterior/Rostral** | Toward the front |
| **Posterior/Caudal** | Toward the back |
| **Dorsal/Superior** | Toward the top |
| **Ventral/Inferior** | Toward the bottom |
| **Medial** | Toward the midline |
| **Lateral** | Away from midline |
| **Ipsilateral** | Same side |
| **Contralateral** | Opposite side |

**Anatomical Planes:**
- **Sagittal** - Divides left/right
- **Coronal/Frontal** - Divides front/back
- **Horizontal/Axial** - Divides top/bottom

### Major Brain Divisions

```
BRAIN (Encephalon)
├── FOREBRAIN (Prosencephalon)
│   ├── Telencephalon (Cerebrum)
│   │   ├── Cerebral cortex
│   │   ├── Basal ganglia
│   │   └── Limbic structures
│   └── Diencephalon
│       ├── Thalamus
│       └── Hypothalamus
├── MIDBRAIN (Mesencephalon)
│   ├── Tectum (superior/inferior colliculi)
│   └── Tegmentum
└── HINDBRAIN (Rhombencephalon)
    ├── Cerebellum
    ├── Pons
    └── Medulla oblongata
```

### The Cerebral Cortex

**Key Features:**
- 2-4mm thick layer of gray matter
- Highly folded (gyri = ridges, sulci = grooves)
- ~16 billion neurons
- Divided into two hemispheres connected by corpus callosum

#### Four Lobes

| Lobe | Location | Key Functions |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Frontal** | Front of brain | Executive function, planning, motor control, speech production |
| **Parietal** | Top-back | Sensory integration, spatial processing, attention |
| **Temporal** | Sides | Auditory processing, memory, language comprehension |
| **Occipital** | Back | Visual processing |

**Major Landmarks:**
- **Central sulcus** - Separates frontal and parietal lobes
- **Lateral (Sylvian) fissure** - Separates temporal from frontal/parietal
- **Parieto-occipital sulcus** - Separates parietal and occipital

#### Cortical Organization

**Primary Sensory/Motor Areas:**
- **Primary motor cortex (M1)** - Precentral gyrus; movement execution
- **Primary somatosensory cortex (S1)** - Postcentral gyrus; touch, proprioception
- **Primary visual cortex (V1)** - Occipital pole; visual processing
- **Primary auditory cortex (A1)** - Superior temporal gyrus; sound processing

**Association Areas:**
- Integrate information across modalities
- Higher-order processing
- Support complex cognition

**Topographic Organization:**
- Sensory/motor maps preserve spatial relationships
- **Homunculus** - Distorted body map in motor/sensory cortex
- **Retinotopy** - Visual field mapped onto visual cortex
- **Tonotopy** - Sound frequency mapped in auditory cortex

### Subcortical Structures

#### Basal Ganglia
**Components:** Striatum (caudate + putamen), globus pallidus, substantia nigra, subthalamic nucleus

**Functions:**
- Motor control and learning
- Action selection
- Reward processing
- Habit formation

**Clinical:** Parkinson's disease (dopamine loss), Huntington's disease

#### Limbic System
**Components:** Hippocampus, amygdala, cingulate cortex, fornix, mammillary bodies

**Functions:**
- Emotion processing (amygdala)
- Memory formation (hippocampus)
- Motivation and reward

#### Thalamus
- "Gateway to cortex"
- Relays sensory information (except smell)
- Multiple nuclei for different modalities
- Involved in attention and consciousness

#### Hypothalamus
- Homeostasis regulation
- Hunger, thirst, temperature, circadian rhythms
- Controls pituitary gland (hormone release)
- Autonomic nervous system regulation

### Brainstem

| Structure | Functions |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Midbrain** | Eye movements, reflexes, dopamine production |
| **Pons** | Sleep, respiration, relay to cerebellum |
| **Medulla** | Vital functions (heart rate, breathing, blood pressure) |

**Reticular Formation:**
- Runs through brainstem
- Arousal, sleep-wake cycles
- Attention modulation

### Cerebellum

- "Little brain" at back of head
- Contains more neurons than rest of brain combined
- **Functions:**
  - Motor coordination and timing
  - Balance and posture
  - Motor learning
  - Cognitive functions (language, attention)

### White Matter

**Major Tracts:**

| Type | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Commissural** | Connect hemispheres | Corpus callosum, anterior commissure |
| **Association** | Connect within hemisphere | Arcuate fasciculus, uncinate fasciculus |
| **Projection** | Connect cortex to subcortical | Corticospinal tract, internal capsule |

### Ventricular System

- Four ventricles filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)
- CSF cushions brain, removes waste
- Produced by choroid plexus

### Meninges

Three protective membranes:
1. **Dura mater** - Tough outer layer
2. **Arachnoid mater** - Web-like middle layer
3. **Pia mater** - Thin, adheres to brain surface

### Vascular Supply

**Arterial Supply:**
- Internal carotid arteries (anterior circulation)
- Vertebral/basilar arteries (posterior circulation)
- Circle of Willis provides redundancy

**Blood-Brain Barrier:**
- Tight junctions between endothelial cells
- Protects brain from toxins/pathogens
- Regulates what enters brain

## Real-World Examples

- **Stroke:** Blocked/burst blood vessel → specific deficits depend on location
- **Parkinson's disease:** Loss of dopamine neurons in substantia nigra → motor symptoms
- **Split-brain surgery:** Corpus callosum severed → hemispheres can't communicate
- **Temporal lobe epilepsy:** Seizures often originate near hippocampus → memory problems

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Gyrus/Sulcus** | Ridge/groove in cortical surface |
| **Gray matter** | Neuron cell bodies (cortex, nuclei) |
| **White matter** | Myelinated axon tracts |
| **Corpus callosum** | Largest commissure connecting hemispheres |
| **Thalamus** | Relay station to cortex |
| **Hippocampus** | Critical for memory formation |
| **Amygdala** | Key structure for emotion processing |
| **Homunculus** | Distorted body map in motor/sensory cortex |

## Summary

The brain is organized hierarchically from brainstem (vital functions) through limbic system (emotion, memory) to cortex (higher cognition). The cerebral cortex divides into four lobes with specialized functions, though most cognition involves distributed networks. Subcortical structures including thalamus, basal ganglia, and limbic structures play crucial roles in relaying information, motor control, emotion, and memory. Understanding this anatomy is fundamental to interpreting brain imaging and lesion studies.
