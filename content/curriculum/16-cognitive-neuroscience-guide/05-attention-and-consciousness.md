# Attention and Consciousness

## Overview

Attention is the brain's mechanism for selecting relevant information from the overwhelming stream of sensory input. Consciousness—subjective, felt experience—remains one of neuroscience's greatest mysteries. This chapter covers attention's mechanisms and neural basis, theories of consciousness, and what brain states like sleep reveal about awareness.

## Key Concepts

### What is Attention?

**Definition:** The cognitive process of selectively concentrating on relevant information while ignoring other perceivable information.

**William James (1890):** "Everyone knows what attention is. It is the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought."

**Why attention is necessary:**
- Limited processing capacity
- Need to prioritize behaviorally relevant information
- Cannot process everything equally

### Types of Attention

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Selective** | Focus on one stimulus, ignore others | Listening to one conversation at party |
| **Divided** | Attend to multiple stimuli | Driving while talking |
| **Sustained** | Maintain focus over time | Monitoring radar screen |
| **Executive** | Control and coordinate attention | Task switching |

**Overt vs. Covert:**
- **Overt** - Attention with eye/head movement
- **Covert** - Attention shifts without movement

**Endogenous vs. Exogenous:**
- **Endogenous (top-down)** - Voluntary, goal-directed
- **Exogenous (bottom-up)** - Stimulus-driven, reflexive

### Selective Attention Phenomena

**Cocktail Party Effect:**
- Can attend to one voice in noisy room
- Unattended information mostly filtered
- But: own name in unattended channel breaks through

**Inattentional Blindness:**
- Failure to notice unexpected objects when attending elsewhere
- "Invisible gorilla" experiment (Simons & Chabris)

**Change Blindness:**
- Failure to detect changes when view is briefly interrupted
- Even large changes missed without attention

**Attentional Blink:**
- Brief period after detecting target where second target is missed
- ~200-500ms window of impairment

### Theories of Attention

#### Filter Theories

**Early Selection (Broadbent, 1958):**
- Physical features filtered early
- Unattended information not processed for meaning
- Evidence: Dichotic listening—little memory for unattended ear

**Late Selection (Deutsch & Deutsch, 1963):**
- All information processed to semantic level
- Selection occurs at response stage
- Evidence: Semantic priming from unattended channel

**Attenuation Theory (Treisman, 1964):**
- Compromise: unattended signals attenuated (not blocked)
- Important/relevant info has low threshold—can break through

#### Feature Integration Theory (Treisman)

**Two stages:**
1. **Preattentive** - Features processed in parallel, automatically
2. **Attentive** - Features bound into objects, requires attention

**Visual search:**
- **Pop-out** - Target defined by single feature (fast, parallel)
- **Conjunction search** - Target defined by feature combination (slow, serial)

**Illusory conjunctions:**
- Without attention, features may be wrongly combined
- Seeing "blue X" when shown red X and blue O

#### Biased Competition Model (Desimone & Duncan)

- Multiple stimuli compete for neural representation
- Attention biases competition toward relevant stimulus
- Top-down signals from frontal/parietal increase activation
- Competitive interactions occur throughout visual hierarchy

### Neural Basis of Attention

#### Attention Networks

**Dorsal Attention Network (goal-directed):**
- Intraparietal sulcus (IPS)
- Frontal eye fields (FEF)
- Top-down control, voluntary attention

**Ventral Attention Network (stimulus-driven):**
- Temporoparietal junction (TPJ)
- Ventral frontal cortex
- Reorienting to unexpected but relevant stimuli

**Cingulo-opercular Network (sustained attention):**
- Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)
- Anterior insula
- Maintaining task set over time

#### Effects of Attention on Neural Processing

**Sensory gain:**
- Attention increases firing rates in sensory cortex
- Enhanced response to attended stimuli

**Baseline shifts:**
- Anticipatory increases before stimulus appears
- Preparatory activity

**Noise reduction:**
- Decreased response to irrelevant stimuli
- Sharper tuning curves

**Synchronization:**
- Gamma-band synchrony (30-80 Hz) increased
- May bind features into coherent objects

### Hemispatial Neglect

**Syndrome:** Failure to attend to contralesional (usually left) space after right parietal damage

**Features:**
- Not blindness—can see if attention directed
- Affects perception, action, mental imagery
- May bisect lines to right of center
- May ignore left side of objects, drawings, even own body

**Theoretical importance:**
- Reveals attention as distinct from perception
- Shows right hemisphere dominance for spatial attention

### Consciousness

#### What is Consciousness?

**Phenomenal consciousness:** Subjective, qualitative experience ("what it's like")

**Access consciousness:** Information available for reasoning, report, behavior control

**The Hard Problem (Chalmers):**
- Why is there subjective experience at all?
- Why doesn't information processing happen "in the dark"?
- Neural correlates don't explain why consciousness exists

#### Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCC)

**Definition:** Minimal neural mechanisms jointly sufficient for conscious experience

**Methods for studying:**
- Compare aware vs. unaware perception (masking, binocular rivalry)
- Contrast waking, sleep, anesthesia, coma
- Study patients with disorders of consciousness

**Key findings:**
- Not just primary sensory cortex—requires further processing
- Frontoparietal network important for reportable awareness
- Recurrent processing between areas (not just feedforward)

#### Theories of Consciousness

**Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene):**
- Consciousness = information broadcast globally
- Local processing unconscious; becomes conscious when "ignited" and broadcast
- Frontoparietal "workspace" makes info available across brain
- Explains limited capacity (one workspace)

**Integrated Information Theory (Tononi):**
- Consciousness = integrated information (Φ, phi)
- System is conscious to degree it is both integrated and differentiated
- Could apply to non-biological systems
- Predicts consciousness varies in degree

**Higher-Order Theories:**
- Consciousness requires representation OF representation
- Meta-awareness: thinking about thinking
- Prefrontal cortex critical

**Predictive Processing:**
- Brain constantly predicting sensory input
- Consciousness = what the brain "settles on" as best model
- Prediction errors update the model

### Altered States of Consciousness

#### Sleep

**Sleep stages:**

| Stage | EEG Pattern | Features |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Waking | Beta (14-30 Hz) | Alert, active |
| Drowsy | Alpha (8-12 Hz) | Relaxed, eyes closed |
| N1 | Theta (4-7 Hz) | Light sleep |
| N2 | Sleep spindles, K-complexes | Deeper sleep |
| N3 (SWS) | Delta (0.5-2 Hz) | Deep sleep, difficult to wake |
| REM | Fast, desynchronized | Dreams, paralysis |

**Functions of sleep:**
- Memory consolidation
- Metabolic clearance (glymphatic system)
- Synaptic homeostasis

**Dreams:**
- Most vivid during REM
- Activation-synthesis theory: Random brainstem activity interpreted by cortex
- May involve memory replay and emotional processing

#### Anesthesia

- Loss of consciousness with preserved vital functions
- Different agents, but common effects on cortical integration
- Disrupts frontoparietal connectivity
- Reduces complexity of brain dynamics

#### Disorders of Consciousness

| Condition | Awareness | Sleep-Wake | Motor Response |
|-----------|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Coma** | None | None | None/reflexive |
| **Vegetative state (UWS)** | None | Present | Reflexive only |
| **Minimally conscious** | Partial | Present | Inconsistent purposeful |
| **Locked-in** | Full | Present | Only eye movement |

## Real-World Examples

- **ADHD:** Attention regulation difficulties; involves dopamine, prefrontal function
- **Meditation:** Training sustained and selective attention; alters brain networks
- **Blindsight:** Unconscious vision—responds to stimuli without awareness
- **Anesthesia awareness:** Rare cases of consciousness during surgery
- **Split-brain patients:** Two streams of consciousness after callosotomy

## Summary

Attention selects relevant information from the environment, with different networks supporting goal-directed versus stimulus-driven attention. Damage to parietal cortex causes neglect, revealing attention's neural basis. Consciousness—subjective experience—remains mysterious but can be studied through its neural correlates. Leading theories emphasize global information broadcast or integrated information. Altered states like sleep, anesthesia, and disorders of consciousness provide windows into how brain activity relates to awareness.
