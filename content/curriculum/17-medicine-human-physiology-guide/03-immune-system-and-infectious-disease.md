# Immune System & Infectious Disease

## Immune System Overview

Two branches, layered defense:

### Innate Immunity (Non-specific, Immediate)
- **Physical barriers:** Skin, mucous membranes, stomach acid, tears (lysozyme)
- **Cellular:** Neutrophils (most abundant WBC; first responders), macrophages (phagocytosis + antigen presentation), natural killer cells (kill virus-infected/cancer cells), dendritic cells (bridge to adaptive immunity)
- **Chemical:** Complement system (cascade of ~30 proteins; opsonization, membrane attack complex), cytokines (signaling), interferons (antiviral)
- **Inflammation:** Redness (rubor), heat (calor), swelling (tumor), pain (dolor), loss of function (functio laesa); vasodilation + increased vascular permeability → immune cell recruitment. Mechanism: tissue damage/pathogen detection → resident macrophages/mast cells release histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) → endothelial activation → selectins slow neutrophils (rolling) → integrins bind (adhesion) → diapedesis (migration through vessel wall) → chemotaxis (follow chemical gradient to infection site). Systemic inflammation: fever (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α → hypothalamus ↑ set point via prostaglandin E2), acute phase proteins (CRP, ferritin from liver), leukocytosis.
- **Pattern recognition:** Innate immune cells detect pathogens via pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) — e.g., LPS (gram-negative bacteria), peptidoglycan (gram-positive), dsRNA (viruses), flagellin. Key PRR families: Toll-like receptors (TLRs, cell surface + endosomal), NOD-like receptors (NLRs, cytoplasmic; NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β, IL-18), RIG-I-like receptors (cytoplasmic viral RNA sensors). Also detect damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) from injured cells → "sterile inflammation."

### Adaptive Immunity (Specific, Develops Over Days)

**Cell-mediated (T-cells, mature in Thymus):**
- **CD4+ Helper T-cells:** Coordinate immune response; activate B-cells, cytotoxic T-cells, macrophages
  - Th1: Intracellular pathogens (viruses, TB)
  - Th2: Parasites, allergies
  - Th17: Extracellular bacteria, fungi
  - Treg: Suppress immune response; prevent autoimmunity
- **CD8+ Cytotoxic T-cells:** Directly kill infected cells (recognize antigen on MHC I)
- **Memory T-cells:** Long-lived; rapid response on re-exposure

**Humoral (B-cells, mature in Bone marrow):**
- Produce antibodies (immunoglobulins) specific to antigens
- **Antibody classes:**

| Class | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| IgG | Blood, tissues | Most abundant (~75%); crosses placenta; main memory response |
| IgA | Mucosal surfaces, breast milk | First-line defense at mucosal barriers |
| IgM | Blood | First antibody produced; pentamer; good at activating complement |
| IgE | Tissues | Allergic reactions; parasites; binds mast cells → histamine release |
| IgD | B-cell surface | B-cell maturation signaling |

**MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex):**
- MHC I: On all nucleated cells; presents intracellular peptides to CD8+ T-cells
- MHC II: On antigen-presenting cells (macrophages, dendritic cells, B-cells); presents extracellular peptides to CD4+ T-cells
- HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen): Human version of MHC; critical for transplant matching

## Infectious Disease

### Bacterial Infections

| Disease | Organism | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| Tuberculosis | *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* | ~10M cases/year; latent in ~25% of world; MDR-TB growing problem |
| Pneumonia | *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, others | Leading infectious killer of children; vaccine-preventable |
| UTI | *E. coli* (80%) | Most common bacterial infection; 50% of women experience |
| Sepsis | Various | Dysregulated immune response → organ failure; ~11M deaths/year; mortality 20-40% |
| C. difficile | *Clostridioides difficile* | Antibiotic-associated diarrhea; hospital-acquired; ~500K cases/year US |
| Syphilis | *Treponema pallidum* | Stages: primary (chancre) → secondary (rash) → latent → tertiary (neuro, cardiac) |
| Cholera | *Vibrio cholerae* | Watery diarrhea → severe dehydration; killed millions historically; sanitation prevents |

### Viral Infections

| Disease | Virus | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| Influenza | Influenza A/B | ~500K deaths/year; antigenic drift (seasonal) and shift (pandemic); 1918 flu killed ~50M |
| HIV/AIDS | HIV-1, HIV-2 | ~39M living with HIV; targets CD4+ T-cells; ART = chronic manageable condition |
| COVID-19 | SARS-CoV-2 | ~7M+ official deaths; mRNA vaccine revolution; long COVID affects ~10% |
| Hepatitis B | HBV | ~300M chronic carriers; liver cancer risk; vaccine-preventable |
| Hepatitis C | HCV | ~58M chronic; NOW CURABLE with DAAs (>95% cure rate) |
| HPV | Human papillomavirus | Most common STI; causes cervical cancer; vaccine prevents ~90% of cancers |
| Rabies | Rabies virus | ~59K deaths/year (mostly Asia/Africa); nearly 100% fatal once symptomatic |
| Ebola | Ebolavirus | ~50% case fatality; outbreaks in Africa; vaccine now available |

### Fungal Infections
- Superficial: Athlete's foot, ringworm, vaginal candidiasis (common, usually mild)
- Systemic: Aspergillosis, cryptococcal meningitis, Pneumocystis pneumonia (immunocompromised patients; high mortality)
- Rising threat: Drug-resistant *Candida auris* (hospital outbreaks)

### Parasitic Infections

| Disease | Parasite | Burden |
|---|---|---|
| Malaria | *Plasmodium* spp. (via Anopheles mosquito) | ~250M cases/year; ~600K deaths; mostly children in Africa |
| Schistosomiasis | *Schistosoma* (via freshwater snails) | ~240M infected; chronic organ damage |
| Intestinal worms | Hookworm, roundworm, whipworm | ~1.5B infected; malnutrition, cognitive impairment in children |

## Epidemiology Basics

### Key Metrics

| Metric | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Incidence** | New cases per population per time period |
| **Prevalence** | Total existing cases at a point in time |
| **R₀ (Basic reproduction number)** | Average secondary infections from one case in fully susceptible population |
| **Rt (Effective reproduction number)** | R₀ adjusted for immunity/interventions; Rt < 1 = declining epidemic |
| **Case fatality rate (CFR)** | Deaths / confirmed cases (biased by testing) |
| **Infection fatality rate (IFR)** | Deaths / all infections (including undetected) |

### R₀ Values

| Disease | R₀ | Herd Immunity Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Measles | 12–18 | ~92–95% |
| Chickenpox | 10–12 | ~90% |
| Smallpox | 5–7 | ~80–85% |
| COVID-19 (Omicron) | ~8–15 | ~85–93% |
| COVID-19 (original) | 2–3 | ~50–67% |
| Influenza | 1.5–2 | ~33–50% |
| Ebola | 1.5–2.5 | ~33–60% |

**Herd immunity threshold** = 1 - (1/R₀)

## Vaccines

### How Vaccines Work (General Principle)
Expose immune system to antigen (or instructions to make antigen) without causing disease → B-cells produce antibodies + memory B-cells; T-cells develop antigen-specific memory. On real infection: rapid, strong secondary immune response (days vs weeks for naive response). Memory cells can persist decades (smallpox vaccination → detectable immunity 75+ years later).

### Types — Detailed Mechanisms

**Live Attenuated**
- Pathogen weakened by serial passage in cell culture/animals until it replicates poorly in humans but retains antigenicity
- Mechanism: Replicates in host (limited) → mimics natural infection → activates both humoral AND cellular immunity; triggers mucosal immunity if given orally/nasally
- Advantages: Strong, long-lasting immunity (often lifelong with 1-2 doses); broad immune response (multiple antigens)
- Disadvantages: Can revert to virulence (rare; oral polio → vaccine-derived polio in ~1/2.7M doses); contraindicated in immunocompromised (could cause actual disease) and pregnancy
- Examples: MMR, chickenpox (varicella), yellow fever, oral polio (Sabin), BCG (TB), rotavirus, nasal flu (FluMist)

**Inactivated (Killed)**
- Pathogen killed by heat, chemicals (formaldehyde), or radiation → can't replicate
- Mechanism: Whole killed organism presented to immune system → primarily humoral response (antibodies); weaker cellular immunity than live vaccines
- Advantages: No risk of reversion; safe for immunocompromised
- Disadvantages: Weaker/shorter immunity → need multiple doses + boosters; no mucosal immunity; more expensive to produce (need to grow pathogen)
- Examples: Injectable flu, hepatitis A, rabies, injectable polio (Salk), whole-cell pertussis

**Subunit/Recombinant**
- Only specific protein(s) from pathogen used as antigen, produced via recombinant DNA technology (gene for antigen inserted into yeast/cell line → protein produced and purified)
- Mechanism: Purified protein + adjuvant → stimulates antibody production against that specific antigen. Adjuvants (aluminum salts, AS04, MF59) enhance immune response by activating innate immunity at injection site.
- Advantages: Very safe (no pathogen at all); can target most immunogenic protein; standardized production
- Disadvantages: Narrow immune response (single protein); usually requires adjuvant + multiple doses; may miss conformational epitopes
- Examples: Hepatitis B (HBsAg produced in yeast), HPV (L1 protein self-assembles into virus-like particles, VLPs — no DNA inside), acellular pertussis (purified toxin + surface proteins), Novavax COVID (spike protein nanoparticle)

**Toxoid**
- Bacterial toxin inactivated by formaldehyde → retains structure for immune recognition but non-toxic
- Mechanism: Antibodies generated against toxoid → neutralize real toxin on exposure (don't prevent infection but prevent disease)
- Examples: Tetanus (anti-tetanospasmin), diphtheria (anti-diphtheria toxin). Given as Td/Tdap boosters every 10 years.

**mRNA**
- Synthetic mRNA encoding target antigen encapsulated in lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)
- Mechanism: LNPs fuse with cell membrane → mRNA released into cytoplasm → ribosomes translate mRNA → antigen protein produced → displayed on cell surface via MHC I → activates CD8+ T-cells; also secreted → picked up by APCs → displayed via MHC II → activates CD4+ T-cells → help B-cells produce antibodies. mRNA degraded within days by normal cellular RNases. Does NOT enter nucleus; cannot integrate into DNA.
- Advantages: No live pathogen; rapid design (Moderna designed COVID vaccine in 2 days after sequence released); scalable manufacturing; strong humoral + cellular response; platform adaptable to new targets quickly
- Disadvantages: Cold chain storage (Pfizer: -70°C initially, now -20°C; Moderna: -20°C); reactogenicity (fever, fatigue from strong innate immune activation); durability of response may be shorter than live vaccines → boosters needed; lipid nanoparticle technology still being optimized
- Examples: Pfizer-BioNTech (BNT162b2) and Moderna (mRNA-1273) COVID-19 vaccines; Moderna RSV vaccine (mRESVIA, 2024)
- Key innovation: Modified nucleosides (pseudouridine replacing uridine, Karikó & Weissman's Nobel Prize-winning work) → prevents innate immune destruction of mRNA → allows sufficient protein production

**Viral Vector**
- Harmless virus (adenovirus, typically non-replicating) engineered to carry gene encoding target antigen
- Mechanism: Vector infects cells → target gene expressed → antigen produced → immune response (similar to mRNA but DNA → mRNA → protein route; gene enters nucleus for transcription but does NOT integrate into host genome). Strong cellular + humoral immunity.
- Advantages: Strong immune response; more stable than mRNA (regular refrigeration); well-suited for resource-limited settings
- Disadvantages: Pre-existing immunity to vector virus can reduce efficacy (why J&J used rare Ad26; AstraZeneca used chimpanzee adenovirus); anti-vector immunity limits repeat dosing with same vector; rare side effect: vaccine-induced thrombotic thrombocytopenia (VITT) with adenoviral vectors (1 in 50,000-100,000)
- Examples: J&J/Janssen COVID (Ad26), AstraZeneca COVID (ChAdOx1), Sputnik V (Ad26 + Ad5), Ebola (rVSV-ZEBOV)

**Conjugate**
- Polysaccharide capsule antigen (from encapsulated bacteria) chemically linked to carrier protein (tetanus/diphtheria toxoid)
- Mechanism: Polysaccharides alone only activate B-cells (T-independent, weak response, no memory, ineffective in children <2 years). Protein conjugation → T-dependent response → T-helper cells activated → memory B-cells formed → effective in infants/young children → long-lasting immunity
- Why it matters: Before conjugate vaccines, polysaccharide vaccines didn't work in children <2 (the group most vulnerable to meningitis, pneumonia, epiglottitis)
- Examples: Pneumococcal (PCV13, PCV20), Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib), Meningococcal (MenACWY)

### Vaccine Impact
- Smallpox: **Eradicated** (1980) — only human disease ever eradicated through vaccination
- Polio: 99.9% reduction; near eradication (endemic in 2 countries as of 2024); surveillance continues for vaccine-derived poliovirus
- Measles vaccine: Estimated 56M deaths prevented (2000–2021)
- Childhood vaccination: Prevents ~4-5M deaths/year
- COVID mRNA vaccines: Estimated 20M deaths averted in first year
- HPV vaccine: Expected to virtually eliminate cervical cancer within decades in countries with high uptake (Australia on track for elimination by ~2035)
- Rinderpest: Second disease eradicated (2011) — cattle plague; eradicated through vaccination

### Vaccine Safety & Anti-Vaccine Movement
- Vaccines are among the most studied medical interventions; serious adverse events extremely rare
- Wakefield (1998): Fraudulent paper claimed MMR → autism; fully retracted; Wakefield lost medical license; no causal link found in studies of millions of children
- Real rare risks: Anaphylaxis (~1 in 1M doses), GBS with some flu vaccines (~1-2 per million), VITT with adenoviral COVID vaccines (~1 in 50K-100K), febrile seizures (benign, with MMR/MMRV)
- Vaccine hesitancy: WHO named it top 10 global health threat (2019); driven by misinformation, distrust, complacency; results in outbreaks of preventable diseases (measles resurgence in 2019, 2024)

## Antibiotic Resistance

**WHO: "One of the greatest threats to global health"**

### Mechanisms
- Enzymatic destruction (β-lactamases destroy penicillins)
- Target modification (altered ribosome → macrolide resistance)
- Efflux pumps (pump drug out of cell)
- Reduced permeability (altered porins)

### Key Resistant Organisms ("ESKAPE" pathogens)
- **E**nterococcus faecium (VRE)
- **S**taphylococcus aureus (MRSA)
- **K**lebsiella pneumoniae (ESBL, CRE)
- **A**cinetobacter baumannii (MDR)
- **P**seudomonas aeruginosa (MDR)
- **E**nterobacter species (ESBL)

### Scale of Problem
- ~1.27M deaths directly attributable to AMR in 2019; ~4.95M associated
- Projected: ~10M deaths/year by 2050 if unaddressed (O'Neill report)
- New antibiotic development has slowed (unprofitable; ~2 new classes in 50 years)
