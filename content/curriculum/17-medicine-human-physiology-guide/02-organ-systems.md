# Organ Systems

## Cardiovascular System

### Structure
- **Heart:** 4 chambers (RA, RV, LA, LV); ~300g; beats ~100,000/day
- **Circulation:** RA → RV → lungs (pulmonary) → LA → LV → body (systemic) → RA
- **Blood vessels:** Arteries (away from heart, high pressure) → arterioles → capillaries (gas exchange) → venules → veins (toward heart, low pressure, valves)
- **Blood:** ~5L total; plasma (55%) + red blood cells (45%, carry O₂ via hemoglobin) + white blood cells + platelets

### Key Physiology
- **Cardiac output** = Heart rate × Stroke volume; ~5 L/min at rest, ~25 L/min exercise
- **Blood pressure:** Systolic (ventricular contraction) / Diastolic (relaxation); regulated by autonomic nervous system, RAAS, kidneys
- **Electrical conduction:** SA node (pacemaker, ~60-100 bpm) → AV node (0.1s delay allows atrial emptying) → Bundle of His → right/left bundle branches → Purkinje fibers (rapid ventricular depolarization)
- **Cardiac cycle:** Diastole (relaxation/filling, ~2/3 of cycle) → Systole (contraction/ejection, ~1/3). Valves ensure unidirectional flow: AV valves (tricuspid, mitral) close at systole onset (S1 sound); semilunar valves (aortic, pulmonic) close at diastole onset (S2 sound). Murmurs = turbulent flow through abnormal valves.
- **Frank-Starling mechanism:** Greater venous return → more stretch → stronger contraction; heart auto-adjusts output to match demand without neural input
- **Coronary arteries:** Supply heart muscle; LAD ("widow maker") supplies anterior LV, most commonly occluded; RCA supplies inferior wall + SA/AV nodes; blockage = myocardial infarction (heart attack)
- **Blood pressure regulation:** Short-term: baroreceptors (carotid sinus, aortic arch) → medulla → adjust HR/vessel tone. Long-term: RAAS (renin from kidneys → angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone → Na⁺/water retention); ADH (water retention); natriuretic peptides (ANP/BNP from heart → Na⁺ excretion, vasodilation)
- **Hemostasis:** Vascular spasm → platelet plug (adhesion, activation, aggregation via vWF, thromboxane A2, ADP) → coagulation cascade (intrinsic/extrinsic → common pathway → fibrin mesh) → fibrinolysis (plasmin dissolves clot). Anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin, DOACs) target different steps.

### Cross-System Interactions (Cardiovascular)
- **CV ↔ Renal:** Kidneys control blood volume/pressure via RAAS; heart failure → renal hypoperfusion → cardiorenal syndrome (each worsens the other)
- **CV ↔ Respiratory:** Pulmonary circulation = gas exchange; pulmonary hypertension → right heart failure (cor pulmonale); hypoxia → pulmonary vasoconstriction (opposite of systemic vessels)
- **CV ↔ Endocrine:** Catecholamines (adrenal medulla) ↑ HR/contractility; thyroid hormones ↑ cardiac output; ANP/BNP from heart are themselves hormones
- **CV ↔ Nervous:** Autonomic control: sympathetic ↑ HR/contractility/vasoconstriction; parasympathetic (vagus) ↓ HR. Vasovagal syncope = excessive parasympathetic → bradycardia + vasodilation → fainting

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Mechanism | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| Hypertension | Chronically elevated BP (>140/90) | ~1.3 billion globally |
| Atherosclerosis | Plaque buildup in arteries (cholesterol, inflammation) | Underlies most CVD |
| Myocardial infarction | Coronary artery blockage → heart muscle death | ~800K/year in US |
| Heart failure | Heart can't pump adequately; HFrEF (reduced ejection) or HFpEF (preserved) | ~64M globally |
| Atrial fibrillation | Irregular heartbeat; stroke risk | ~33M globally |
| Aortic aneurysm | Aortic wall weakening/ballooning; rupture = usually fatal | ~200K/year US |

## Respiratory System

### Structure
- **Upper:** Nose, pharynx, larynx
- **Lower:** Trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli (~480 million; surface area ~70 m²)
- **Diaphragm:** Primary breathing muscle; innervated by phrenic nerve (C3-C5: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive")

### Key Physiology
- **Gas exchange:** O₂ from alveoli → blood (binds hemoglobin); CO₂ from blood → alveoli → exhaled. Driven by partial pressure gradients (Fick's law of diffusion). Alveolar pO₂ ~100 mmHg, venous blood pO₂ ~40 mmHg → O₂ moves into blood. Blood pCO₂ ~46 mmHg, alveolar pCO₂ ~40 mmHg → CO₂ moves into alveoli.
- **Ventilation:** ~12-20 breaths/min; ~500 mL tidal volume; ~6 L/min total. Dead space (~150 mL) = airways where no gas exchange occurs. Alveolar ventilation = (tidal volume - dead space) × rate.
- **Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) matching:** Ideal V/Q ratio ~0.8. Mismatch = most common cause of hypoxemia. High V/Q = ventilated but not perfused (dead space, e.g., PE). Low V/Q = perfused but not ventilated (shunt, e.g., pneumonia). Body compensates via hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (redirects blood from poorly ventilated areas).
- **Oxygen transport:** Hemoglobin saturation curve (sigmoidal); right shift = easier O₂ release (exercise, acidosis, heat, ↑ 2,3-DPG); left shift = tighter O₂ binding (alkalosis, cold, carbon monoxide, fetal hemoglobin). CO poisoning: binds hemoglobin 250x stronger than O₂; cherry-red appearance; treat with 100% O₂ or hyperbaric O₂.
- **CO₂ transport:** 7% dissolved, 23% bound to hemoglobin (carbaminohemoglobin), 70% as bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻, via carbonic anhydrase in RBCs); CO₂ is primary driver of respiratory rate (central chemoreceptors in medulla sense pH/CO₂; peripheral chemoreceptors in carotid/aortic bodies sense O₂/CO₂/pH)
- **Acid-base balance:** Respiratory system adjusts CO₂ to compensate for metabolic disturbances. Hyperventilation → ↓ CO₂ → respiratory alkalosis. Hypoventilation → ↑ CO₂ → respiratory acidosis. Works with kidneys (HCO₃⁻ regulation) to maintain pH 7.35-7.45.
- **Lung mechanics:** Compliance (stretchability; ↓ in fibrosis, ↑ in emphysema), elastance (recoil), surfactant (reduces surface tension in alveoli; premature infants lack it → neonatal respiratory distress syndrome). Inspiration = active (diaphragm contracts, negative intrapleural pressure); expiration = passive at rest, active during exercise.

### Cross-System Interactions (Respiratory)
- **Resp ↔ Renal:** Kidneys compensate for respiratory acid-base disorders by retaining/excreting HCO₃⁻ (takes 3-5 days). Respiratory system compensates for metabolic disorders within minutes (Kussmaul breathing in DKA = deep/rapid breathing to blow off CO₂).
- **Resp ↔ Immune:** Lungs have own immune defenses — alveolar macrophages, mucociliary escalator (cilia + mucus trap particles), IgA in airways. Failure → pneumonia, especially in immunocompromised.
- **Resp ↔ Nervous:** Breathing is automatic (medullary respiratory center) but can be voluntarily overridden (cortex). Sleep apnea: obstructive (airway collapse) or central (brain fails to signal breathing).

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Asthma | Bronchospasm + inflammation → airway narrowing; reversible |
| COPD (emphysema + chronic bronchitis) | Irreversible airway destruction; smoking primary cause |
| Pneumonia | Lung infection (bacterial, viral, fungal); alveolar filling |
| Pulmonary embolism | Blood clot in pulmonary artery; from DVT; can be fatal |
| Lung cancer | #1 cancer killer; ~85% from smoking |
| Pulmonary fibrosis | Scarring of lung tissue; progressive; poor prognosis |

## Digestive System

### Structure (mouth to anus: ~9 meters)
- **Mouth:** Mechanical + enzymatic digestion (amylase)
- **Esophagus:** Peristalsis; lower esophageal sphincter (prevents reflux)
- **Stomach:** HCl (pH ~2) + pepsin → protein digestion; churning; 2-4 hour retention
- **Small intestine** (~6m): Duodenum (bile + pancreatic enzymes), jejunum, ileum; primary absorption site; villi increase surface area to ~32 m²
- **Large intestine** (~1.5m): Water absorption, gut microbiome fermentation, feces formation
- **Liver:** 500+ functions; detoxification, bile production, protein synthesis, glycogen storage, drug metabolism
- **Pancreas:** Exocrine (digestive enzymes) + endocrine (insulin, glucagon)
- **Gallbladder:** Stores/concentrates bile for fat digestion

### Key Digestive Physiology (Mechanisms)
- **Digestion phases:** Cephalic (sight/smell → vagus → gastric secretion, ~20%), gastric (stretch/food → gastrin → HCl/pepsinogen, ~70%), intestinal (chyme enters duodenum → secretin + CCK → pancreatic/bile secretion, ~10%)
- **Nutrient absorption:** Carbs → glucose/fructose/galactose via SGLT1/GLUT transporters; Proteins → amino acids/dipeptides/tripeptides via H⁺-coupled transporters; Fats → monoglycerides + fatty acids → micelles → enterocyte → chylomicrons → lymphatics (bypass portal circulation). Iron absorbed in duodenum; B12 absorbed in terminal ileum (requires intrinsic factor from stomach).
- **Gut microbiome:** ~100 trillion organisms; 500-1000 species; essential for fermentation of fiber → short-chain fatty acids (butyrate feeds colonocytes), vitamin K/B12 synthesis, immune training, bile acid metabolism, pathogen resistance. Disruption (dysbiosis) linked to IBD, obesity, metabolic syndrome, neuropsychiatric conditions.
- **Liver metabolism:** Phase I (CYP450 oxidation) + Phase II (conjugation) = drug/toxin clearance. First-pass effect: oral drugs pass through liver before systemic circulation → reduced bioavailability. Liver synthesizes albumin, clotting factors, bile acids, urea (ammonia detox). Regenerative capacity remarkable: can regrow from 25% of original mass.
- **Enterohepatic circulation:** Bile salts secreted into duodenum → assist fat absorption → reabsorbed in terminal ileum → returned to liver via portal vein → re-secreted. ~95% recycled; cycle occurs 6-8x/day.

### Cross-System Interactions (Digestive)
- **GI ↔ Endocrine:** Gut is largest endocrine organ — secretes GLP-1, GIP (incretins → insulin release), ghrelin (hunger), PYY (satiety), CCK (gallbladder contraction, satiety). GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide) leverage this pathway for diabetes/obesity treatment.
- **GI ↔ Immune:** ~70% of immune tissue (GALT) in gut; Peyer's patches, mesenteric lymph nodes. Intestinal barrier dysfunction ("leaky gut") → immune activation → possible role in autoimmunity, metabolic disease.
- **GI ↔ Nervous:** Enteric nervous system ("second brain") = ~500M neurons; can function independently of CNS. Gut-brain axis: vagus nerve bidirectional communication; serotonin (95% produced in gut), gut microbiome metabolites influence mood/cognition.

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Key Facts |
|---|---|
| GERD | Acid reflux; chronic → Barrett's esophagus (intestinal metaplasia) → esophageal adenocarcinoma risk; treated with PPIs (omeprazole) |
| Peptic ulcer | 60-70% from H. pylori; 20% from NSAIDs; acid + compromised mucosal defense; triple therapy (PPI + 2 antibiotics) cures H. pylori |
| Celiac disease | Autoimmune; gluten triggers small intestinal damage (villous atrophy); ~1% prevalence; diagnosed by anti-tTG antibodies + biopsy; lifelong gluten-free diet |
| IBD (Crohn's, UC) | Autoimmune; chronic intestinal inflammation; Crohn's (any GI, transmural, skip lesions, fistulas, granulomas); UC (colon only, mucosal, continuous, bloody diarrhea). Treatment: 5-ASA, steroids, immunomodulators, biologics (anti-TNF, vedolizumab) |
| Cirrhosis | End-stage liver scarring; causes: alcohol, hepatitis B/C, NAFLD/NASH (now most common in West). Complications: portal hypertension → varices/ascites, hepatic encephalopathy (ammonia → brain), coagulopathy, hepatocellular carcinoma. Child-Pugh/MELD scores assess severity. |
| Pancreatitis | Inflammation; acute (gallstones #1, alcohol #2) → autodigestion by own enzymes; lipase elevation diagnostic. Chronic → exocrine insufficiency, diabetes, calcification. |
| NAFLD/NASH | Non-alcoholic fatty liver → steatohepatitis; affects ~25% of adults globally; linked to metabolic syndrome; can progress to cirrhosis; becoming #1 indication for liver transplant |
| Colorectal cancer | #3 cancer; adenoma → carcinoma sequence over ~10 years; screening colonoscopy at 45+ detects/removes polyps before cancer; MSI-high tumors respond well to immunotherapy |

## Musculoskeletal System

### Structure
- **Bones:** 206 in adult; functions: support, protection, movement, mineral storage, blood cell production (marrow)
- **Joints:** Synovial (freely movable: knee, hip, shoulder), cartilaginous (limited: spine), fibrous (fixed: skull)
- **Skeletal muscle:** ~600 muscles; ~40% of body mass; voluntary control; striated
- **Tendons** connect muscle to bone; **Ligaments** connect bone to bone

### Key Physiology
- **Bone remodeling:** Continuous; osteoblasts (build, from mesenchymal stem cells) vs osteoclasts (resorb, from monocyte lineage); regulated by PTH (↑ resorption), calcitonin (↓ resorption), vitamin D (↑ Ca²⁺ absorption), mechanical stress (Wolff's law: bone adapts to loads placed on it). Peak bone mass ~30 years; declines ~1%/year after; accelerates post-menopause (estrogen loss removes brake on osteoclasts).
- **Muscle contraction (sliding filament):** Neural signal → acetylcholine at neuromuscular junction → action potential along sarcolemma → T-tubules → sarcoplasmic reticulum releases Ca²⁺ → Ca²⁺ binds troponin → tropomyosin shifts → exposes myosin binding sites on actin → myosin heads pull actin (power stroke, requires ATP) → filaments slide → muscle shortens. Relaxation: Ca²⁺ pumped back into SR (requires ATP). Rigor mortis = no ATP → myosin stuck to actin.
- **Muscle fiber types:** Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative, fatigue-resistant, endurance — marathon); Type IIa (fast-twitch, oxidative-glycolytic, moderate fatigue — middle distance); Type IIx (fast-twitch, glycolytic, fatigues quickly, power — sprinting). Fiber type ratio partly genetic, partly trainable.
- **Muscle types:** Skeletal (voluntary, striated), Cardiac (involuntary, striated, intercalated discs enable synchronized contraction), Smooth (involuntary, non-striated, walls of organs/vessels, slow sustained contraction)

### Cross-System Interactions (Musculoskeletal)
- **MSK ↔ Endocrine:** Bone is endocrine organ — osteocalcin (from osteoblasts) regulates insulin sensitivity and testosterone. PTH/vitamin D/calcitonin regulate calcium homeostasis. GH/IGF-1 drive growth plate activity. Estrogen/testosterone critical for bone density.
- **MSK ↔ Metabolic:** Skeletal muscle = largest insulin-sensitive organ; primary site of glucose disposal post-meal. Exercise ↑ GLUT4 transporters independent of insulin. Myokines (IL-6, irisin) released during exercise have anti-inflammatory, metabolic effects. Sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) → insulin resistance → metabolic syndrome.
- **MSK ↔ Immune:** Bone marrow = site of hematopoiesis (all blood cells including immune cells produced here). Chronic inflammation (RA, IBD) → bone loss via cytokine-driven osteoclast activation.

### Key Pathologies
- **Osteoporosis:** Bone density loss (T-score ≤ -2.5 on DEXA scan); fracture risk; post-menopausal women most affected; ~200M globally. Treatment: bisphosphonates (alendronate — inhibit osteoclasts), denosumab (anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody), teriparatide (PTH analog, stimulates osteoblasts), weight-bearing exercise, calcium + vitamin D.
- **Osteoarthritis:** Cartilage degradation + subchondral bone changes; most common joint disease; age + obesity + joint injury are key risks; weight-bearing joints (knee, hip). No disease-modifying treatment; management: exercise, weight loss, NSAIDs, joint replacement.
- **Rheumatoid arthritis:** Autoimmune; symmetric joint inflammation and destruction; pannus formation erodes cartilage/bone. Treatment: DMARDs (methotrexate first-line), biologics (anti-TNF, rituximab). Early aggressive treatment prevents joint destruction.
- **Fractures:** Colles' (distal radius, FOOSH injury), hip (elderly, 30% mortality within 1 year in 80+), stress (overuse, common in runners/military), pathologic (through weakened bone — cancer metastasis, osteoporosis). Healing: hematoma → soft callus (cartilage) → hard callus (woven bone) → remodeling (lamellar bone). ~6-12 weeks typical.

## Endocrine System

### Major Glands and Hormones

| Gland | Key Hormones | Functions |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypothalamus** | Releasing/inhibiting hormones | Master regulator; links nervous + endocrine systems |
| **Pituitary (anterior)** | GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, Prolactin | "Master gland"; controls other endocrine glands |
| **Pituitary (posterior)** | ADH, Oxytocin | Water balance, labor/bonding |
| **Thyroid** | T3, T4 (thyroxine), Calcitonin | Metabolism rate, growth, development; Ca²⁺ regulation |
| **Parathyroid** | PTH | ↑ Blood calcium (bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, vitamin D activation) |
| **Adrenal cortex** | Cortisol, Aldosterone, Androgens | Stress response, sodium/water balance, secondary sex characteristics |
| **Adrenal medulla** | Epinephrine, Norepinephrine | Fight-or-flight; sympathetic nervous system |
| **Pancreas (islets)** | Insulin (β-cells), Glucagon (α-cells) | Blood glucose regulation |
| **Gonads** | Estrogen, Progesterone (ovaries); Testosterone (testes) | Reproduction, secondary sex characteristics |
| **Pineal** | Melatonin | Circadian rhythm |

### Key Concept: Feedback Loops
- **Negative feedback:** Most hormone regulation; output inhibits further production (e.g., high T3/T4 → ↓ TSH from pituitary → ↓ TRH from hypothalamus). Axes: HPA (hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal), HPT (hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid), HPG (hypothalamus-pituitary-gonadal).
- **Positive feedback:** Rare; output amplifies production (e.g., oxytocin during labor → uterine contractions → more oxytocin; LH surge in ovulation triggered by rising estrogen)
- **Pulsatile secretion:** Many hormones released in pulses, not constant (GH, GnRH, cortisol). Cortisol follows circadian rhythm — peaks early morning, nadir at midnight. Disruption → disease (Cushing's loses circadian rhythm).

### Hormone Signaling Mechanisms
- **Peptide hormones** (insulin, GH, ADH): Water-soluble; bind cell surface receptors; signal via second messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG, JAK-STAT); fast onset, short duration.
- **Steroid hormones** (cortisol, estrogen, testosterone, aldosterone): Lipid-soluble (derived from cholesterol); cross cell membrane; bind intracellular/nuclear receptors → alter gene transcription; slow onset, long duration.
- **Thyroid hormones** (T3/T4): Amino acid-derived but act like steroids; cross membrane; bind nuclear receptors; regulate basal metabolic rate of virtually every cell.
- **Catecholamines** (epinephrine, NE): Amino acid-derived; act like peptides; bind adrenergic receptors (α1, α2, β1, β2, β3) → different effects by tissue.

### Cross-System Interactions (Endocrine)
- **Endocrine ↔ Immune:** Cortisol = immunosuppressive (why chronic stress → immune dysfunction). Cytokines from immune cells activate HPA axis ("sickness behavior"). Autoimmune diseases frequently target endocrine glands (Hashimoto's, Graves', T1D, Addison's).
- **Endocrine ↔ Renal:** ADH → aquaporin insertion in collecting duct → water reabsorption. Aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption in distal tubule. Kidneys produce erythropoietin and activate vitamin D (1-alpha hydroxylation).
- **Endocrine ↔ Reproductive:** HPG axis controls fertility. GnRH → FSH/LH → gonads. Oral contraceptives work by negative feedback — exogenous estrogen/progesterone suppress FSH/LH → no ovulation.

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Diabetes (see Ch. 04) | Insulin deficiency or resistance |
| Hypothyroidism | ↓ Thyroid hormones; fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance; Hashimoto's (autoimmune) most common cause |
| Hyperthyroidism | ↑ Thyroid hormones; weight loss, anxiety, tremor, heat intolerance; Graves' disease most common |
| Cushing's syndrome | Excess cortisol; moon face, central obesity, diabetes, hypertension |
| Addison's disease | Adrenal insufficiency; fatigue, hypotension, hyperpigmentation |
| PCOS | Hyperandrogenism, anovulation, polycystic ovaries; ~10% of women |

## Renal System

### Structure
- **Kidneys** (2): ~150g each; ~1 million nephrons per kidney
- **Nephron:** Functional unit; glomerulus (filtration) → proximal tubule → loop of Henle → distal tubule → collecting duct
- **Ureters** → **Bladder** → **Urethra**

### Key Physiology
- Filters ~180 L/day; excretes ~1-2 L urine (99% reabsorbed)
- Regulates: water balance, electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺), acid-base balance (pH 7.35-7.45), blood pressure (RAAS)
- Produces: erythropoietin (stimulates red blood cell production), active vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D)
- **GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate):** Normal ~120 mL/min; key measure of kidney function; estimated by serum creatinine (eGFR)

### Nephron Function (Segment by Segment)

| Segment | What Happens | Key Transporters/Hormones |
|---|---|---|
| **Glomerulus** | Filtration by pressure; filters small molecules (<70 kDa), retains proteins/cells | Filtration barrier: endothelium + basement membrane + podocytes |
| **Proximal tubule** | Reabsorbs 65% of Na⁺/water, all glucose/amino acids, 85% HCO₃⁻, phosphate | SGLT2 (glucose; target of SGLT2 inhibitor drugs), Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger |
| **Loop of Henle (descending)** | Water reabsorption (permeable to water, not solute) → concentrates filtrate | Aquaporins |
| **Loop of Henle (ascending)** | Na⁺/K⁺/Cl⁻ reabsorption (impermeable to water) → dilutes filtrate; creates medullary gradient | NKCC2 transporter (target of loop diuretics like furosemide) |
| **Distal tubule** | Fine-tuning Na⁺/Cl⁻ reabsorption; Ca²⁺ reabsorption | NCC transporter (target of thiazide diuretics); PTH promotes Ca²⁺ reabsorption |
| **Collecting duct** | Final water reabsorption (ADH-dependent); K⁺ secretion; H⁺ secretion (acid-base) | Aquaporin-2 (inserted by ADH); ENaC (Na⁺ channel, target of aldosterone; K⁺-sparing diuretics block this) |

- **RAAS mechanism in detail:** ↓ renal perfusion/↓ Na⁺ at macula densa → juxtaglomerular cells release renin → renin cleaves angiotensinogen (liver) → angiotensin I → ACE (lungs) converts → angiotensin II → (1) vasoconstriction, (2) aldosterone release from adrenals → Na⁺/water retention, (3) ADH release, (4) thirst. ACE inhibitors and ARBs block this cascade.
- **Countercurrent multiplier:** Loop of Henle creates osmotic gradient in renal medulla (300 → 1200 mOsm/L from cortex to inner medulla); allows concentration of urine. Vasa recta (capillaries) preserve gradient via countercurrent exchange.

### Cross-System Interactions (Renal)
- **Renal ↔ CV:** Kidneys control blood volume → BP. Heart failure → ↓ renal perfusion → RAAS activation → fluid retention → worsens HF (vicious cycle). BNP released by heart → promotes Na⁺/water excretion (counterregulatory).
- **Renal ↔ Endocrine:** EPO production (CKD → anemia from ↓ EPO); vitamin D activation (CKD → ↓ active vitamin D → hypocalcemia → secondary hyperparathyroidism → renal osteodystrophy). SGLT2 inhibitors (originally for diabetes) now standard treatment for CKD regardless of diabetes status.
- **Renal ↔ Acid-Base:** Kidneys regenerate HCO₃⁻ and excrete H⁺ as titratable acid or NH₄⁺. Metabolic acidosis (e.g., DKA): kidneys increase NH₄⁺ excretion and HCO₃⁻ generation. Metabolic alkalosis: kidneys excrete excess HCO₃⁻.

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Key Facts |
|---|---|
| Acute kidney injury (AKI) | Sudden ↓ GFR; causes: dehydration, sepsis, nephrotoxic drugs, obstruction |
| Chronic kidney disease (CKD) | Progressive GFR decline; stages 1-5; stage 5 = ESRD (needs dialysis or transplant) |
| Kidney stones | Calcium oxalate most common (~80%); severe pain (renal colic) |
| Urinary tract infections | Women >> men (short urethra); E. coli most common; cystitis → pyelonephritis if ascending |
| Diabetic nephropathy | #1 cause of ESRD in developed world; glomerular damage from hyperglycemia |

## Reproductive System

### Male
- Testes (spermatogenesis ~74 days in seminiferous tubules; Sertoli cells support sperm maturation; Leydig cells produce testosterone in response to LH) → epididymis (sperm maturation/storage) → vas deferens → ejaculatory duct → urethra
- Prostate gland: produces ~30% of seminal fluid (zinc, PSA, citric acid); benign hyperplasia (BPH) very common with age (~50% of men by 50); prostate cancer = most common male cancer (usually slow-growing; PSA screening controversial — overdiagnosis concern)
- **HPG axis in males:** GnRH (pulsatile) → LH → testosterone from Leydig cells; FSH → Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis. Testosterone feeds back negatively on GnRH/LH. Inhibin B from Sertoli cells feeds back on FSH.
- Testosterone: muscle mass, bone density, erythropoiesis, libido, deepened voice, body hair. Declines ~1%/year after 30 (no abrupt "male menopause" but gradual andropause).

### Female
- Ovaries (oogenesis; estrogen/progesterone) → fallopian tubes (site of fertilization; cilia + peristalsis move egg) → uterus (endometrium: implantation site) → cervix → vagina
- **Menstrual cycle (~28 days):**
  - **Follicular phase (days 1-14):** GnRH → FSH → follicular development → rising estrogen from granulosa cells → endometrial proliferation. Dominant follicle selected by day 7.
  - **Ovulation (day 14):** Estrogen reaches threshold → positive feedback → LH surge → follicle ruptures → egg released. Fertile window: ~5 days before to 1 day after ovulation.
  - **Luteal phase (days 14-28):** Corpus luteum (from ruptured follicle) produces progesterone → endometrial maturation/secretory changes. If no implantation → corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone drops → endometrial shedding (menstruation). If pregnancy → hCG from embryo rescues corpus luteum → progesterone maintained.
- **Pregnancy:** hCG detectable ~10 days post-fertilization (basis of pregnancy tests). Placenta takes over hormone production by ~10 weeks. Gestation ~40 weeks. Key: progesterone maintains pregnancy; estrogen promotes uterine growth; relaxin loosens ligaments; hPL (human placental lactogen) modifies maternal metabolism to supply fetus.
- Menopause: ~51 years average; ovarian follicle depletion → ↓ estrogen/progesterone → ↑ FSH/LH (no negative feedback). Vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes), vaginal atrophy, bone loss (accelerated for 5-7 years post-menopause), ↑ CVD risk. HRT controversial: ↓ symptoms and osteoporosis but ↑ breast cancer and thromboembolism risk (timing hypothesis: benefit > risk if started within 10 years of menopause).

### Key Reproductive Pathologies
- Endometriosis (~10% of women): Endometrial tissue outside uterus; pain, infertility
- Ectopic pregnancy: Implantation outside uterus (usually fallopian tube); emergency
- Cervical cancer: HPV-caused; largely preventable with vaccine + screening (Pap smear)
- Infertility: Affects ~15% of couples; causes: ovulatory dysfunction, tubal, male factor, unexplained

## Integumentary System (Skin)

- **Largest organ:** ~1.7 m², ~4 kg
- **Layers:** Epidermis (keratinocytes proliferate at basal layer → migrate up over ~28 days → cornified layer sloughs off; melanocytes produce melanin → UV protection; Langerhans cells = immune surveillance) → Dermis (collagen + elastin for strength/flexibility, blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands) → Hypodermis (adipose tissue → insulation, cushioning, energy storage)
- **Functions:** Barrier (infection, dehydration, UV), temperature regulation (eccrine sweat glands → evaporative cooling; arteriolar vasodilation/constriction → radiant heat loss/conservation), sensation (Meissner's corpuscles = light touch, Pacinian = pressure/vibration, free nerve endings = pain/temperature), vitamin D synthesis (UVB converts 7-dehydrocholesterol → cholecalciferol in skin)
- **Wound healing phases:** (1) Hemostasis (minutes: platelet plug + fibrin clot), (2) Inflammation (hours-days: neutrophils then macrophages clean debris), (3) Proliferation (days-weeks: fibroblasts deposit collagen, angiogenesis, granulation tissue, epithelial cells migrate), (4) Remodeling (weeks-years: collagen reorganization, scar maturation; scar never reaches >80% of original strength)
- **Key pathologies:** Melanoma (most dangerous skin cancer; UV exposure; ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolution; BRAF mutation in ~50% → vemurafenib; immunotherapy revolutionized advanced treatment), basal cell carcinoma (most common skin cancer; locally invasive, rarely metastasizes), squamous cell carcinoma (can metastasize; sun-exposed areas), psoriasis (autoimmune; T-cell mediated → skin cell turnover accelerated from ~28 days to ~3-4 days; silver scaly plaques; treated with topical steroids, phototherapy, biologics anti-IL-17/IL-23), eczema/atopic dermatitis (barrier dysfunction + immune dysregulation; filaggrin gene mutations; itch-scratch cycle), burns (rule of nines for body surface area; first-degree = epidermis only; second-degree = partial dermis, blisters; third-degree = full thickness, painless due to nerve destruction)

## Hematologic System (Blood)

Often overlooked as a system — blood is a connective tissue and organ in itself.

### Components
- **Plasma (55%):** Water (90%), proteins (albumin = osmotic pressure, globulins = antibodies, fibrinogen = clotting), electrolytes, glucose, hormones, waste products
- **Red blood cells (44%):** ~5M/μL; no nucleus; lifespan ~120 days; hemoglobin carries O₂ (4 O₂ per molecule); produced in bone marrow (erythropoiesis stimulated by EPO from kidneys); recycled in spleen
- **White blood cells (<1%):** Neutrophils (60-70%, bacterial defense), lymphocytes (20-25%, T/B cells, NK cells), monocytes (5-10%, → macrophages), eosinophils (1-4%, parasites/allergies), basophils (<1%, histamine release)
- **Platelets:** Fragments of megakaryocytes; ~150K-400K/μL; lifespan ~10 days; critical for hemostasis

### Key Pathologies

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| Iron-deficiency anemia | ↓ Hemoglobin from iron lack | Most common anemia worldwide; microcytic, hypochromic; fatigue, pallor |
| B12/folate deficiency anemia | Impaired DNA synthesis → megaloblastic | Large RBCs; neurological symptoms with B12 deficiency |
| Sickle cell disease | HbS mutation (Glu→Val in β-globin) → RBC sickling under hypoxia | Vaso-occlusive crises, hemolysis, organ damage; HbS heterozygotes protected from malaria (balanced polymorphism); cured by CRISPR (Casgevy) |
| Hemophilia | X-linked; ↓ Factor VIII (A) or IX (B) | Bleeding into joints/muscles; treated with factor replacement or emicizumab |
| DVT/PE | Venous thromboembolism; Virchow's triad: stasis + hypercoagulability + endothelial injury | DVT in leg → PE in lung; can be fatal; treated with anticoagulants |
| Leukemia | Malignant WBC proliferation | Acute (ALL, AML) = aggressive; Chronic (CLL, CML) = indolent. CML revolutionized by imatinib (BCR-ABL inhibitor) |
| Lymphoma | Malignant lymphocyte proliferation in lymph nodes | Hodgkin (Reed-Sternberg cells, curable >85%) vs Non-Hodgkin (diverse group) |
