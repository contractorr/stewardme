# Chronic Disease & Pathology

## Cancer

### What Is Cancer?
Uncontrolled cell growth from accumulated genetic mutations. Not one disease — hundreds of diseases sharing common hallmarks.

### Hallmarks of Cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg)
1. **Sustaining proliferative signaling** — Growth signals always "on"
2. **Evading growth suppressors** — Ignore stop signals (p53, Rb mutations)
3. **Resisting cell death** — Avoid apoptosis
4. **Enabling replicative immortality** — Telomerase activation
5. **Inducing angiogenesis** — Grow new blood vessels for nutrients
6. **Activating invasion & metastasis** — Spread to distant organs
7. **Deregulating cellular energetics** — Warburg effect (aerobic glycolysis)
8. **Avoiding immune destruction** — Evade immune surveillance (PD-L1 expression)
9. **Tumor-promoting inflammation** — Chronic inflammation aids growth
10. **Genome instability & mutation** — Accelerated mutation rate

### Cancer Genetics (Key Concepts)
- **Oncogenes:** Proto-oncogenes mutated to be constitutively active → uncontrolled growth. Act as "accelerator stuck on." Examples: HER2 (breast), KRAS (pancreatic, colorectal, lung — "undruggable" until sotorasib/adagrasib 2021), BRAF V600E (melanoma, CRC), BCR-ABL (CML), MYC (many cancers).
- **Tumor suppressors:** Normally restrain growth; both copies must be lost (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis). Act as "broken brakes." Examples: TP53 ("guardian of the genome" — mutated in ~50% of all cancers), RB (retinoblastoma), APC (colorectal), BRCA1/2 (breast, ovarian — DNA repair).
- **Driver vs passenger mutations:** Most mutations in cancer are passengers (neutral); only 2-8 driver mutations needed for malignancy. Cancer evolves through clonal expansion — Darwinian selection within the tumor.
- **Tumor mutational burden (TMB):** Higher TMB → more neoantigens → better immunotherapy response. MSI-high (mismatch repair deficient) tumors have very high TMB → respond well to checkpoint inhibitors regardless of cancer type.
- **Hereditary cancer syndromes:** ~5-10% of cancers. BRCA1/2 (breast, ovarian, prostate, pancreatic), Lynch syndrome/HNPCC (colorectal, endometrial, ovarian — MSI-high), Li-Fraumeni (TP53 germline — multiple cancers, childhood onset), FAP (APC — 100% colorectal cancer by age 40 without surgery).

### Cancer Risk Factors

| Factor | Cancers | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Tobacco | Lung, bladder, head/neck, pancreas, kidney, stomach | Carcinogens (>70 in tobacco smoke) → DNA damage |
| Alcohol | Liver, breast, colorectal, oropharyngeal, esophageal | Acetaldehyde (carcinogen), hormonal effects, oxidative stress |
| Obesity | Breast (post-menopause), colorectal, endometrial, kidney, pancreatic, liver, esophageal + 7 more | Insulin/IGF-1 signaling, chronic inflammation, estrogen from adipose tissue |
| UV radiation | Melanoma, BCC, SCC | Pyrimidine dimers → DNA mutations |
| Infections | Cervical (HPV), liver (HBV/HCV), stomach (H. pylori), nasopharyngeal (EBV), Kaposi (HHV-8) | Chronic inflammation, direct oncogenic mechanisms; ~15-20% of cancers globally caused by infection |
| Radiation (ionizing) | Leukemia, thyroid, breast | DNA double-strand breaks |

### Cancer Statistics (Global, 2022)
- ~20M new cases/year; ~10M deaths/year
- **Most common:** Lung (2.5M), breast (2.3M), colorectal (1.9M), prostate (1.5M), stomach (970K)
- **Most deadly:** Lung (1.8M deaths), colorectal (900K), liver (760K), stomach (660K), breast (670K)
- ~30-50% of cancers are preventable (tobacco cessation, HPV/HBV vaccination, screening, obesity prevention, alcohol reduction)

### Staging (TNM System)
- **T:** Tumor size (T1-T4)
- **N:** Lymph node involvement (N0-N3)
- **M:** Metastasis (M0 = no; M1 = yes)
- Stages I-IV: Higher = more advanced; Stage IV = metastatic

### Treatment Modalities

| Modality | Mechanism | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| Surgery | Physical removal | Localized solid tumors; often curative if early |
| Radiation | DNA damage via ionizing radiation → cell death | Localized; curative or palliative; ~50% of cancer patients |
| Chemotherapy | Cytotoxic drugs kill rapidly dividing cells | Systemic; adjuvant or palliative; side effects from killing normal dividing cells |
| Targeted therapy | Block specific molecular targets (e.g., imatinib for BCR-ABL in CML) | When specific targets identified |
| Immunotherapy | Activate immune system against cancer (checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T) | Many cancers; revolutionary in melanoma, lung |
| Hormone therapy | Block hormones that fuel growth | Breast (tamoxifen), prostate (androgen deprivation) |

### 5-Year Survival Rates (US, approximate)

| Cancer | Stage I | Stage IV |
|---|---|---|
| Breast | ~99% | ~30% |
| Prostate | ~99% | ~32% |
| Colorectal | ~91% | ~15% |
| Lung | ~63% | ~8% |
| Pancreatic | ~44% | ~3% |

**Key takeaway:** Early detection is everything. Screening saves lives (mammography, colonoscopy, low-dose CT for lung in high-risk, Pap smear for cervical).

## Cardiovascular Disease (CVD)

### Atherosclerosis (Underlying Mechanism)
1. Endothelial injury (smoking, hypertension, diabetes, inflammation)
2. LDL cholesterol deposits in arterial wall
3. Macrophages ingest LDL → foam cells → fatty streak
4. Smooth muscle proliferation → fibrous cap over lipid core
5. Cap rupture → thrombosis → acute event (MI or stroke)

### Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)
- **STEMI:** Complete coronary occlusion; ST-elevation on ECG; emergency PCI (stent) or thrombolytics; "time is muscle" (door-to-balloon <90 min)
- **NSTEMI:** Partial occlusion; medical management initially; risk-stratified approach
- **Symptoms:** Chest pain/pressure, radiation to jaw/arm, diaphoresis, shortness of breath; women may present atypically (fatigue, nausea)
- **Complications:** Arrhythmia (VF = leading cause of death), heart failure, cardiogenic shock

### Stroke
- **Ischemic (87%):** Blood clot blocks cerebral artery; tPA within 4.5 hours or thrombectomy within 24h
- **Hemorrhagic (13%):** Blood vessel ruptures; intracerebral or subarachnoid; worse prognosis
- **"Time is brain":** ~1.9M neurons die per minute during stroke
- Risk factors same as MI: hypertension (#1), diabetes, smoking, AF, hyperlipidemia

### Heart Failure
- Not "stopped heart" — inadequate cardiac output for body's needs
- **HFrEF (reduced EF <40%):** Weakened pump; guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) — "4 pillars": (1) ACE-I/ARB/ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan = ARNI, superior to ACE-I alone; PARADIGM-HF trial), (2) beta-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol), (3) mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (spironolactone/eplerenone), (4) SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin). Together ↓ mortality ~60% vs no treatment. Also: diuretics for symptoms (loop diuretics), hydralazine/nitrate (particularly in Black patients — A-HeFT trial), ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator) if EF ≤35%, CRT (cardiac resynchronization therapy) if wide QRS.
- **HFpEF (preserved EF ≥50%):** Stiff ventricle; historically no proven therapies. Now: SGLT2 inhibitors (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER trials) = first class to show benefit. Also: diuretics for volume, treat comorbidities (HTN, obesity, AF). GLP-1 agonists showing promise.
- ~50% mortality within 5 years of diagnosis; HF is the "final common pathway" of most cardiac diseases

### CVD Risk Assessment & Prevention
- **10-year ASCVD risk calculator:** Uses age, sex, race, BP, cholesterol, diabetes, smoking → estimates 10-year risk of MI/stroke. <5% = low, 5-7.5% = borderline, 7.5-20% = intermediate, ≥20% = high. Guides statin/aspirin initiation.
- **Modifiable risk factors** (responsible for ~90% of MI risk globally — INTERHEART study): smoking, dyslipidemia, hypertension, diabetes, abdominal obesity, psychosocial stress, low fruit/vegetable intake, physical inactivity, excessive alcohol
- **Coronary artery calcium (CAC) score:** CT scan quantifies coronary calcification; refines risk prediction when intermediate; CAC 0 = very low risk (can defer statin even if calculator suggests); CAC >300 = high risk
- **Lp(a):** Genetically determined lipoprotein; independently ↑ CVD risk; elevated in ~20% of population; no approved treatment yet (antisense oligonucleotide pelacarsen in Phase III); can't be lowered by lifestyle. Measured once in lifetime is sufficient (genetically fixed).

## Diabetes Mellitus

### Types

| Feature | Type 1 | Type 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Autoimmune destruction of β-cells | Insulin resistance + relative deficiency |
| Onset | Usually childhood/young adult | Usually adult (increasingly younger) |
| Prevalence | ~5-10% of diabetes | ~90-95% of diabetes |
| Body habitus | Usually lean | Often overweight/obese |
| Treatment | Insulin (always required) | Lifestyle → metformin → other oral agents → insulin |
| Autoantibodies | Present (GAD, IA-2, etc.) | Absent |
| C-peptide | Low/absent | Normal/high initially |

### Global Scale
- ~537M adults with diabetes (2021); projected 783M by 2045
- ~6.7M deaths/year attributable to diabetes
- ~$966B in health expenditure

### Complications (from chronic hyperglycemia)

| Type | Manifestation |
|---|---|
| **Microvascular** | Retinopathy (leading cause of blindness), nephropathy (#1 cause of ESRD), neuropathy (pain, numbness, foot ulcers → amputation) |
| **Macrovascular** | Accelerated atherosclerosis → MI, stroke, peripheral arterial disease |
| **Metabolic emergency** | DKA (Type 1: high glucose, ketones, acidosis); HHS (Type 2: extreme hyperglycemia, dehydration) |

### Pathophysiology of T2D (Detailed)
1. **Insulin resistance:** Excess visceral fat → chronic low-grade inflammation (↑ TNF-α, IL-6 from adipocytes) → interferes with insulin receptor signaling (IRS-1 serine phosphorylation instead of tyrosine) → muscle/fat cells take up less glucose → blood glucose rises
2. **Compensatory hyperinsulinemia:** Pancreatic β-cells produce more insulin to overcome resistance → blood glucose stays normal temporarily (prediabetes may have insulin levels 2-5x normal)
3. **β-cell exhaustion:** Chronic overwork + glucotoxicity + lipotoxicity → β-cell apoptosis → insulin production declines → frank hyperglycemia → diagnosis of T2D
4. **Glucagon dysregulation:** α-cells normally suppress glucagon after meals (insulin-dependent); in T2D → inappropriate glucagon secretion → hepatic glucose overproduction even when blood glucose already high
5. **"Ominous octet" (DeFronzo):** 8 defects contributing to T2D: muscle (↓ glucose uptake), liver (↑ glucose production), β-cell (↓ insulin), α-cell (↑ glucagon), fat cell (↑ lipolysis), GI (↓ incretin effect), kidney (↑ glucose reabsorption), brain (appetite dysregulation). Modern drugs target different defects.

### Diabetes Management: Treatment Ladder
1. **Lifestyle + metformin** (first-line always)
2. **Add second agent based on comorbidities:** CVD/CKD → SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 RA (regardless of glucose level); otherwise → choice based on weight, cost, hypoglycemia risk
3. **Add third agent if still not at target** (HbA1c <7% for most; individualized)
4. **Basal insulin** if oral agents insufficient (often needed as β-cell function declines over years)
5. **Basal-bolus insulin** for advanced disease

### HbA1c Targets
- General: <7% (~154 mg/dL average glucose)
- Elderly/frail with comorbidities: <8% (avoid hypoglycemia risk)
- Newly diagnosed young: <6.5% (aggressive early)
- Each 1% ↓ HbA1c → ~37% ↓ microvascular complications (UKPDS trial)

### Metabolic Syndrome (Precursor to T2D and CVD)
3+ of: waist circumference >40" (M) / >35" (F), TG >150, HDL <40 (M) / <50 (F), BP >130/85, fasting glucose >100. Affects ~35% of US adults. Central pathology: insulin resistance + visceral adiposity. Not just a risk factor cluster — represents a distinct pathophysiological state driven by chronic caloric excess and physical inactivity.

## Autoimmune Diseases

Immune system attacks self-tissue. ~80 known autoimmune diseases; affect ~5-8% of population; 78% female.

| Disease | Target | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Rheumatoid arthritis | Joints (synovium) | Symmetric joint inflammation/destruction; morning stiffness |
| Lupus (SLE) | Multiple organs (skin, joints, kidneys, brain) | "Butterfly rash"; flares/remissions; nephritis |
| Type 1 diabetes | Pancreatic β-cells | Insulin-dependent; see above |
| Multiple sclerosis | CNS myelin | Demyelination → neurological deficits; relapsing-remitting or progressive |
| Hashimoto's thyroiditis | Thyroid | Most common cause of hypothyroidism |
| Graves' disease | TSH receptor (stimulatory) | Hyperthyroidism; exophthalmos |
| Celiac disease | Small intestine (gluten trigger) | Villous atrophy → malabsorption |
| Psoriasis | Skin | Thick, scaly plaques; T-cell mediated |
| IBD (Crohn's/UC) | GI tract | Chronic inflammation; see Ch. 02 |

**Why autoimmunity?** Genetic susceptibility (HLA associations) + environmental triggers (infections, gut microbiome, vitamin D, smoking). Female predominance possibly related to X-chromosome immune genes and hormonal factors.

| Disease | Pathology | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| Alzheimer's | Amyloid plaques, tau tangles | ~55M globally; #1 cause of dementia; no disease-modifying treatment proven effective |

## Mental Health

### Depression (Major Depressive Disorder)
- ~280M affected globally; leading cause of disability worldwide
- Monoamine hypothesis (↓ serotonin, NE, dopamine) — oversimplified but basis for drug treatment
- Treatment: SSRIs/SNRIs (first-line), psychotherapy (CBT effective), exercise, ECT (for severe/treatment-resistant)
- Newer: ketamine/esketamine (rapid-acting); psilocybin (research promising)

### Anxiety Disorders
- Most common mental health category: GAD, panic disorder, social anxiety, specific phobias
- ~301M affected globally
- Treatment: SSRIs, CBT, benzodiazepines (short-term only — dependence risk)

### Schizophrenia
- ~24M globally; onset typically late teens/early 20s
- Positive symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought
- Negative symptoms: flat affect, social withdrawal, anhedonia
- Treatment: antipsychotics (D2 blockers); clozapine for treatment-resistant (requires blood monitoring for agranulocytosis)
- Dopamine hypothesis: excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathway → positive symptoms; deficit in mesocortical → negative symptoms

### Substance Use Disorders

**Neuroscience of addiction:** All addictive substances hijack mesolimbic dopamine pathway (VTA → nucleus accumbens). Repeated exposure → neuroplastic changes: tolerance (↓ response → need more), sensitization (↑ craving response to cues), ↓ prefrontal cortex executive control → compulsive use despite consequences. Addiction is a chronic brain disorder, not a moral failing; relapse rates (~40-60%) comparable to diabetes/hypertension non-compliance.

| Substance | Deaths/Year | Mechanism of Addiction | Key Medical Consequences | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tobacco** | ~8M (1.3M secondhand) | Nicotine → nAChR activation → dopamine release; extremely rapid (10 seconds to brain via inhalation); ~32% of users become dependent | Lung/bladder/head-neck cancer, COPD, CVD, stroke, peripheral vascular disease | Varenicline (partial nAChR agonist; most effective), NRT (patch, gum, lozenge), bupropion; combination therapy best; relapse rate high (~95% without treatment, ~25% with optimal treatment at 1 year) |
| **Alcohol** | ~3M | GABA-A agonism (sedation) + dopamine release + endorphin release; withdrawal = GABA downregulation → excitotoxicity → seizures, delirium tremens (5% mortality untreated) | Liver disease (steatosis → hepatitis → cirrhosis), cardiomyopathy, pancreatitis, 7 cancer types, Wernicke-Korsakoff (thiamine deficiency → encephalopathy), fetal alcohol syndrome | Detox (benzodiazepine taper for withdrawal), naltrexone (↓ craving by blocking opioid-mediated reward), acamprosate (restores GABA/glutamate balance), disulfiram (aversion — inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase → severe nausea with alcohol), AA/12-step (evidence: modest), CBT |
| **Opioids** | ~500K globally (~80K US overdose) | μ-opioid receptor → massive dopamine release; physical dependence within days of continuous use; withdrawal intensely unpleasant but not fatal | Overdose death (respiratory depression), constipation, endocrinopathy, infections (IV use → HIV, HCV, endocarditis), neonatal abstinence syndrome | **MAT is gold standard:** methadone (full μ-agonist, daily supervised dosing, ↓ mortality 50%), buprenorphine (partial μ-agonist, office-based, ↓ mortality 50%), naltrexone (μ-antagonist, monthly injection); naloxone for overdose reversal; abstinence-only approaches have highest relapse/death rates |
| **Stimulants** | — | Cocaine: blocks DAT (dopamine transporter) → ↑ synaptic dopamine. Methamphetamine: reverses DAT → massive dopamine release + neurotoxicity. | Cardiovascular (MI, stroke, arrhythmia), psychosis, dental destruction (meth), nasal perforation (cocaine) | No FDA-approved medications; CBT + contingency management (voucher-based reinforcement); stimulant use disorder is treatment-resistant |
| **Cannabis** | — | THC: CB1 receptor agonist → dopamine modulation; ~9% of users develop dependence | Generally lower harm profile; but: adolescent use → ↑ psychosis risk (dose-dependent), cognitive impairment, amotivation, cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome, impaired driving | CBT; no approved pharmacotherapy |

**US Opioid Crisis Timeline:**
1. **Wave 1 (1990s-2010):** Prescription opioids; OxyContin (Purdue Pharma) marketed aggressively with false safety claims; "pain as 5th vital sign" campaign; prescriptions quadrupled 1999-2010; Purdue settled for $8.3B, dissolved.
2. **Wave 2 (2010-2013):** Heroin; as prescriptions restricted, users switched to cheaper heroin.
3. **Wave 3 (2013-present):** Synthetic opioids; illicit fentanyl (100x morphine, made in clandestine labs, contaminating heroin/counterfeit pills); now causes ~70% of US overdose deaths. Xylazine ("tranq") — veterinary sedative mixed with fentanyl; causes severe skin necrosis; not reversed by naloxone.

## Leading Causes of Death: The Pattern

**In wealthy countries:** Heart disease > Cancer > Stroke > COPD > Alzheimer's > Diabetes > Accidents
**In poor countries:** Lower respiratory infections > Diarrheal diseases > HIV/AIDS > Malaria > TB alongside NCDs

**Epidemiological transition:** As countries develop, infectious → chronic disease burden. But "double burden" in middle-income countries (both simultaneously).
