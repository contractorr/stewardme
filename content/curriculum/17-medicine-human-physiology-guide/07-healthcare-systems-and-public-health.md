# Healthcare Systems & Public Health

## Healthcare System Models

### Typology

| Model | Financing | Provision | Examples | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Beveridge** | Tax-funded (general revenue) | Government-owned facilities/employed doctors | UK (NHS), Spain, Italy, Nordics, Cuba | Universal coverage, low admin costs (~5%), cost control (global budgets), equity | Wait times (elective surgery), potential underfunding, limited patient choice, political vulnerability (budget cuts) |
| **Bismarck** | Employer/employee mandated insurance (non-profit "sickness funds"/statutory insurance) | Private providers | Germany, France, Japan, Switzerland, Belgium, Netherlands | Universal coverage, patient choice, high quality, competition among insurers | More expensive than Beveridge, complexity with multiple insurers, employer-tied (though with individual mandate backup) |
| **National Health Insurance** | Single-payer tax-funded | Private providers | Canada, Taiwan, South Korea, Australia (hybrid) | Low admin costs, universal, patient choice of provider, monopsony purchasing power → price control | Wait times for specialists/elective procedures, may underfund capital investment, no competition among payers |
| **Out-of-pocket** | Individual payment at point of service | Mix | Many low-income countries; India (~63% OOP), most of sub-Saharan Africa | Market signals | Catastrophic spending → medical bankruptcy; poor/rural excluded; ↓ preventive care; worst health outcomes |
| **US system** | All of the above simultaneously (employer, government, individual, OOP) | Mix of private/public | Unique hybrid | Innovation, specialist access (for insured), no wait times (for insured) | Most expensive; 27M uninsured; worst outcomes among wealthy nations; medical bankruptcy (~530K/year); profound inequity |

### Detailed Country Comparisons

**UK (NHS — Beveridge):**
- Founded 1948; free at point of service; funded through general taxation (~£180B/year). GP gatekeeping model (must see GP before specialist). Long wait times for elective procedures (hip replacement: ~40 weeks). Strong primary care. NICE evaluates cost-effectiveness (£20-30K/QALY threshold — will reject expensive treatments). Chronic underfunding → crisis in 2020s (ambulance delays, ED crowding, workforce shortages). Private supplementary insurance available (~10% of population).

**Germany (Bismarck):**
- Statutory health insurance (Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung): ~87% of population; ~100 non-profit sickness funds; contributions ~14.6% of income (split employer/employee). Private insurance for high earners (~11%). No GP gatekeeping (direct specialist access). Highest hospital bed density in Europe (8 per 1K vs 2.5 in US). High patient satisfaction. Challenges: coordination between ambulatory and inpatient care; cost growth; physician overwork.

**Canada (National Health Insurance):**
- Single-payer provincial systems (13 provinces/territories); covered: physician and hospital services; NOT covered: dental, vision, outpatient drugs (varies by province), physiotherapy → ~30% of health spend is private. Wait times: median ~25 weeks for specialist referral to treatment. Strong primary care; physician choice. Drug costs not negotiated nationally (no pharmacare — being implemented slowly). US comparison: Canadians spend ~60% less per capita with 4 years longer life expectancy.

**Japan:**
- Universal insurance since 1961; mix of employer-based and community-based insurance. All citizens covered. Fees set nationally (uniform fee schedule → tight cost control). Very high utilization (most physician visits/capita in OECD: ~13/year). Longest life expectancy globally. Low drug costs (national pricing). Challenges: aging population (29% over 65 — highest in world), physician shortage in rural areas, mental health stigma.

**Taiwan (NHI — often cited as model):**
- Implemented 1995 after studying 13 countries. Single-payer NHI; covers 99.9% of population. Smart card system (all medical records on one card). Admin costs: ~2% (vs ~34% in US). Patient cost-sharing low but present. Monthly premiums ~$25-50 based on income. Out-of-pocket: ~12% of health spend. Life expectancy: ~81 years. Spend: ~6.5% of GDP. Challenge: physician burnout from high volume, low reimbursement per visit.

**Singapore (unique model):**
- Three-tier system: Medisave (mandatory health savings account, 8-10.5% of income), MediShield Life (catastrophic coverage), Medifund (safety net for poor). Emphasizes personal responsibility + government subsidies. Public hospitals compete with each other. Health spend: ~4.5% of GDP (lowest among wealthy nations). Life expectancy: ~84. Extremely efficient. Criticism: may underserve chronic conditions; high out-of-pocket for those who deplete Medisave.

## The US Healthcare System

### Structure
- **Private insurance:** Employer-sponsored (~155M people; employers get tax deduction; employees get tax-free benefit — $300B/year tax subsidy); individual market (~16M); ACA exchanges
- **Medicare:** Federal; 65+ and disabled (~65M); Part A (hospital — funded by payroll tax, no premium), Part B (outpatient — ~$175/month premium, general revenue funded), Part C (Medicare Advantage — private plans paid by Medicare, ~50% of beneficiaries; cherry-picking/upcoding concerns), Part D (drugs — private plans; "donut hole" coverage gap being closed). Total spend: ~$1T/year. Trust fund projected insolvency ~2031.
- **Medicaid:** Federal-state; low-income (~85M — largest insurer by enrollment); varies dramatically by state (income eligibility ranges from ~17% FPL to 138% FPL depending on expansion). Underfunded: reimbursement ~60% of Medicare rates → many providers refuse Medicaid patients → access gap. Total spend: ~$800B/year. Dual-eligibles (Medicare + Medicaid): ~12M people, ~35% of Medicaid spending.
- **VA:** Veterans (~9M enrolled); single-payer government-run system (closest to Beveridge in US). Generally good outcomes for enrolled population. Challenges: access in rural areas, wait time scandals (2014).
- **Uninsured:** ~27M (8%); ~45% eligible for Medicaid/marketplace subsidies but not enrolled. Uninsured receive care in ERs (EMTALA mandate — unfunded) → hospitals absorb costs → higher prices for insured → cost shift. Uninsured have ~40% higher mortality for many conditions.
- **CHIP:** Children in low-income families (~7M); near-universal child coverage when combined with Medicaid.
- **Indian Health Service (IHS):** ~2.6M enrolled; chronically underfunded (per capita spend ~40% of national average); worst health outcomes of any US population.

### How Insurance Actually Works in the US
- **Premium:** Monthly payment to insurer (~$7,900/year individual, ~$22,400/year family for employer plans, 2023).
- **Deductible:** Amount paid before insurance kicks in (average: ~$1,700 individual). High-deductible health plans (HDHPs): $1,600-$8,050 deductible; paired with HSAs (tax-advantaged health savings accounts).
- **Copay/coinsurance:** Patient share after deductible (e.g., $30 copay per visit, or 20% coinsurance).
- **Out-of-pocket maximum:** Cap on annual patient spending (~$9,100 individual, 2024). After this, insurance pays 100%.
- **Prior authorization:** Insurer must approve certain treatments/drugs before coverage. ~35% of physicians report patient harm from PA delays. ~34 hours/week spent by physician practices on PA.
- **In-network vs out-of-network:** Insurers negotiate rates with providers; out-of-network = patient may owe full charges (No Surprises Act 2022 protects emergency + some non-emergency).
- **Medical debt:** ~100M Americans have medical debt; #1 cause of personal bankruptcy; ~$220B total medical debt. Unique to US among wealthy nations.

### ACA (Affordable Care Act, 2010)
- Individual mandate (later zeroed out in 2017 tax bill — but exchanges still function), exchanges/marketplaces with income-based subsidies (400% FPL → enhanced to no income cap via IRA)
- Medicaid expansion (states could opt in per SCOTUS 2012; ~40 states expanded; 10 non-expansion states → coverage gap: too rich for pre-expansion Medicaid, too poor for marketplace subsidies)
- Pre-existing condition protections (community rating; guaranteed issue)
- Children on parents' plan until 26
- Essential health benefits: 10 required categories (hospitalization, Rx drugs, maternity, mental health, preventive, etc.)
- Reduced uninsured from ~16% (48M) to ~8% (27M)
- Politically contested: 70+ repeal attempts; near-repeal 2017 (McCain vote saved it); SCOTUS upheld twice (2012: individual mandate = tax; 2021: no standing)

### Why So Expensive? (Deep Dive)

| Factor | Explanation | Magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| **Administrative costs** | ~34% of health spend; billing complexity (thousands of different fee schedules); prior authorization; credentialing; denied claims + appeals; each hospital averages ~770 billing employees. Canada: ~12%. | ~$1.2T/year in admin (~$2,500/person more than Canada) |
| **Drug prices** | No federal price negotiation until IRA 2022 (now limited to 10 Medicare drugs, expanding slowly). Prices 2-3x other OECD countries. Insulin: US ~$100/vial vs $8 globally. Humira: $77K/year (US) vs $15K (UK). PBMs (pharmacy benefit managers): opaque middlemen; spread pricing; rebate retention. | ~$405B Rx spending (2022); ~15% of health spend |
| **Provider consolidation** | Hospital mergers → monopoly pricing (prices 12-40% higher in concentrated markets; evidence from Dafny, Gaynor). Physician practice acquisition by PE firms → price increases. 70% of US metro areas are "highly concentrated" hospital markets. | Mergers add ~$12B-$25B/year in excess costs |
| **Fee-for-service** | Pays for volume not value; incentivizes procedures over prevention; specialist-heavy (US does 2-3x more MRIs, knee replacements, cardiac procedures vs peers). Move toward value-based care slow (~40% of payments have some value component but most still fundamentally FFS). | Drives utilization of high-cost procedures |
| **Defensive medicine** | Malpractice liability → CYA testing; estimates: 2-10% of total health spend. Tort reform (Texas, 2003): modest effect on costs, did not dramatically reduce unnecessary care. | $50-200B/year (debated) |
| **Prices, not utilization** | Key insight (Anderson et al., 2003: "It's the Prices, Stupid"): US doesn't use more healthcare than peers — prices per unit are dramatically higher. MRI: US $1,100 vs $130 (Netherlands). Hip replacement: $40K (US) vs $12K (Netherlands). Appendectomy: $15K (US) vs $4K (Switzerland). | Prices explain majority of spending gap |
| **Chronic disease burden** | Obesity: 42% (vs 25-30% in most peers); diabetes: 11% (vs 6-8%); → higher comorbidity burden → more intensive treatment needed. But US spends more even after adjusting for disease burden. | Contributes ~20% of excess spending |
| **End-of-life care** | ~25% of Medicare spend in last year of life; ~6 months = ~13% of total spend. ICU use at end of life far higher in US than peers. Advance directive/palliative care underutilized. | ~$200B/year |
| **Workforce costs** | Specialist physician average: $350-600K/year (vs $150-250K in Europe). Training debt (~$200K average) partially explains salary expectations. But physician compensation is ~8% of total health spend (not the main driver). | ~$330B/year physician compensation |

### Key Policy Debates (Expanded)
- **Single-payer ("Medicare for All"):** Eliminate private insurance; single government payer; all residents covered. Estimated savings: $450B/year in admin + price negotiation. Estimated cost: ~$3T/year (vs ~$4.3T current total health spend). Opposition: insurance industry (3M jobs), providers (lower reimbursement), political (socialism label). Polling: popular in concept, less when told "eliminates private insurance."
- **Public option:** Government insurance plan competing with private plans on exchanges. Less disruptive; could negotiate lower prices; may lead to adverse selection against private plans. Biden proposed but didn't pass.
- **Drug pricing reform:** IRA 2022 = first step (Medicare negotiation for 10 drugs, capped insulin at $35 for Medicare). Bipartisan support for expansion. International reference pricing (proposed: pay median of other wealthy nations) would cut drug costs ~40%.
- **Value-based care:** Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs), bundled payments, capitation. Success mixed; ACOs saved modest amounts. Fundamental incentive misalignment hard to overcome in for-profit system.
- **Surprise billing:** No Surprises Act (2022): patients held harmless for out-of-network emergency care + certain non-emergency scenarios. Arbitration between insurer and provider. Working but implementation contentious.
- **Mental health parity:** MHPAEA (2008) + ACA require equal coverage. Reality: reimbursement still lower for mental health providers; workforce shortage (~50% of US counties have zero psychiatrists); prior auth more burdensome for mental health.
- **Rural healthcare:** 136+ rural hospitals closed since 2010; 700+ at risk. Provider shortages in primary care, OB/GYN, psychiatry. Telehealth helping but insufficient. CMS Rural Emergency Hospital designation (2023) allows hospitals to close inpatient but keep ER.
- **Prior authorization reform:** Bipartisan momentum; CMS rule (2024) requiring faster decisions, electronic PA, reason for denial. States passing PA reform bills.
- **Vertical integration:** Payer-PBM-provider consolidation (UnitedHealth/Optum, CVS/Aetna/Caremark) → concerns about conflicts of interest, market power, steering. DOJ increasing scrutiny.

### Global Health Disparities

| Metric | High-Income | Low-Income | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Life expectancy | ~80 years | ~63 years | 17 years |
| Under-5 mortality (per 1K live births) | ~5 | ~67 | 13x |
| Maternal mortality (per 100K births) | ~12 | ~462 | 39x |
| Physician density (per 10K) | ~35 | ~2.5 | 14x |
| Health spend per capita | ~$5,000+ | ~$41 | 120x |
| Access to essential surgery | ~95% | ~7% | — |

**5 billion people** lack access to safe, timely, affordable surgical care. ~18M deaths/year from conditions treatable by surgery. Lancet Commission on Global Surgery (2015): surgery is "the neglected stepchild of global health."

**Neglected tropical diseases (NTDs):** Affect ~1.7B people; diseases of poverty (river blindness, lymphatic filariasis, schistosomiasis, soil-transmitted helminths, trachoma, Chagas, leishmaniasis). Often treatable/preventable for <$0.50/person/year. WHO NTD roadmap 2021-2030 targeting elimination.

**Global health transitions:**
- **Epidemiological transition:** Infectious → chronic disease as primary burden with development (but "double burden" in LMICs — both simultaneously)
- **Nutrition transition:** Undernutrition → overweight/obesity as countries urbanize + adopt Western diets
- **Demographic transition:** High birth/death rates → low birth/death rates → aging population → ↑ chronic disease burden + ↓ working-age support ratio

### DALYs and QALYs

**DALY (Disability-Adjusted Life Year):**
- 1 DALY = 1 year of healthy life lost
- DALY = YLL (years of life lost to premature death) + YLD (years lived with disability)
- Used by WHO/World Bank to compare disease burden globally

**QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year):**
- 1 QALY = 1 year of perfect health
- Used in cost-effectiveness analysis; interventions compared by cost per QALY gained
- UK NICE threshold: ~£20,000-30,000/QALY; US has no official threshold (~$50,000-150,000 commonly used)

## Public Health

### The Great Public Health Achievements

| Achievement | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Clean water and sanitation** | Reduced cholera, typhoid, dysentery; arguably saved more lives than any medical intervention |
| **Vaccination** | Eradicated smallpox; near-eradication of polio; prevents ~4-5M deaths/year |
| **Tobacco control** | Taxes, bans, campaigns; US smoking declined 42% (1965) → 11% (2024) |
| **Motor vehicle safety** | Seatbelts, airbags, drunk driving laws; death rate per mile down 90% since 1960s |
| **Food safety** | Pasteurization, refrigeration, inspection; foodborne illness dramatically reduced |
| **Maternal/child health** | Prenatal care, skilled birth attendance, neonatal care; maternal mortality ↓ 99% in wealthy countries since 1900 |
| **Fluoridation** | Reduced dental caries 25-40% |

### Epidemiology as Practice
- **Surveillance:** Tracking disease occurrence (CDC, WHO)
- **Investigation:** Outbreak detection and response (contact tracing, source identification)
- **Screening:** Early detection before symptoms (mammography, colonoscopy, HIV testing)
- **Prevention:** Primary (prevent occurrence: vaccines, sanitation), Secondary (early detection: screening), Tertiary (minimize disability: rehabilitation)

### Social Determinants of Health
- **Income/poverty:** Strongest predictor of health status; 10-15 year life expectancy gap between richest and poorest quintile in US
- **Education:** More education → better health (health literacy, higher income, healthier behaviors)
- **Race/ethnicity:** Structural racism → disparities in access, treatment, outcomes; Black-white life expectancy gap ~5 years in US
- **Geography:** Rural vs urban; food deserts; environmental exposures; healthcare access
- **Housing:** Homelessness → ~12 years lower life expectancy; lead paint; mold
- **Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs):** Dose-response relationship with adult disease (CVD, depression, substance use)

## Healthcare Economics

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Moral hazard** | Insurance reduces incentive to avoid costs (insured patients use more care) |
| **Adverse selection** | Sicker people more likely to buy insurance → risk pool deteriorates → premiums rise |
| **Information asymmetry** | Doctors know more than patients → patients can't evaluate treatment necessity |
| **Supplier-induced demand** | Physicians can create demand for their own services |
| **Cost-effectiveness analysis** | Comparing interventions by cost per QALY or outcome |
| **Opportunity cost** | Resources spent on one intervention can't be spent on another |

### Cost Drivers
1. **Aging population** — Older people use 3-5x more healthcare
2. **Technology** — New treatments/devices; often additive not substitutive
3. **Chronic disease** — 86% of US health spend is on chronic conditions
4. **Administrative complexity** — US uniquely burdened
5. **Drug prices** — Biologics particularly expensive ($100K+/year common)
6. **Workforce costs** — Physician salaries 2-4x higher in US vs Europe

### Healthcare Spending Curve
- Healthcare as % of GDP rises with income (Wagner's Law applied)
- US is extreme outlier above the curve
- Diminishing returns: spending above ~$5,000/capita adds little to life expectancy
