# Introduction to Medicine & Human Physiology

## Overview

Medicine: applied science of diagnosing, treating, and preventing disease. This guide covers how the body works, how it breaks, how we fix it, and how healthcare systems organize around these tasks. Complements existing cognitive-neuroscience-guide (brain) and genetics-biotech-guide (DNA/genomics).

## Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM)

### Hierarchy of Evidence (strongest → weakest)
1. **Systematic reviews / meta-analyses** (Cochrane reviews)
2. **Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)** — gold standard for interventions
3. **Cohort studies** (prospective > retrospective)
4. **Case-control studies**
5. **Case series / case reports**
6. **Expert opinion / mechanism-based reasoning**

### Key Concepts
- **NNT (Number Needed to Treat):** Patients treated for one to benefit. Statins for primary prevention: NNT ~40-100 over 5 years. Lower = better drug.
- **NNH (Number Needed to Harm):** Patients treated for one to experience adverse effect.
- **Absolute vs relative risk reduction:** "50% risk reduction" could mean 2%→1% (absolute reduction = 1%, NNT = 100) or 20%→10% (absolute = 10%, NNT = 10). Always ask for absolute numbers. Drug ads/media almost always use relative risk (sounds more impressive).
- **p-value:** Probability of observing result if null hypothesis true. p < 0.05 is convention, not magic threshold. Does NOT measure effect size or clinical importance. p-hacking: running multiple analyses until p < 0.05 found → replication crisis.
- **Confidence interval:** Range within which true effect likely lies. Narrow = precise; if crosses zero/null = not statistically significant.
- **Sensitivity vs specificity:** Sensitivity = true positive rate (catches sick people; "SnNOut" — sensitive test, negative result rules out). Specificity = true positive rate for healthy (correctly identifies well people; "SpPIn" — specific test, positive result rules in). No test is perfect; trade-off between the two. Example: rapid COVID test: sensitivity ~70% (misses 30% of positives), specificity ~99% (few false positives).
- **Intention-to-treat (ITT) vs per-protocol:** ITT analyzes all randomized patients regardless of compliance → preserves randomization, real-world applicability. Per-protocol analyzes only those who completed treatment → shows efficacy under ideal conditions. ITT is standard for regulatory approval.
- **Replication crisis:** ~50-70% of published findings in psychology/preclinical biomedical research failed to replicate (Open Science Collaboration, 2015; Amgen study replicated only 6/53 landmark cancer papers). Causes: small samples, publication bias (positive results published preferentially), p-hacking, conflicts of interest. Pre-registration of trials helps.
- **Cochrane Collaboration:** Gold standard for systematic reviews; independent, no industry funding; Cochrane reviews are most trusted evidence summaries globally.

## Global Disease Burden

### Leading Causes of Death Globally (2019, pre-COVID)

| Rank | Cause | Deaths/year | % of total |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ischemic heart disease | 8.9 million | 16% |
| 2 | Stroke | 6.2 million | 11% |
| 3 | COPD | 3.2 million | 6% |
| 4 | Lower respiratory infections | 2.6 million | 5% |
| 5 | Neonatal conditions | 2.0 million | 4% |
| 6 | Trachea/bronchus/lung cancer | 1.8 million | 3% |
| 7 | Alzheimer's/dementia | 1.6 million | 3% |
| 8 | Diarrheal diseases | 1.5 million | 3% |
| 9 | Diabetes mellitus | 1.5 million | 3% |
| 10 | Kidney disease | 1.3 million | 2% |

**Key pattern:** Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) dominate globally (71% of deaths). In low-income countries, infectious diseases still prominent. As countries develop, disease burden shifts from infectious → chronic (epidemiological transition).

### DALYs (Disability-Adjusted Life Years)
- Combines years of life lost (premature death) + years lived with disability
- Better measure than mortality alone — captures burden of non-fatal conditions
- Top DALY causes: neonatal disorders, ischemic heart disease, stroke, lower respiratory infections, diarrheal diseases, road injuries, diabetes, depression

## Vital Signs and Normal Values

| Parameter | Normal Range | Critical Values |
|---|---|---|
| Heart rate | 60–100 bpm | <40 or >150 |
| Blood pressure | <120/80 mmHg | >180/120 (hypertensive crisis) |
| Respiratory rate | 12–20 breaths/min | <8 or >30 |
| Temperature | 36.1–37.2°C (97–99°F) | >40°C (104°F) or <35°C (95°F) |
| Oxygen saturation (SpO2) | 95–100% | <90% |
| Blood glucose (fasting) | 70–100 mg/dL | <54 or >400 mg/dL |

## The Body in Numbers

- ~37 trillion human cells
- ~38 trillion bacteria (slightly more than human cells)
- 206 bones (adult)
- ~600 skeletal muscles
- ~100,000 km of blood vessels
- Heart beats ~100,000 times/day, ~2.5 billion in lifetime
- Kidneys filter ~180 liters of blood/day
- Liver performs ~500 metabolic functions
- Brain: ~86 billion neurons, uses ~20% of body's energy (2% of mass)
- DNA in each cell: ~2 meters if uncoiled; total in body would stretch to Pluto and back
