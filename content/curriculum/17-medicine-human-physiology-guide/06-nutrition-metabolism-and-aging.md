# Nutrition, Metabolism & Aging

## Macronutrients

| Nutrient | Energy | Functions | Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbohydrates** | 4 kcal/g | Primary energy source; brain requires ~120g glucose/day | Grains, fruits, vegetables, sugar |
| **Protein** | 4 kcal/g | Structural (muscle, enzymes, antibodies); 20 amino acids, 9 essential | Meat, fish, eggs, legumes, dairy |
| **Fat** | 9 kcal/g | Energy storage, cell membranes, hormone synthesis, insulation, vitamin absorption | Oils, nuts, dairy, meat, fish |
| **Alcohol** | 7 kcal/g | No nutritional value; metabolized by liver | — |

### Fat Types
- **Saturated:** Solid at room temp; ↑ LDL. Sources: butter, red meat, coconut oil
- **Monounsaturated:** ↓ LDL, ↑ HDL. Sources: olive oil, avocado, nuts
- **Polyunsaturated:** Omega-3 (anti-inflammatory: fish, flaxseed) and Omega-6 (pro-inflammatory in excess: vegetable oils)
- **Trans fats:** Artificially hydrogenated; worst for CVD; largely banned

### Protein
- RDA: 0.8 g/kg/day (adult); higher for athletes (1.2-2.0 g/kg), elderly (1.0-1.2 g/kg)
- Complete proteins (all essential AAs): animal sources, soy, quinoa
- Plant proteins often incomplete individually; combined easily (rice + beans)

## Metabolic Pathways (Essentials)

### Energy Production

**Glycolysis** (cytoplasm): Glucose → 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH. Anaerobic possible.

**Krebs/Citric Acid Cycle** (mitochondria): Acetyl-CoA → CO₂ + NADH + FADH₂. Requires O₂ indirectly.

**Oxidative Phosphorylation** (mitochondrial membrane): NADH/FADH₂ → electron transport chain → ~34 ATP + H₂O. Requires O₂.

**Total:** 1 glucose → ~36-38 ATP (aerobic) vs 2 ATP (anaerobic)

### Fat Metabolism
- **Lipolysis:** Triglycerides → glycerol + fatty acids
- **Beta-oxidation:** Fatty acids → Acetyl-CoA → Krebs cycle; very energy-dense (1 palmitate → ~106 ATP)
- **Ketogenesis:** When glucose scarce (fasting, low-carb, DKA), liver converts fatty acids → ketone bodies (acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone) → alternative fuel for brain/muscles

### Blood Sugar Regulation
- **Fed state:** Insulin (from β-cells) → glucose uptake into muscle/fat, glycogen synthesis in liver
- **Fasting state:** Glucagon (from α-cells) → glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose), gluconeogenesis (amino acids/glycerol → glucose)
- **Prolonged fasting:** Ketogenesis + gluconeogenesis; protein sparing over time

## Nutrition Science: Established vs Contested

### Well-Established
- Caloric excess → weight gain; deficit → weight loss (thermodynamics)
- Trans fats are harmful; should be eliminated
- Excessive sodium → hypertension in susceptible individuals
- Fiber is beneficial (gut health, satiety, blood sugar regulation)
- Fruits and vegetables reduce all-cause mortality
- Mediterranean diet reduces CVD events (PREDIMED trial)
- Processed meat is probably carcinogenic (IARC Group 1 for colorectal cancer)
- Alcohol: no safe level for cancer risk; mild cardiovascular benefit at low intake debated and likely confounded

### Contested/Nuanced

**Saturated fat:**
- Old model (Ancel Keys, 1960s): saturated fat → ↑ LDL → heart disease. Led to low-fat dietary guidelines.
- Current evidence: More complex. Replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat → ↓ CVD (strong evidence). Replacing with refined carbs → no benefit or worse. Coconut oil, dairy fat, red meat fat may have different effects. LDL particle size matters (small dense = more atherogenic than large buoyant).
- Meta-analyses (2010s-2020s): No clear association between saturated fat alone and CVD when not accounting for replacement nutrients — frequently misreported as "saturated fat is fine" (oversimplification).

**Dietary cholesterol:**
- Body produces ~80% of cholesterol endogenously; dietary cholesterol has modest effect on blood levels for most (~75% of population are hypo-responders).
- 2015-2020 US Dietary Guidelines removed 300mg/day cholesterol limit. Eggs (~186mg each) largely rehabilitated; 1-3 eggs/day appears safe for most.

**Red/processed meat:**
- IARC: Processed meat = Group 1 carcinogen (colorectal cancer); red meat = Group 2A (probable). Each 50g/day processed meat → ~18% ↑ colorectal cancer risk (relative).
- Probable mechanisms: Heme iron → oxidative stress, N-nitroso compounds, heterocyclic amines from high-temperature cooking, TMAO from L-carnitine via gut bacteria.
- NutriRECS (2019) controversially found effect sizes small; recommended no consumption change. Widely criticized.

**Artificial sweeteners:**
- FDA-approved ones generally safe at approved doses.
- WHO 2023: Advised against long-term use for weight control. Some evidence of gut microbiome disruption (Suez et al., 2014: saccharin). Erythritol linked to thrombotic events in one 2023 observational study — needs replication.

**Supplements:**
- US supplement industry: ~$60B/year; largely unregulated (DSHEA 1994 — no pre-market approval).
- **Proven beneficial:** Vitamin D (widespread deficiency; 1000-2000 IU/day reasonable), B12 (vegans; elderly with ↓ absorption), folate (pregnancy: 400-800mcg/day prevents neural tube defects), iron (if deficient — don't supplement without documented deficiency).
- **Probably not beneficial:** Multivitamins (no mortality benefit in meta-analyses), vitamin C mega-doses (no cold prevention), vitamin E (↑ mortality at high doses), fish oil at OTC doses (mixed evidence; prescription icosapent ethyl ↓ CV events in high-TG patients).
- **Actively harmful at high doses:** Vitamin A (teratogenic, liver damage), beta-carotene (↑ lung cancer in smokers — CARET/ATBC trials), iron (hemochromatosis if excess).

**Intermittent fasting:**
- Variants: 16:8 (time-restricted eating), 5:2, alternate-day fasting.
- Weight loss: Comparable to continuous caloric restriction in RCTs; no metabolic "magic." Most benefit from eating less total.
- Claimed mechanisms: Autophagy upregulation, insulin sensitivity improvement, circadian alignment, ketone production. Evidence primarily animal models.
- Concerns: Muscle loss if protein insufficient; disordered eating risk; large 2024 observational study showed possible ↑ cardiovascular mortality — not definitive but prompted caution.

**Gut microbiome manipulation:**
- FMT: proven for recurrent C. diff (~90% cure). Everything else early stage.
- Probiotics: Modest benefit for antibiotic-associated diarrhea, infant colic. Most commercial claims unsubstantiated. May delay post-antibiotic microbiome recovery (Suez et al., 2018).
- Prebiotics (dietary fiber → feeds beneficial bacteria → ↑ SCFA): More promising long-term than probiotics.
- Personalized nutrition by microbiome: Glycemic response to same foods varies by individual (Zeevi et al., 2015). Commercialized but long-term outcomes data insufficient.

**Ketogenic diet:**
- Very low carb (<20-50g/day), high fat → ketogenesis. Originally for pediatric epilepsy (still effective).
- Weight loss: Effective short-term; not clearly superior long-term. May suppress appetite via hypothalamic ketone effects.
- Concerns: ↑ LDL in some, kidney stones, nutrient deficiencies, difficult adherence, potential ↑ mortality in observational data (confounders limit interpretation).

**Alcohol:**
- Old claim: J-curve (moderate drinkers healthier than abstainers). Now largely debunked — "sick quitter" bias (abstainers include former heavy drinkers with health problems). Mendelian randomization studies: no cardiovascular benefit at any level.
- Cancer risk: No safe level. Alcohol → acetaldehyde (carcinogen). ↑ Risk of breast, liver, colorectal, oropharyngeal, esophageal, stomach cancers.
- Overall: Global Burden of Disease study (2018): optimal alcohol intake for health = zero. WHO (2023): no safe level.

## Obesity

### Scale
- ~650M adults obese (BMI ≥30) globally; ~1.9B overweight (BMI ≥25)
- US: ~42% obese (2024); tripled since 1960
- Childhood obesity tripled since 1975

### Mechanisms
- **Energy imbalance** (calories in > out) — necessary but insufficient explanation
- **Hormonal:** Leptin resistance (satiety signal ignored), ghrelin (hunger hormone), insulin
- **Environmental:** Food environment (calorie-dense, hyper-palatable, cheap processed food), sedentary lifestyle, sleep deprivation, stress
- **Genetic:** ~40-70% heritability for BMI; >1,000 genetic loci identified; but environment is required trigger
- **Set point theory:** Body defends a weight range; metabolic adaptation to weight loss (↓ BMR, ↑ hunger)

### Comorbidities
T2D, CVD, stroke, sleep apnea, osteoarthritis, NAFLD/NASH, depression, 13 types of cancer (breast, colorectal, endometrial, kidney, etc.)

### Treatment
- Lifestyle (diet + exercise): Modest long-term success (~3-5% weight loss maintained)
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** (semaglutide/Wegovy, tirzepatide/Mounjaro): ~15-22% weight loss; game-changer; also CV benefits
- Bariatric surgery: Most effective long-term (~25-35% weight loss maintained); gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy; also resolves T2D in many

## Aging

### Hallmarks of Aging (López-Otín et al., 2013; updated 2023)

Organized into three categories: **primary** (causes of damage), **antagonistic** (responses that become harmful), **integrative** (functional decline).

**Primary Hallmarks (Causes of Damage):**
1. **Genomic instability** — DNA damage accumulates from replication errors, oxidative stress, radiation, chemicals. Repair mechanisms (base excision, nucleotide excision, homologous recombination, non-homologous end joining) become less efficient with age. Progeroid syndromes (Werner, Hutchinson-Gilford progeria) = accelerated aging from DNA repair defects, proving genomic instability drives aging.
2. **Telomere attrition** — Telomeres (TTAGGG repeats) protect chromosome ends; shorten ~50-200bp per cell division (end-replication problem). Critically short telomeres → replicative senescence or apoptosis. Telomerase (active in stem cells, germ cells, cancer cells) maintains telomeres. Shorter telomeres correlate with age-related diseases, but causation complex. Telomere length: partly heritable, shortened by stress, smoking, obesity; lengthened by exercise.
3. **Epigenetic alterations** — DNA methylation patterns change (global hypomethylation + focal hypermethylation at CpG islands); histone modifications shift; chromatin remodeling disrupted. **Epigenetic clocks** (Horvath, GrimAge, DunedinPACE): DNA methylation patterns predict biological age more accurately than chronological age. Reprogramming experiments (Yamanaka factors) can reverse epigenetic age in vitro — key question: can partial reprogramming rejuvenate without causing cancer?
4. **Loss of proteostasis** — Protein homeostasis requires: chaperones (HSP70, HSP90 assist folding), ubiquitin-proteasome system (degrades misfolded proteins), autophagy-lysosomal pathway (degrades larger aggregates/organelles). All decline with age → accumulation of protein aggregates → Alzheimer's (amyloid, tau), Parkinson's (α-synuclein), cataracts (crystallin aggregation).
5. **Disabled macroautophagy** — Autophagy = cellular self-eating; clears damaged organelles, protein aggregates, intracellular pathogens. Declines with age. All longevity interventions (CR, rapamycin, exercise) upregulate autophagy. Constitutively active autophagy extends lifespan in mice.

**Antagonistic Hallmarks (Beneficial at Low Levels, Harmful When Chronic):**
6. **Deregulated nutrient sensing** — Four key pathways, all interconnected:
   - **mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin):** Nutrient sensor; when active → cell growth, protein synthesis, suppresses autophagy. Chronically active mTOR → accelerated aging. Rapamycin inhibits mTOR → extends lifespan in every organism tested.
   - **AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase):** Energy sensor; activated by low energy (↑ AMP/ATP ratio), exercise, metformin. Activates autophagy, inhibits mTOR → pro-longevity. Declines with age.
   - **Sirtuins (SIRT1-7):** NAD⁺-dependent deacetylases; activated by fasting, exercise, NAD⁺ precursors. Regulate DNA repair, inflammation, metabolism. SIRT1 overexpression extends mouse lifespan.
   - **Insulin/IGF-1 signaling (IIS):** Reduced IIS → extended lifespan (dramatic in worms: daf-2 mutants live 2x longer). Growth hormone receptor knockout mice live 40% longer. Human centenarians have IGF-1 polymorphisms. Paradox: low IGF-1 is pro-longevity but causes frailty/sarcopenia — balance matters.
7. **Mitochondrial dysfunction** — ↓ Electron transport chain efficiency → ↓ ATP + ↑ reactive oxygen species (ROS). Old "free radical theory of aging" (Harman, 1956): ROS damage drives aging. Now recognized as oversimplified — ROS also serve as signaling molecules (hormesis: mild stress → adaptive response). Mitochondrial DNA mutations accumulate (no histones, poor repair). Mitochondrial membrane potential declines. NAD⁺ levels drop 40-50% by age 60 (NAD⁺ required for sirtuin activity and electron transport).
8. **Cellular senescence** — Triggered by: telomere shortening, DNA damage, oncogene activation, oxidative stress. Senescent cells permanently exit cell cycle (beneficial: prevents cancer) but secrete SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype): IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α, matrix metalloproteinases → chronic inflammation, tissue remodeling, cancer promotion in neighbors. Senescent cells accumulate with age (~15% of cells in old tissue). Senolytics target these cells.

**Integrative Hallmarks (Downstream Consequences):**
9. **Stem cell exhaustion** — Hematopoietic, intestinal, neural, muscle (satellite cells) stem cells ↓ in number and function → impaired tissue regeneration. Stem cell niche deterioration contributes. Clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP): aging hematopoietic stem cells acquire mutations → associated with ↑ CVD and cancer risk.
10. **Altered intercellular communication** — Chronic low-grade inflammation ("inflammaging"): ↑ NF-κB, ↑ IL-6, ↑ TNF-α, ↑ CRP even without infection. Sources: senescent cells, visceral adipose tissue, gut barrier dysfunction, persistent viral reactivation (CMV). Immunosenescence: adaptive immunity declines (thymic involution → ↓ naive T-cells; exhausted T-cells accumulate), innate immunity becomes hyperactive. → Poor vaccine response in elderly, ↑ infection susceptibility, ↑ autoimmunity.
11. **Chronic inflammation** — Overlaps with #10; NF-κB pathway is master regulator. Anti-inflammatory interventions (exercise, caloric restriction, senolytics) all improve healthspan markers.
12. **Dysbiosis** — Gut microbiome diversity ↓ with age; ↑ pro-inflammatory species; ↓ butyrate-producing species; gut barrier integrity ↓ → bacterial translocation → systemic inflammation. Centenarians maintain more diverse microbiomes than typical elderly.

### Theories of Aging: Summary

| Theory | Core Idea | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Programmed aging | Aging is genetically programmed (like development) | Clock genes, epigenetic clocks, but no clear "aging program" gene |
| Damage/error accumulation | Random molecular damage accumulates beyond repair capacity | Strong evidence: DNA damage, oxidized proteins, lipofuscin |
| Free radical theory | ROS damage drives aging | Partially supported; antioxidant supplements don't extend lifespan though; ROS also serve as signals |
| Telomere theory | Telomere shortening limits cell division | Correlative; telomerase activation extends mouse lifespan but ↑ cancer |
| Inflammaging | Chronic inflammation drives age-related disease | Strong epidemiological evidence; anti-inflammatory interventions improve outcomes |
| Evolutionary | Aging occurs because natural selection is weak after reproductive age; genes beneficial early may be harmful late (antagonistic pleiotropy) | Best theoretical framework; explains why aging exists |

### Longevity Research

| Intervention | Mechanism | Evidence | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Caloric restriction (CR)** | ↓ mTOR, ↑ AMPK, ↑ sirtuins, ↑ autophagy, ↓ insulin/IGF-1, ↓ inflammation | Extends lifespan 20-50% in yeast, worms, flies, mice. Rhesus monkey studies: one showed ↑ lifespan, one didn't (different control diets). CALERIE human trial: 15% CR for 2 years → improved cardiometabolic markers, ↓ biological age. | Proven in model organisms; human healthspan benefits likely but compliance extremely difficult |
| **Rapamycin** | mTOR inhibitor → ↑ autophagy, ↓ cell growth, ↑ immune function (paradoxically — by clearing exhausted T-cells) | Most robust pharmacological lifespan extension in mice (~10-25%); effective even when started late in life. Improved vaccine response in elderly humans (Mannick et al., 2014). | Dog Aging Project testing in companion dogs; human trials for aging ongoing. Side effects at high doses: immunosuppression, metabolic (used in transplant medicine). Low-dose intermittent may be key. |
| **Metformin** | ↑ AMPK, ↓ mTOR, ↓ mitochondrial complex I (mild stress → hormesis), ↓ inflammation, altered gut microbiome | Epidemiological: diabetics on metformin live as long or longer than matched non-diabetics (Bannister et al., 2014). Animal data mixed (strain-dependent). | TAME trial (Targeting Aging with Metformin): 3,000 humans, 65-79, tracking age-related disease onset. Results expected ~2026-2027. If positive: first FDA-recognized aging intervention. |
| **Senolytics** (dasatinib + quercetin, fisetin, navitoclax) | Selectively kill senescent cells by targeting anti-apoptotic pathways (BCL-2 family). D+Q: dasatinib = tyrosine kinase inhibitor; quercetin = flavonoid (BCL-2 inhibitor). | Mice: improved physical function, extended healthspan and lifespan, ↓ age-related pathology. First human trial (Mayo Clinic, 2019): improved physical function in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis patients. | Multiple Phase I/II trials ongoing: Alzheimer's, diabetic kidney disease, frailty. Unity Biotechnology: UBX1325 for age-related macular degeneration. Intermittent dosing (hit-and-run) minimizes side effects. |
| **NAD⁺ precursors** (NMN, NR) | Restore NAD⁺ levels (decline ~50% by age 60; NAD⁺ required for sirtuin activity, DNA repair via PARPs, mitochondrial function). NMN/NR → NMN → NAD⁺ via salvage pathway. | Mice: improved metabolic function, exercise capacity, insulin sensitivity, cardiovascular function. Human trials: NR (CHROMAVIT): modest ↑ NAD⁺ levels but no clear functional benefits yet. NMN: small trials show ↑ NAD⁺, improved muscle insulin sensitivity. | Commercially available as supplements; human evidence for aging outcomes still limited. Key question: does raising NAD⁺ translate to healthspan/lifespan? David Sinclair lab has been primary champion (controversial — disclosed commercial interests). |
| **Exercise** | Most proven intervention for healthspan; affects virtually every hallmark: ↑ autophagy, ↑ mitochondrial biogenesis, ↑ AMPK/sirtuins, ↓ inflammation, ↑ telomere maintenance, ↑ stem cell function, ↑ BDNF (neuroplasticity), ↑ muscle/bone, ↓ insulin resistance | Reduces all-cause mortality 30-40%; ↓ risk of ~13 cancers; ↓ dementia ~30%; ↓ depression ~25-30%. Low VO₂ max = higher mortality risk than smoking. | Dose-response: any amount helps; more is better up to ~150-300 min/week moderate; beyond that, diminishing returns. Combination of aerobic + resistance = optimal. |
| **Yamanaka factor reprogramming** | Express OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, MYC transiently → reverse epigenetic age without full dedifferentiation. Partial reprogramming could rejuvenate tissues. | Mice: cyclic expression → improved function in aged tissues, extended lifespan in progeroid mice. Belmonte lab (2016, 2022). Altos Labs (billions in funding from Bezos et al.) pursuing this approach. | Very early; cancer risk from MYC (oncogene); duration/dosing of reprogramming factors critical. "Most exciting and most dangerous" approach in longevity. |
| **Young blood/plasma dilution** | Parabiosis experiments (2005, Conboy lab): young blood rejuvenates old tissues in mice. May be driven by ↓ harmful old blood factors rather than ↑ beneficial young factors. | Plasma dilution (saline + albumin replacement) in old mice → rejuvenated tissues comparable to young blood. Human equivalent: therapeutic plasma exchange. | Small human trials underway. GDF11, klotho, oxytocin identified as candidate rejuvenation factors. Commercial offerings (Ambrosia) were premature and FDA warned against. |

### Life Expectancy
- Global: ~73.4 years (2023); up from ~47 in 1950 (driven primarily by ↓ infant/child mortality, not ↑ max lifespan)
- Japan: ~84; Switzerland/Australia: ~83; US: ~77 (lower due to obesity, opioids, gun violence, healthcare access, inequality)
- Maximum human lifespan: ~120-122 years (Jeanne Calment, 1997). Disputed whether this is a hard limit or can be extended. Gompertz law: mortality rate increases exponentially with age, but may plateau after ~105 (debated).
- **Longevity escape velocity:** Concept (Aubrey de Grey): if medical advances add >1 year of life expectancy per calendar year, effective biological immortality. Currently adding ~0.2 years/year globally. Controversial/speculative.
- **Blue Zones:** Regions with unusually high centenarian rates (Okinawa, Sardinia, Ikaria, Loma Linda, Nicoya). Common features: plant-heavy diet, natural movement, social connection, purpose, moderate alcohol (wine), family. Some data quality concerns (Gerontology Research Group has questioned age verification in some zones).

## Exercise Physiology

### Cardiovascular Adaptation
- ↑ Stroke volume, ↑ cardiac output, ↓ resting HR (athlete: 40-60 bpm)
- ↑ Capillary density in muscle, ↑ oxygen extraction
- ↓ Blood pressure, ↑ HDL, ↓ triglycerides

### Muscle Adaptation
- **Hypertrophy:** Resistance training → muscle fiber thickening (primarily Type II)
- **Endurance:** Aerobic training → ↑ mitochondrial density, ↑ oxidative enzymes, ↑ glycogen storage
- **Protein synthesis:** Peaks ~24h post-exercise; requires adequate protein intake

### VO₂ Max
- Maximum oxygen consumption during exercise; gold standard of cardiorespiratory fitness
- Average untrained: ~35-45 mL/kg/min; elite endurance athletes: 70-90+
- Declines ~10% per decade after 30; exercise slows decline
- **Strong predictor of all-cause mortality** — low VO₂ max = higher risk than smoking, diabetes, or hypertension

### Exercise Recommendations (WHO)
- 150-300 min moderate OR 75-150 min vigorous aerobic activity per week
- Muscle-strengthening activities 2+ days per week
- Even small amounts of activity reduce mortality (dose-response, no minimum threshold)
